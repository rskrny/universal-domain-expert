# Shared Context: Mental Models

> Cross-domain thinking tools. These models apply regardless of which domain file
> is loaded. Load when facing complex, ambiguous, or high-stakes decisions.

---

## First Principles Thinking

**What:** Decompose a problem to its fundamental truths (things that cannot be
reduced further), then reason up from there. Strip away assumptions, conventions,
and analogies.

**Application protocol:**
1. Identify what you're trying to solve
2. List all assumptions you're making about it
3. For each assumption, ask: "Is this a fundamental truth, or is it conventional wisdom?"
4. Discard conventions. Keep only fundamentals.
5. Rebuild the solution from fundamentals only

**When to use:** When existing approaches feel wrong but you can't articulate why.
When entering a new domain. When conventional wisdom contradicts evidence.

**Watch out for:** It's slow. Don't use for routine decisions. Also, "first principles"
is often used to justify ignoring hard-won domain knowledge — that's not what this is.
Fundamental truths include established science and evidence, not just physics-level axioms.

## Inversion

**What:** Instead of asking "How do I achieve X?", ask "How would I guarantee failure
at X?" then avoid those things.

**Application protocol:**
1. Define the desired outcome
2. Ask: "What would make this fail with certainty?"
3. List all failure causes
4. Invert each: ensure your approach avoids every failure cause
5. What remains is more likely to succeed

**When to use:** When the direct path to success is unclear. When planning risk
mitigation. When reviewing a strategy or plan.

## Second-Order Thinking

**What:** First-order thinking asks "What happens next?" Second-order asks "And
then what happens after that?" Most people stop at first order.

**Application protocol:**
1. Identify the action or decision
2. List first-order consequences (what happens immediately)
3. For each first-order consequence, list second-order effects (what does THAT cause?)
4. Continue to third-order if the stakes warrant it
5. Look for: unintended consequences, feedback loops, cascade effects

**When to use:** Any strategic decision. Especially: pricing changes, organizational
restructuring, policy changes, market entry, competitive moves.

## Systems Thinking

**What:** See the interconnected whole rather than individual parts. Understand
feedback loops, delays, emergent behavior, and leverage points.

**Key concepts:**
- **Feedback loops:** Reinforcing (growth/decline) and balancing (stabilizing)
- **Delays:** Effects that lag behind causes (most strategy errors come from ignoring delays)
- **Leverage points:** Places where a small change produces a large effect
- **Emergent behavior:** System properties that don't exist in any individual part

**Application protocol:**
1. Map the system: what are the components and how do they interact?
2. Identify feedback loops: which are reinforcing, which are balancing?
3. Find delays: where does cause precede effect by weeks/months/years?
4. Locate leverage points: where can a small intervention change the system?
5. Test: what happens if you change one part? Trace through the system.

## Opportunity Cost

**What:** The value of the next best alternative you give up when making a choice.
Every decision has a cost — even if no money is spent.

**Application protocol:**
1. Identify the decision and the chosen option
2. Identify the best alternative option(s) you're giving up
3. Quantify what you lose by not pursuing the alternative
4. Compare: is what you gain worth what you give up?

**Critical insight:** Opportunity cost applies to time, attention, and organizational
focus — not just money. The most expensive resource in any organization is leadership
attention.

---

## The Map Is Not the Territory

**What:** Models, frameworks, and plans are simplifications of reality. They are
useful precisely because they leave things out. But never confuse the model with
the real thing.

**Application:**
- Every framework in this system is a map, not the territory
- When reality contradicts the framework, trust reality
- Use frameworks to structure thinking, not to replace it
- Always ask: "What does this framework NOT capture?"

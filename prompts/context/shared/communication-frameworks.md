# Shared Context: Communication Frameworks

> Cross-domain communication structures. These frameworks apply to any deliverable
> that requires clear, persuasive, structured communication.

---

## The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto) — Detailed

### The Core Idea
Start with the answer. Then support it. Audiences want the conclusion first, then
the evidence. This is the opposite of how most people naturally communicate
(situation → analysis → conclusion). Invert it.

### Structure
```
Governing Thought (your main point / recommendation)
├── Key Argument 1
│   ├── Supporting evidence
│   ├── Supporting evidence
│   └── Supporting evidence
├── Key Argument 2
│   ├── Supporting evidence
│   ├── Supporting evidence
│   └── Supporting evidence
└── Key Argument 3
    ├── Supporting evidence
    ├── Supporting evidence
    └── Supporting evidence
```

### Rules
1. **Ideas at any level must be summaries of the ideas below them**
2. **Ideas within a group must be MECE** (no overlaps, no gaps)
3. **Ideas within a group must be logically ordered:**
   - Deductive: major premise → minor premise → conclusion
   - Inductive: grouped by similarity, then ordered by importance or chronology
4. **The governing thought must answer the question the audience is asking**

### Application by Audience
- **Time-poor executives:** Read only the governing thought + key arguments (top 2 levels)
- **Decision-makers:** Read through the evidence level for the arguments relevant to their decision
- **Implementers:** Read everything, including appendices

## SCQA Framework (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer)

### When to Use
Opening any document, presentation, or conversation where you need to orient the
audience before delivering your point.

### Structure
- **Situation:** What we all agree on (shared context, facts not in dispute)
- **Complication:** What has changed, what's wrong, what's at risk
- **Question:** The question this naturally raises (the one the audience is now asking)
- **Answer:** Your governing thought (which then unfolds via the Pyramid)

### Example
- S: "Company X has grown revenue 20% YoY for three years through direct sales."
- C: "Growth is decelerating — last quarter was 12% — and our largest competitor
  just raised $200M to undercut our pricing."
- Q: "How do we sustain 20%+ growth in this new competitive environment?"
- A: "We recommend a three-pronged approach: expand into adjacent markets,
  launch a mid-market product tier, and build a partner channel."

## Assertion-Evidence Structure

### When to Use
Individual slides, sections, or paragraphs within a larger document.

### Structure
- **Assertion:** A complete, declarative sentence stating the point (NOT a topic label)
  - Bad: "Market Analysis"
  - Good: "The mid-market segment is growing 3x faster than enterprise and is underserved"
- **Evidence:** Data, charts, examples, or logic that prove the assertion

### Rule
If you can't write the assertion as a complete sentence, you don't know what
point you're making. Figure that out before building the evidence.

## The Rule of Three

### What
Humans process information most effectively in groups of three. Three arguments,
three recommendations, three data points. Not two (feels thin), not four (feels
like a list), but three.

### Application
- Structure recommendations in groups of three
- Support each argument with three pieces of evidence
- Use three scenarios (base, optimistic, pessimistic)
- When you have more than three items, group them into three categories

### Exception
When accuracy requires more or fewer items, don't force it. The Rule of Three is
a communication heuristic, not a truth about the universe. Five real arguments
beats three padded ones.

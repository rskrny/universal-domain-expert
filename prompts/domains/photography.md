## Core Frameworks

> These frameworks are decision tools for real shooting situations. They work under
> pressure, in bad light, and when clients are waiting.

### Framework 1: Exposure Decision Framework
**What:** A systematic approach to choosing aperture, shutter speed, and ISO based
on the creative intent and environmental constraints of the shot.
**When to use:** Every single shot. This is the foundation. Even in auto modes,
understanding why the camera chose what it chose requires this framework.
**How to apply:**
1. Identify the creative priority. What matters most in this image?
   - **Subject isolation** (blurred background) -> aperture is primary. Open wide
     (f/1.4 to f/2.8). Shutter and ISO follow.
   - **Motion control** (freeze or blur) -> shutter speed is primary. Fast shutter
     (1/500+) to freeze. Slow shutter (1/30 or slower) to blur. Aperture and ISO follow.
   - **Maximum sharpness** (landscape, architecture) -> aperture is primary.
     Sweet spot (f/8 to f/11). Use tripod if shutter drops.
   - **Low light survival** -> ISO tolerance is primary. Push ISO as high as
     acceptable, open aperture, slow shutter to minimum handheld speed.
2. Set the primary variable based on creative priority.
3. Set the secondary variable to achieve correct exposure.
4. Adjust ISO to balance the equation. Keep ISO as low as possible unless
   noise is acceptable or intentional.
5. Check the result. Histogram should show data across the range without
   clipping highlights (unless intentional).
**Common misapplication:** Treating ISO as something to fear. Modern sensors
(2020+) produce clean images at ISO 3200-6400. A sharp image at ISO 6400
beats a blurry image at ISO 100 every time. Noise is fixable. Motion blur
from a slow shutter is permanent.

### Framework 2: The Exposure Triangle Deep Dive
**What:** The three interdependent variables that control how much light reaches
the sensor and how that light is rendered.
**When to use:** Understanding this framework is prerequisite to everything else.
**How to apply:**

**Aperture (f-stop):**
- Controls depth of field and lens sharpness
- Wide open (f/1.4, f/1.8, f/2.0): shallow depth of field, more light, slightly
  softer (on most lenses)
- Mid-range (f/5.6, f/8): sharpest zone for most lenses ("sweet spot")
- Closed down (f/16, f/22): deep depth of field, less light, diffraction softening
  begins around f/16 on full frame
- Each full stop (f/1.4 -> f/2 -> f/2.8 -> f/4 -> f/5.6 -> f/8 -> f/11 -> f/16)
  halves the light

**Shutter Speed:**
- Controls motion rendering and handheld stability
- Reciprocal rule: minimum handheld speed = 1/(focal length). 50mm lens = 1/50s
  minimum. Image stabilization buys 2-5 stops.
- 1/1000s+: freezes fast action (sports, birds, kids)
- 1/250s: general handheld shooting with moderate focal lengths
- 1/60s: minimum for handheld without stabilization on standard lenses
- 1/15s to 1s: intentional motion blur, requires tripod or stabilization
- 1s to 30s: long exposure (water smoothing, light trails, star points)
- 30s+: bulb mode (star trails, extreme long exposure with ND filters)

**ISO:**
- Controls sensor sensitivity (amplification of signal)
- Base ISO (100 or 200): cleanest image, maximum dynamic range
- ISO 400-1600: minimal noise on modern cameras, safe for most uses
- ISO 3200-6400: visible noise begins, still very usable on modern full frame
- ISO 12800+: heavy noise, acceptable for photojournalism and documentation,
  aggressive noise reduction needed for commercial use
- Each doubling (100 -> 200 -> 400 -> 800) doubles sensitivity, allows one
  stop faster shutter or one stop smaller aperture
**Common misapplication:** Chasing the "lowest ISO possible" at the expense of
shutter speed. A noisy sharp photo is always better than a clean blurry one.
The second mistake: ignoring diffraction. Stopping down past f/16 on full frame
(f/11 on APS-C) actually reduces sharpness.

### Framework 3: Lighting Setup Selection
**What:** A decision tree for choosing the right lighting approach based on
subject, mood, environment, and available equipment.
**When to use:** Any time you are adding or modifying light. This covers
natural light manipulation through full studio setups.
**How to apply:**
1. **Assess available light first.**
   - What direction is it coming from?
   - What quality is it? (hard/direct vs. soft/diffused)
   - What color temperature? (warm golden hour vs. cool overcast vs. mixed indoor)
   - Is it sufficient for the exposure you need?
2. **Define the mood.**
   - High key (bright, airy, minimal shadows) -> large, soft light sources,
     fill shadows, white or light backgrounds
   - Low key (dramatic, moody, deep shadows) -> smaller light sources, minimal
     fill, dark backgrounds, controlled spill
   - Natural and authentic -> work with existing light, modify minimally,
     use reflectors before adding artificial light
3. **Choose the light source type.**
   - Natural light only: reflectors, diffusers, scrims, window positioning
   - Speedlight (portable flash): on-camera or off-camera, 1-3 lights,
     battery powered, good for location work
   - Studio strobe: higher power, consistent color, modeling light for preview,
     best for controlled environments
   - Continuous light (LED panels, tubes): what you see is what you get,
     great for beginners and video/photo hybrid, lower power than strobe
4. **Select modifiers based on quality needed.**
   - Softbox (rectangular): even, controllable soft light, great for portraits
     and products
   - Octabox (octagonal softbox): round catchlights, flattering for portraits
   - Beauty dish: slightly harder than softbox, defined shadow edges, classic
     fashion look
   - Umbrella (shoot-through or reflective): broad, soft, inexpensive, less
     controllable spill
   - Strip box: narrow light, good for rim/edge lighting and products
   - Grid/snoot: focused, controlled beam, minimal spill, good for hair
     lights and background accents
   - Barn doors: controllable spill on continuous lights or strobes
5. **Place lights using established patterns** (see Portrait Lighting Patterns
   below for people, Product Lighting Setup for objects).
**Common misapplication:** Defaulting to complex multi-light setups when a
single well-placed light with a reflector would produce a better result. The
best photographers master one-light setups before adding complexity.

### Framework 4: Composition Checklist
**What:** A systematic evaluation of visual elements in the frame before and
during capture.
**When to use:** Every shot. Run this mentally in 2-3 seconds. With practice
it becomes instinctive.
**How to apply:**
1. **Subject placement.** Where is the subject in the frame?
   - Rule of thirds: place key elements on the intersection points of a 3x3 grid
   - Golden ratio (phi grid): slightly off-center placement, more dynamic
     than centered, less rigid than rule of thirds
   - Center composition: works for symmetry, reflections, and formal portraits
   - Edge placement: creates tension, works for editorial and fine art
2. **Leading lines.** Do lines in the scene guide the eye toward the subject?
   - Roads, fences, rivers, shadows, architecture, body lines
   - Diagonal lines create energy. Horizontal lines create calm. Vertical
     lines create strength.
   - S-curves are the most pleasing leading line in landscape photography
3. **Framing.** Is there a natural frame around the subject?
   - Doorways, windows, arches, tree branches, foreground elements
   - Frames create depth and draw attention inward
4. **Negative space.** Is there intentional empty space?
   - Negative space gives the subject room to breathe
   - Useful for text placement in commercial and editorial work
   - More negative space = more minimalist, contemplative feel
5. **Depth.** Does the image have foreground, middle ground, and background?
   - Layering creates three-dimensionality in a 2D medium
   - Use aperture to control which layers are sharp
   - Overlapping elements reinforce depth perception
6. **Balance and visual weight.** Does the image feel balanced?
   - Large objects, bright areas, and high-contrast elements carry more visual weight
   - Balance can be symmetrical (formal) or asymmetrical (dynamic)
   - A small bright element can balance a large dark area
7. **Simplification.** What can be removed from the frame?
   - If an element does not serve the image, exclude it
   - Move your feet. Change your angle. Wait for distractions to clear.
   - The strongest compositions are often the simplest
8. **Edge check.** What is happening at the edges of the frame?
   - Watch for distracting elements entering the frame at edges
   - Avoid cutting people at joints (wrists, ankles, knees, neck)
   - Ensure nothing important is clipped that should be included
**Common misapplication:** Applying the rule of thirds mechanically to every
image. The rules are starting points. Centered compositions, edge placements,
and broken "rules" create some of the most powerful images. Learn the rules,
then learn when to break them intentionally.

### Framework 5: Portrait Lighting Patterns
**What:** Five classical lighting patterns for portraiture, each creating a
different mood and facial modeling.
**When to use:** Any portrait session. Choose the pattern based on face shape,
mood, and intent.
**How to apply:**

**Rembrandt Lighting:**
- Key light 45 degrees to one side, slightly above eye level
- Creates a triangle of light on the shadow side of the face (under the eye)
- Named after the painter who used this pattern extensively
- Mood: dramatic, painterly, classic
- Best for: angular faces, editorial, character portraits
- The triangle should be no wider than the eye and no longer than the nose

**Butterfly (Paramount) Lighting:**
- Key light directly in front of the subject, above eye level
- Creates a butterfly-shaped shadow under the nose
- Mood: glamorous, beauty, fashion
- Best for: women's beauty portraits, high cheekbones, symmetrical faces
- Add a reflector below the chin to fill shadows (clamshell setup)

**Split Lighting:**
- Key light at 90 degrees to one side
- Lights exactly half the face, other half in shadow
- Mood: dramatic, mysterious, edgy
- Best for: editorial, moody portraits, character studies
- Hardest pattern to make flattering. Use with intention.

**Loop Lighting:**
- Key light 30-45 degrees to one side, slightly above eye level
- Creates a small shadow of the nose on the opposite cheek
- Shadow does not touch the shadow of the cheek (that would be Rembrandt)
- Mood: natural, approachable, versatile
- Best for: general portraiture, corporate headshots, the most universally
  flattering pattern
- This is the workhorse pattern. When in doubt, start here.

**Broad vs. Short Lighting (modifier, not a pattern):**
- Broad: the lit side of the face is closest to camera. Opens up the face,
  makes it appear wider. Good for narrow faces.
- Short: the shadow side of the face is closest to camera. Slims the face,
  adds depth and drama. More commonly used.
- Apply broad or short to any of the patterns above.
**Common misapplication:** Obsessing over pattern names during a session instead
of watching the actual light on the face. Move the light, watch the shadows,
and adjust. The patterns are reference points for communication and planning.
In the moment, trust your eyes.

### Framework 6: Product Photography Workflow
**What:** End-to-end process for creating product images that serve ecommerce
and marketing needs.
**When to use:** Any commercial product shoot, from Amazon listings to brand
campaigns.
**How to apply:**
1. **Brief and shot list.** Before touching a camera:
   - What platform are the images for? (Amazon has specific requirements.
     Shopify is flexible. Social media needs different crops.)
   - How many SKUs? How many angles per SKU?
   - White background, lifestyle, or both?
   - Any mandatory views? (front, back, detail, in-use, scale reference)
   - What is the hero image? (The one image that must be perfect.)
2. **Set design and styling.**
   - White background: use a sweep (seamless paper or acrylic) with even lighting
   - Lifestyle: choose props that tell the story without competing with the product
   - Flatlay: plan the layout on paper first. Grid or organic arrangement.
   - Food: prep the hero dish last. Food has a 5-10 minute window before it
     degrades visually. Have stand-ins for lighting setup.
3. **Lighting for products.**
   - White background: two lights on background (one stop brighter than subject
     light), one key light on product with appropriate modifier, fill as needed
   - Reflective products (jewelry, glass, metal): use a light tent or carefully
     placed cards. Light the environment, not the product directly.
   - Matte products (fabric, paper, food): direct soft light works well. Add
     texture with slightly harder side light.
   - Translucent products (drinks, bottles): backlight to show color and
     transparency. Add front/side light for label detail.
4. **Camera settings for products.**
   - Aperture: f/8 to f/11 for maximum sharpness across the product
   - ISO: base ISO (100 or 200). Always. You are on a tripod.
   - Shutter speed: whatever the exposure requires. You are on a tripod.
   - Focus: manual focus or live view magnified focus on the critical detail
   - Shoot tethered to a laptop when possible for immediate review
5. **Post-processing for products.**
   - Background removal or cleanup (Photoshop pen tool or AI-assisted)
   - Color accuracy check against physical product (use a color checker in
     reference shots)
   - Consistent shadow style across the product line
   - Output in required formats and sizes for each platform
**Common misapplication:** Rushing the lighting setup. Product photography is
90% lighting and 10% clicking the shutter. Spending an extra 30 minutes on
lighting saves hours of retouching.

### Framework 7: Post-Processing Workflow
**What:** A structured approach to editing that maintains consistency and
efficiency across large volumes of images.
**When to use:** Every editing session. The workflow prevents both under-editing
and over-editing.
**How to apply:**
1. **Ingest and backup.**
   - Import to Lightroom, Capture One, or your DAM (digital asset manager)
   - Verify backup to secondary drive before deleting from cards
   - Apply metadata: date, client, project, photographer credit
   - Apply copyright information in metadata
2. **Culling (selection).**
   - First pass: reject obvious failures (out of focus, bad exposure, closed eyes)
   - Second pass: rate remaining images 1-5 stars or use color labels
   - Select final deliverables. For portraits, aim to deliver 20-30% of total shots.
     For events, 10-15%. For products, 100% of shot list.
   - Do not spend more than 2-3 seconds per image on the first cull pass
3. **Global adjustments (Lightroom/Capture One).**
   - White balance: correct to neutral or set creative intent
   - Exposure: set overall brightness using histogram
   - Highlights and shadows: recover detail in both directions
   - Whites and blacks: set the dynamic range endpoints
   - Contrast: add or reduce based on mood
   - Clarity/texture: add midtone contrast for detail or reduce for softness
   - Vibrance (preferred) over saturation for natural color enhancement
4. **Color grading.**
   - HSL (Hue, Saturation, Luminance) adjustments for individual color control
   - Split toning or color wheels for shadow/midtone/highlight color shifts
   - Develop a signature look and save it as a preset
   - Consistency across a set matters more than perfection on a single image
5. **Local adjustments.**
   - Dodge and burn for directing attention
   - Graduated filters for skies and foregrounds
   - Radial filters for vignettes and local emphasis
   - Spot removal for sensor dust and small blemishes
6. **Retouching (Photoshop, for images that need it).**
   - Frequency separation for skin retouching (preserve texture, smooth tone)
   - Dodge and burn on a 50% gray layer for precise light sculpting
   - Liquify with restraint for subtle proportion adjustments
   - Compositing for sky replacements, object removal, or creative edits
   - Healing and clone stamp for cleanup
7. **Export and delivery.**
   - Full resolution TIFF or PSD for print
   - High-quality JPEG (quality 85-95) for web at appropriate dimensions
   - sRGB color space for web delivery
   - Adobe RGB or ProPhoto RGB for print
   - Watermark if delivering proofs
   - Organize deliverables in client-facing folder structure
**Common misapplication:** Editing each image from scratch instead of using
presets and sync. Edit one image to perfection, sync settings to similar images,
then fine-tune individually. This is 5x faster than starting fresh each time.
The second mistake: over-retouching skin until it looks plastic. Skin has
texture. Preserve it.

### Framework 8: Color Management Framework
**What:** The system that ensures colors look consistent from capture through
editing to final output (screen or print).
**When to use:** Always. Color management is invisible when done right and
catastrophic when ignored.
**How to apply:**
1. **Capture.**
   - Shoot a color checker (X-Rite ColorChecker or similar) in the first frame
     of each lighting setup
   - Set white balance correctly in camera or shoot RAW and correct in post
   - RAW files preserve maximum color data. Always shoot RAW for professional work.
2. **Monitor calibration.**
   - Calibrate your editing monitor monthly using a hardware calibrator
     (X-Rite i1Display, Datacolor SpyderX, or Calibrite ColorChecker Display)
   - Target: D65 white point, 120 cd/m2 brightness, gamma 2.2
   - A $200 calibrator is more valuable than a $2000 uncalibrated monitor
   - Edit in a controlled lighting environment. Avoid direct light on screen.
3. **Editing color space.**
   - Edit in ProPhoto RGB or Adobe RGB for maximum gamut
   - Convert to sRGB only on export for web
   - Convert to the printer's ICC profile for print output
   - Soft-proof in Lightroom or Photoshop before printing
4. **Output.**
   - Web: sRGB, 72-150 PPI (resolution doesn't matter for screen, but convention
     is 72 or 150)
   - Print (inkjet fine art): Adobe RGB or printer ICC profile, 300 PPI, 16-bit
     TIFF preferred
   - Print (commercial offset): CMYK conversion using the printer's ICC profile,
     300 PPI, check total ink coverage limits
   - Large format: 150-200 PPI is sufficient for viewing distances over 3 feet
**Common misapplication:** Editing on an uncalibrated laptop screen and wondering
why prints look different. The monitor is lying to you. Calibrate it. The second
mistake: delivering sRGB files for high-end print work. sRGB clips colors that
Adobe RGB and ProPhoto RGB can represent.

### Framework 9: Gear Selection by Scenario
**What:** A practical decision matrix for choosing camera bodies, lenses, and
accessories based on the shooting scenario.
**When to use:** Planning for a shoot, advising on gear purchases, or building
a kit.
**How to apply:**

**Portrait photography:**
- Body: full frame mirrorless (Sony a7 IV, Canon R6 III, Nikon Z6 III)
- Primary lens: 85mm f/1.4 or f/1.8 (the portrait king)
- Secondary: 35mm f/1.4 for environmental portraits, 70-200mm f/2.8 for
  compression and working distance
- Lighting: one strobe with a large softbox or octabox, reflector, and
  optional second light for hair/rim

**Wedding photography:**
- Body: two full frame mirrorless bodies (redundancy is mandatory)
- Primary: 24-70mm f/2.8 (80% of the day)
- Secondary: 70-200mm f/2.8 (ceremony, candids)
- Third: 35mm f/1.4 or 50mm f/1.4 (reception, low light)
- Flash: two speedlights with off-camera capability, one on-camera for
  bounce flash
- Backup: extra batteries (10+), extra memory cards, rain cover

**Landscape photography:**
- Body: high-resolution full frame or medium format (Sony a7R V, Fujifilm
  GFX 100S, Nikon Z8)
- Primary: 16-35mm f/4 or equivalent ultra-wide
- Secondary: 70-200mm f/4 for compressed landscapes and details
- Tripod: carbon fiber, rated for 2x your heaviest setup
- Filters: circular polarizer (always in the bag), ND filters (6-stop and
  10-stop for long exposure), graduated ND (2-stop and 3-stop soft grad)
- Remote shutter release or 2-second timer

**Product photography:**
- Body: any modern mirrorless or DSLR (resolution over speed)
- Primary: 90-105mm macro for small products, 50mm for larger items
- Tripod: sturdy with geared head for precise positioning
- Lighting: two strobes minimum, softboxes, a white sweep or table,
  reflector cards (white and black)
- Tethering cable and laptop

**Street photography:**
- Body: small mirrorless (Fujifilm X100VI, Ricoh GR III, Sony a7C)
- Lens: 28mm or 35mm equivalent (fixed lens cameras excel here)
- No flash (stay invisible), no tripod
- Minimal gear. One camera, one lens. Speed and discretion matter.

**Event photography:**
- Body: two fast-autofocus mirrorless bodies
- Primary: 24-70mm f/2.8
- Secondary: 70-200mm f/2.8
- Wide: 16-35mm f/2.8 for venue shots and group photos
- Flash: speedlight with diffuser, spare batteries
- High ISO capability is critical (ISO 6400+ must be usable)

**Budget tiers for a starting kit:**

| Budget | Body | Lens | Lighting |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| $500-1000 | Used Sony a6000/a6100 or Canon M50 | Kit zoom + 50mm f/1.8 | Reflector + window light |
| $1000-2000 | Sony a6400, Canon R10, Nikon Z30 | 35mm f/1.8 + kit zoom | Godox TT600 speedlight + modifier |
| $2000-4000 | Sony a7 III (used), Canon R6 (used), Nikon Z5 | 50mm f/1.8 + 85mm f/1.8 | Godox AD200 + softbox + stand |
| $4000-7000 | Sony a7 IV, Canon R6 III, Nikon Z6 III | 24-70 f/2.8 + 85mm f/1.8 | Godox AD300 + two modifiers |

**Common misapplication:** Buying gear you don't need yet. A $500 camera with
great light and strong composition beats a $5000 camera with bad light and no
composition. Invest in lenses before bodies. Invest in lighting before lenses.
Invest in education before any of it.

### Framework 10: Portfolio Curation Strategy
**What:** The process of selecting and organizing images to represent your work
at its strongest.
**When to use:** Building or updating a portfolio website, preparing for client
pitches, or applying for representation.
**How to apply:**
1. **Ruthless selection.** Show only your best 15-30 images per genre. More
   is not better. Your portfolio is only as strong as your weakest image.
2. **Consistency of quality.** Every image must be at the same quality level.
   One mediocre image among great ones damages credibility more than having
   a smaller portfolio.
3. **Sequence matters.** Open strong. Close strong. The middle can carry
   the narrative. First and last images are remembered most.
4. **Show what you want to shoot.** If you want to shoot commercial beauty,
   show commercial beauty. If you show weddings, you will get hired for weddings.
   Your portfolio is a magnet for the work it contains.
5. **Genre separation.** Create separate portfolios or sections for distinct
   genres. A client looking for product photography does not want to scroll
   through your landscape work.
6. **Update regularly.** Remove older work as new work surpasses it. A portfolio
   that hasn't changed in 2 years signals stagnation.
7. **Get outside feedback.** You are too close to your own work. Show your
   portfolio to other photographers, art directors, and clients. Listen to
   what they say about weak images. They are right and you are wrong about
   your favorites.
**Common misapplication:** Including images because of the effort they took
rather than the quality they achieved. A technically difficult shot that
doesn't look impressive to a viewer does nothing for your portfolio. The
viewer doesn't know (or care) how hard it was.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: Natural Light vs. Artificial Light

**Consider:**
- What is the quality and direction of available natural light?
- Does the scene require controllable, repeatable lighting?
- Is the shoot location indoors or outdoors?
- Does the creative brief call for a natural or polished look?
- Is there power available for strobes?
- How many setups are needed in how much time?

**Default recommendation:** Start with natural light. Modify with reflectors
and diffusers first. Add artificial light only when natural light cannot achieve
the creative goal or when repeatability is required (product photography,
headshot sessions with many subjects).

**Override conditions:** When the shoot requires consistent lighting across
many subjects or setups (school portraits, corporate headshots, product lines),
artificial light is mandatory from the start. When shooting in a windowless
space or at night, artificial light is the only option.

### Decision Type: Prime Lens vs. Zoom Lens

**Consider:**
- Do you need to change focal lengths quickly? (event, documentary -> zoom)
- Is maximum aperture critical? (low light, shallow DOF -> prime)
- Is weight/size a priority? (travel, street -> prime)
- Is image quality the top priority at a specific focal length? (commercial -> prime)
- How many lenses can you carry? (solo shooter -> fewer zooms cover more range)

**Default recommendation:** Own a versatile zoom (24-70mm f/2.8) for flexibility
and at least one fast prime (50mm or 85mm f/1.4-f/1.8) for low light and
subject isolation. Zooms for coverage, primes for quality and creative control.

**Override conditions:** When budget is very tight, a single 50mm f/1.8 ($100-250)
outperforms any zoom in its price range. When shooting wildlife or sports, long
zooms (100-400mm, 200-600mm) are mandatory and primes at those focal lengths
cost $6000+.

### Decision Type: RAW vs. JPEG

**Consider:**
- Is post-processing required? (RAW gives maximum flexibility)
- Is storage space or file size a constraint?
- Does the workflow require immediate delivery? (JPEG is faster)
- Is the photographer skilled in RAW processing?
- Are exposure or white balance conditions unpredictable?

**Default recommendation:** Shoot RAW always for professional work. Shoot
RAW+JPEG if you need immediate sharing (social media during events). Shoot
JPEG only for casual documentation where post-processing is not planned and
storage is genuinely constrained.

**Override conditions:** Sports photographers on tight deadlines often shoot
JPEG with carefully dialed-in camera profiles for direct-from-camera delivery.
Photo booth setups typically use JPEG for speed.

### Decision Type: Pricing Model Selection

**Consider:**
- What is the genre? (wedding, commercial, portrait, event)
- What does the client value? (time, images, usage rights, exclusivity)
- What is the local market rate?
- What is your cost of doing business? (CODB: gear, insurance, software,
  education, marketing, taxes, profit margin)
- Is this a one-time project or ongoing relationship?

**Default recommendation:** Price based on the value delivered and usage rights
granted. Never price by the hour alone for creative work. Include licensing
terms in every quote.

**Override conditions:** For high-volume, repeatable work (school portraits,
real estate photography), per-unit or per-image pricing makes sense. For
editorial work, day rates are the industry standard.

**Pricing models by genre:**
- **Wedding:** Package-based. Base package includes coverage time + defined
  deliverables. Additional hours, albums, prints, and second shooter as add-ons.
  Typical range: $2000-8000+ for US markets.
- **Commercial/advertising:** Day rate + licensing fee. Day rate covers
  the photographer's time. Licensing fee covers usage rights (duration, territory,
  media). Licensing often exceeds the day rate. Day rates: $1500-5000+.
- **Portrait (personal):** Session fee + print/digital sales. Session fee covers
  the shoot time. Revenue comes from print or digital package sales.
  Session fees: $150-500+. Packages: $300-2000+.
- **Product (ecommerce):** Per-image pricing for white background. Day rate
  for lifestyle. Per-image: $25-100+ depending on complexity and volume.
  Day rate: $1000-3000+.
- **Event:** Half-day or full-day rate with defined deliverables. Half-day:
  $500-1500. Full-day: $1000-3000+.
- **Real estate:** Per-property pricing. Includes defined number of images,
  optional drone, optional video. Per property: $150-500+.

### Decision Type: When to Upgrade Gear

**Consider:**
- Is the current gear preventing you from getting shots you need?
- Is the gear unreliable? (misfires, failing autofocus, shutter count near limit)
- Has the technology gap become a competitive disadvantage?
- Will the upgrade generate more revenue than its cost within 12 months?
- Is this a want or a need?

**Default recommendation:** Upgrade when gear is the bottleneck, not when
marketing makes you want something new. A body upgrade every 4-5 years is
reasonable for working professionals. Lenses last 10+ years.

**Override conditions:** When gear fails during a paid shoot, replace it
immediately. When a client specifically requires capabilities your current
gear lacks (higher resolution, specific video specs, weather sealing),
upgrade or rent for the job.

## Quality Standards

### The Photography Quality Bar

Every delivered image must pass three tests:

1. **Technical quality.** Sharp focus on the intended subject. Correct exposure
   (or intentionally creative exposure). Accurate or intentionally styled color.
   No distracting artifacts (noise, chromatic aberration, sensor dust, banding).

2. **Compositional strength.** The image has a clear subject. The eye moves
   through the frame intentionally. There are no distracting elements that the
   photographer failed to notice. The crop serves the image.

3. **Emotional or functional impact.** The image evokes a feeling (for editorial,
   portrait, fine art) or clearly communicates product information (for commercial).
   An image that is technically perfect and compositionally sound but says nothing
   is a failure.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Portrait deliverables:**
- Must include: flattering light on face, sharp focus on near eye, intentional
  background treatment, consistent editing across the set, appropriate retouching
  (blemish removal, skin smoothing without plastic appearance)
- Must avoid: unflattering angles, dead eyes (no catchlight), over-retouching,
  inconsistent skin tone across images, visible flyaway hairs in commercial work
- Gold standard: the subject looks like the best version of themselves. Natural,
  authentic, and confident. The light sculpts the face. The background supports
  the story.

**Product deliverables:**
- Must include: accurate color representation, sharp detail on key features,
  consistent lighting and shadow style across all products, clean background,
  proper clipping path or mask if required
- Must avoid: color casts, reflections showing studio equipment, inconsistent
  shadow direction, soft focus on product details, visible dust or fingerprints
- Gold standard: the product looks exactly as it does in person (or slightly
  better). A customer who buys based on the image is not disappointed by the
  real product. Every product in the line has identical lighting treatment.

**Landscape deliverables:**
- Must include: strong composition with clear foreground-to-background depth,
  appropriate dynamic range (no blown highlights or crushed shadows unless
  intentional), level horizon (or intentionally tilted), considered color palette
- Must avoid: over-saturated skies, halos from aggressive HDR processing,
  crooked horizons, blown-out clouds, muddy shadow detail
- Gold standard: the image transports the viewer to the location. The light
  feels authentic. The composition reveals something about the place that a
  snapshot would miss.

**Wedding deliverables:**
- Must include: key moments (first look, ceremony, first kiss, first dance,
  family formals, details, venue), consistent editing style across entire day,
  mix of candid and posed, flattering images of all family members
- Must avoid: missing must-have moments, inconsistent white balance across
  images, unflattering candids delivered without review, over-processing that
  dates the images within 2 years
- Gold standard: the couple relives their day through the images. The story
  flows chronologically. The editing style is timeless. Every formal portrait
  is technically flawless. Candids capture genuine emotion.

**Event deliverables:**
- Must include: venue/atmosphere shots, key speakers/performers, audience
  engagement, branding/signage, candid interactions, group shots
- Must avoid: dark/underexposed images in challenging venues, only shooting
  from one position, missing key moments, red-eye in flash photos
- Gold standard: someone who wasn't there understands the energy and purpose
  of the event through the images alone

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)
- [ ] Focus is tack sharp on the intended subject (zoom to 100% to verify)
- [ ] Exposure is correct with full histogram data (no unintentional clipping)
- [ ] White balance is accurate or intentionally styled
- [ ] Composition has a clear subject and intentional structure
- [ ] No distracting elements in the frame (check edges and background)
- [ ] Color is consistent across the delivered set
- [ ] Retouching is invisible (the edit should not be visible to the viewer)
- [ ] Output is in the correct color space for delivery medium
- [ ] File naming follows the agreed convention
- [ ] Metadata includes copyright and contact information
- [ ] Image resolution meets the delivery platform requirements
- [ ] Backup of all RAW files and edits is confirmed

## Communication Standards

### Structure

Photography communication varies by context:

**Client proposals:** Lead with the deliverable and its value. Then supporting
details (timeline, logistics, pricing). End with terms and call to action.
The client wants to know what they get and what it costs before anything else.

**Shot lists:** Organized by location or time block. Each entry includes: shot
description, lighting notes, lens/focal length, and priority (must-have vs.
nice-to-have). Keep it scannable.

**Image delivery:** Organized in clearly labeled folders. Include a README or
delivery note explaining what's included, usage rights, and how to download
full resolution.

**Technical instruction:** Show, then explain. Lead with a visual example
(reference image, lighting diagram, settings screenshot). Follow with the
explanation of why and how.

### Tone

- **Confident and practical** -- speak from experience, offer clear
  recommendations, avoid hedging when you know the answer
- **Visual language** -- use concrete visual descriptions. "A warm directional
  light from camera left at 45 degrees" is better than "nice lighting"
- **Direct about quality** -- if an image has a problem, name it specifically
  and offer the fix. "The shadow under the chin is too harsh. Move the key
  light up 6 inches and add a white reflector below" is better than "the
  lighting could be improved"
- **Respectful of all skill levels** -- explain fundamentals without
  condescension. Offer advanced techniques without gatekeeping.

### Audience Adaptation

**For professional photographers:**
- Use technical shorthand freely (stop, EV, f-number, color temp in Kelvin)
- Focus on the "why" behind decisions, not the "how" of basic operations
- Reference specific gear by model name
- Discuss business operations candidly (pricing, client management, industry rates)

**For enthusiast/hobbyist photographers:**
- Define technical terms on first use
- Provide specific settings as starting points
- Explain the creative reasoning behind technical choices
- Recommend gear at accessible price points first

**For non-photographer clients:**
- No technical jargon. Describe outcomes and feelings.
- "We will create bright, clean images on white backgrounds" instead of
  "We will shoot at f/8 on seamless with two-stop overexposed background strobes"
- Focus on what the images will do for their business or personal goals
- Provide visual references (mood boards, example images) instead of
  technical specifications

### Language Conventions

**Use:** "stop" (unit of exposure change), "key light" (main light source),
"fill" (shadow-side illumination), "rim/edge light" (backlight outlining
subject), "catchlight" (reflection in the eye), "bokeh" (quality of
out-of-focus rendering), "dynamic range" (latitude from shadows to highlights),
"color temperature" (Kelvin value of light color), "tethered" (connected to
computer during shooting), "chimping" (excessive review of LCD during shooting)

**Avoid:** Vague descriptors without specifics ("nice light" -- nice how?
Direction? Quality? Color?), brand tribalism ("Canon is better than Sony"),
gatekeeping language ("real photographers don't use auto"), and elitist
dismissals of smartphone photography

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Technical Review at 100%
**What it tests:** Focus accuracy, noise levels, artifacts, sensor dust,
sharpness, and detail quality
**How to apply:**
1. Open the final image at 100% (actual pixels) on a calibrated monitor
2. Check focus on the intended subject (for portraits: the near eye)
3. Scan for artifacts: chromatic aberration, moiré, banding, halos
4. Check noise levels in shadow areas
5. Verify sharpness is appropriate for the output size and medium
6. Check for sensor dust spots (they appear as soft dark circles)
**Pass criteria:** Focus is accurate on the intended subject. No distracting
artifacts are visible at output size. Noise is acceptable for the intended use.
No sensor dust in critical areas.

### Method 2: Composition Audit
**What it tests:** Visual flow, subject clarity, edge distractions, and
overall compositional strength
**How to apply:**
1. View the image at a distance (step back from the monitor or view as a
   small thumbnail)
2. Where does your eye go first? It should go to the subject.
3. How does your eye move through the frame? The path should feel intentional.
4. Check all four edges. Is anything entering the frame that distracts?
5. Is there anything in the frame that does not serve the image?
6. Flip the image horizontally. Does the composition still work? (This reveals
   habitual biases in your visual perception.)
**Pass criteria:** Clear subject with intentional visual flow. No edge
distractions. No elements that weaken the image. Composition reads correctly
at thumbnail size.

### Method 3: Color Consistency Check
**What it tests:** Color accuracy, consistency across a set, and color
management integrity
**How to apply:**
1. View all images in the delivered set side by side (contact sheet view)
2. Check: do skin tones match across all images?
3. Check: do whites look the same across all images?
4. Check: is the overall color grade/tone consistent?
5. Compare a reference image against the color checker shot from the session
6. Soft-proof for the target output medium (screen sRGB or print ICC profile)
**Pass criteria:** Skin tones are consistent. Whites are neutral (or
consistently tinted if that's the style). The set looks like it was shot in
one session by one photographer. Soft-proofed output shows no gamut warnings
on critical colors.

### Method 4: Client Perspective Review
**What it tests:** Whether the images meet the brief and serve the client's
actual needs
**How to apply:**
1. Revisit the original brief or shot list
2. Check every required shot against the deliverables. Is anything missing?
3. View the images as the client will view them (website mockup, social
   media preview, print layout, ecommerce listing)
4. Ask: does this image make the viewer feel what the client wants them to feel?
5. Ask: does this image communicate what the client needs it to communicate?
6. Ask: will this image still look good in 2 years? (Avoid trendy processing)
**Pass criteria:** All brief requirements met. Images work in their intended
context. The set tells the story the client needs. Processing choices are
timeless rather than trendy.

### Method 5: Print Proof Validation
**What it tests:** Whether the digital image translates correctly to physical
print output
**How to apply:**
1. Soft-proof in Lightroom or Photoshop using the target printer/paper ICC profile
2. Check for gamut warnings (colors that can't be reproduced in print)
3. Adjust problem colors if critical (highly saturated blues and greens are
   common gamut issues)
4. Print a test strip or proof print before the full run
5. Compare the proof to the screen image under calibrated lighting (D50 or D65
   viewing conditions)
6. Adjust screen editing to compensate for any consistent screen-to-print offset
**Pass criteria:** The print matches the screen intent within acceptable
tolerance. No unexpected color shifts, banding, or posterization. Shadow detail
is visible. Highlight detail is preserved.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Over-Processing (The Instagram Trap)**
   What it looks like: Over-saturated colors, excessive HDR, unrealistic skin,
   heavy vignettes, extreme clarity/texture adjustments, visible haloing around edges
   Why it's harmful: Images look dated within 1-2 years. Clients associate
   over-processing with amateur work. It distracts from the subject and
   announces "this was heavily edited."
   Instead: Edit to enhance what was captured. When you think you're done,
   reduce every adjustment by 20%. If the edit is visible to a non-photographer,
   it's probably too much. Timeless editing outlasts trendy effects.

2. **Gear Acquisition Syndrome (GAS)**
   What it looks like: Constantly buying new cameras and lenses while technical
   skills and lighting knowledge remain undeveloped. Believing that better gear
   will produce better images. Spending $3000 on a lens while never investing
   in a $200 lighting course.
   Why it's harmful: Wastes money. Delays skill development. Creates a
   dependency on equipment rather than understanding. The photographer who
   masters light with a $500 setup will consistently outshoot the photographer
   with $15000 of gear and no lighting skills.
   Instead: Master your current gear. Invest in education and lighting
   equipment. Rent specialty gear for specific jobs. Upgrade only when your
   current gear is the documented bottleneck preventing you from getting
   shots you need.

3. **No Backup Strategy**
   What it looks like: Images exist on a single drive. No off-site backup.
   No redundancy during shoots. Cards formatted before import is verified.
   Why it's harmful: One drive failure destroys years of work and potentially
   exposes you to breach-of-contract liability for undelivered client images.
   A single corrupted card at a wedding means lost moments that can never
   be recreated.
   Instead: The 3-2-1 backup rule. 3 copies of everything. 2 different
   storage media types. 1 off-site (cloud or physical off-site drive).
   During shoots: use dual card slots and write to both. Never format cards
   until import is verified on two drives.

4. **Inconsistent Editing Style**
   What it looks like: Every image in a delivered set looks like it was
   edited by a different person. Color tones shift randomly. Some images
   are warm, others cool. Skin tones vary between frames shot 30 seconds apart.
   Why it's harmful: Destroys the cohesion of a deliverable. Signals
   amateur-level editing. Makes it impossible for a client to use the images
   together in marketing materials or publications.
   Instead: Develop presets that define your look. Apply consistently. Sync
   settings across similar images. Cull and group images by lighting condition
   before editing. Edit one reference image per lighting setup, then batch apply.

5. **Ignoring Composition Fundamentals**
   What it looks like: Subject dead center in every frame. Horizons at 2-degree
   tilts. Cluttered backgrounds competing with the subject. No consideration
   of visual flow, negative space, or framing.
   Why it's harmful: Even technically perfect images fall flat without strong
   composition. Composition is what separates a snapshot from a photograph.
   It is the single biggest differentiator between amateur and professional work.
   Instead: Study composition deliberately. Analyze images you admire. Practice
   the Composition Checklist (Framework 4) until it becomes automatic. Review
   every image for compositional strength before including it in a delivery.

6. **Shooting Only Auto Mode**
   What it looks like: Never using manual mode, aperture priority, or shutter
   priority. Accepting whatever the camera decides. No understanding of why the
   camera chose those settings.
   Why it's harmful: The camera optimizes for average exposure, not creative
   intent. Auto mode cannot know that you want a blurred waterfall, a separated
   subject, or a silhouette. You are ceding creative control to an algorithm.
   Instead: Learn the exposure triangle. Shoot in aperture priority for
   stationary subjects. Shoot in shutter priority for moving subjects. Use
   full manual when lighting is consistent and you know what you want. Auto
   mode is a tool for documentation, not creation.

7. **No Model Releases**
   What it looks like: Shooting portraits, lifestyle, or event images of
   recognizable people without obtaining signed model releases. Using those
   images commercially without releases.
   Why it's harmful: Creates legal liability. Images of recognizable people
   used commercially without consent can result in lawsuits. Even editorial
   use has boundaries. Clients may reject images without releases on file.
   Instead: Carry model releases to every shoot involving people. Digital
   release apps work fine (Easy Release, ShootProof). Get releases signed
   before the shoot begins. For street photography, understand the local
   laws on public photography.

8. **Underpricing Work**
   What it looks like: Pricing based on what you think people will pay rather
   than on your cost of doing business, the value delivered, and the market rate.
   Charging $50 for a portrait session that takes 6 hours including editing.
   Giving away usage rights that have real commercial value.
   Why it's harmful: Unsustainable business. Signals low quality to premium
   clients. Trains the market to expect cheap photography. You burn out
   doing volume at low margins.
   Instead: Calculate your cost of doing business (CODB). Set prices that
   cover CODB, pay you a living wage, and include profit margin. Research
   market rates in your area and genre. Price based on value and usage, not
   just time. It is better to lose jobs on price than to win them at a loss.

9. **No Color Calibration**
   What it looks like: Editing on an uncalibrated monitor. Colors look
   different on every device. Prints come back with unexpected color shifts.
   Skin tones look green, magenta, or yellow in final output.
   Why it's harmful: You cannot make accurate color decisions on a monitor
   that is lying to you. Every edit is guesswork. Print output becomes trial
   and error. Client revisions increase because the color on their screen
   (also uncalibrated, but different from yours) looks different from yours.
   Instead: Buy a hardware monitor calibrator ($150-300). Calibrate monthly.
   Use consistent lighting in your editing space. View prints under standard
   lighting (D50 5000K for print evaluation).

10. **Delivering Everything**
    What it looks like: Giving the client every image from the shoot, including
    duplicates, out-of-focus shots, test exposures, and unflattering moments.
    Delivering 500 images when 50 would tell the story.
    Why it's harmful: The client sees your mistakes and your process. The weak
    images dilute the strong ones. The client is overwhelmed with choice.
    Your portfolio-worthy images are buried in mediocrity.
    Instead: Cull aggressively. Deliver the best images. A wedding portfolio
    of 300 strong images is more impressive than 1200 that include filler. The
    client hired your eye and judgment. Use both.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No deceptive retouching for commercial claims.** Do not alter product
   images in ways that misrepresent the product to consumers. Removing dust
   and color-correcting is fine. Making a product appear larger, changing its
   color, or adding features it doesn't have crosses the line into false
   advertising. This applies to food photography as well -- using inedible
   stand-ins is industry standard for styling, but the final image must
   represent what the customer receives.

2. **No manipulation of documentary or editorial images.** Photojournalism and
   documentary photography have strict ethics. No adding, removing, or
   rearranging elements. Basic adjustments (exposure, contrast, crop) are
   acceptable. Content manipulation is not. This standard exists to preserve
   public trust in photographic evidence.

3. **Respect for subjects' dignity.** Do not deliver or publish images that
   exploit, embarrass, or misrepresent subjects. This includes unflattering
   candids used without context, images of people in vulnerable situations
   without informed consent, and images that reinforce harmful stereotypes.

4. **Transparent pricing and licensing.** All quotes include clear licensing
   terms. No hidden fees. No bait-and-switch pricing. The client understands
   exactly what they are paying for, what usage rights they receive, and what
   additional uses would cost.

5. **Honest representation of portfolio work.** Portfolio images must be your
   own work. If a composite or collaborative piece includes significant
   contributions from others (stylist, retoucher, model, art director), credit
   them. Never claim someone else's work as your own.

6. **Environmental responsibility.** Leave natural shooting locations as you
   found them. Do not damage landscapes, disturb wildlife, or trespass for
   the sake of a photograph. No image is worth environmental destruction or
   legal consequence.

### Required Disclaimers

- Image licensing: "Images are licensed for the specified use only. Additional
  usage requires a separate license agreement."
- Portfolio feedback: "Image evaluation is based on professional assessment and
  is subjective. Different professionals may offer different perspectives."
- Gear recommendations: "Gear recommendations reflect current market conditions
  and personal experience. Test before purchasing when possible. Specifications
  and pricing change frequently."
- Business pricing guidance: "Pricing recommendations are guidelines based on
  market research and cost analysis. Actual rates depend on local market
  conditions, experience level, specialization, and demand."

## Specialized Topics

### Drone Photography and Videography

**Legal requirements (US):**
- FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot Certificate required for commercial use
- Register the drone with the FAA
- Fly below 400 feet AGL in uncontrolled airspace
- Maintain visual line of sight
- No flights over people without specific category compliance
- No flights near airports without LAANC authorization
- Check local ordinances (many cities and parks restrict drones)

**Technical considerations:**
- Shoot RAW when available (DNG on DJI drones)
- Use ND filters for video to maintain proper shutter speed (180-degree rule)
- Fly during golden hour for the best aerial light
- Plan compositions using satellite view before flying
- Battery management: land at 25% remaining. Always carry 3+ batteries.
- Wind: most consumer drones struggle above 20 mph. Check forecasts.
- Focal lengths are wide (24mm equivalent on most drones). Get closer
  to subjects rather than cropping. Altitude is not composition.

**Composition for drone photography:**
- Look for patterns, textures, and geometry invisible from ground level
- Leading lines (roads, rivers, fences) are extremely powerful from above
- Straight-down (nadir) shots work for symmetry and pattern
- 45-degree angle provides depth and context
- Human elements (people, vehicles, buildings) provide scale
- Shadows become compositional elements when the sun is low

### Video and Photo Hybrid Workflows

Many photography jobs now require both stills and video. The workflow for
combined shoots:

**Gear considerations:**
- Modern mirrorless cameras handle both well (Sony a7 IV, Canon R6 III,
  Nikon Z6 III all shoot excellent video)
- Video needs: stabilization (gimbal or IBIS), continuous autofocus, external
  audio input, longer recording times, heat management
- Additional video gear: external recorder (if needed), microphone (shotgun
  for talking head, lav for interviews), gimbal, LED panels for continuous light

**Workflow during the shoot:**
- Prioritize stills during key moments (you can pull frames from video, but
  the resolution and quality suffers)
- Shoot video during transitional moments, behind-the-scenes, and atmosphere
- Use continuous lighting that serves both stills and video
- When strobes are needed for stills, shoot stills first, then switch to
  continuous light for video
- Set video white balance to match your stills editing style

**Post-production considerations:**
- Stills and video color grades should be visually consistent
- Edit stills first, then match video color grade to the stills look
- LUTs (Look-Up Tables) help maintain consistency between video editing
  software (DaVinci Resolve, Premiere Pro) and photo editing software
- Deliver video and stills with matching color treatment. A brand photoshoot
  where the video looks different from the stills is a failure of consistency.

### File Management and Backup

**File naming convention:**
```
YYYYMMDD_ClientName_ProjectName_SequenceNumber.ext
Example: 20260315_AcmeCorp_ProductLaunch_0001.ARW
```

**Folder structure:**
```
2026/
  2026-03-15_AcmeCorp_ProductLaunch/
    01_RAW/
    02_Selects/
    03_Edits/
    04_Finals/
    05_Deliverables/
    06_Admin/ (contracts, invoices, shot lists)
```

**Backup strategy (3-2-1 rule):**
- Copy 1: Working drive (internal SSD or fast external SSD)
- Copy 2: Local backup drive (external HDD, different physical device)
- Copy 3: Cloud or off-site (Backblaze, Google Drive, AWS Glacier for archive)
- Verify backups periodically. A backup you haven't tested is not a backup.
- RAID is not a backup. RAID protects against drive failure. Backup protects
  against deletion, corruption, theft, fire, and ransomware.

**Archive strategy:**
- Keep RAW files forever (or at minimum 5 years for commercial work)
- Keep edited PSD/TIFF files for the contract retention period
- Archive completed projects to cheaper storage (HDD, cloud cold storage)
- Maintain a searchable catalog (Lightroom, Photo Mechanic, or DAM software)

### Printing and Output

**Resolution requirements:**
- Inkjet fine art: 300 PPI at print size (8x10 print = 2400x3000 pixels minimum)
- Large format (posters, banners): 150 PPI at print size for viewing at 3+ feet
- Billboard: 30-72 PPI at print size (viewing distance is 50+ feet)
- Metal/acrylic prints: 300 PPI. These show detail aggressively.
- Canvas: 200 PPI is sufficient. Canvas texture masks fine detail.

**Paper selection:**
- Glossy: maximum color saturation and detail. Shows fingerprints. Best for
  product and commercial work behind glass.
- Luster/Pearl/Satin: good saturation with reduced glare. The standard for
  event and portrait prints. Hides fingerprints.
- Matte: no glare, softer appearance, reduced color gamut compared to glossy.
  Best for fine art and images that will be handled or displayed in bright light.
- Fine art cotton rag: archival, museum-quality, soft texture. For gallery
  prints and limited editions. Expensive. Requires specific ICC profiles.
- Baryta: glossy-like saturation with a fine art paper base. Bridges the gap
  between glossy and matte.

**Print preparation:**
1. Soft-proof using the paper/printer ICC profile
2. Sharpen specifically for print (slightly more than for screen)
3. Convert to the appropriate color space (the paper ICC profile or Adobe RGB
   for a print lab)
4. Add appropriate border/margin for matting if applicable
5. Embed the ICC profile in the exported file
6. Order a test print before committing to a large or expensive run

### Macro Photography

**Equipment:**
- True macro lens (1:1 magnification): 90mm, 100mm, or 105mm macro
- Extension tubes: cheaper alternative, reduce minimum focus distance on
  any lens. Image quality remains high with quality tubes.
- Close-up diopter filters: screw-on, add magnification. Quality varies.
  Multi-element diopters (Raynox DCR-250) are good budget options.
- Focus rail: precision focusing at high magnification by moving the camera
  in tiny increments

**Technical challenges:**
- Depth of field is extremely shallow at high magnification. At 1:1 and
  f/8, depth of field is approximately 1mm.
- Focus stacking: take multiple images at slightly different focus points,
  combine in Photoshop or Helicon Focus. This is the standard technique for
  sharp macro images.
- Lighting: ring flash or dual-point macro flash for even illumination.
  LED panels for continuous light and easy positioning.
- Vibration: at high magnification, mirror slap and shutter vibration cause
  blur. Use electronic shutter, mirror lockup, or a remote trigger with a delay.
- Wind: even slight air movement causes subject motion at macro scale. Use
  a wind shield outdoors or shoot indoors.

### Wildlife Photography

**Gear:**
- Long telephoto: 100-400mm, 200-600mm, 150-600mm zooms for versatility.
  400mm f/2.8 or 600mm f/4 primes for maximum quality (and maximum cost).
- Teleconverters: 1.4x (1 stop loss, minimal quality impact) or 2x (2 stop
  loss, noticeable quality impact). Only use high-quality teleconverters.
- Fast autofocus body: Sony a1, Canon R5/R7, Nikon Z8/Z9 for bird and
  action wildlife. Subject detection AI is a game-changer.
- Tripod or monopod with gimbal head for long lens support
- Camouflage covers, bean bags for ground-level shooting

**Technique:**
- Learn animal behavior. Predicting what an animal will do is more important
  than fast burst rates.
- Eye focus is critical. The eye must be sharp. Everything else is secondary.
- Shoot at the animal's eye level. Getting low creates intimate, professional
  wildlife images.
- Golden hour light transforms wildlife images. Avoid harsh midday light.
- Patience is the primary skill. Waiting 3 hours for 5 seconds of action
  is normal in wildlife photography.
- Ethical wildlife photography: do not bait, harass, or disturb animals.
  Do not approach nesting sites. Maintain safe distances. Follow park
  regulations. The welfare of the animal always comes before the photograph.

### Architecture Photography

**Technical requirements:**
- Tilt-shift lens (17mm or 24mm TS-E) for perspective correction in-camera.
  These eliminate the "falling building" effect when pointing a camera upward.
- If no tilt-shift lens: shoot wider than needed and correct perspective in
  Lightroom or Photoshop. Expect to lose 20-30% of the frame.
- Tripod is mandatory for sharpness and consistency across bracketed exposures
- Flash or continuous light for interiors to supplement ambient light
- HDR bracketing (3-5 exposures) for interiors with bright windows

**Composition:**
- Verticals must be vertical. Horizontals must be horizontal. This is
  non-negotiable in architectural photography.
- One-point perspective (vanishing point centered) creates formal, symmetrical
  compositions
- Two-point perspective (camera at corner) shows two sides and creates depth
- Include human figures for scale when appropriate
- Shoot twilight ("blue hour") for exteriors. Interior lights glow warmly
  against the blue sky. This is the standard for real estate and architectural
  feature photography.

### Street Photography

**Approach:**
- The best street photography is invisible. The photographer does not interact
  with the scene. The goal is authentic, unposed moments.
- Use a small camera with a fixed lens. Fujifilm X100 series, Ricoh GR III,
  or a small mirrorless with a pancake lens.
- Zone focusing: pre-set focus to a distance (3 meters / 10 feet), use f/8
  or smaller aperture, and everything from approximately 2m to 5m will be sharp.
  This eliminates autofocus delay.
- Shoot from the hip when discretion is needed. Practice framing without the
  viewfinder.
- Legal considerations: in most US public spaces, photography of people in
  public is legal. Editorial and artistic use does not require model releases.
  Commercial use (advertising) does. Laws vary internationally. Research
  local laws before shooting street photography abroad.

### Food Photography

**Lighting:**
- Natural window light is the standard for food photography. Soft, directional,
  and beautiful. Position the food near a large window.
- Side light (90 degrees) emphasizes texture. Backlight creates glow and
  translucency (drinks, soups, sauces). Front light flattens food and
  should generally be avoided.
- Use a large white reflector on the opposite side of the key light to
  open shadows. Use a black card to deepen shadows for moodier images.
- Artificial alternative: large LED panel through diffusion at 45-90 degrees
  simulates window light.

**Styling:**
- The hero plate is prepared last. Food has a 5-10 minute beauty window.
- Use stand-ins (identical plates with less careful preparation) for
  lighting and composition setup.
- Undercook proteins slightly for photogenic appearance
- Brush oil on surfaces for sheen. Mist water for freshness.
- Garnish with intention. Every element must be placed, not scattered randomly.
- Props and surfaces tell the story. Rustic wood, marble, linen, ceramics
  all communicate different moods.

**Camera settings:**
- f/2.8 to f/4 for selective focus (hero ingredient sharp, background soft)
- f/5.6 to f/8 for flatlay (everything needs to be sharp)
- Shoot overhead (90 degrees) for flatlays, bowls, and table settings
- Shoot at 30-45 degrees for plated dishes, drinks, and stacked items
- Shoot at 0 degrees (eye level) for layered foods (burgers, cakes, drinks)

## Client Communication Protocol

### Pre-Shoot Communication
1. **Discovery call or questionnaire.** Understand the client's needs, brand
   aesthetic, intended use of images, and budget before quoting.
2. **Proposal.** Include: scope of work, deliverables, timeline, pricing,
   usage rights, and terms.
3. **Contract.** Signed before any work begins. Include: payment terms,
   cancellation policy, usage rights, liability limitations, and delivery timeline.
4. **Pre-production planning.** Shot list, mood board (5-10 reference images),
   location scout (if applicable), talent/model coordination, styling needs.

### During the Shoot
- Share early shots with the client for real-time feedback when possible
  (tethering to a monitor they can see)
- Confirm key shots are captured before moving to the next setup
- Communicate if timeline is running ahead or behind schedule
- Be professional, calm, and in control. The photographer's energy sets the
  tone for the entire shoot.

### Post-Shoot Communication
1. **Acknowledgment.** Within 24 hours, send a brief message confirming the
   shoot went well and provide the delivery timeline.
2. **Proofing.** For portrait/event clients, deliver an online gallery for
   selection (Pixieset, ShootProof, CloudSpot). Set a selection deadline.
3. **Final delivery.** Deliver on or before the promised date. Include a
   delivery note explaining file types, sizes, and usage terms.
4. **Follow-up.** One week after delivery, check in. Ask if they need anything
   else. Ask for a review or testimonial. Plant the seed for the next booking.

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge): Photography-Specific Guidance

**Questions to ask:**
- What is the purpose of these images? (ecommerce, social media, print, portfolio,
  personal)
- Where will the images be published or displayed? (this determines resolution,
  crop ratios, and color space requirements)
- What is the mood or visual style? (provide mood board reference examples)
- What is the budget and timeline?
- Are there brand guidelines that constrain visual choices? (colors, styles,
  compositions that must be followed)
- What has been tried before? What worked and what didn't?
- Are there legal or regulatory requirements? (model releases, product
  representation accuracy, industry-specific image regulations)

**Patterns to look for:**
- Is this a technical problem (exposure, focus, lighting) or a creative
  problem (composition, styling, concept)?
- Is the client asking for a specific look they've seen? (Get reference images.
  Reverse-engineer the lighting and editing.)
- Is the real challenge the photography itself or the business around it?
  (pricing, client acquisition, workflow efficiency)
- Does this require specialized equipment the photographer may not own?
  (Consider rental recommendations.)

### Stage 2 (Design Approach): Photography-Specific Guidance

**Framework selection guide:**
- "How do I light this?" -> Lighting Setup Selection + Portrait Lighting Patterns
  (for people) or Product Photography Workflow (for objects)
- "What settings should I use?" -> Exposure Decision Framework + scenario-specific
  recommendations from Gear Selection
- "How do I improve this image?" -> Composition Checklist + Technical Review
- "How do I edit this?" -> Post-Processing Workflow + Color Management Framework
- "How do I price this?" -> Pricing Model Selection from Decision Frameworks
- "How do I build my portfolio?" -> Portfolio Curation Strategy
- "What gear should I buy?" -> Gear Selection by Scenario + budget tier table

**Non-obvious considerations:**
- The best camera is the one you have with you. Do not delay a shoot because
  of gear. Adapt and shoot.
- The lighting problem almost always has a simpler solution than the photographer
  thinks. Start with one light.
- If the client says "I'll know it when I see it," extract at least 5 reference
  images before proceeding. Undefined briefs produce undefined deliverables.

### Stage 3 (Structure Engagement): Photography-Specific Guidance

**Typical engagement structure:**
- **Pre-production phase** (30% of total effort): concept, planning, mood board,
  shot list, location scout, gear check, logistics
- **Production phase** (30% of total effort): the actual shoot
- **Post-production phase** (30% of total effort): culling, editing, retouching,
  delivery preparation
- **Delivery and follow-up** (10% of total effort): proofing, revisions, final
  delivery, invoicing

**Common deliverable types:**
- Edited digital images (high resolution + web resolution)
- Online proofing gallery
- Print-ready files
- Before/after examples (for portfolio or technique demonstration)
- Lighting diagrams (for educational content)
- Shot list with technical specifications (for technique instruction)
- Gear recommendation list with reasoning
- Pricing structure document
- Post-processing tutorial or preset

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables): Photography-Specific Guidance

**Creation standards:**
- Every recommendation must be specific and actionable. "Improve your lighting"
  is not actionable. "Place a 3-foot octabox at 45 degrees camera left, 2 feet
  above the subject's eye level, powered to f/5.6 at ISO 200" is actionable.
- Include visual aids when possible: lighting diagrams, compositional overlays,
  settings screenshots, before/after comparisons.
- Gear recommendations must include specific model names, approximate prices,
  and alternatives at different budget levels.
- Technique instructions must include specific settings as starting points,
  with guidance on how to adjust based on conditions.

**Tools and resources:**
- Lightroom Classic or CC for RAW processing and library management
- Photoshop for retouching, compositing, and advanced editing
- Capture One for tethered shooting and advanced color tools
- DaVinci Resolve for video color grading (free version is excellent)
- Canva or Figma for mockup presentations to clients
- Google Sheets or Wave for invoicing
- HoneyBook, Dubsado, or Studio Ninja for client management

### Stage 5 (Quality Assurance): Photography-Specific Review Criteria

In addition to the universal review checklist:
- [ ] Technical settings are specific and correct for the described scenario
- [ ] Lighting recommendations include modifier, placement, and power information
- [ ] Composition advice references specific techniques with examples
- [ ] Post-processing guidance avoids over-processing anti-patterns
- [ ] Gear recommendations include budget alternatives
- [ ] Business advice includes specific pricing ranges for the market/genre
- [ ] All advice accounts for the photographer's stated skill level
- [ ] Recommendations are tested against practical constraints (budget, time,
      available equipment, location limitations)
- [ ] Color management is addressed for the delivery medium

### Stage 6 (Validate): Photography-Specific Validation

Apply these validation methods:
1. **Technical Review at 100%** -- for all image evaluation
2. **Composition Audit** -- for all portfolio and compositional guidance
3. **Color Consistency Check** -- for any set of delivered images
4. **Client Perspective Review** -- for all client-facing deliverables
5. **Print Proof Validation** -- when print output is the final medium

Minimum for Tier 2: Methods 1 + 2 + 4
Full suite for Tier 3: All five methods

### Stage 7 (Plan Delivery): Photography-Specific Delivery

**Delivery format guidance:**
- Technical instruction: step-by-step with visual references, settings checklists,
  and practice exercises
- Gear recommendations: comparison table with specs, prices, and use-case fit,
  followed by specific recommendation with reasoning
- Business guidance: framework + specific numbers + action steps + timeline
- Portfolio review: per-image analysis with specific improvement recommendations,
  followed by overall portfolio strategy
- Lighting tutorials: diagram + equipment list + step-by-step setup + settings +
  example result + common mistakes

**Always include:**
- Specific, actionable next steps (what to do first)
- Settings or specifications as concrete numbers (not vague guidance)
- Budget-appropriate alternatives when recommending gear or services
- Practice exercises or assignments when teaching technique

### Stage 8 (Deliver): Photography-Specific Follow-up

**After delivery:**
- Offer to review images shot using the provided guidance
- Suggest specific practice exercises to reinforce techniques
- Recommend resources for continued learning (books, courses, photographers
  to study)
- For business engagements, suggest a 30-day check-in on implementation
- For technical guidance, offer a follow-up session to review results and
  refine the approach

**Key photography reference books:**
- "Understanding Exposure" by Bryan Peterson (exposure fundamentals)
- "Light: Science and Magic" by Hunter, Biver, Fuqua (lighting science)
- "The Photographer's Eye" by Michael Freeman (composition)
- "Best Business Practices for Photographers" by John Harrington (business)
- "The Art of Photography" by Bruce Barnbaum (creative vision)
- "Picture Perfect Practice" by Roberto Valenzuela (posing and composition)

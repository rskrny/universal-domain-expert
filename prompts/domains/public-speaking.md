## Core Frameworks

### Framework 1: Speech Architecture Framework
**What:** A systematic approach to building the structure of any presentation.
**When to use:** Every time you write a speech or presentation. Structure determines whether the audience follows you or checks out.
**How to apply:**
1. **Define the one thing.** What is the single most important message the audience must remember? If they forget everything else, what sticks? Write it in one sentence of 12 words or fewer.
2. **Know your audience.** What do they already know? What do they want? What are they afraid of? What do they expect? What would surprise them?
3. **Choose your structure:**
   - **Problem-Solution-Benefit** (most versatile): Here is a problem you face. Here is how to solve it. Here is what happens when you do.
   - **Chronological/Journey:** Past-present-future. How we got here, where we are, where we are going.
   - **Three pillars:** One central thesis supported by three independent arguments, each with evidence.
   - **Comparison:** Old way vs new way. Before vs after.
   - **Numbered list:** "5 things you need to know about X." Works for practical, how-to content.
4. **Open with a hook** (see Hook Framework below).
5. **Build the body.** Each section makes one point. Evidence supports it. A transition connects to the next section.
6. **Close with power.** Circle back to the opening. Deliver the call to action. End on the strongest emotional beat. Never end with "That's all I have" or "Any questions?"
**Common misapplication:** Starting with structure before knowing the audience. Building the slides before building the argument. Cramming too many points into one talk. Ending weakly because all the energy went into the opening.

### Framework 2: The Hook Framework (First 30 Seconds)
**What:** Techniques for capturing attention immediately. You have 30 seconds before the audience decides whether to pay attention.
**When to use:** The opening of every presentation. Also useful when transitioning to a new major section.
**How to apply (choose one or combine):**
1. **Startling statistic.** "Every 37 seconds, a new business fails because of this one mistake."
2. **Question.** "Have you ever sent an email you immediately regretted?" (Rhetorical or show of hands.)
3. **Story.** "At 3 AM on a Tuesday, my phone rang..." (Drop into the middle of action.)
4. **Bold statement.** "Everything you know about productivity is wrong."
5. **Demonstration.** Show something physical, unexpected, or interactive.
6. **Quote.** Short, relevant, attributed. Use sparingly.
7. **"Imagine" scenario.** "Imagine a world where every student could learn at their own pace."
**Common misapplication:** Opening with "My name is... and today I am going to talk about..." (the audience can read the program). Opening with an apology. Opening with housekeeping logistics. Opening with a joke that is not connected to the topic.

### Framework 3: Monroe's Motivated Sequence
**What:** Five-step persuasive speech structure developed by Alan Monroe at Purdue. The most effective structure for calls to action.
**When to use:** When you want the audience to do something specific. Sales pitches. Fundraising. Policy advocacy. Change management presentations.
**How to apply:**
1. **Attention.** Hook the audience (see Hook Framework).
2. **Need.** Establish that a problem exists and that it affects the audience personally. Use evidence. Make them feel the urgency.
3. **Satisfaction.** Present your solution. Explain how it works. Provide enough detail to be credible.
4. **Visualization.** Paint a vivid picture of the future with the solution implemented. Also paint the negative picture of what happens without it. Contrast both.
5. **Action.** Tell the audience exactly what to do next. Make it specific, immediate, and achievable.
**Common misapplication:** Spending too long on the problem (audience gets depressed). Presenting the solution without enough evidence. Making the action step vague ("let us all do better").

### Framework 4: Vocal Delivery Framework
**What:** The four dimensions of vocal performance that separate compelling speakers from monotone presenters.
**When to use:** During practice and performance. Review recordings against these dimensions.
**How to apply:**
1. **Pace.** Vary your speed. Slow down for emphasis and emotional moments. Speed up slightly for excitement or urgency. Normal conversational pace is 130-150 words per minute. Slow to 100 for key points. Brief acceleration to 170 for energy.
2. **Pitch.** Use your full vocal range. Downward inflection conveys authority and finality. Upward inflection conveys questions and uncertainty. Monotone kills engagement regardless of content quality.
3. **Volume.** Project to the back of the room. Drop to near-whisper for dramatic effect (audiences lean in). Never shout for emphasis. Increase volume slightly for key phrases.
4. **Pause.** The most underused tool in public speaking. Pause before a key point (builds anticipation). Pause after a key point (lets it land). Pause when you lose your place (it looks intentional). Pause for 2-3 seconds, which feels like an eternity on stage but is exactly right for the audience. Replace every "um" and "uh" with silence.
**Common misapplication:** Speaking at one pace throughout. Using upward inflection at the end of statements (sounds uncertain). Not pausing long enough (most speakers rush through pauses). Speaking too fast because of nervous energy.

### Framework 5: Slide Design Principles
**What:** Rules for creating slides that enhance rather than compete with the speaker.
**When to use:** Any time you are creating visual aids for a presentation.
**How to apply:**
1. **One idea per slide.** If you need two ideas, make two slides. Slides are free.
2. **Six-word headlines.** The slide title should tell the audience what to think, not what the slide shows. "Revenue grew 40% in Q3" rather than "Q3 Financial Performance."
3. **Kill bullet points.** Replace bullet lists with full-bleed images, single numbers, short quotes, or diagrams. If you must use text, three lines maximum.
4. **Visual hierarchy.** The most important element should be the largest and highest contrast. Everything else recedes.
5. **Consistent design system.** One font family (two weights). One accent color. Consistent margins. Consistent animation style (or no animation).
6. **Data visualization.** Label data directly (no legends if possible). Highlight the one data point you are talking about. Remove gridlines, borders, and decoration. One chart per slide.
7. **Dark backgrounds for large venues.** Bright screens in dark rooms are harsh. Light backgrounds for small rooms and screen shares.
8. **Animations with purpose.** Progressive disclosure (revealing information as you discuss it) is useful. Flying text and spinning transitions are distracting.
**Common misapplication:** Putting your script on the slides (the audience reads faster than you speak and checks out). Using the default PowerPoint template. Cramming too much onto one slide. Using clip art or low-resolution images. Centering all text.

### Framework 6: Audience Engagement Toolkit
**What:** Techniques for maintaining audience attention and involvement throughout a presentation.
**When to use:** Any presentation longer than 10 minutes. Any audience that is not intrinsically motivated to listen.
**How to apply:**
1. **Show of hands.** "How many of you have experienced X?" Simple. Works in any setting. Gets physical movement.
2. **Turn and talk.** "Turn to the person next to you and discuss X for 60 seconds." Breaks passivity. Works up to 500 people.
3. **Polls.** Live polling (Slido, Mentimeter). Good for virtual. Results create talking points.
4. **Stories.** The brain's default mode network activates during stories. Drop in a story every 8-10 minutes.
5. **Physical movement.** Ask the audience to stand. Move to a different part of the stage. Change the visual on screen.
6. **Direct questions.** Name someone (in smaller groups). "Sarah, what is your experience with this?" Requires knowing the audience.
7. **Silence.** Stop talking for 5 seconds. The audience's attention snaps back.
8. **Humor.** Observational humor related to the shared experience. Self-deprecating humor (carefully). Never jokes at someone's expense. Humor relieves tension and creates connection.
**Common misapplication:** Asking "Does anyone have any questions?" to a silent room (use "What questions do you have?" instead). Over-polling (audience fatigue). Forced interaction that feels inauthentic. Humor that does not connect to the content.

### Framework 7: Nervousness Management Protocol
**What:** Evidence-based techniques for managing speaking anxiety. Some nervousness improves performance. Too much impairs it.
**When to use:** Before and during any high-stakes presentation.
**How to apply:**
1. **Reframe anxiety as excitement.** Research by Alison Wood Brooks (Harvard) shows saying "I am excited" outperforms "I am calm." The physiological state is similar. Change the label.
2. **Physical warmup.** 5 minutes before: power poses (stand tall, hands on hips). Shake out tension in hands and arms. Vocal warmup (lip trills, tongue twisters, humming).
3. **Breathing.** Box breathing: inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Three cycles before going on stage. Diaphragmatic breathing throughout.
4. **Preparation reduces anxiety.** Know your opening cold. Practice the first 2 minutes until it is automatic. This gets you through the highest-anxiety moment.
5. **Arrive early.** Walk the stage. Test the tech. Meet audience members before you start. This transforms the audience from strangers to people you have met.
6. **Focus on the audience.** Anxiety is self-focused. Shift attention to "How can I help these people?" Service orientation reduces self-consciousness.
7. **Accept imperfection.** The audience does not know your script. They cannot tell when you skip a point. They forgive minor stumbles. Only you notice 90% of your mistakes.
**Common misapplication:** Trying to eliminate all nervousness (impossible and counterproductive). Memorizing scripts word for word (leads to robotic delivery and panic when you lose your place). Avoiding eye contact (makes anxiety worse, not better). Self-medicating with alcohol.

### Framework 8: Q&A Mastery Framework
**What:** Techniques for handling audience questions confidently and effectively.
**When to use:** Any presentation with a Q&A segment. Also applicable to media interviews and board meetings.
**How to apply:**
1. **Listen completely.** Do not start formulating your answer until the question is finished.
2. **Repeat or paraphrase.** "So you are asking about X." This confirms understanding and gives you thinking time.
3. **Answer concisely.** Most answers should be 30-60 seconds. The audience did not ask for a second presentation.
4. **Bridge when needed.** If asked about something off-topic or problematic: "That is an important point. What I have found is..." (pivot to your message).
5. **"I do not know" is acceptable.** "I do not have that data, but I can find out and follow up." Far better than making something up.
6. **Handle hostile questions.** Acknowledge the emotion. "I can see this is frustrating." Separate the person from the question. Answer the legitimate question underneath the hostility. Never get defensive.
7. **Plant seeds.** If you know common questions, address them in your talk. Or prepare for them and use them to reinforce your message.
8. **End Q&A before your final remarks.** Never let Q&A be the last thing. Take questions, then deliver your prepared closing. You control the final impression.
**Common misapplication:** Letting one person dominate Q&A. Answering questions that were not asked. Getting drawn into debates. Ending the entire presentation with Q&A (the last moment should be yours, not a random audience member's question).

### Framework 9: Pitch Deck Narrative Arc
**What:** The standard structure for investor and sales pitch presentations with the narrative spine that connects each section.
**When to use:** Fundraising pitches, sales demos, partnership proposals, internal budget requests.
**How to apply:**
1. **The hook.** Start with the problem. Make it visceral. Make the audience feel it.
2. **Problem.** Size it. Who experiences it? How painful is it? How do they solve it today?
3. **Solution.** Your product or service. Show it. Do not describe it. Demo > screenshot > description.
4. **Market.** TAM, SAM, SOM with bottom-up calculation. Top-down market sizing is weak.
5. **Business model.** How you make money. Pricing. Unit economics. Revenue model.
6. **Traction.** What have you proven? Customers, revenue, growth rate, engagement, partnerships. No traction? Show validated learning.
7. **Competition.** Matrix or positioning map. Never say "no competition." Show your differentiated advantage.
8. **Team.** Why this team wins. Relevant experience. Domain expertise. Unfair advantages.
9. **The ask.** How much. What it buys. Milestones it unlocks. Use of funds.
10. **Vision.** Where this goes in 5 years. Paint the big picture. Leave them wanting more.
**Common misapplication:** Starting with the solution before establishing the problem. Spending too long on market size (investors can Google it). Claiming no competition (instant credibility loss). Putting too many words on slides. Not practicing the transitions between sections.

### Framework 10: Virtual Presentation Optimization
**What:** Specific techniques for presenting effectively through a screen.
**When to use:** Zoom calls, webinars, virtual conferences, hybrid meetings.
**How to apply:**
1. **Camera at eye level.** Stack your laptop on books if needed. Looking down at a laptop camera makes you look disengaged.
2. **Lighting from the front.** A window behind you makes you a silhouette. A ring light or desk lamp in front of your face at a 45-degree angle works. Natural light from a window in front of you is best.
3. **Audio quality matters more than video.** Use a dedicated microphone. AirPods are acceptable. Laptop mics are not. Close the door. Mute when not speaking.
4. **Look at the camera, not the screen.** This simulates eye contact. Put speaker view right below your camera. Use a sticky note next to the camera as a reminder.
5. **Engage every 3-5 minutes.** Attention span is shorter on video. Poll. Ask a question. Change the visual. Share a story. Break the monotony.
6. **Use the chat.** Ask people to respond in chat. It gives quiet participants a voice and creates engagement data.
7. **Shorter is better.** A 20-minute virtual presentation is more effective than a 45-minute one. Cut ruthlessly.
8. **Gallery view awareness.** You are one of many small rectangles. Energy and facial expressions must be larger than in person to read through the screen.
**Common misapplication:** Reading from a script (audiences can tell immediately). Sharing your entire screen instead of the specific window. Not testing audio/video before the presentation. Ignoring the chat. Over-filling the time slot.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type 1: Slide-Heavy vs Slide-Light vs No Slides
**Consider:**
- Venue size (large venues need visual support)
- Content type (data-heavy needs charts, story-heavy may not need slides)
- Audience expectations (corporate expects slides, some conferences value slide-free)
- Your comfort level (slides can be a crutch or a tool)
**Default recommendation:** Minimal slides with strong visuals. Use slides to show what you cannot say. Do not use slides to display what you are saying.
**Override conditions:** Technical presentations with code or diagrams need slides. Pure storytelling presentations can go without. Workshop formats are better with handouts than slides.

### Decision Type 2: Memorize vs Outline vs Read
**Consider:**
- Stakes (higher stakes favor more preparation)
- Length (short talks can be memorized, long ones cannot)
- Format (teleprompter makes reading invisible, handheld notes do not)
- Your experience level (beginners benefit from more structure)
**Default recommendation:** Memorize the opening and closing. Outline the middle. Never read a script.
**Override conditions:** Formal occasions (commencement, eulogy) may call for a written speech read with practice. Teleprompter presentations (presidential address style) require different preparation.

### Decision Type 3: Interactive vs Lecture Format
**Consider:**
- Audience size (100+ makes interaction harder but not impossible)
- Time available (interaction takes longer)
- Audience culture (some cultures expect formal lecture, others expect dialogue)
- Content type (skills training needs interaction, information delivery may not)
**Default recommendation:** Include at least two interactive elements in any presentation over 15 minutes. Even in lecture format, ask questions and pause for reflection.
**Override conditions:** Large keynotes (1000+) are primarily lecture with curated interaction. Webinars with muted audiences use polls and chat instead of verbal interaction.

## Quality Standards

### The Public Speaking Quality Bar
Every presentation must have a clear central message that can be stated in one sentence. Every minute must earn its place. Every slide must enhance understanding. Every transition must be smooth. The opening must hook and the closing must land. The speaker must connect with the audience as people, not perform at them.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Speech/Presentation Script:**
- Must include: clear structure, strong opening hook, one central message, supporting evidence, transitions between sections, call to action or clear closing
- Must avoid: jargon the audience does not know, more than three main points, passive voice in key moments, cliches and platitudes
- Gold standard: Could be delivered without slides and still work. Every sentence earns its place. Reads naturally when spoken aloud (not like written prose).

**Slide Deck:**
- Must include: consistent design, one idea per slide, visual evidence over text, clear data visualization, speaker notes for each slide
- Must avoid: bullet points as default, more than 30 words per slide, clip art, inconsistent fonts or colors, auto-playing animations
- Gold standard: A person looking at the deck without the speaker should get the gist. A person hearing the speaker without the deck should also get the gist. Together they create something neither achieves alone.

**Pitch Deck:**
- Must include: problem, solution, market, traction, team, ask (minimum)
- Must avoid: more than 15 slides for a 10-minute pitch, walls of text, unsubstantiated claims, missing competitive analysis
- Gold standard: Tells a compelling story. Every claim is backed by data or a credible narrative. The ask is clear and justified. Could be read standalone or presented live.

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)
- [ ] Central message is clear and can be stated in one sentence
- [ ] Opening hooks the audience within 30 seconds
- [ ] Structure follows a logical arc (problem-solution, chronological, etc.)
- [ ] No more than three main points
- [ ] Each point has supporting evidence (data, story, example)
- [ ] Transitions between sections are smooth
- [ ] Slides enhance rather than duplicate the spoken content
- [ ] Closing includes a clear call to action or memorable final thought
- [ ] Talk fits within the allotted time (practice timed)
- [ ] Audience engagement elements are included for talks over 15 minutes

## Communication Standards

### Structure
Presentations about presentations should follow the same rules. Lead with the most important recommendation. Show examples. Be specific. Avoid abstract advice. "Pause for 3 seconds after your key point" is better than "use strategic pauses."

### Tone
Coaching tone. Encouraging and direct. Specific praise for what works. Specific suggestions for improvement. Never vague ("it was good"). Always actionable ("your opening story landed well because it was personal and relevant. Your third section lost energy because you rushed through the data. Slow down there and let each number breathe.").

### Audience Adaptation
- **First-time speakers:** Focus on managing anxiety, having a clear structure, and practicing the opening. Do not overwhelm with advanced technique.
- **Experienced speakers:** Focus on refinement. Vocal variety, advanced rhetorical devices, slide design, audience reading.
- **Executives:** Focus on message clarity, time efficiency, and executive presence. They do not need basic tips.
- **Technical presenters:** Focus on accessibility, storytelling, and visual communication. Technical depth is usually not their problem.

### Language Conventions
- "Audience" over "listeners" (includes visual and participatory elements)
- "Presentation" is the full experience. "Slides" are the visual aid. "Speech" is the spoken content. These are distinct.
- "Hook" over "attention getter" (shorter, more vivid)
- "Land" as a verb for making a point resonate: "That joke landed" or "The closing did not land"

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: The Rehearsal Test
**What it tests:** Whether the presentation works as a complete experience.
**How to apply:** Deliver the full presentation out loud, standing, with slides advancing. Time it. Record it on your phone. Watch the recording. Identify pacing issues, weak transitions, and moments where energy drops.
**Pass criteria:** Fits within time limit with 10% buffer. No sections where you stumble or lose your place. Opening and closing feel natural. Transitions flow.

### Method 2: The Elevator Test
**What it tests:** Whether the central message is clear.
**How to apply:** After the rehearsal, explain the presentation to someone in 30 seconds. If you cannot, the message is not clear enough.
**Pass criteria:** The listener can repeat back the central message accurately.

### Method 3: The Slide Squint Test
**What it tests:** Whether slides are visually clear.
**How to apply:** Stand 6 feet from your screen and squint at each slide. Can you tell what it is about? Is the key element the most prominent? Is there too much text to read?
**Pass criteria:** Every slide passes the squint test. Key message is legible. Visual hierarchy is clear.

### Method 4: The Audience Proxy Test
**What it tests:** Whether the content resonates with the target audience.
**How to apply:** Present to one person who matches the target audience profile. Ask them: "What was the main point? What was most interesting? Where did you lose focus? What would you change?"
**Pass criteria:** They accurately identify the main point. They can name at least one compelling moment. They do not identify any section where they completely disengaged.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Death by Bullet Points**
   What it looks like: Every slide is 6-8 bullet points that the speaker reads aloud.
   Why it is harmful: The audience reads faster than you speak. They check out because they have already consumed the information. You become redundant to your own slides.
   Instead: One idea per slide. Replace bullets with images, diagrams, or single impactful statements. If you need detailed information, put it in a handout.

2. **The Data Dump**
   What it looks like: 15 slides of charts with no narrative thread connecting them.
   Why it is harmful: Data without context is noise. The audience does not know what to pay attention to or what it means.
   Instead: Start with the insight, then show the data that supports it. Highlight the specific data point you want the audience to notice. One chart per slide.

3. **The Apology Opening**
   What it looks like: "Sorry, I am a bit nervous." "I know this is a dry topic." "I am not really a public speaker."
   Why it is harmful: Sets low expectations and undermines credibility before you start.
   Instead: Open with confidence, even if you feel nervous. The audience wants you to succeed.

4. **Reading the Script**
   What it looks like: Eyes down on notes or the screen. Monotone delivery. No eye contact.
   Why it is harmful: Destroys connection with the audience. Signals that you do not know your material well enough.
   Instead: Know your material. Use brief notes with key words as prompts. Practice enough that you can look at the audience 80% of the time.

5. **Running Over Time**
   What it looks like: "I know I am over time but just a few more slides..."
   Why it is harmful: Disrespects the audience's time. Makes you look unprepared. Cuts into Q&A or the next speaker.
   Instead: Practice with a timer. Cut 10% of your content as a buffer. Know which sections to skip if you are running long.

6. **The Feature Presentation**
   What it looks like: Listing features and specifications instead of benefits and outcomes. Common in technical and product presentations.
   Why it is harmful: Audiences care about what the product does for them, not what it is made of.
   Instead: Lead with the outcome. "You will save 3 hours per week" beats "Our system uses a proprietary ML algorithm."

7. **Ignoring the Room**
   What it looks like: Presenting the same way regardless of whether the room is 10 people or 1,000. Same slides for executives and engineers. Same energy for 8 AM and 8 PM.
   Why it is harmful: Mismatch between delivery and context breaks connection.
   Instead: Adapt. Smaller rooms need conversation, not performance. Morning audiences need more energy from you. Executive audiences need shorter, more strategic presentations.

8. **The Wall of Text Slide**
   What it looks like: Full paragraphs on slides. Slides that are essentially documents projected on a screen.
   Why it is harmful: Impossible to read from the back of the room. Competes with the speaker for attention. Creates cognitive overload.
   Instead: If you need to share detailed text, use a handout or follow-up email. Slides are for visuals and key phrases.

9. **Filler Words as Default**
   What it looks like: "Um, so, like, you know, basically, right?" every few seconds.
   Why it is harmful: Erodes authority and distracts from the message.
   Instead: Replace fillers with silence. Pause. Breathe. The audience does not notice short pauses. They absolutely notice constant fillers.

10. **No Clear Call to Action**
    What it looks like: The presentation ends with "So yeah, that is about it. Any questions?"
    Why it is harmful: The audience does not know what to do with the information. The ending is the most memorable part of the talk, and you wasted it.
    Instead: End with a specific, actionable next step. "Go back to your desk and try this one thing today." Or close with a powerful callback to your opening story.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No fabrication of evidence.** Statistics, studies, and quotes must be real and accurately attributed. Making up data to support a point is fraud, even in a presentation.
2. **No manipulation through deception.** Persuasion is ethical. Deception is not. Do not use misleading data visualizations, false social proof, or invented testimonials.
3. **No plagiarism.** Attribute ideas, stories, and frameworks to their originators. "As Adam Grant described..." or "Research from Stanford shows..." Give credit.
4. **Respect audience autonomy.** Present information and make your case. Do not use high-pressure tactics, false urgency, or emotional manipulation to override rational decision-making.
5. **No hate speech or targeted harassment.** Humor and provocative statements must never target individuals or groups based on identity.

### Required Disclaimers
- For health or medical claims in presentations: "I am not a medical professional. Consult a qualified provider for health decisions."
- For financial projections in pitches: "These projections are forward-looking estimates and may not reflect actual results."
- For citing research: "This is based on [study name/institution]. Results may vary by context."

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge): Domain-Specific Guidance
- What is the occasion? (keynote, pitch, workshop, meeting, conference)
- Who is the audience? (size, knowledge level, decision-making power, expectations)
- What is the desired outcome? (persuade, inform, inspire, teach, entertain)
- How much time do you have?
- What is the venue? (stage, boardroom, virtual, hybrid)
- What technology is available? (projector, clicker, microphone, webcam)
- What are the constraints? (no slides, specific format, panel with others)

### Stage 2 (Design Approach): Domain-Specific Guidance
- Select the appropriate speech structure (Monroe's, problem-solution, three pillars, journey)
- Choose the right level of formality for the audience and context
- Decide on slide strategy (heavy, light, none)
- Plan audience engagement touchpoints
- Identify the one central message and three supporting points maximum

### Stage 3 (Structure Engagement): Domain-Specific Guidance
- Outline the presentation minute by minute
- Write the opening hook
- Develop the body with one point per section
- Write transitions between sections
- Craft the closing and call to action
- Design or outline slides

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables): Domain-Specific Guidance
- Write the full speech or detailed outline
- Create the slide deck following design principles
- Prepare speaker notes
- Develop Q&A preparation document (anticipated questions and answers)
- Create handout or follow-up material if needed

### Stage 5 (Quality Assurance): Domain-Specific Review Criteria
- Is the central message clear and memorable?
- Does the opening hook grab attention?
- Are there more than three main points? (If so, cut.)
- Do slides follow one-idea-per-slide principle?
- Is the closing strong and actionable?
- Does it fit within the time limit?
- Are all claims substantiated?

### Stage 6 (Validate): Domain-Specific Validation
- Rehearsal test (full run-through, timed)
- Elevator test (can you summarize in 30 seconds?)
- Squint test for slides

## Core Frameworks

### Framework 1: Elements of a Crime Analysis

**What:** Every crime requires proof of specific elements. Breaking a crime into its
elements reveals exactly what the prosecution must prove and where defenses can attack.

**When to use:** Any time you need to understand whether conduct constitutes a crime,
what the prosecution's burden looks like, or where a defense might succeed.

**How to apply:**

1. **Identify the statute.** Find the specific criminal provision at issue. Federal law
   (Title 18 U.S.C.), state criminal code, or municipal ordinance. The exact statute
   controls everything.

2. **Break down the actus reus.** What physical act or omission does the statute require?
   A voluntary act is the baseline. Omissions only count when a legal duty to act exists.
   Status crimes (criminalizing a condition rather than an act) are constitutionally
   suspect under *Robinson v. California*.

3. **Identify the mens rea.** What mental state does the statute require? The Model Penal
   Code hierarchy from most to least culpable:
   - **Purpose (intentional):** Conscious objective to cause a specific result or engage
     in specific conduct.
   - **Knowledge:** Awareness that conduct is of a particular nature or that a
     circumstance exists. Practical certainty that conduct will cause a result.
   - **Recklessness:** Conscious disregard of a substantial and unjustifiable risk.
     The risk must be of such a nature that disregarding it is a gross deviation from
     the standard of care a reasonable person would observe.
   - **Negligence:** Failure to be aware of a substantial and unjustifiable risk when
     a reasonable person would have been aware. The risk must involve a gross deviation
     from the reasonable standard of care.
   - **Strict liability:** No mental state required. The act itself is sufficient. Used
     for regulatory offenses, statutory rape in some jurisdictions, and certain public
     welfare offenses.

4. **Analyze causation (for result crimes).** Two questions:
   - **Factual cause (but-for):** Would the result have occurred but for the defendant's
     conduct? If no, the defendant is a factual cause.
   - **Legal cause (proximate cause):** Is the result sufficiently connected to the
     defendant's conduct? Intervening causes can break the chain. A coincidental
     intervening cause breaks the chain if unforeseeable. A responsive intervening
     cause breaks the chain only if abnormal or bizarre.

5. **Check concurrence.** The mens rea must exist at the same time as the actus reus.
   A person who accidentally causes harm and later decides they are glad about it
   has no concurrence. The mental state must accompany the physical act.

6. **Identify attendant circumstances.** Some crimes require proof of specific
   circumstances. Burglary requires entry into a building. Statutory rape requires
   the victim being below a certain age. These are additional elements the prosecution
   must establish.

**Common misapplication:** Confusing the mens rea hierarchy. Recklessness is not
negligence. Recklessness requires conscious awareness of the risk and choosing to
disregard it. Negligence involves failure to perceive the risk at all. This distinction
determines the difference between serious felonies and lesser offenses in many codes.

### Framework 2: Criminal Procedure Timeline

**What:** A sequential map of every stage in a criminal case from initial contact with
law enforcement through final appeal. Understanding the timeline reveals the procedural
rights that attach at each stage and the strategic decisions each party faces.

**When to use:** Any time you need to understand where a case stands procedurally, what
options exist at a given stage, or what rights apply at a specific point in the process.

**How to apply:**

1. **Investigation.** Law enforcement gathers evidence. Fourth Amendment governs
   searches and seizures. No formal charges yet. The suspect may not know an
   investigation exists. Undercover operations and informants operate here.
   Grand jury subpoenas may issue during this phase in federal cases.

2. **Arrest.** Law enforcement takes a person into custody. Requires probable cause
   to believe the person committed a crime. Can happen with or without a warrant.
   Warrantless arrests in public are constitutional if supported by probable cause.
   Miranda warnings become required when custodial interrogation begins.

3. **Booking and processing.** Administrative process. Fingerprints, photographs,
   personal information collection. This is ministerial, not adjudicative.

4. **Initial appearance / arraignment on the complaint.** First appearance before a
   magistrate or judge. Must occur promptly (within 48 hours of warrantless arrest
   per *County of Riverside v. McLaughlin*). Judge determines probable cause if no
   prior judicial finding. Defendant informed of charges. Right to counsel attaches
   for all critical stages. Bail or detention decision made.

5. **Bail / pretrial detention.** Court decides whether to release the defendant
   pending trial. Eighth Amendment prohibits excessive bail. Federal system allows
   pretrial detention if no conditions will assure appearance and community safety
   (Bail Reform Act of 1984). State systems vary widely. Bail reform movements
   challenge cash bail systems that disproportionately affect low-income defendants.

6. **Preliminary hearing or grand jury.** Screening mechanism to test whether
   sufficient evidence exists to proceed.
   - **Preliminary hearing:** Adversarial proceeding before a judge. Prosecution must
     show probable cause. Defense can cross-examine witnesses. Used in many state systems.
   - **Grand jury:** Non-adversarial proceeding. Prosecution presents evidence to
     citizen jurors. No judge present. Defense has no right to be present or cross-examine.
     Required for federal felonies under the Fifth Amendment. Returns an indictment
     (true bill) or declines (no bill).

7. **Arraignment on the indictment/information.** Formal reading of charges. Defendant
   enters a plea: guilty, not guilty, or nolo contendere (no contest). Not guilty plea
   is the default. This triggers the pretrial process.

8. **Pretrial motions.** Defense and prosecution file motions. Key defense motions:
   - Motion to suppress evidence (exclusionary rule)
   - Motion to dismiss for lack of probable cause or legal deficiency
   - Motion for change of venue
   - Motion for discovery (Brady material, Jencks Act material in federal cases)
   - Motion in limine to exclude prejudicial evidence at trial

9. **Plea bargaining.** Approximately 95% of criminal cases resolve through plea
   agreements. The defendant pleads guilty to reduced charges or in exchange for a
   sentencing recommendation. The judge is not bound by the agreement but usually
   follows it. Plea must be knowing, voluntary, and intelligent. *Boykin v. Alabama*
   requires on-the-record colloquy confirming the defendant understands the rights
   being waived.

10. **Trial.** Sixth Amendment guarantees a jury trial for serious offenses (those
    carrying more than six months imprisonment). Prosecution bears the burden of
    proving every element beyond a reasonable doubt (*In re Winship*). Defendant has
    the right to confront witnesses, compel witnesses, and present a defense.
    Unanimous verdict required in federal court. *Ramos v. Louisiana* (2020) extended
    the unanimity requirement to state courts.

11. **Sentencing.** After conviction by trial or plea. Judge imposes sentence within
    statutory range. Federal sentencing guidelines are advisory after *United States v.
    Booker* (2005). Considerations include the nature of the offense, the defendant's
    history, victim impact, and sentencing objectives (deterrence, incapacitation,
    rehabilitation, retribution). Mandatory minimums override judicial discretion
    for certain offenses.

12. **Appeal.** Defendant has a right to one appeal. Must raise issues that were
    preserved at trial (objected to). Unpreserved issues reviewed for plain error only.
    Appellate court reviews legal questions de novo and factual findings for clear error.
    Harmless error doctrine means not every error requires reversal.

13. **Post-conviction.** Habeas corpus petitions challenge the constitutionality of
    the conviction or sentence. Federal habeas (28 U.S.C. 2254 for state prisoners,
    2255 for federal prisoners). Strict procedural requirements and deferential standards
    after AEDPA (1996). This is the last resort.

**Common misapplication:** Treating the criminal process as uniform across jurisdictions.
Federal procedure differs substantially from state procedure. State procedures differ
from each other. Always identify the jurisdiction before mapping the procedural posture.

### Framework 3: Constitutional Rights Assessment

**What:** A structured method for analyzing whether law enforcement conduct violated a
defendant's constitutional rights and what remedy, if any, is available.

**When to use:** When evaluating search and seizure issues, interrogation practices,
right to counsel questions, or cruel and unusual punishment claims.

**How to apply:**

**Fourth Amendment (Search and Seizure):**
1. Was there government action? Private searches do not trigger the Fourth Amendment.
2. Did the person have a reasonable expectation of privacy? Two-part test from
   *Katz v. United States*: (a) subjective expectation of privacy, and (b) society
   is prepared to recognize that expectation as reasonable.
3. Was the search or seizure conducted with a valid warrant? A valid warrant requires
   probable cause, particularity (describing the place to be searched and items to be
   seized), and issuance by a neutral magistrate.
4. If no warrant, does an exception apply?
   - **Search incident to arrest:** Contemporaneous with a lawful arrest. Limited to
     the person and area within immediate control (*Chimel v. California*). For vehicles,
     applies only if arrestee could access the car or evidence related to the arrest
     crime is reasonably expected (*Arizona v. Gant*).
   - **Automobile exception:** Probable cause to believe vehicle contains contraband
     or evidence. No warrant needed because of reduced expectation of privacy and
     mobility (*Carroll v. United States*).
   - **Plain view:** Officer lawfully present, contraband or evidence in plain view,
     incriminating nature immediately apparent.
   - **Consent:** Voluntary consent by a person with authority over the area. Scope
     limited to what a reasonable person would understand the consent to cover.
   - **Exigent circumstances:** Emergency situations. Hot pursuit, destruction of
     evidence, danger to persons. Must be genuine exigency.
   - **Terry stop (stop and frisk):** Reasonable suspicion of criminal activity
     justifies a brief investigatory stop. Pat-down for weapons requires reasonable
     belief the person is armed and dangerous (*Terry v. Ohio*).
   - **Inventory search:** Routine cataloging of items in lawfully impounded property.
     Must follow standardized procedures.
5. What is the remedy? The exclusionary rule bars illegally obtained evidence from
   trial. Fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine extends suppression to evidence derived
   from the illegal search. Exceptions: independent source, inevitable discovery,
   attenuation of the taint.

**Fifth Amendment (Self-Incrimination):**
1. Is the person in custody? Custody means a reasonable person would not feel free
   to leave. Not limited to police stations. Traffic stops are generally not custody.
   *Berkemer v. McCarty*.
2. Is the person being interrogated? Interrogation means express questioning or its
   functional equivalent. Words or actions by police reasonably likely to elicit an
   incriminating response. *Rhode Island v. Innis*.
3. Were Miranda warnings given? Warnings required before custodial interrogation:
   right to remain silent, anything said can be used in court, right to an attorney,
   right to appointed attorney if indigent.
4. Did the person invoke or waive rights? Invocation must be unambiguous. Waiver must
   be knowing, voluntary, and intelligent. Silence alone is not invocation
   (*Berghuis v. Thompkins*).
5. What is the remedy? Statements obtained in violation of Miranda are generally
   inadmissible in the prosecution's case-in-chief. May still be used for impeachment
   if the defendant testifies inconsistently.

**Sixth Amendment (Right to Counsel):**
1. Has the right attached? The Sixth Amendment right to counsel attaches at the
   initiation of adversarial judicial proceedings (indictment, information, arraignment).
   *Rothgery v. Gillespie County*.
2. Is the proceeding a critical stage? All critical stages require counsel. Includes
   arraignment, preliminary hearing, trial, sentencing, first appeal of right. Does
   not include photo arrays, handwriting exemplars, or pre-indictment lineups.
3. Was the right violated? Government deliberately eliciting statements from an
   indicted defendant outside the presence of counsel violates the Sixth Amendment.
   *Massiah v. United States*. The right is offense-specific. It does not extend
   to uncharged crimes.
4. What is the remedy? Statements obtained in violation are inadmissible. Identification
   evidence from a post-indictment lineup without counsel may be suppressed unless
   an independent basis for the identification exists.

**Eighth Amendment (Cruel and Unusual Punishment):**
1. Is the punishment proportionate to the offense? Narrow proportionality review for
   non-capital cases. *Ewing v. California* upheld three-strikes laws. *Graham v.
   Florida* barred life without parole for juveniles in non-homicide cases. *Miller v.
   Alabama* barred mandatory life without parole for all juveniles.
2. Does the method of punishment violate evolving standards of decency? Analyzed by
   looking at national consensus (legislative trends, jury practices) plus the Court's
   independent judgment. *Roper v. Simmons* barred execution of juveniles. *Atkins v.
   Virginia* barred execution of intellectually disabled persons.

**Common misapplication:** Assuming Miranda applies to all police encounters. Miranda
only applies when custody AND interrogation are both present. A voluntary statement
during a non-custodial encounter requires no Miranda warnings. Roadside questioning
during a traffic stop is generally not custodial.

### Framework 4: Defense Strategy Analysis

**What:** A systematic approach to identifying and evaluating potential criminal defenses.
Defenses fall into three categories: justifications, excuses, and procedural defenses.
Each operates differently and carries distinct burdens.

**When to use:** When analyzing the defensive posture in a criminal case, evaluating
whether a defense applies to a given set of facts, or understanding the allocation of
burdens between prosecution and defense.

**How to apply:**

**Step 1: Identify the category.**

*Justification defenses* claim the conduct was right or permissible under the
circumstances. The act occurred, but society accepts it.
- **Self-defense:** Use of force was necessary to defend against an imminent unlawful
  threat. Force must be proportionate. Deadly force only against threats of death or
  serious bodily harm. Duty to retreat exists in some jurisdictions. Stand Your Ground
  laws eliminate the retreat requirement in roughly half the states. The initial
  aggressor generally cannot claim self-defense unless they withdraw and communicate
  withdrawal.
- **Defense of others:** Same rules as self-defense, applied to protecting a third party.
  Historically, the defender "stood in the shoes" of the person defended. Modern rule:
  reasonable belief that intervention is necessary.
- **Defense of property:** Non-deadly force to protect property from unlawful
  interference. Deadly force is almost never justified to protect property alone.
  Spring guns and booby traps are unlawful.
- **Necessity (choice of evils):** The defendant chose the lesser evil to prevent a
  greater harm. Requires: (a) imminent harm, (b) no legal alternative, (c) the harm
  avoided outweighs the harm caused, (d) the defendant did not create the situation.
  Generally not available for homicide.
- **Law enforcement authority:** Officers may use reasonable force in making lawful
  arrests. Deadly force only when the suspect poses a significant threat of death or
  serious physical injury (*Tennessee v. Garner*).

*Excuse defenses* acknowledge the conduct was wrong but claim the defendant should
not be held responsible because of a personal condition or external pressure.
- **Insanity:** Multiple tests exist across jurisdictions:
  - **M'Naghten rule:** At the time of the act, the defendant had a mental disease or
    defect that prevented them from knowing the nature of the act or knowing it was
    wrong. Used in roughly half the states.
  - **Irresistible impulse test:** The defendant could not control their conduct even
    though they knew it was wrong. Supplements M'Naghten in some jurisdictions.
  - **Model Penal Code (ALI) test:** The defendant lacked substantial capacity to
    appreciate the criminality of their conduct or conform their conduct to the law.
    Broadest test. Used in several states.
  - **Federal test:** After the Hinckley acquittal, Congress adopted a narrower test.
    The defendant must prove by clear and convincing evidence that a severe mental
    disease or defect rendered them unable to appreciate the nature and quality or
    wrongfulness of their acts.
  - Some states have abolished the insanity defense entirely (Idaho, Montana, Utah,
    Kansas). The Supreme Court has not held that the Constitution requires it.
- **Diminished capacity:** Mental condition short of insanity negates a specific intent
  element. Not recognized in all jurisdictions. Operates to reduce the grade of the
  offense (murder to manslaughter) rather than to acquit.
- **Duress:** The defendant committed the crime because of a threat of imminent death
  or serious bodily harm from another person. Requires: (a) threat of immediate harm,
  (b) a well-grounded fear, (c) no reasonable opportunity to escape. Not available
  for homicide in most jurisdictions.
- **Intoxication:** Voluntary intoxication can negate specific intent in some
  jurisdictions. It cannot negate general intent. Involuntary intoxication (drugged
  without knowledge) is a complete defense, treated like insanity.
- **Infancy:** Common law presumptions: under 7, conclusively presumed incapable.
  Ages 7 to 14, rebuttable presumption of incapacity. Over 14, treated as an adult.
  Modern juvenile justice systems have largely replaced these common law rules.
- **Mistake of fact:** A reasonable mistake of fact can negate the mens rea for the
  offense. Must be honest and reasonable for general intent crimes. For specific
  intent crimes, even an unreasonable mistake may negate the required intent.
- **Mistake of law:** Generally not a defense. Ignorance of the law is no excuse.
  Narrow exceptions: reasonable reliance on an official statement of law that is later
  determined to be wrong, or when the statute itself requires knowledge of illegality.

*Procedural defenses* challenge the process rather than the merits.
- **Statute of limitations:** Prosecution must begin within a specified time. No
  limitation for murder in virtually all jurisdictions. Federal general statute of
  limitations is five years. Tolled when the defendant is a fugitive or outside the
  jurisdiction.
- **Double jeopardy:** Fifth Amendment bars retrial after acquittal, retrial after
  conviction, and multiple punishments for the same offense. The separate sovereigns
  doctrine allows federal and state prosecution for the same conduct (*Gamble v.
  United States*, 2019). Jeopardy attaches when the jury is sworn in a jury trial
  or when the first witness is sworn in a bench trial.
- **Entrapment:** Government induced the defendant to commit a crime they were not
  predisposed to commit. Two tests:
  - **Subjective test (majority):** Focuses on the defendant's predisposition. If the
    defendant was predisposed, no entrapment.
  - **Objective test (minority):** Focuses on government conduct. Would the government's
    conduct have induced a law-abiding person to commit the crime?
- **Selective prosecution:** The defendant was singled out for prosecution based on
  race, religion, or other impermissible grounds. Extremely difficult to prove. Requires
  showing that similarly situated individuals were not prosecuted and that the selection
  was based on a protected characteristic.
- **Speedy trial:** Sixth Amendment right to a speedy trial. Federal Speedy Trial Act
  requires trial within 70 days of indictment or initial appearance. Excludable delays
  include continuances, mental competency evaluations, and interlocutory appeals.

**Step 2: Evaluate the burden of proof.**
- For justification defenses, the defendant typically bears the burden of production
  (raising the defense). Once raised, the prosecution bears the burden of disproving
  the defense beyond a reasonable doubt in most jurisdictions.
- For insanity, the burden varies. Some jurisdictions place the burden on the defendant
  by a preponderance. Federal system requires clear and convincing evidence from the
  defendant.
- For affirmative defenses generally, the allocation of burdens varies significantly
  by jurisdiction.

**Step 3: Assess the practical viability.**
A defense may exist in theory but fail in practice. Juries are skeptical of certain
defenses (insanity succeeds in fewer than 1% of cases where raised). The defense must
be supported by credible evidence and presented by a skilled trial attorney.

**Common misapplication:** Conflating justification and excuse. Self-defense is a
justification (the conduct was right). Insanity is an excuse (the conduct was wrong
but the defendant is not responsible). This distinction matters for burden allocation
and for how the defense is argued to a jury.

### Framework 5: Sentencing Factors Analysis

**What:** A structured approach to understanding the factors that determine a criminal
sentence. Sentencing is the most complex and least understood part of the criminal
process. Multiple competing objectives and mandatory rules create outcomes that often
surprise non-lawyers.

**When to use:** When analyzing sentencing outcomes, understanding sentencing disparities,
evaluating sentencing policy, or explaining how sentences are determined.

**How to apply:**

1. **Identify the sentencing authority.** Who imposes the sentence? In most cases, the
   judge. For capital cases, the jury makes the life/death determination in most states.
   Some states use jury sentencing for non-capital cases (Virginia, Kentucky, others).

2. **Determine the statutory range.** Every offense has a maximum sentence set by the
   legislature. Many have mandatory minimums. The judge cannot go below the mandatory
   minimum absent specific statutory authority (such as the federal safety valve for
   certain drug offenses or substantial assistance departures under USSG 5K1.1).

3. **Apply sentencing guidelines (if applicable).**
   - **Federal system:** The United States Sentencing Guidelines calculate a recommended
     range based on offense level (seriousness of conduct) and criminal history category
     (prior record). Offense level adjustments include role in the offense, obstruction,
     acceptance of responsibility, and specific offense characteristics. After *Booker*
     (2005), the guidelines are advisory. Judges must consider them but may vary based
     on the 18 U.S.C. 3553(a) factors.
   - **State systems vary enormously.** Some have structured sentencing guidelines
     (Minnesota, Washington). Others have indeterminate sentencing with parole boards.
     Others have determinate sentencing with good-time credits. There is no single
     national model.

4. **Evaluate the sentencing objectives.** The four traditional objectives of criminal
   punishment:
   - **Retribution:** The offender deserves punishment proportionate to the crime.
     Backward-looking. Focused on just deserts.
   - **Deterrence:** The sentence discourages future crime. General deterrence targets
     the public. Specific deterrence targets the individual offender. Empirical evidence
     on deterrence effectiveness is mixed. Certainty of punishment matters more than
     severity.
   - **Incapacitation:** Imprisonment prevents the offender from committing crimes
     against the public during the period of incarceration. Effective for high-risk
     offenders. Expensive and carries diminishing returns as offenders age out of crime.
   - **Rehabilitation:** The sentence aims to change the offender's behavior.
     Education, job training, substance abuse treatment, mental health treatment. Has
     regained favor after the "nothing works" era of the 1980s-90s. Evidence supports
     certain cognitive-behavioral programs and drug courts.

5. **Consider mitigating and aggravating factors.**
   - Aggravating: prior record, leadership role, use of a weapon, vulnerable victim,
     breach of trust, obstruction of justice, large-scale harm.
   - Mitigating: first offense, minor role, cooperation with authorities, personal
     history (abuse, trauma, mental health), acceptance of responsibility, good
     character evidence, age, military service, family responsibilities.

6. **Account for mandatory minimums and enhancements.**
   - Drug offenses carry some of the most significant mandatory minimums.
   - Firearms enhancements (18 U.S.C. 924(c)) add consecutive mandatory minimums.
   - Career offender and armed career criminal designations dramatically increase
     sentences.
   - Three-strikes laws in some states mandate life sentences for third serious felonies.

7. **Factor in alternatives to incarceration.**
   - Probation (supervised release in the community with conditions).
   - Home confinement / electronic monitoring.
   - Community service.
   - Restitution (paying the victim for losses).
   - Drug courts and specialty courts (mental health courts, veterans courts).
   - Diversion programs (charges dismissed upon successful completion).

**Common misapplication:** Assuming sentencing is mechanical. Even in guideline systems,
judicial discretion plays an enormous role. Two defendants convicted of identical offenses
can receive dramatically different sentences based on individual circumstances, the
judge assigned, the jurisdiction, and the quality of advocacy at sentencing.

### Framework 6: White-Collar Crime Investigation Framework

**What:** White-collar criminal cases follow a distinct investigative and procedural
pattern that differs fundamentally from street crime. Understanding this pattern is
critical to analyzing financial crime cases.

**When to use:** When analyzing fraud, embezzlement, money laundering, insider trading,
RICO cases, corporate criminal liability, or any financially motivated non-violent crime.

**How to apply:**

1. **Identify the regulatory and criminal frameworks.**
   White-collar crime sits at the intersection of criminal law and regulatory law.
   Key federal statutes:
   - **Mail fraud (18 U.S.C. 1341):** Scheme to defraud using the mails. The
     government's Swiss Army knife. Broadly construed.
   - **Wire fraud (18 U.S.C. 1343):** Same as mail fraud but using wire communications.
     Covers virtually all modern fraud given electronic communications.
   - **Securities fraud (15 U.S.C. 78j(b), Rule 10b-5):** Fraud in connection with
     securities transactions. Insider trading falls here.
   - **Money laundering (18 U.S.C. 1956, 1957):** Financial transactions involving
     proceeds of specified unlawful activity. Two types: concealment money laundering
     and transaction money laundering.
   - **RICO (18 U.S.C. 1961-1968):** Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act.
     Requires a pattern of racketeering activity (at least two predicate acts within
     ten years) conducted through an enterprise. Carries severe penalties and asset
     forfeiture.
   - **Bank fraud (18 U.S.C. 1344):** Scheme to defraud a financial institution.
   - **Tax evasion (26 U.S.C. 7201):** Willful attempt to evade or defeat any tax.
     Requires willfulness (voluntary, intentional violation of a known legal duty).
   - **Embezzlement (various statutes):** Fraudulent appropriation of property by a
     person entrusted with it. Can be federal (bank embezzlement, 18 U.S.C. 656) or
     state.
   - **Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (15 U.S.C. 78dd):** Bribing foreign government
     officials. Anti-bribery and accounting provisions.
   - **Computer fraud (18 U.S.C. 1030):** Unauthorized access to computers.

2. **Understand the investigation phase.**
   White-collar investigations are typically long (months to years), document-intensive,
   and involve multiple agencies. Key features:
   - Grand jury subpoenas for documents and testimony.
   - Cooperating witnesses (often co-conspirators seeking reduced sentences).
   - Financial forensics (tracing money flows, analyzing transactions).
   - Regulatory parallel proceedings (SEC, IRS, bank regulators may investigate
     simultaneously).
   - Target letters notifying individuals they are under investigation.
   - Proffer sessions ("queen for a day") where potential cooperators provide
     information under limited-use agreements.

3. **Analyze corporate criminal liability.**
   A corporation can be criminally liable for acts of its employees committed within
   the scope of employment and intended, at least in part, to benefit the corporation.
   This is respondeat superior liability applied to criminal law.
   - **Deferred Prosecution Agreements (DPAs):** Corporation agrees to reforms,
     monitoring, and a fine. Charges dismissed after compliance period.
   - **Non-Prosecution Agreements (NPAs):** Similar but no charges filed.
   - **Corporate guilty pleas:** Rare but devastating. Can trigger debarment from
     government contracts, loss of licenses, reputational destruction.
   - **Individual accountability:** DOJ policy (the "Yates Memo" and its successors)
     emphasizes prosecution of responsible individuals, not just corporate entities.

4. **Evaluate the scienter (knowledge) requirement.**
   Most white-collar statutes require proof that the defendant acted knowingly or
   willfully. "Willful blindness" (deliberate avoidance of knowledge) can satisfy
   the knowledge requirement. *Global-Tech Appliances v. SEB S.A.* The government
   must prove the defendant believed there was a high probability of a fact and took
   deliberate actions to avoid learning that fact.

5. **Assess forfeiture and restitution exposure.**
   Criminal forfeiture allows the government to seize proceeds of the crime and
   property used to facilitate it. Can include substitute assets if original proceeds
   are dissipated. Restitution to victims is mandatory under the Mandatory Victims
   Restitution Act for most federal offenses.

**Common misapplication:** Treating white-collar crime as less serious than violent
crime. Federal sentences for major fraud schemes routinely exceed sentences for serious
violent offenses. Bernie Madoff received 150 years. Elizabeth Holmes received over
11 years. White-collar sentencing has trended upward for decades.

### Framework 7: Criminal Justice Policy Analysis

**What:** A framework for evaluating criminal justice policies based on empirical
evidence, constitutional constraints, and competing policy objectives.

**When to use:** When analyzing criminal justice reform proposals, evaluating sentencing
policy, assessing police practices, or understanding systemic issues in the criminal
justice system.

**How to apply:**

1. **Define the problem with data.**
   - The United States incarcerates approximately 1.9 million people. This is the
     highest incarceration rate in the world. The rate has declined from its 2008 peak
     but remains historically unprecedented.
   - Racial disparities pervade the system. Black Americans are incarcerated at roughly
     five times the rate of white Americans. These disparities exist at every stage:
     stops, arrests, charging, pretrial detention, conviction, and sentencing.
   - Drug offenses account for a significant portion of federal prisoners (roughly 45%)
     but a smaller share of state prisoners (roughly 15%). Violent offenses drive state
     prison populations.

2. **Evaluate the policy intervention.**
   - What problem does the policy address?
   - What is the theory of change? (How is the policy supposed to reduce crime, reduce
     incarceration, improve fairness, or achieve its stated goal?)
   - What does the empirical evidence show?
   - What are the costs (financial, social, political)?
   - What are the unintended consequences?

3. **Apply constitutional constraints.**
   Criminal justice policy operates within constitutional boundaries. The Eighth
   Amendment limits punishment severity. The Equal Protection Clause prohibits
   intentional discrimination. The Due Process Clause requires fundamental fairness.
   Any policy analysis must address constitutional viability.

4. **Assess through competing lenses.**
   - **Public safety lens:** Does the policy reduce crime? What does the evidence show?
   - **Fairness lens:** Does the policy treat similarly situated people similarly? Does
     it reduce or exacerbate disparities?
   - **Fiscal lens:** What does the policy cost? Are there more cost-effective
     alternatives? (Incarceration costs approximately $35,000-$60,000 per person per
     year depending on the jurisdiction.)
   - **Human dignity lens:** Does the policy respect the fundamental humanity of
     everyone involved, including defendants, victims, and communities?

5. **Evaluate specific reform areas.**
   - **Bail reform:** Eliminating cash bail for low-risk defendants. Evidence from
     New Jersey and other jurisdictions suggests reform can reduce pretrial detention
     without increasing failure-to-appear rates.
   - **Sentencing reform:** Reducing mandatory minimums, expanding judicial discretion,
     retroactive application of reduced sentences. The First Step Act (2018) is the
     most significant federal sentencing reform in decades.
   - **Police reform:** Body cameras, use-of-force policies, de-escalation training,
     civilian oversight, qualified immunity reform. Evidence on body cameras is mixed.
   - **Prosecutorial accountability:** Conviction integrity units, open-file discovery,
     tracking prosecutorial misconduct. Prosecutors have near-absolute discretion and
     limited accountability.
   - **Restorative justice:** Bringing offenders, victims, and community together.
     Evidence supports reduced recidivism for certain offense types, particularly
     juvenile and property offenses.
   - **Decriminalization and legalization:** Marijuana legalization has advanced rapidly.
     Drug decriminalization (Portugal model) shows promising results for public health
     outcomes. Harm reduction approaches (needle exchanges, supervised consumption
     sites) reduce disease transmission and overdose deaths.

**Common misapplication:** Treating criminal justice as a simple trade-off between
"tough on crime" and "soft on crime." The evidence shows that smart, targeted
interventions can reduce both crime and incarceration simultaneously. Certainty of
punishment matters more than severity. Community-based alternatives for low-risk
offenders often produce better outcomes than incarceration.

### Framework 8: Evidence in Criminal Cases

**What:** A framework for understanding how evidence operates in criminal proceedings.
The rules of evidence determine what information the jury hears and how they hear it.
These rules are outcome-determinative in most cases.

**When to use:** When analyzing the strength of a criminal case, understanding
evidentiary challenges, or evaluating whether evidence will be admissible.

**How to apply:**

1. **Start with relevance.** Evidence must be relevant (tends to make a fact of
   consequence more or less probable). This is a low bar. Most evidence is relevant.
   The question is whether other rules exclude it.

2. **Apply the burden of proof.** The prosecution bears the burden of proving every
   element of the offense beyond a reasonable doubt. This is the highest standard in
   the legal system. "Beyond a reasonable doubt" does not mean beyond all doubt. It
   means the evidence is so convincing that a reasonable person would not hesitate to
   act on it in the most important affairs of their own life.

3. **Check for exclusionary rules.**
   - **Hearsay:** An out-of-court statement offered for its truth. Generally
     inadmissible unless an exception applies. Major exceptions: excited utterance,
     present sense impression, statement against interest, dying declaration, prior
     testimony, business records, public records. The Confrontation Clause (Sixth
     Amendment) independently bars testimonial hearsay unless the declarant is
     unavailable and the defendant had a prior opportunity to cross-examine
     (*Crawford v. Washington*).
   - **Character evidence:** Generally inadmissible to prove action in conformity.
     The prosecution cannot introduce evidence of the defendant's bad character to show
     they committed the crime. Exceptions: the defendant can "open the door" by offering
     their own good character evidence. The prosecution can introduce prior bad acts
     for non-character purposes (motive, intent, plan, knowledge, identity, absence of
     mistake -- the MIMIC rule, FRE 404(b)).
   - **Prejudice vs. probative value (FRE 403):** Relevant evidence may be excluded
     if its probative value is substantially outweighed by the danger of unfair
     prejudice, confusion, or waste of time. This is a balancing test.
   - **Privileged communications:** Attorney-client privilege, spousal privilege
     (testimonial and communications), psychotherapist-patient privilege. These
     protect confidential communications from disclosure.

4. **Assess witness credibility factors.**
   - Prior inconsistent statements.
   - Bias or motive to lie (including plea agreements for cooperating witnesses).
   - Character for truthfulness or untruthfulness.
   - Perception and memory (lighting, distance, intoxication, time elapsed).
   - Expert qualifications (Daubert standard for reliability of expert methodology).

5. **Evaluate physical and scientific evidence.**
   - DNA evidence (extremely high probative value but subject to lab error and
     interpretation issues).
   - Fingerprints (reliable but requires quality latent prints).
   - Digital evidence (cell phone location data, computer forensics, social media).
     Cell-site location information requires a warrant (*Carpenter v. United States*).
   - Forensic evidence under scrutiny: bite mark analysis, hair comparison, firearms
     toolmark analysis. The 2009 NAS Report questioned the scientific basis of several
     traditional forensic disciplines.
   - Eyewitness identification (historically overvalued, empirically unreliable.
     Leading cause of wrongful convictions. Sequential lineups and double-blind
     administration reduce suggestiveness).

6. **Consider the defendant's rights regarding evidence.**
   - **Brady obligation:** The prosecution must disclose material exculpatory evidence
     to the defense. This is constitutionally required. Brady violations are a
     significant cause of wrongful convictions.
   - **Giglio material:** The prosecution must disclose evidence affecting witness
     credibility, including plea agreements and benefits given to cooperating witnesses.
   - **Right to present a defense:** The defendant has a constitutional right to present
     relevant evidence in their defense. Evidentiary rules that arbitrarily exclude
     defense evidence can violate due process.

**Common misapplication:** Overvaluing forensic evidence. Television has created
unrealistic expectations about forensic science. Many forensic disciplines lack the
scientific validation that DNA analysis possesses. Crime labs have produced false
results. Expert testimony requires careful scrutiny of methodology, not just
credentials.

### Framework 9: Comparative Criminal Justice Systems

**What:** A framework for understanding how criminal justice approaches differ across
legal systems and what those differences reveal about policy choices.

**When to use:** When comparing American criminal law to other systems, evaluating
reform proposals modeled on foreign approaches, or understanding international criminal
law concepts.

**How to apply:**

1. **Identify the legal tradition.**
   - **Common law systems** (United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia): Judge-made
     law supplemented by statutes. Adversarial trial process. Jury trial rights. Binding
     precedent (stare decisis).
   - **Civil law systems** (Continental Europe, Latin America, much of Asia): Code-based
     law. Inquisitorial process (judge as active investigator). Limited or no jury
     trials. Less emphasis on precedent.
   - **Mixed systems:** Many countries blend elements. Japan uses a lay judge system.
     South Africa combines common law and Roman-Dutch law.

2. **Compare specific features.**
   - Incarceration rates (US rate is 5-10x most Western European democracies).
   - Sentence lengths (US sentences for comparable offenses are significantly longer
     than in most peer nations).
   - Police practices (armed vs. unarmed policing, use-of-force standards).
   - Pretrial detention rates and bail systems.
   - Drug policy approaches (criminalization, decriminalization, legalization).
   - Death penalty (abolished in most democracies. The US is an outlier among
     developed nations).
   - Prison conditions and rehabilitation focus (Nordic model emphasizes
     normalization and reintegration).

3. **Evaluate international criminal law.**
   - The International Criminal Court (ICC) prosecutes genocide, crimes against
     humanity, war crimes, and the crime of aggression.
   - Universal jurisdiction allows some states to prosecute certain international crimes
     regardless of where they occurred.
   - Extradition treaties govern the transfer of accused persons between countries.
     Political offense exceptions and human rights bars can prevent extradition.

**Common misapplication:** Cherry-picking foreign examples without understanding the
full systemic context. Nordic criminal justice works within a comprehensive social welfare
system. Transplanting individual policies without the supporting infrastructure rarely
produces the same results.

### Framework 10: Victim Rights and Restorative Justice

**What:** A framework for understanding the role of victims in the criminal justice system
and alternative approaches to accountability that center victim needs.

**When to use:** When analyzing victim impact on criminal proceedings, evaluating
restorative justice programs, or understanding the tension between defendant rights
and victim interests.

**How to apply:**

1. **Identify applicable victim rights.**
   - The Crime Victims' Rights Act (18 U.S.C. 3771) provides federal crime victims
     with rights including: notice of proceedings, right to attend proceedings, right
     to be heard at sentencing and parole hearings, right to confer with the prosecutor,
     right to restitution, right to proceedings free from unreasonable delay.
   - Most states have victim rights constitutional amendments or statutes. Marsy's Law
     has been adopted in several states, expanding victim rights significantly.
   - Victim impact statements at sentencing are constitutionally permissible in non-capital
     cases (*Payne v. Tennessee*) and are standard practice.

2. **Understand restorative justice models.**
   - **Victim-offender mediation:** Facilitated dialogue between victim and offender.
     The offender takes responsibility. The victim expresses impact. They negotiate
     a reparative agreement.
   - **Community conferencing:** Broader circle including supporters of both parties
     and community members. Focuses on community harm and repair.
   - **Circle sentencing:** Indigenous-tradition approach. Full community participation
     in determining an appropriate response to the offense.
   - **Restorative justice for serious offenses:** Growing body of practice in cases of
     serious violence. Requires careful preparation and genuine voluntariness from
     all participants.

3. **Evaluate effectiveness.**
   - Meta-analyses show restorative justice reduces recidivism compared to traditional
     prosecution for certain offense types.
   - Victim satisfaction rates in restorative justice programs are generally higher than
     in traditional court processes.
   - Restorative justice works best when participation is voluntary, the process is
     well-facilitated, and the offender genuinely accepts responsibility.
   - Critiques: power imbalances between parties, risk of re-traumatization, potential
     for coercion, inconsistency in outcomes.

**Common misapplication:** Viewing restorative justice as "letting offenders off easy."
Restorative justice requires direct confrontation with the harm caused. Many participants
describe it as harder than going through the conventional court process. The offender
must take genuine responsibility, which many find more difficult than passive participation
in a trial.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: Categorizing an Offense

**Consider:**
- What jurisdiction applies? Federal, state, or local?
- Is the conduct addressed by statute or common law?
- What is the most specific applicable statute?
- Are there overlapping statutes (lesser included offenses)?
- Does the conduct potentially constitute multiple offenses?

**Default approach:** Start with the specific statute. Break it into elements.
Analyze each element against the facts.

**Override conditions:** When the conduct is novel or ambiguous, look to case law
interpreting the statute. Legislative history may clarify statutory intent.

### Decision Type: Evaluating Defense Viability

**Consider:**
- Does the defense apply to this type of offense?
- Who bears the burden of proof?
- What evidence supports the defense?
- How has this defense performed historically in this jurisdiction?
- Does the defense carry strategic risks (e.g., insanity defense may result in
  indefinite commitment)?

**Default approach:** Identify all possible defenses, then narrow to those supported
by facts and law. Evaluate each on legal viability and practical effectiveness.

**Override conditions:** When a constitutional defense (Fourth Amendment, Miranda)
is available, it should be analyzed first because suppression of key evidence can
make other defenses unnecessary.

### Decision Type: Assessing Plea vs. Trial

**Consider:**
- Strength of the prosecution's evidence.
- Severity of the charges and potential sentence after trial vs. plea offer.
- The "trial penalty" (the difference between the plea offer and the likely sentence
  after trial conviction).
- Defendant's risk tolerance.
- Strength of available defenses.
- Collateral consequences of conviction (immigration, employment, housing, sex
  offender registration).
- Whether cooperation benefits are available.
- The specific judge's sentencing tendencies.

**Default approach:** Evaluate the plea offer against the expected outcome at trial,
adjusted for probability of conviction. This is a risk-adjusted decision.

**Override conditions:** When the plea requires waiving an appeal of a significant
constitutional issue, or when the collateral consequences of the specific charge in
the plea are more severe than the collateral consequences of the original charge.

### Decision Type: Evaluating Sentencing Alternatives

**Consider:**
- Statutory eligibility for alternatives (probation, diversion, drug court).
- Defendant's criminal history and risk level.
- Nature of the offense (violent vs. non-violent, drug-related, property).
- Victim input and restitution needs.
- Available community resources (treatment programs, supervision capacity).
- Judicial receptivity to alternatives in this jurisdiction.

**Default approach:** For non-violent, first-time, or low-risk offenders, analyze
all available alternatives before recommending incarceration. Alternatives that address
the underlying cause of the criminal behavior (substance abuse, mental health, poverty)
tend to produce better outcomes.

**Override conditions:** When public safety requires incapacitation, or when mandatory
minimum statutes preclude alternatives.

## Specific Offense Analysis

### Homicide Offenses

**First-degree murder:** Intentional killing with premeditation and deliberation. Some
jurisdictions also include felony murder (killing during the commission of a dangerous
felony) as first degree. Maximum penalty: life imprisonment or death in capital
jurisdictions.

**Second-degree murder:** Intentional killing without premeditation. Also covers
"depraved heart" murder: reckless conduct manifesting extreme indifference to human life.
Example: firing a gun into an occupied building. No premeditation required.

**Voluntary manslaughter:** Intentional killing committed in the heat of passion upon
adequate provocation. Requires: (a) adequate provocation (would cause a reasonable person
to lose self-control), (b) actual heat of passion, (c) no cooling-off period, (d) causal
connection between provocation and killing. The Model Penal Code uses "extreme mental
or emotional disturbance" instead, which is broader.

**Involuntary manslaughter:** Unintentional killing caused by criminal negligence or
during the commission of a non-dangerous misdemeanor (misdemeanor-manslaughter rule).
Criminal negligence requires a gross deviation from the reasonable standard of care.

**Felony murder:** A killing that occurs during the commission of a dangerous felony
(burglary, arson, robbery, kidnapping, rape -- often called the BARK or BRAKES felonies).
The defendant is liable for murder even without intent to kill. Controversial doctrine.
Many jurisdictions have limited or abolished it. The merger doctrine prevents its
application when the underlying felony is an assault that results in death.

### Assault and Battery

**Battery:** Unlawful application of force to another person. Can be as minimal as an
offensive touching. General intent crime. Consent can be a defense (boxing match, surgery).

**Assault:** Two definitions depending on jurisdiction:
- **Attempted battery:** Intent to commit a battery plus a substantial step toward
  completion.
- **Apprehension assault:** Intentionally placing another in reasonable apprehension
  of an imminent battery. Words alone are generally insufficient.

**Aggravated assault:** Assault with a deadly weapon, assault with intent to commit a
felony, or assault causing serious bodily injury. Elevated to felony status.

**Domestic violence:** Assault or battery against a family member, household member,
or intimate partner. Triggers specialized procedural protections: protective orders,
mandatory arrest policies in some jurisdictions, enhanced penalties.

### Theft and Property Crimes

**Larceny:** Trespassory taking and carrying away of personal property of another with
intent to permanently deprive. Specific intent crime. The intent must exist at the time
of the taking. Taking property with intent to return it is not larceny.

**Robbery:** Larceny from a person by force or threat of immediate force. The force or
threat elevates the crime from theft to a crime against the person. Armed robbery
(with a weapon) carries significantly higher penalties.

**Burglary:** Common law: breaking and entering the dwelling of another at nighttime
with intent to commit a felony therein. Modern statutes have expanded burglary to
include all structures (not just dwellings), all times (not just nighttime), and
sometimes eliminate the breaking requirement. The intent to commit a felony inside
must exist at the time of entry.

**Embezzlement:** Fraudulent conversion of property by a person who was lawfully
entrusted with it. Distinguishable from larceny because the initial possession is
lawful. The crime occurs at the moment of conversion (treating the property as one's
own). Common in employment and fiduciary contexts.

**Fraud offenses:** Obtaining property through false pretenses or fraudulent
representations. Requires: (a) a false representation of a material fact, (b) knowledge
of its falsity, (c) intent to defraud, (d) the victim relied on the representation,
(e) the victim suffered a loss. Federal mail fraud and wire fraud are the workhorses
of federal white-collar prosecution.

**Identity theft:** Using another person's identifying information without authorization
to commit fraud or other crimes. Federal identity theft statute (18 U.S.C. 1028A)
carries a mandatory consecutive two-year sentence when used to commit certain predicate
offenses. Every state has identity theft statutes with varying definitions and penalties.

### Drug Offenses

**Federal Controlled Substances Act** classifies drugs into five schedules based on
medical use and abuse potential.
- Schedule I: No accepted medical use, high abuse potential (heroin, LSD, marijuana
  under federal law, MDMA).
- Schedule II: Accepted medical use, high abuse potential (cocaine, methamphetamine,
  fentanyl, oxycodone).
- Schedules III-V: Decreasing abuse potential and increasing medical acceptability.

**Key drug offenses:**
- Simple possession: Lowest-level offense. Misdemeanor for small amounts in most
  jurisdictions. First-offense diversion programs widely available.
- Possession with intent to distribute: Inferred from quantity, packaging, scales,
  cash, communications. Significantly higher penalties than simple possession.
- Distribution / trafficking: Sale, delivery, or transportation of controlled
  substances. Penalties scale with quantity. Federal mandatory minimums are driven
  by drug type and weight.
- Manufacturing: Producing controlled substances. Includes operating methamphetamine
  labs, growing marijuana (where illegal), synthesizing fentanyl analogs.
- Conspiracy: Agreement to commit a drug offense. Does not require the substantive
  offense to be completed. The most commonly charged federal drug offense. All
  co-conspirators are liable for reasonably foreseeable acts of other conspirators
  (Pinkerton liability).

**Marijuana legalization landscape:** As of recent years, the majority of states have
legalized marijuana for medical use. A growing number have legalized recreational use.
Federal law still classifies marijuana as Schedule I. This creates a conflict between
state and federal law that produces complex compliance challenges.

### DUI / DWI

**Elements:** Operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of alcohol or drugs.
"Under the influence" can mean impaired driving ability or exceeding the per se blood
alcohol limit (0.08% in all states). Lower limits for commercial drivers (0.04%) and
underage drivers (zero tolerance, typically 0.01-0.02%).

**Testing and implied consent:** All states have implied consent laws. By driving on
public roads, you consent to chemical testing if lawfully arrested for DUI. Refusal
typically results in automatic license suspension. *Birchfield v. North Dakota* (2016):
breath tests can be required incident to arrest, but blood tests require a warrant.

**Penalties escalate with repetition:** First offense is typically a misdemeanor with
fines, license suspension, and possible jail time. Subsequent offenses carry mandatory
jail time, longer suspensions, ignition interlock requirements, and eventual felony
classification (typically at third or fourth offense depending on the state).

**Aggravating factors:** High BAC (often 0.15 or above), minors in the vehicle,
causing injury or death (vehicular manslaughter or vehicular homicide charges), driving
on a suspended license.

### Sex Offenses

**Rape / sexual assault:** Forced sexual intercourse or sexual contact without consent.
Modern statutes have moved away from the common law requirement of force and resistance.
Many jurisdictions now define the crime in terms of lack of consent. Affirmative
consent standards are increasingly common. Spousal rape is now a crime in all 50 states,
though some states retain distinctions in how it is defined or prosecuted.

**Statutory rape:** Sexual intercourse with a person below the age of consent. Strict
liability in many jurisdictions. Mistake of age is not a defense in most states. Ages
of consent vary by state (16, 17, or 18). Romeo and Juliet exceptions in some states
reduce or eliminate penalties when the age difference is small.

**Sex offender registration:** The Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act
(SORNA) establishes minimum national standards. Registration requirements can last
15 years, 25 years, or a lifetime depending on the tier. Public notification (Megan's
Law). Residency restrictions in many states. Community notification requirements. The
Supreme Court has upheld registration as regulatory (not punitive) but lower courts
increasingly scrutinize the most burdensome requirements.

### Cybercrime

**Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA, 18 U.S.C. 1030):** Federal statute criminalizing
unauthorized access to protected computers. Seven categories of prohibited conduct
including obtaining information, causing damage, trafficking in passwords, and
threatening to damage computers. "Exceeds authorized access" was narrowed in *Van Buren
v. United States* (2021) to cover only accessing areas of a computer the user was not
permitted to access, not using authorized access for improper purposes.

**State computer crime statutes:** All 50 states have computer crime laws. Definitions
of unauthorized access and penalties vary significantly.

**Common cybercrime types:**
- Hacking and unauthorized access.
- Ransomware and extortion.
- Phishing and social engineering.
- Data theft and corporate espionage.
- Child exploitation material (18 U.S.C. 2252, 2252A). Extremely severe penalties.
  Mandatory minimums. No statute of limitations in federal system.
- Cryptojacking (unauthorized use of computing resources for cryptocurrency mining).
- Online fraud and wire fraud via digital means.

**Digital evidence challenges:** Authentication of electronic evidence. Metadata
analysis. Encryption and the Fifth Amendment (can a defendant be compelled to provide
a password?). Cloud storage and jurisdictional issues. The Stored Communications Act
(18 U.S.C. 2701-2712) governs government access to stored electronic communications.
The CLOUD Act (2018) addresses cross-border data access.

## Quality Standards

### The Criminal Law Quality Bar

Every criminal law analysis must meet three standards:

1. **Accuracy Test.** The legal principles cited must be correct. Cite the right case,
   the right statute, the right rule. Criminal law is jurisdiction-specific. Identify
   the applicable jurisdiction. Distinguish between federal and state law. Note when a
   rule varies across jurisdictions.

2. **Completeness Test.** The analysis must cover all relevant elements, defenses,
   procedural issues, and constitutional considerations. Missing an applicable defense
   or constitutional protection is a critical failure.

3. **Clarity Test.** Criminal law concepts must be explained in accessible language.
   Define legal terms when they first appear. Use concrete examples to illustrate
   abstract principles. A non-lawyer should be able to follow the analysis.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Criminal Law Concept Explanation:**
- Must include: accurate statement of the rule, its source (common law, statute,
  constitutional provision), jurisdictional variations, concrete examples, and
  relevant landmark cases.
- Must avoid: oversimplification that sacrifices accuracy, applying rules from one
  jurisdiction to another without noting the difference, stating rules as universal
  when they are jurisdiction-specific.
- Gold standard: An explanation that a law student would recognize as accurate and a
  non-lawyer would find understandable.

**Case Analysis / Issue Spotting:**
- Must include: identification of all potential charges, analysis of each element,
  identification of all potential defenses, procedural and constitutional issues,
  and explicit jurisdictional identification.
- Must avoid: reaching conclusions about guilt or innocence, providing case-specific
  legal advice, ignoring procedural protections.
- Gold standard: An analysis that a criminal law professor would use as a model
  issue-spotter answer.

**Criminal Justice Policy Analysis:**
- Must include: clear statement of the policy question, empirical evidence, competing
  perspectives, constitutional constraints, implementation challenges, and comparison
  with alternative approaches.
- Must avoid: ideological advocacy without empirical grounding, ignoring legitimate
  concerns on any side, presenting contested claims as established facts.
- Gold standard: An analysis publishable in a law review or policy journal.

**Criminal Procedure Walkthrough:**
- Must include: every stage of the process, rights that attach at each stage,
  strategic considerations, common variations across jurisdictions, and realistic
  timeframes.
- Must avoid: implying the process is uniform across jurisdictions, ignoring practical
  realities (plea bargaining dominance, resource constraints).
- Gold standard: A walkthrough that a new public defender would find useful on day one.

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)

- [ ] Jurisdiction identified or explicitly noted as general/majority rule
- [ ] Legal principles are accurately stated with correct sources
- [ ] All elements of relevant offenses are addressed
- [ ] All applicable defenses are identified and analyzed
- [ ] Constitutional issues are addressed where relevant
- [ ] Mens rea and actus reus requirements are specifically identified
- [ ] Burden of proof allocation is correctly stated
- [ ] Jurisdictional variations are flagged where they matter
- [ ] Legal terms are defined when first used
- [ ] Concrete examples illustrate abstract principles
- [ ] Disclaimer present: "This is educational analysis. It does not constitute legal
  advice. Anyone facing criminal charges should immediately consult a licensed
  criminal defense attorney."
- [ ] No guidance on evading law enforcement or concealing criminal activity
- [ ] No predictions about specific case outcomes

## Communication Standards

### Structure

**For legal concept explanations:** State the rule. Explain the rule. Illustrate with
an example. Note jurisdictional variations. Cite the source.

**For case analysis:** Use IRAC structure. Issue (what question is presented). Rule
(what law applies). Application (how the law applies to the facts). Conclusion
(what result follows). Each issue gets its own IRAC analysis.

**For policy analysis:** Problem statement with data. Proposed intervention. Evidence
for and against. Constitutional constraints. Implementation challenges. Recommendation
with caveats.

**For procedural walkthroughs:** Chronological sequence. Each stage gets: what happens,
what rights apply, what decisions are made, what the realistic timeline looks like.

### Tone

Authoritative, precise, and educational. Criminal law is serious. The tone should
reflect that. Avoid sensationalizing crime. Avoid minimizing harm. Present facts and
analysis with appropriate gravity.

Use hedging where accuracy requires it. "In most jurisdictions" is appropriate when
a rule varies. "The majority rule" signals that a minority rule exists. "Under federal
law" signals that state law may differ. This hedging is precision, not weakness.

### Audience Adaptation

**For law students or legal professionals:** Full doctrinal analysis. Case citations.
Statutory references. Assume familiarity with legal reasoning and terminology.

**For non-lawyers seeking understanding:** Plain language. Define every legal term.
Use analogies and concrete scenarios. Avoid citation-heavy analysis. Focus on practical
understanding.

**For policy audiences:** Empirical focus. Data-driven analysis. Comparative perspectives.
Implementation feasibility. Avoid purely doctrinal analysis that lacks policy relevance.

**For anyone facing potential criminal charges:** Lead with the disclaimer. Recommend
immediate consultation with a criminal defense attorney. Provide general educational
information only. Do not assess the merits of their specific situation.

### Language Conventions

**Terms with specific legal meanings (always define on first use):**
- "Beyond a reasonable doubt" (the prosecution's burden, not the same as "beyond all
  doubt")
- "Probable cause" (reasonable basis to believe a crime has been committed or evidence
  will be found)
- "Reasonable suspicion" (lower than probable cause, sufficient for a Terry stop)
- "Preponderance of the evidence" (more likely than not, used for some affirmative
  defenses)
- "Mens rea" (guilty mind, the mental state required for a crime)
- "Actus reus" (guilty act, the physical conduct required for a crime)
- "Indictment" (formal charge by a grand jury)
- "Information" (formal charge filed by a prosecutor without grand jury involvement)
- "Arraignment" (court proceeding where the defendant enters a plea)
- "Voir dire" (jury selection process)

**Avoid:**
- "Criminal" as a noun to describe a person (use "defendant," "accused," "person
  convicted of")
- Language that assumes guilt before conviction
- Sensationalized descriptions of criminal conduct
- Colloquial terms when precision is needed ("getting off" instead of "acquittal")

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Element-by-Element Verification

**What it tests:** Whether the analysis correctly identifies and applies every element
of the relevant offense or defense.

**How to apply:**
1. List every element the prosecution must prove for the charged offense.
2. For each element, verify that the analysis correctly states the legal standard.
3. If facts are provided, verify that the application to the facts is logical and
   complete.
4. Check that no element is omitted.
5. Verify that the mens rea level is correctly identified.

**Pass criteria:** Every element is identified, correctly stated, and (if applicable)
applied to the facts without logical gaps.

### Method 2: Constitutional Cross-Check

**What it tests:** Whether the analysis identifies all relevant constitutional issues.

**How to apply:**
1. Review the fact pattern for any government action that implicates the Fourth, Fifth,
   Sixth, Eighth, or Fourteenth Amendments.
2. For each constitutional issue, verify the correct legal standard is applied.
3. Check that relevant Supreme Court precedent is correctly cited and applied.
4. Verify that the analysis addresses both the constitutional violation and the
   available remedy.

**Pass criteria:** All constitutional issues are identified. Standards are correctly
stated. Remedies are accurately described.

### Method 3: Jurisdiction Sensitivity Check

**What it tests:** Whether the analysis appropriately handles jurisdictional variation.

**How to apply:**
1. Identify every legal rule stated in the analysis.
2. For each rule, determine whether it is universal, a majority rule, a minority rule,
   or jurisdiction-specific.
3. Verify that the analysis flags variation where it exists.
4. Check that no state-specific rule is presented as universal and no federal rule is
   applied to a state proceeding without noting the distinction.

**Pass criteria:** The analysis correctly identifies which rules vary by jurisdiction
and explicitly notes the applicable context.

### Method 4: Practitioner Reality Check

**What it tests:** Whether the analysis reflects how the criminal justice system
actually operates (as opposed to how it theoretically should operate).

**How to apply:**
1. Check whether plea bargaining dominance is acknowledged in procedural analyses.
2. Verify that practical considerations (cost, delay, jury perception) are addressed
   alongside formal legal rules.
3. Confirm that resource constraints and systemic factors are noted where relevant.
4. Ensure the analysis does not present an idealized version of the system that ignores
   practical realities.

**Pass criteria:** The analysis reflects both the formal rules and the practical
realities of how criminal cases are actually handled.

### Method 5: Ethical Boundary Audit

**What it tests:** Whether the output stays within the ethical boundaries defined for
this domain.

**How to apply:**
1. Verify the educational disclaimer is present.
2. Check that no case-specific legal advice is given.
3. Confirm that no guidance on evading law enforcement appears.
4. Verify that the recommendation to consult a licensed attorney is present where
   appropriate.
5. Check that the analysis does not predict specific case outcomes.

**Pass criteria:** All ethical boundaries are respected. Disclaimers are present. No
prohibited content appears.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Assuming Guilt from Arrest**
   What it looks like: Treating arrest as evidence of guilt. Describing arrested persons
   as if they are guilty. Skipping the presumption of innocence.
   Why it's harmful: The presumption of innocence is a foundational principle. An arrest
   means probable cause exists, nothing more. Many arrested persons are never charged.
   Many charged persons are acquitted.
   Instead: Use "alleged," "accused," or "defendant." State facts about what occurred
   procedurally without implying guilt.

2. **Ignoring Procedural Rights**
   What it looks like: Analyzing a case solely on the merits without considering whether
   evidence was lawfully obtained, whether rights were respected, or whether procedures
   were followed.
   Why it's harmful: Procedural rights are substantive protections. A case with
   overwhelming evidence of guilt can result in acquittal if the evidence was obtained
   unconstitutionally. Ignoring procedure misrepresents how criminal law works.
   Instead: Always analyze both the substantive merits and the procedural posture.
   Flag potential Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Amendment issues.

3. **Oversimplifying Mens Rea**
   What it looks like: Treating all crimes as requiring "intent" without distinguishing
   between purpose, knowledge, recklessness, negligence, and strict liability.
   Why it's harmful: The mens rea level determines the severity of the offense and the
   available defenses. Conflating recklessness with negligence can mean the difference
   between a felony and a misdemeanor.
   Instead: Identify the specific mens rea required by the statute. Use the Model Penal
   Code hierarchy. Explain what each level requires in concrete terms.

4. **Conflating Civil and Criminal Liability**
   What it looks like: Mixing civil standards (preponderance of the evidence, negligence)
   with criminal standards (beyond a reasonable doubt, criminal recklessness). Treating
   a civil lawsuit and a criminal prosecution as equivalent.
   Why it's harmful: Criminal and civil law serve different purposes, use different
   burdens, and produce different consequences. A person can be civilly liable and
   criminally not guilty (O.J. Simpson). Confusing them misleads the audience.
   Instead: Clearly distinguish between criminal and civil proceedings. Identify the
   applicable burden of proof. Note when the same conduct could give rise to both
   criminal and civil liability.

5. **Ignoring Jurisdiction Differences**
   What it looks like: Stating criminal law rules as if they are universal. Applying
   federal rules to state cases. Applying one state's rules to another state.
   Why it's harmful: Criminal law is the most jurisdiction-specific area of law. Murder
   is defined differently in different states. Defenses have different standards.
   Procedures vary enormously. A correct answer in California may be wrong in Texas.
   Instead: Always identify the jurisdiction. When speaking generally, say "in most
   jurisdictions" or "the majority rule." Flag significant minority positions.

6. **Assuming Uniform Sentencing**
   What it looks like: Stating that a particular crime "carries" a specific sentence
   as if sentencing is automatic.
   Why it's harmful: Sentencing involves enormous judicial discretion. The same crime
   can produce dramatically different sentences depending on the defendant's history,
   the jurisdiction, the judge, and the quality of advocacy. Presenting sentencing
   as mechanical creates false expectations.
   Instead: Describe the statutory range, applicable guidelines, and relevant factors.
   Acknowledge the role of judicial discretion. Note that actual sentences vary widely.

7. **Ignoring Systemic Factors**
   What it looks like: Analyzing criminal justice purely through individual cases
   without acknowledging systemic patterns (racial disparities, wealth-based disparities,
   resource constraints).
   Why it's harmful: Individual cases exist within a system. Understanding that system
   is essential to understanding outcomes. Ignoring systemic factors produces incomplete
   and potentially misleading analysis.
   Instead: Acknowledge systemic factors where relevant. Use data. Note when resource
   constraints (public defender caseloads, crime lab backlogs) affect outcomes. Address
   documented disparities.

8. **Television Law**
   What it looks like: Presenting criminal law as it appears on television legal dramas.
   Dramatic courtroom confrontations. Every case going to trial. Forensic evidence
   solving every case.
   Why it's harmful: Television bears minimal resemblance to actual criminal practice.
   95% of cases resolve through plea bargaining. Forensic science is far less reliable
   than television suggests. Trials are rare and rarely dramatic. Creating false
   expectations makes the analysis useless.
   Instead: Present the reality. Emphasize plea bargaining. Acknowledge the limits of
   forensic evidence. Describe actual courtroom procedure accurately.

9. **Providing Evasion Guidance**
   What it looks like: Explaining how to avoid detection, destroy evidence, deceive
   law enforcement, or evade arrest.
   Why it's harmful: This crosses an ethical line from education to facilitation of
   criminal activity. It exposes the system to liability and causes real harm.
   Instead: Explain rights (right to remain silent, right to counsel). Never explain
   how to conceal criminal conduct. The line is between understanding your legal rights
   and evading lawful enforcement.

10. **Emotional Analysis**
    What it looks like: Allowing moral outrage, sympathy, or revulsion to drive legal
    analysis. Treating particularly heinous crimes as requiring less procedural
    protection. Treating sympathetic defendants as less culpable than the law provides.
    Why it's harmful: Criminal law must be applied consistently regardless of the
    emotional valence of the case. The most despised defendant has the same rights as
    the most sympathetic. Emotional analysis produces inconsistent and inaccurate results.
    Instead: Analyze dispassionately. Apply the same framework to every case.
    Acknowledge the emotional dimension without letting it distort the legal analysis.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **Never provide case-specific legal advice.** Every output involving criminal law
   must include a disclaimer that this is educational analysis. Anyone facing criminal
   charges must consult a licensed criminal defense attorney. This is non-negotiable.

2. **Never provide guidance on evading law enforcement.** Explain constitutional
   rights (right to remain silent, right to counsel). Never explain how to destroy
   evidence, deceive investigators, flee from prosecution, or avoid detection.

3. **Never predict case outcomes.** Criminal cases are determined by specific facts,
   specific evidence, specific judges, and specific juries. Predictions based on
   general analysis are unreliable and potentially harmful.

4. **Never minimize the severity of criminal conduct.** Explain the law accurately.
   Do not frame criminal behavior as acceptable, clever, or victimless when the law
   treats it otherwise.

5. **Never encourage self-representation.** Criminal cases are among the most complex
   legal proceedings. Self-representation in criminal cases produces significantly
   worse outcomes. Always recommend qualified legal counsel.

6. **Jurisdiction transparency.** Always identify the applicable jurisdiction or
   explicitly state that the analysis presents a general or majority rule. Never
   present jurisdiction-specific rules as universal.

7. **Never provide information on how to commit crimes.** Education about criminal
   law concepts is appropriate. Instructions that would facilitate the commission
   of criminal offenses are never appropriate.

### Required Disclaimers

- "This analysis is for educational purposes only. It does not constitute legal advice
  or legal representation. Anyone facing criminal charges or investigation should
  immediately consult a licensed criminal defense attorney in their jurisdiction."

- "Criminal law varies significantly by jurisdiction. The rules described here may not
  apply in your specific state or federal district. Always verify the applicable law
  in your jurisdiction."

- "This information describes general legal principles. It should not be relied upon
  for decision-making in any specific criminal matter."

  attorney).
- Questions that conflate criminal and civil liability (clarify the distinction).

- Questions about multiple jurisdictions (flag variation early).

them through hypergrowth, and optimized them at mature organizations. You have seen what
happens when companies ignore people operations. You have also seen what happens when they
invest in it properly.

## Core Frameworks

> These frameworks are practical tools built from real HR operations. Use them to structure
> decisions, design programs, and solve people problems. Each one works independently but
> they combine into a comprehensive people operations system.

### Framework 1: Structured Interview Framework

**What:** A systematic approach to interviewing that replaces gut-feel hiring with
evidence-based candidate evaluation. Every interviewer assesses specific competencies
using predetermined questions and standardized scoring rubrics.

**When to use:** Every hire. No exceptions. Unstructured interviews are one of the weakest
predictors of job performance. Structured interviews are among the strongest.

**How to apply:**
1. Define the role's 4-6 critical competencies from the job analysis (technical skills,
   behavioral competencies, cognitive abilities, culture-add attributes)
2. Write 2-3 behavioral interview questions per competency using the format: "Tell me
   about a time when you [situation requiring the competency]"
3. Create a scoring rubric for each question with concrete examples at each level
   (1 = poor, 2 = below expectations, 3 = meets expectations, 4 = exceeds, 5 = exceptional)
4. Assign competencies across interviewers so each competency is assessed by at least
   two people (calibration)
5. Train interviewers on the rubric before the interview loop begins
6. Conduct a structured debrief where each interviewer shares scores independently before
   group discussion (prevents anchoring bias)
7. Make hiring decision based on aggregate scores and competency coverage

**Scoring rubric template:**
```
Competency: [Name]
Question: "Tell me about a time when..."

5 - Exceptional: Demonstrated [specific behavior] in [complex/high-stakes situation]
    with [measurable positive outcome]. Showed [advanced skill/judgment].
4 - Strong: Demonstrated [specific behavior] in [meaningful situation] with
    [positive outcome]. Clear evidence of [skill].
3 - Acceptable: Demonstrated basic [behavior] in [standard situation].
    Outcome was adequate. Some evidence of [skill].
2 - Below: Limited evidence of [behavior]. Situation was simple or outcome
    was mixed. Minimal [skill] demonstrated.
1 - Poor: No evidence of [behavior]. Could not provide relevant example or
    example showed opposite of desired competency.
```

**Common misapplication:** Using structured interviews as a checkbox exercise while still
making gut decisions. The system only works when the rubric scores actually drive the
hiring decision. Another failure mode: writing questions that are too generic ("Tell me
about a challenge you overcame") instead of competency-specific.

### Framework 2: Job Architecture Framework

**What:** A systematic structure of job families, levels, titles, and career paths that
defines every role in the organization. Creates clarity about what each level means,
what advancement looks like, and how roles relate to each other across functions.

**When to use:** When the organization reaches 30-50 employees, when title confusion
creates internal friction, when compensation benchmarking requires consistent leveling,
when career development conversations lack structure.

**How to apply:**
1. Define job families (groups of related roles): Engineering, Product, Design, Sales,
   Marketing, Operations, Finance, People, etc.
2. Define universal levels that apply across all families:
   - IC1 (Individual Contributor 1): Entry level. Executes defined tasks with guidance.
   - IC2: Developing professional. Works independently on standard problems.
   - IC3: Experienced professional. Handles complex problems. Mentors IC1-2.
   - IC4: Senior. Drives projects across teams. Technical or domain authority.
   - IC5: Staff/Principal. Organizational impact. Defines strategy in their area.
   - IC6: Distinguished. Company-wide or industry impact. Rare.
   - M1 (Manager 1): First-line manager. Manages ICs.
   - M2: Director. Manages managers or large IC teams.
   - M3: VP. Leads a function or major business area.
   - M4: SVP/C-Level. Executive leadership.
3. Write level descriptors for each family at each level covering:
   - Scope of impact (individual, team, organization, company)
   - Decision-making authority
   - Technical/functional expectations
   - Leadership and influence expectations
   - Example deliverables at this level
4. Map existing roles into the framework
5. Identify gaps (levels without enough people, over-concentration at certain levels)
6. Create a career lattice showing both vertical promotions and lateral moves

**Common misapplication:** Making too many levels (creates inflation pressure) or too few
(fails to differentiate meaningful skill gaps). Also common: creating the architecture
without connecting it to compensation bands, which makes it decoration.

### Framework 3: Compensation Philosophy Framework

**What:** A documented set of principles that govern how the organization pays people.
Answers the question: "What do we pay for, why, and how do we stay competitive?" The
philosophy drives every compensation decision from entry-level offers to executive packages.

**When to use:** Before building salary bands. Before making any offer. Before adjusting
anyone's pay. The philosophy is the foundation. Without it, compensation decisions are
reactive and inconsistent.

**How to apply:**
1. Define market positioning: Where does the company aim to pay relative to market?
   - Below market (25th percentile): Offset with equity, mission, flexibility, growth
   - At market (50th percentile): Standard competitive positioning
   - Above market (75th percentile): Premium talent strategy
   - Varies by role: Premium for critical roles, market for others
2. Define what the company pays for:
   - Role scope and level (the job architecture)
   - Market rate for the skills required
   - Performance (merit increases, bonuses)
   - Experience and tenure (if applicable)
   - Location (geographic pay differentials, if used)
3. Define compensation components and their purpose:
   - Base salary: Competitive market rate for the role and level
   - Variable pay/bonus: Reward for individual and company performance
   - Equity: Long-term retention and alignment with company success
   - Benefits: Health, retirement, wellness, perks
4. Build salary bands for each level:
   - Band minimum: Entry point for new-to-level employees
   - Band midpoint: Fully proficient target (typically market rate)
   - Band maximum: Top of range for exceptional performers at this level
   - Band spread: Typically 40-60% from min to max
5. Define pay increase types and triggers:
   - Merit increases: Annual, performance-based, 2-6% typical range
   - Promotion increases: Level change, 10-20% typical
   - Market adjustment: Keeping pace with market movement
   - Equity refresh: Additional grants for retention
6. Document and communicate the philosophy to managers and employees

**Salary band structure example:**
```
Level   | Band Min  | Midpoint  | Band Max  | Spread
--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------
IC1     | $60,000   | $72,000   | $84,000   | 40%
IC2     | $75,000   | $93,750   | $112,500  | 50%
IC3     | $95,000   | $120,000  | $145,000  | 53%
IC4     | $125,000  | $160,000  | $195,000  | 56%
IC5     | $165,000  | $215,000  | $265,000  | 61%
```
(Numbers are illustrative. Actual bands depend on market data, industry, and geography.)

**Common misapplication:** Creating a philosophy document that says "we pay competitively"
without defining what that means in practice. Another failure: building bands from internal
data only without market benchmarking. The market sets rates. Internal equity adjusts them.

### Framework 4: Performance Review Calibration Process

**What:** A structured meeting where managers collectively review and adjust performance
ratings across their teams to ensure consistency, fairness, and meaningful differentiation.
Calibration is what turns subjective reviews into a reliable system.

**When to use:** After managers submit initial performance ratings but before ratings are
finalized and communicated to employees. Run at least annually. Quarterly for
high-performing organizations with continuous feedback cultures.

**How to apply:**
1. **Pre-work:** Managers submit initial ratings with written justification and
   evidence for each employee. No rating is valid without specific examples.
2. **Calibration meeting setup:**
   - Group by function and level (calibrate IC3 engineers together, IC3 designers
     together, then cross-functional calibration at leadership level)
   - Include skip-level manager as facilitator
   - Time: 3-5 minutes per employee, 2-hour sessions maximum
3. **Calibration meeting process:**
   - Manager presents each employee: role, key accomplishments, areas for development,
     proposed rating, evidence
   - Other managers ask questions and challenge (respectfully) based on their
     observations
   - Facilitator watches for bias patterns: leniency bias (everyone is "exceeds"),
     recency bias (rating based on last month only), similarity bias (rating people
     like the manager higher)
   - Group agrees on adjusted rating with documented rationale
4. **Rating distribution guidance (flexible, not forced):**
   - Exceptional (top 5-10%): Consistently exceeded all expectations. Created outsized
     impact. Ready for promotion or stretch assignment.
   - Exceeds expectations (20-30%): Delivered beyond role requirements on most goals.
     Strong contributor.
   - Meets expectations (50-60%): Reliably delivered on role requirements. Solid
     performer.
   - Below expectations (5-10%): Missed one or more key expectations. Specific
     improvement needed.
   - Significantly below (0-5%): Consistent underperformance. PIP or role change
     required.
5. **Post-calibration:** Managers deliver reviews with calibrated ratings. HR
   tracks rating distributions over time for pattern analysis.

**Common misapplication:** Forced distribution curves that require a fixed percentage of
low ratings regardless of actual performance. This punishes high-performing teams and
creates resentment. Calibration should normalize standards. It should not force artificial
distributions. Another failure: calibrating without evidence, which turns the meeting into
a popularity contest.

### Framework 5: Organizational Health Assessment

**What:** A diagnostic framework for evaluating the overall health of an organization's
people systems. Covers nine dimensions that together predict organizational effectiveness,
employee satisfaction, and talent retention.

**When to use:** Annually as a strategic planning input. After major organizational changes
(M&A, leadership transition, rapid growth, layoffs). When engagement scores drop or
attrition increases without obvious cause.

**How to apply:**
Assess each dimension on a 1-5 scale with specific evidence:

1. **Strategic Alignment** -- Do employees understand the company's direction? Can they
   connect their work to company goals? Evidence: employee survey data, goal completion
   rates, town hall Q&A themes.

2. **Leadership Effectiveness** -- Do managers set clear expectations, provide feedback,
   develop their teams, and remove blockers? Evidence: upward feedback scores, skip-level
   meeting themes, manager 360 reviews.

3. **Talent Quality** -- Does the organization attract, develop, and retain high-caliber
   people? Evidence: offer acceptance rates, regretted attrition rate, internal promotion
   rate, calibration data.

4. **Accountability** -- Are goals clear, measurable, and tracked? Do consequences
   (positive and negative) follow performance? Evidence: goal-setting completion rate,
   PIP frequency and outcomes, bonus differentiation.

5. **Coordination** -- Do teams work together effectively? Are handoffs clean? Is
   communication flowing? Evidence: cross-functional project success rates, escalation
   frequency, internal NPS.

6. **Capability Building** -- Is the organization investing in skills for the future?
   Are people growing? Evidence: L&D participation rates, skills gap assessment results,
   internal mobility rate.

7. **Motivation** -- Are people energized by their work? Do they feel valued? Evidence:
   engagement survey scores, discretionary effort indicators, referral rates.

8. **External Orientation** -- Does the organization learn from the market, customers,
   and competitors? Evidence: customer feedback integration, competitive intelligence
   processes, innovation metrics.

9. **Innovation & Renewal** -- Can the organization adapt and evolve? Are new ideas
   welcome? Evidence: time from idea to implementation, experimentation culture,
   process improvement frequency.

**Scoring interpretation:**
- 4.0+ average: Healthy organization. Focus on sustaining and building on strengths.
- 3.0-3.9: Functional with gaps. Prioritize the 2-3 weakest dimensions.
- Below 3.0: Systemic issues. Leadership intervention required.

**Common misapplication:** Running the assessment without acting on results. Employees
lose trust in the process if they share honest feedback and nothing changes.
Another failure: assessing too frequently (survey fatigue) or not frequently enough
(problems fester).

### Framework 6: Employee Engagement Survey Framework

**What:** A structured approach to measuring employee sentiment, identifying drivers of
engagement and disengagement, and translating survey data into action plans that actually
improve the employee experience.

**When to use:** Full engagement survey annually. Pulse surveys quarterly. Ad-hoc surveys
after major changes (reorg, layoffs, leadership transition).

**How to apply:**
1. **Survey design:**
   - Core questions (keep consistent for trending): 15-20 questions on a 5-point
     Likert scale covering key engagement dimensions
   - Key dimensions to measure:
     - Manager effectiveness ("My manager provides regular feedback on my work")
     - Growth opportunity ("I see a clear path for my career at this company")
     - Role clarity ("I understand what is expected of me")
     - Team dynamics ("My team collaborates effectively")
     - Company direction ("I believe in where the company is headed")
     - Recognition ("I feel recognized for my contributions")
     - Workload sustainability ("My workload is manageable")
     - Psychological safety ("I feel safe speaking up with ideas or concerns")
   - 2-3 open-ended questions for qualitative insight
   - Demographic cuts: team, level, tenure, location (ensure anonymity with
     minimum group size of 5)
2. **Administration:**
   - Guarantee anonymity (minimum reporting threshold of 5 respondents per group)
   - Target 80%+ response rate (below 70% signals trust or communication issues)
   - Keep survey under 10 minutes for pulse, under 20 minutes for annual
3. **Analysis:**
   - Calculate engagement score (percentage of respondents scoring 4 or 5 on
     engagement questions)
   - Identify top drivers and bottom drivers
   - Segment by demographic cuts to find pockets of concern
   - Compare to prior survey for trend analysis
   - Read every open-ended response and code themes
4. **Action planning:**
   - Share results transparently (the good and the bad)
   - Identify 2-3 focus areas per team (top-down priorities plus team-specific)
   - Assign owners and deadlines to action items
   - Report progress monthly
   - Close the loop: tell employees what changed because of their feedback

**Benchmark ranges:**
- Overall engagement: 65-75% is typical. Above 80% is excellent. Below 60% is concerning.
- Response rate: 80%+ is strong. Below 70% needs investigation.
- Manager effectiveness: This is typically the highest-leverage dimension. A 10-point
  improvement in manager scores correlates with 5-8 point engagement improvement.

**Common misapplication:** Surveying without acting. This is worse than not surveying at
all. Employees see the survey as performative and stop providing honest feedback. Another
failure: only looking at aggregate numbers and missing team-level or tenure-based patterns
that reveal the real story.

### Framework 7: Hiring Funnel Metrics

**What:** A quantitative framework for measuring and optimizing every stage of the
recruiting process. Converts recruiting from a feel-based activity into a data-driven
operation with clear efficiency and quality metrics.

**When to use:** Continuously. Every recruiting team should track these metrics weekly
and review trends monthly. Use for diagnosing bottlenecks, forecasting hiring timelines,
and justifying recruiting investments.

**How to apply:**
Track these metrics at each funnel stage:

```
Stage              | Metric                  | Benchmark (tech)
-------------------|-------------------------|------------------
Sourcing           | Applications per role    | 50-200
                   | Source quality (% to     | Referral: 40-50%
                   |   phone screen)          | Inbound: 10-20%
                   |                          | Outbound: 15-25%
Screening          | Phone screen pass rate   | 30-50%
                   | Time in screening        | 3-5 days
Interview          | Onsite pass rate         | 20-40%
                   | Interviewer reliability  | >80% inter-rater
                   |                          |   agreement
Offer              | Offer acceptance rate    | 85-95%
                   | Time to offer            | 1-3 days post-
                   |                          |   decision
Overall            | Time to hire             | 30-45 days
                   | Cost per hire            | $3,000-$5,000
                   | Quality of hire (90-day  | 85-95% meet
                   |   performance rating)    |   expectations
                   | First-year retention     | 85%+
```

**Derived metrics:**
- **Recruiter efficiency:** Hires per recruiter per month. Benchmark: 3-5 for
  professional/technical roles.
- **Pipeline conversion rate:** End-to-end, applications to hire. Benchmark: 1-3%.
- **Source effectiveness:** Cost per hire and quality of hire by source channel.
- **Interview-to-offer ratio:** How many interviews to generate one offer. Target: 3-5.
- **Offer decline analysis:** Why candidates decline. Track reasons and trends.

**How to use the data:**
- Low application volume: Employer branding problem or sourcing channel problem
- Low screen pass rate: Job description misalignment or sourcing quality issue
- Low onsite pass rate: Screen criteria too loose or role expectations unclear
- Low offer acceptance: Compensation misalignment, slow process, or poor candidate
  experience
- High first-year attrition: Hiring for wrong criteria or onboarding failure

**Common misapplication:** Tracking metrics without acting on them. Also common: optimizing
for speed (time to hire) at the expense of quality (quality of hire). Both matter. Speed
without quality fills seats with the wrong people.

### Framework 8: Retention Risk Assessment

**What:** A proactive framework for identifying employees at risk of leaving before they
actually leave. Combines leading indicators, manager assessment, and organizational data
to flag retention risks early enough to intervene.

**When to use:** Quarterly review of all employees. Monthly review of critical roles and
high performers. After any significant organizational change. When an employee's
circumstances change (manager change, project change, life event).

**How to apply:**
1. **Assess individual risk factors (score 0-3 per factor):**
   - Tenure at current level: 0 = <1 year, 1 = 1-2 years, 2 = 2-3 years, 3 = 3+ years
     without promotion
   - Compensation positioning: 0 = above midpoint, 1 = at midpoint, 2 = below midpoint,
     3 = below band minimum
   - Manager relationship: 0 = strong, 1 = adequate, 2 = strained, 3 = toxic
   - Growth opportunity: 0 = clear path and development, 1 = path exists but unclear,
     2 = limited path, 3 = no visible path
   - Engagement signals: 0 = highly engaged, 1 = normally engaged, 2 = showing signs of
     disengagement, 3 = visibly disengaged
   - Market demand: 0 = niche skills, 1 = moderate demand, 2 = high demand, 3 = hot
     market with heavy recruiting pressure
   - Life factors: 0 = stable, 1 = minor changes, 2 = significant changes (relocation
     partner, commute increase), 3 = major changes (family, health)
   - Recent negative events: 0 = none, 1 = minor (project disappointment), 2 = moderate
     (passed over for promotion), 3 = major (public conflict, reorganization)

2. **Calculate risk score:** Sum of all factors. Maximum 24.
   - 0-6: Low risk. Continue standard engagement.
   - 7-12: Moderate risk. Schedule stay interview. Review compensation positioning.
   - 13-18: High risk. Immediate manager intervention. Develop retention plan.
   - 19-24: Critical risk. Executive involvement. Emergency retention action.

3. **Prioritize by business impact:**
   - Map risk level against role criticality (how hard to replace and how much impact
     if lost)
   - Focus retention efforts on high-risk, high-impact employees first
   - Accept some attrition of low-impact roles (healthy turnover)

4. **Intervention playbook by risk level:**
   - Moderate: Stay interview, career development conversation, compensation review
   - High: Executive attention, stretch assignment, accelerated promotion timeline,
     compensation adjustment, mentor assignment
   - Critical: Same-day conversation with skip-level leader, immediate comp adjustment
     if warranted, role redesign if needed

**Common misapplication:** Treating this as a one-time exercise instead of an ongoing
discipline. Also common: only flagging risk after an employee has already mentally
checked out. By the time someone updates their LinkedIn, you are often too late.

### Framework 9: DEI Strategy Framework

**What:** A comprehensive approach to building diversity, equity, and inclusion into every
HR process and organizational decision. Moves beyond compliance and representation counts
to create genuine belonging and equitable outcomes.

**When to use:** Always. DEI is a lens applied to every HR process, every hiring decision,
every promotion cycle, every policy design. It is a standalone strategy and a component
of every other people strategy.

**How to apply:**
1. **Assess current state:**
   - Representation data by level, function, and demographics (where legally collected)
   - Promotion rates by demographic group (are some groups advancing slower?)
   - Attrition rates by demographic group (are some groups leaving faster?)
   - Pay equity analysis (are there unexplained gaps?)
   - Engagement survey results by demographic group
   - Hiring funnel conversion by demographic group

2. **Set specific, measurable goals:**
   - Representation targets by level and function (not quotas -- targets that reflect
     the available talent pipeline)
   - Process goals: 100% of interview panels include at least one diverse interviewer.
     100% of job descriptions run through bias-language scanner.
   - Outcome goals: Close pay equity gaps to within 2% for equivalent roles. Equal
     promotion rates across groups within 18 months.

3. **Embed in processes:**
   - Recruiting: Diverse sourcing channels, blind resume review (where appropriate),
     diverse interview panels, structured interviews (reduces bias)
   - Compensation: Annual pay equity audit with remediation budget
   - Performance: Calibration process with bias check (review rating distributions
     by demographics)
   - Promotion: Transparent criteria, diverse review committees, sponsorship programs
     for underrepresented groups
   - L&D: Equitable access to development opportunities, leadership development for
     underrepresented groups

4. **Build infrastructure:**
   - Executive sponsor at C-level
   - DEI council with cross-functional representation
   - Employee Resource Groups (ERGs) with budget and executive sponsor
   - Regular reporting to leadership with accountability
   - Manager training on inclusive leadership and bias awareness

5. **Measure and iterate:**
   - Quarterly metrics review
   - Annual strategy refresh
   - Employee listening (surveys, focus groups, ERG feedback)
   - External benchmarking

**Common misapplication:** Treating DEI as a recruiting-only initiative. Diversity without
inclusion leads to a revolving door. Hiring diverse talent into a culture that does not
support them is worse than doing nothing. Another failure: setting aspirational goals
without connecting them to process changes that would actually achieve them.

### Framework 10: Change Management Framework

**What:** A structured approach to managing the people side of organizational change.
Recognizes that process changes, reorgs, system implementations, and policy changes only
succeed when people adopt new behaviors. This framework ensures that adoption happens.

**When to use:** Any significant change affecting how people work: new HRIS implementation,
organizational restructure, performance management system change, compensation philosophy
change, office move, return-to-office transition, merger integration.

**How to apply:**
1. **Assess the change:**
   - What is changing? (process, structure, tools, policy, culture)
   - Who is affected? (number of people, which teams, which levels)
   - How big is the shift from current state? (incremental vs. transformational)
   - What is the history of change in this organization? (change fatigue, past failures)

2. **Build the case:**
   - Why is this change happening? (clear, honest, specific)
   - What happens if we do nothing? (cost of status quo)
   - What does success look like? (measurable outcomes)
   - What is in it for the affected employees? (WIIFM -- "what's in it for me")

3. **Identify stakeholders and resistance:**
   - Map stakeholders by impact level and influence level
   - Identify likely supporters (leverage as champions)
   - Identify likely resisters (engage early, address concerns directly)
   - Identify the fence-sitters (they follow the winners)

4. **Design the communication plan:**
   - Leaders hear first (cascade top-down, never bottom-up or sideways)
   - Communicate the "why" before the "what"
   - Repeat the message through multiple channels (town hall, email, manager toolkit,
     FAQ, Slack)
   - Create space for questions and feedback
   - Acknowledge what is being lost (not just what is being gained)

5. **Enable adoption:**
   - Training for new skills or processes
   - Manager enablement (they are the front line of change management)
   - Quick wins to build momentum
   - Support systems for those struggling with the transition

6. **Sustain the change:**
   - Track adoption metrics (not just launch metrics)
   - Celebrate milestones and early adopters
   - Address backsliding quickly
   - Adjust based on feedback

**Common misapplication:** Announcing a change and assuming adoption will follow. Another
failure: over-engineering the change management plan for small changes or under-engineering
it for major ones. Scale the effort to the change.

### Framework 11: Workforce Planning Model

**What:** A forward-looking model that aligns headcount and skills capacity with business
strategy. Answers: "What people do we need, where, when, and at what cost to execute our
strategy?"

**When to use:** Annual strategic planning. Before any major growth or contraction
decision. When entering new markets or launching new products. When technology changes
will alter skill requirements.

**How to apply:**
1. **Demand modeling:**
   - Start with business plan: revenue targets, product roadmap, customer growth
   - Translate business goals into work volume (how many deals, projects, support
     tickets, features)
   - Convert work volume into headcount by role and level using productivity ratios
   - Factor in planned attrition (use historical rates, adjust for market conditions)
   - Result: headcount demand by role, level, location, and quarter

2. **Supply analysis:**
   - Current headcount by role, level, skills, tenure, performance, location
   - Projected attrition (historical rate + retention risk assessment)
   - Projected internal movement (promotions, transfers, lateral moves)
   - Internal talent pipeline (who is ready or near-ready for advancement)
   - External labor market conditions (supply of talent, compensation trends)

3. **Gap analysis:**
   - Compare demand to supply for each role, level, and skill
   - Identify gaps: where demand exceeds supply (need to hire or develop)
   - Identify surpluses: where supply exceeds demand (potential redeployment or
     reduction)
   - Quantify the cost of closing each gap (recruiting costs, training costs,
     compensation costs)

4. **Scenario planning:**
   - Build three scenarios: base case, growth case, contraction case
   - For each scenario, model the headcount plan and associated costs
   - Identify trigger points that shift from one scenario to another
   - Build flexibility: what can be done with contingent workforce, contractors,
     or automation?

5. **Execution plan:**
   - Prioritize hiring by business impact and time-to-fill
   - Build a recruiting capacity plan (do we have enough recruiters?)
   - Identify build-vs-buy decisions for skills (train existing talent vs. hire new)
   - Create a quarterly review cadence to adjust plan as conditions change

**Common misapplication:** Building a headcount plan based on last year plus a percentage.
This is budgeting, not workforce planning. Real workforce planning starts with business
strategy and works backward to people needs.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: Hire vs. Promote vs. Contract

**Consider:**
- Skill availability internally: Is the capability present in the current team?
- Timeline: How quickly does the role need to be filled?
- Strategic importance: Is this a core capability the company needs to own long-term?
- Budget: What is the total cost comparison (fully loaded)?
- Culture impact: What signal does the decision send to existing employees?
- Market conditions: How hard is this skill to hire externally?

**Default recommendation:** Promote from within when the capability exists internally
and the timeline allows for a development curve. This sends a strong retention signal.
Hire externally when new capabilities are needed or when internal candidates are not
ready within the required timeline. Contract for time-limited needs, surge capacity, or
specialized expertise that is not needed permanently.

**Override conditions:** When the internal talent pool has been stagnant and needs fresh
perspective. When speed is critical and no internal candidate is within 6 months of
readiness. When the role requires market credibility that internal candidates lack (e.g.,
a head of sales when entering a new market).

### Decision Type: Terminate vs. PIP vs. Coach

**Consider:**
- Performance gap severity: Minor skill gap vs. fundamental capability mismatch
- Duration: Recent dip vs. sustained underperformance
- Root cause: Skills, motivation, fit, personal circumstances, manager support
- Employee history: Previously strong performer or always borderline?
- Legal risk: Protected class, recent complaints, documentation quality
- Team impact: How is the underperformance affecting others?
- Replaceability: How hard is the role to fill?

**Default recommendation:** Start with coaching for minor gaps in otherwise strong
performers. Move to a formal PIP (30-60-90 days depending on severity) when coaching
has not worked within 4-6 weeks and the gap is addressable. Terminate for egregious
misconduct (no PIP needed), sustained inability to meet basic role requirements despite
support, or when the role is fundamentally the wrong fit.

**Override conditions:** Immediate termination without PIP for policy violations
(harassment, theft, violence, fraud, confidentiality breach). Also override toward
termination when the employee's continued presence is toxic to the team and remediation
is unrealistic. Always consult employment attorney before any termination.

### Decision Type: Remote vs. Hybrid vs. In-Office

**Consider:**
- Role requirements: Does the work require physical presence?
- Team collaboration needs: How much synchronous collaboration is required?
- Talent market: Can we access better talent with remote/hybrid flexibility?
- Culture: What does our culture require for new employee integration?
- Employee preference: What do existing employees want?
- Cost implications: Office space costs, location-based compensation differentials
- Legal/tax: Multi-state or multi-country employment creates compliance complexity
- Management capability: Can managers effectively manage remote teams?

**Default recommendation:** Hybrid (2-3 days in office) for roles that benefit from
in-person collaboration. Remote-first for roles where the work is primarily individual
and asynchronous. In-office for roles requiring physical presence or intensive
collaboration in early-stage teams (under 20 people).

**Override conditions:** When the labor market makes remote-only the only competitive
option for critical roles. When office costs create unsustainable overhead for the
company's stage.

### Decision Type: Build vs. Buy HR Technology

**Consider:**
- Scale: Current headcount and projected growth over 3 years
- Complexity: Number of entities, countries, pay types, compliance requirements
- Integration needs: How many systems need to connect?
- Budget: Total cost of ownership including implementation, ongoing fees, admin time
- Time to value: How quickly does the solution need to be operational?
- Customization needs: Can an off-the-shelf solution fit our processes, or do we need
  highly customized workflows?
- Internal technical capability: Do we have engineers who can build and maintain?

**Default recommendation:** Buy (SaaS) for HRIS, ATS, payroll, and benefits
administration. These are commodity functions with mature vendors. The cost of building
and maintaining these systems internally is almost never justified. Build custom solutions
only for analytics dashboards, internal mobility platforms, or other differentiating
capabilities where no vendor solution fits.

**HRIS selection by company stage:**
- 1-50 employees: Gusto, Rippling, or Justworks (all-in-one, low admin burden)
- 50-500 employees: BambooHR, Lattice + payroll provider, Rippling (growing feature set)
- 500-2000: Workday, HiBob, or ADP Workforce Now
- 2000+: Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, Oracle HCM

**Override conditions:** When the company is in a highly regulated industry where
off-the-shelf compliance features do not cover jurisdiction-specific requirements.
When the company's operating model is so unique that standard HR processes do not apply.

### Decision Type: Counter-Offer vs. Let Go

**Consider:**
- Employee's reason for leaving: Is it solvable (compensation, role) or unsolvable
  (manager, culture, mission)?
- Performance level: Is this a top performer, solid contributor, or average performer?
- Role criticality: What is the business impact of losing this person?
- Team dynamics: Will the counter-offer create internal equity issues?
- Counter-offer success rates: Industry data shows 50-80% of counter-offered employees
  leave within 12 months anyway
- Precedent: Will a counter-offer encourage others to generate competing offers?
- Budget: Can we afford the counter-offer without creating compression?

**Default recommendation:** Do not counter-offer. The research consistently shows
counter-offers have poor long-term outcomes. The employee has already decided to leave
emotionally. Instead, conduct a genuine exit interview, learn from it, and address root
causes for the next person. The money spent on a counter-offer is usually better spent on
the replacement's sign-on bonus or the team's compensation adjustment.

**Override conditions:** When the employee is truly irreplaceable (deep institutional
knowledge, critical customer relationships, singular technical expertise) AND the reason
for leaving is fixable AND the counter-offer does not create internal equity problems.
Even then, treat it as buying time, not solving the problem.

## Quality Standards

### The HR Quality Bar

Every HR deliverable must pass three tests:

1. **The Business Impact Test** -- Does this HR initiative connect to a measurable
   business outcome? "Improve engagement" is not enough. "Improve engineering retention
   from 82% to 90%, saving $2.4M in replacement costs" passes the test.

2. **The Employee Experience Test** -- Does this process, policy, or program make
   employees' work lives better, clearer, or fairer? If it only serves HR's administrative
   needs, redesign it.

3. **The Legal Soundness Test** -- Has this been reviewed for compliance with applicable
   employment law? Would it withstand scrutiny from a plaintiff's attorney? If unsure,
   flag for legal review before implementation.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Job Description:**
- Must include: Clear title aligned to job architecture, reporting relationship, 5-8 key
  responsibilities with measurable outcomes, 4-6 required qualifications (skills and
  experience), 2-3 preferred qualifications, compensation range (required by law in many
  jurisdictions), benefits summary, equal opportunity statement
- Must avoid: Gendered language, unnecessary degree requirements, unrealistic experience
  requirements ("10 years of experience in a 5-year-old technology"), vague
  responsibilities ("other duties as assigned" as the primary content)
- Gold standard: A candidate reads it and knows exactly what success looks like in the
  first 6 months. A hiring manager reads it and can build an interview plan directly
  from the requirements.

**Compensation Analysis:**
- Must include: Market data sources (name the surveys), percentile positioning, internal
  equity comparison, total compensation view (base + variable + equity + benefits),
  cost analysis with scenarios
- Must avoid: Single-source market data, ignoring geographic differentials, presenting
  base salary only without total compensation context
- Gold standard: A CFO can look at the analysis and understand exactly what the company
  is paying, why, and what it would cost to change the approach.

**Performance Review:**
- Must include: Assessment against specific, pre-set goals with evidence. Behavioral
  competency assessment with examples. Development plan with specific actions. Clear
  overall rating with calibration context.
- Must avoid: Vague feedback ("good job"), recency bias (only citing last-month
  examples), personality assessment instead of performance assessment, surprises (the
  review should contain nothing the employee has not heard before)
- Gold standard: The employee walks away knowing exactly where they stand, why, what
  to do next, and feeling that the process was fair.

**Employee Handbook / Policy Document:**
- Must include: Clear, plain-language explanation of the policy. Who it applies to.
  Effective date. How to report violations or ask questions. Consequences of non-
  compliance. Legal disclaimers appropriate to jurisdiction.
- Must avoid: Legalese that employees cannot understand. Policies that conflict with
  each other. Policies that create obligations the company cannot enforce.
- Gold standard: An employee at any level can read it, understand it, and know exactly
  what is expected. An employment attorney can review it and find no material liability
  exposure.

**HR Analytics Report:**
- Must include: Clear metrics with definitions, trend data (minimum 3 data points for
  trends), benchmark comparisons, segmentation by relevant dimensions (team, level,
  tenure, demographics where appropriate), actionable insights, recommended actions
- Must avoid: Vanity metrics, unsegmented averages that hide important patterns,
  correlation presented as causation, data without context or recommendations
- Gold standard: Leadership reads it and makes at least one decision differently than
  they would have without the data.

**Onboarding Program:**
- Must include: Pre-boarding checklist (before day 1), Day 1 agenda, Week 1 plan,
  30/60/90 day milestones, buddy/mentor assignment, manager check-in schedule, tool
  and access provisioning checklist, culture orientation, role-specific training plan
- Must avoid: Information overload on Day 1, no structured check-ins after week 1,
  no success criteria at 90 days, treating onboarding as an HR event instead of a
  manager-owned process
- Gold standard: A new hire is fully productive by day 60, feels connected to the team
  and company by day 30, and hits their 90-day milestones on track.

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)
- [ ] Deliverable connects to a stated business outcome
- [ ] Legal compliance has been considered for applicable jurisdictions
- [ ] Employee impact has been assessed (who is affected, how, and what support is needed)
- [ ] Manager enablement is included (managers need tools and talking points, not just
  instructions)
- [ ] Metrics and success criteria are defined
- [ ] Communication plan accompanies the deliverable (not just the policy, but how it
  will be rolled out)
- [ ] Cost impact is quantified (direct costs and opportunity costs)
- [ ] Timeline is realistic with milestones
- [ ] Stakeholder alignment is documented (who has approved, who needs to be informed)
- [ ] Feedback mechanism exists (how will we know if this is working?)
- [ ] The deliverable has been reviewed for bias, inclusivity, and accessibility
- [ ] If applicable, the deliverable has been reviewed by employment counsel

## Communication Standards

### Structure

HR communications follow one of two structures depending on the audience:

**For leadership/strategy communications:** Use the Pyramid Principle.
1. Recommendation or key finding (lead with the answer)
2. Supporting arguments with data
3. Implementation details
4. Next steps

**For employee-facing communications:** Use the Empathy-First structure.
1. Acknowledge the context (what employees are experiencing or feeling)
2. State the change or information clearly and simply
3. Explain why (the honest reason, not corporate speak)
4. Explain what it means for the employee specifically
5. Provide resources and support
6. Invite questions

### Tone

- **For leadership:** Data-driven, strategic, direct. Connect every people initiative to
  business outcomes. Use financial language (ROI, cost avoidance, productivity gains).
  Leaders listen when HR speaks in business terms.

- **For managers:** Practical, supportive, tool-oriented. Managers are the front line of
  people management. Give them scripts, templates, FAQs, and playbooks. Make it easy
  for them to do the right thing.

- **For employees:** Clear, honest, human. Avoid HR jargon. Use plain language. Acknowledge
  that policies and changes affect real people's lives. Never be condescending. Assume
  intelligence but not insider knowledge.

- **For candidates:** Warm, professional, transparent. The candidate experience is a
  reflection of the employee experience. Every interaction is a data point for the
  candidate. Respond promptly. Provide feedback. Be honest about timeline, process,
  and expectations.

### Audience Adaptation

**C-Suite / Board:**
- Focus on: Strategic talent risks, workforce costs, competitive positioning, compliance
  exposure, culture as a business driver
- Format: Executive summary with data, 1-2 pages maximum, deck if presenting
- Language: Business impact, ROI, risk quantification, competitive benchmarks

**People Managers:**
- Focus on: How to have conversations, what the process is, what tools are available,
  what to do in specific situations
- Format: Manager toolkits, FAQ documents, step-by-step guides, templates
- Language: Practical, supportive, action-oriented

**All Employees:**
- Focus on: What is changing, why, how it affects them, what they need to do, where to
  go for help
- Format: Email, intranet post, town hall talking points, FAQ
- Language: Plain, clear, human, empathetic

**External (Candidates, Public):**
- Focus on: Employer brand, culture, values in action, growth opportunity
- Format: Career page content, job descriptions, social media, interview communications
- Language: Authentic, specific, differentiated (avoid generic "great place to work")

### Language Conventions

**Use these terms precisely:**
- "Attrition" = voluntary departures. "Turnover" = all departures including terminations.
- "Total compensation" = base + variable + equity + benefits value.
- "Headcount" = number of people. "FTE" = full-time equivalents (accounts for part-time).
- "Regretted attrition" = departures of employees you wanted to keep.
- "Involuntary termination" = company-initiated departure. "Reduction in force" = layoff.
- "Performance improvement plan" = formal documented improvement program with specific
  goals and timeline.
- "At-will employment" = employment relationship that either party can end at any time
  for any lawful reason.
- "Exempt" = not eligible for overtime (salaried, meets FLSA duties test).
  "Non-exempt" = eligible for overtime.

**Avoid:**
- "Human capital" (dehumanizing in employee-facing communications)
- "Resources" when referring to people (same issue)
- "Best practice" without specifying what practice and what evidence supports it
- "Culture fit" (use "culture add" to focus on what someone brings, not whether they
  match existing patterns)
- "Soft skills" (use "professional skills" or name the specific skill)

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Legal Compliance Review

**What it tests:** Whether the HR deliverable complies with applicable employment law
and does not create unnecessary legal exposure.

**How to apply:**
1. Identify all applicable jurisdictions (federal, state, local, international)
2. Review the deliverable against key employment law areas:
   - Wage and hour (FLSA, state wage laws): Proper classification? Overtime compliance?
   - Anti-discrimination (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, state laws): Neutral criteria? Disparate
     impact risk? Accommodation considered?
   - Leave (FMLA, state and local leave laws): Proper notice? Correct eligibility
     criteria?
   - Privacy: Employee data handling? Consent requirements? State-specific privacy laws?
   - At-will: Does the deliverable inadvertently create contractual obligations?
3. Flag any areas of ambiguity for employment attorney review
4. Document the compliance review and date

**Pass criteria:** No identified violations of applicable employment law. All ambiguous
areas flagged for legal review. Required disclaimers included. Documentation supports
defensibility.

### Method 2: Equity Impact Analysis

**What it tests:** Whether the HR deliverable or decision creates or perpetuates
inequitable outcomes across demographic groups.

**How to apply:**
1. Define the affected population and relevant demographic dimensions
2. Analyze the impact on each group:
   - Does this policy or process disproportionately affect any group?
   - Will this change improve or worsen equity gaps?
   - Are there unintended consequences for specific populations?
3. Review historical data where available (promotion rates, pay equity, attrition by group)
4. Identify mitigation strategies for any disproportionate impact
5. Document findings and adjustments

**Pass criteria:** No disproportionate negative impact on any protected group. If
disparate impact is identified, there is a documented business necessity and less
discriminatory alternatives have been considered. Plan includes monitoring for equity
impact after implementation.

### Method 3: Manager Usability Test

**What it tests:** Whether managers can actually implement the HR process, policy, or
tool effectively. Managers are the execution layer of HR. If they cannot use it, it
will not work.

**How to apply:**
1. Select 3-5 managers across levels and functions
2. Present the deliverable and ask them to walk through implementation in their team
3. Observe: Where do they get confused? Where do they push back? Where do they need
   more guidance?
4. Ask: "If you had to do this Monday, what would you need?"
5. Revise based on feedback before full rollout

**Pass criteria:** At least 4 of 5 managers can execute the process without HR
hand-holding. No manager identifies a scenario the process does not address. Every
manager can explain the "why" to their team.

### Method 4: Employee Experience Walkthrough

**What it tests:** Whether the process or policy works from the employee's perspective.
Many HR processes are designed for HR's convenience, creating friction for the people
they are supposed to serve.

**How to apply:**
1. Map the employee journey through the process (every step, every touchpoint, every
   decision point)
2. Identify friction points: Where does the employee have to wait? Where are they confused?
   Where do they need information they do not have?
3. Measure: How long does the process take from the employee's perspective? How many
   interactions? How many systems?
4. Compare to best-in-class consumer experiences (employees now expect consumer-grade
   experiences at work)
5. Simplify or redesign to reduce friction

**Pass criteria:** Employee can complete the process in minimum steps with clear guidance
at each point. Process takes less than 50% of the time of the previous version (if
replacing an existing process). No step requires the employee to ask HR "what do I do
next?"

### Method 5: Business Impact Projection

**What it tests:** Whether the HR initiative will generate the claimed business value
and whether the investment is justified.

**How to apply:**
1. Define the specific business outcome (retention improvement, time-to-hire reduction,
   engagement score increase, compliance risk reduction)
2. Quantify the baseline (current state with data)
3. Project the improvement with conservative, moderate, and aggressive estimates
4. Calculate the investment required (direct costs, opportunity costs, time costs)
5. Calculate ROI or cost avoidance for each scenario
6. Identify leading indicators that will confirm progress before the lagging outcome is
   measurable

**Pass criteria:** Positive ROI in the moderate scenario. Leading indicators are defined
and measurable within 90 days of launch. Investment is proportional to expected return
(do not spend $500K to save $100K).

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Culture Fit Over Culture Add**
   What it looks like: Hiring people who "feel right" because they share the same
   background, communication style, or interests as the existing team. Using "culture fit"
   as a veto without being able to articulate what specifically does not fit.
   Why it's harmful: Creates homogeneous teams that reinforce blind spots. Systematically
   excludes people who think differently. Reduces innovation. Creates legal risk
   (discrimination claims).
   Instead: Define your culture as specific behaviors and values (not personality types).
   Hire for "culture add" by asking what new perspective, skill, or experience this
   person brings that the team currently lacks.

2. **Unstructured Interviews**
   What it looks like: Interviewers asking whatever questions come to mind. No rubric. No
   competency mapping. Hiring decisions based on "I liked them" or "great conversation."
   Why it's harmful: Unstructured interviews have near-zero predictive validity for job
   performance. They primarily measure likability and social fluency. They also maximize
   bias (affinity bias, halo effect, first impression bias).
   Instead: Use the Structured Interview Framework. Every interview has assigned
   competencies, standardized questions, scoring rubrics, and independent scoring before
   debrief. Train interviewers annually.

3. **Paying Below Market and Expecting Loyalty**
   What it looks like: Offering 20-30% below market with vague promises of "growth
   opportunity" or "mission." Expressing surprise when top performers leave for
   market-rate offers.
   Why it's harmful: Attracts candidates who cannot get market-rate offers elsewhere.
   Loses top performers who can. Creates a team of people who stay because they have no
   better option. Increases recruiting costs through constant backfilling.
   Instead: Define a clear compensation philosophy. If you pay below market, offset with
   specific, quantifiable value (equity, flexibility, accelerated growth). Be transparent
   about your positioning and why.

4. **Stack Ranking**
   What it looks like: Forcing a distribution curve on performance ratings regardless of
   actual performance. Requiring that a fixed percentage of every team be rated as
   underperformers.
   Why it's harmful: Punishes high-performing teams. Creates zero-sum competition between
   teammates. Incentivizes sabotage over collaboration. Demoralizes solid performers who
   are rated poorly because someone has to be.
   Instead: Use calibration (not forced distribution) to ensure rating consistency.
   Allow for the possibility that an entire team performs well. Rate against role
   expectations and goals. Differentiate through merit increase budgets and bonus pools.

5. **Annual-Only Feedback**
   What it looks like: Saving all feedback for the annual review. Employees discover
   performance issues 6-11 months after they occurred.
   Why it's harmful: Eliminates the opportunity to course-correct. Creates review anxiety.
   Makes the review feel arbitrary ("why am I hearing about this now?"). Reduces trust.
   Violates the "no surprises" principle of good management.
   Instead: Implement continuous feedback as the norm. Managers should give specific
   feedback within 48 hours of the event. Monthly 1:1 meetings should include performance
   discussion. The annual review should be a summary of conversations already had.

6. **Ignoring Attrition Signals**
   What it looks like: Being surprised when employees leave. No stay interviews. No
   retention risk assessment. No tracking of leading indicators (engagement scores,
   skip-level feedback, compensation positioning, tenure at level).
   Why it's harmful: Reactive retention is expensive and often too late. By the time
   someone has an offer, they have emotionally committed to leaving. Prevention is 10x
   cheaper than cure.
   Instead: Use the Retention Risk Assessment Framework quarterly. Conduct stay interviews
   with high performers annually. Track leading indicators. Act on early warning signs
   before they become resignations.

7. **HR as Compliance-Only Function**
   What it looks like: HR exists solely to process paperwork, manage benefits enrollment,
   and protect the company from lawsuits. No strategic voice. No seat at the leadership
   table. No involvement in business decisions.
   Why it's harmful: Misses the leverage that good people strategy creates. Compliance is
   table stakes. Strategic HR drives business outcomes through talent quality, culture,
   and organizational effectiveness.
   Instead: Build HR's credibility through business impact. Speak in business terms.
   Connect every initiative to a measurable outcome. Earn the seat at the table by
   delivering results, not by demanding it.

8. **No Career Development Paths**
   What it looks like: Employees have no visibility into what advancement looks like.
   Promotion criteria are opaque. Career conversations happen only when someone threatens
   to leave.
   Why it's harmful: High performers leave for companies that offer clarity and growth.
   Employees feel stuck. Manager-dependent promotion creates inequity. Internal mobility
   drops, increasing external hiring costs.
   Instead: Build a job architecture with clear level definitions and promotion criteria.
   Publish it. Train managers on career conversations. Create both vertical (promotion)
   and lateral (new skills, new function) paths. Review career progression in every
   performance review.

9. **Copy-Paste Policies from Other Companies**
   What it looks like: Taking Google's performance management system and implementing it
   at a 50-person startup. Copying Netflix's culture deck and expecting it to apply.
   Why it's harmful: Policies designed for one context rarely work in another. Company
   stage, industry, culture, and workforce composition all affect what works. Borrowed
   policies often conflict with existing norms and create confusion.
   Instead: Learn from other companies' principles. Design your own processes for your
   own context. Test and iterate. What works at 5,000 people does not work at 50.

10. **Treating Every Employee Equally Instead of Equitably**
    What it looks like: Applying the same policies, same development opportunities, and
    same management approach to every person regardless of role, performance, or potential.
    Why it's harmful: Top performers need different development than struggling performers.
    Critical roles need different retention strategies than commodity roles. One-size-fits-
    all dilutes investment across the entire workforce instead of concentrating it where
    the impact is highest.
    Instead: Differentiate investment based on performance and role criticality. Give top
    performers stretch assignments, executive exposure, and accelerated development.
    Give struggling performers clear expectations and coaching. Invest retention resources
    where loss would hurt most.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No legal advice.** This domain provides frameworks, general compliance guidance, and
   risk identification. It does not provide legal advice. Employment law is jurisdiction-
   specific, complex, and constantly changing. Always recommend consulting a qualified
   employment attorney for specific legal questions, termination decisions, accommodation
   requests, and any situation with potential litigation exposure.

2. **No discrimination.** Every framework and recommendation must be applied consistently
   across all employees regardless of race, gender, age, religion, disability, sexual
   orientation, national origin, or any other protected characteristic. Any recommended
   process must be evaluated for disparate impact.

3. **No retaliation guidance.** Never provide guidance that could be used to retaliate
   against an employee for filing a complaint, reporting harassment, requesting
   accommodation, taking protected leave, or engaging in other legally protected activity.

4. **No privacy violations.** Employee data is confidential. Recommendations must respect
   privacy laws (HIPAA for health information, state privacy laws, GDPR for EU employees).
   Never recommend sharing employee health, performance, or personal information beyond
   those with a legitimate business need to know.

5. **No shortcuts on safety.** Never recommend skipping safety-related processes
   (harassment investigations, workplace violence assessments, accommodation evaluations)
   to save time or avoid discomfort.

6. **Honest assessment of uncertainty.** When the answer depends on jurisdiction-specific
   law, say so. When best practices are debated, present the debate. When a recommendation
   carries risk, quantify and disclose the risk.

7. **No guarantees of legal outcomes.** Employment disputes are fact-specific and
   jurisdiction-dependent. Never guarantee that a process or policy will prevent lawsuits
   or win legal challenges.

### Required Disclaimers

- **Employment law matters:** "This guidance is for informational and strategic planning
  purposes. Employment law varies by jurisdiction and changes frequently. Consult a
  qualified employment attorney before making termination decisions, responding to
  complaints, implementing policies that affect employee rights, or taking any action
  with potential legal implications."

- **Compensation recommendations:** "Compensation data and benchmarks are illustrative.
  Actual market rates vary by geography, industry, company size, and specific role
  requirements. Validate with current, role-specific market data from reputable
  compensation surveys before making pay decisions."

- **Benefits and tax:** "Benefits program design and tax treatment of compensation vary
  by jurisdiction and are subject to regulatory requirements. Consult a benefits attorney,
  benefits broker, or tax advisor before implementing benefits programs or compensation
  structures."

## Practical Templates and Metrics Reference

### Key HR Metrics Dashboard

**Recruiting Metrics:**
| Metric | Calculation | Healthy Range | Action Trigger |
|--------|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| Time to hire | Days from job opening to offer acceptance | 30-45 days | >60 days |
| Cost per hire | (Internal costs + external costs) / total hires | $3,000-$5,000 | >$8,000 |
| Offer acceptance rate | Accepted offers / total offers | 85-95% | <80% |
| Quality of hire | 90-day performance rating average | Meets+ expectations | >15% below expectations |
| Source effectiveness | Hires / applications by source | Varies by source | Track quarterly |
| Recruiter efficiency | Hires / recruiter / month | 3-5 (professional) | <2 |

**Retention Metrics:**
| Metric | Calculation | Healthy Range | Action Trigger |
|--------|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| Overall attrition | Departures / average headcount (annualized) | 10-15% | >20% |
| Regretted attrition | Regretted departures / average headcount | <8% | >10% |
| First-year attrition | Departures <1 year tenure / new hires | <15% | >20% |
| Manager attrition | Manager departures / total managers | <10% | >15% |

### Stay Interview Question Guide

Ask these questions to high performers and critical role holders at least annually:

1. What do you look forward to each day when you come to work?
2. What are you learning here? What do you want to learn?
3. Why do you stay at this company?
4. When was the last time you thought about leaving? What prompted it?
5. What would make your job better?
6. What talent do you have that we are not using?
7. What about your job would you change if you could?
8. What would tempt you to leave? (A competitor? A different role? More money? A different
   manager? A different location?)
9. Is there something you need from me that you are not getting?
10. What can I do to make your experience here better?

**How to use the responses:** Code themes across stay interviews. Identify patterns.
Act on the top 3 themes within 30 days. Report back to participants on what changed
because of their input. This closes the feedback loop and builds trust that the process
matters.

## Remote and Hybrid Work Policy Framework

### Policy Components

**Work arrangement definitions:**
- **Fully remote:** Employee works from a location of their choice. No regular in-office
  expectation. May be required to attend in-person events 2-4 times per year.
- **Hybrid:** Employee works some days in office, some days remote. Specify the model:
  - Fixed hybrid: Specific days in office (e.g., Tuesday and Thursday)
  - Flexible hybrid: Minimum days in office per week/month, employee chooses which days
  - Team-based hybrid: Team decides together which days to be in office
- **In-office:** Employee works from the office full-time. Occasional remote days may be
  approved by manager.

**Key policy decisions to make:**
1. Which roles are eligible for which arrangements? (Based on role requirements,
   not employee preference alone)
2. Location-based compensation: Pay the same regardless of location, or adjust for
   local cost of labor? (Arguments exist for both. Be explicit about the approach.)
3. Home office stipend: What equipment and setup costs does the company cover?
   (Common range: $500-$2,000 one-time setup + $50-$100/month ongoing)
4. Communication norms: Core hours for meetings, expected response times, async-first
   or sync-first culture
5. In-person requirements: How many days per quarter or per year for remote employees?
   Who pays for travel?
6. Tax and compliance: Multi-state employment creates withholding obligations, benefits
   requirements, and employment law compliance in each state where employees reside.
   Consult tax advisor and employment attorney.

### Remote Onboarding Adjustments

For remote employees, standard onboarding needs these additions:
- Ship equipment at least 3 days before start date with setup instructions
- Schedule a dedicated IT support session on Day 1 for technical setup
- Assign a remote-experienced buddy (not just any buddy)
- Increase manager check-in frequency in the first month (daily for week 1, three times
  per week for weeks 2-4, then weekly)
- Create intentional social opportunities (virtual coffee chats, informal team calls)
- Fly the employee to the office within the first 30 days if possible for in-person
  relationship building

## Labor Relations Essentials

### Union Avoidance (Where Legal and Appropriate)

The best union avoidance strategy is simple: be a good employer. Employees organize when
they feel they have no other way to address their concerns. Companies that pay fairly,
communicate openly, resolve problems quickly, and treat people with respect rarely face
organizing efforts.

**Key principles:**
- Pay at or above market for the roles most likely to be organizing targets
- Ensure fair and consistent application of policies (inconsistency breeds resentment)
- Train managers to listen, respond, and escalate employee concerns promptly
- Create internal feedback channels that actually lead to change
- Address working conditions issues proactively (safety, scheduling, workload)

**What NOT to do during an organizing campaign (TIPS rule):**
- **T**hreaten: No threats of job loss, plant closure, or benefit reduction
- **I**nterrogate: No questioning employees about union sympathies or activities
- **P**romise: No promises of benefits or improvements to dissuade organizing
- **S**urveil: No monitoring of union-related activities

These are unfair labor practices under the NLRA. Violations can result in the NLRB
ordering recognition of the union without an election. Consult a labor attorney
immediately if organizing activity begins.

### If Your Workforce Is Unionized

- Bargain in good faith (legal obligation)
- Train managers on the collective bargaining agreement (CBA) and grievance procedures
- Maintain clear documentation of all disciplinary actions
- Build a professional working relationship with union representatives
- Consult labor attorney before any action that could be interpreted as changing terms
  and conditions of employment

## Offboarding Best Practices

### Voluntary Departure Process

1. **Resignation received:** Manager confirms with HR. Two-week notice is standard in the
   US (varies internationally). Assess whether to have the employee work the notice period
   or depart immediately (role-dependent).
2. **Knowledge transfer:** Identify critical knowledge and projects. Create a transition
   plan within 48 hours of notice. Assign recipients for each knowledge area.
3. **Exit interview:** Conduct within the last 3 days. HR conducts (not the direct
   manager). Use structured questions. Focus on understanding the real reason for leaving,
   what would have made them stay, and feedback on the employee experience.
4. **Systems and access:** Deactivate all accounts on the last day. Follow IT security
   protocol. Recover company equipment.
5. **Final pay:** Process in compliance with state law. Some states require final pay on
   the last day. Others allow the next regular pay cycle. Know your state's requirements.
6. **Benefits:** Provide COBRA information (or state equivalent). Explain benefits
   continuation timeline.
7. **Alumni relationship:** Thank them genuinely. Leave the door open for return (boomerang
   hires are often excellent). Add to alumni network if one exists.

### Involuntary Departure Process

1. **Pre-termination:** Documentation complete (PIP, prior feedback, policy violations).
   Legal review complete. Separation agreement prepared if applicable. Manager briefed
   on conversation script.
2. **The conversation:** Private setting. Manager and HR present. Direct, compassionate,
   brief (under 10 minutes). State the decision, explain the reason, outline next steps.
   Do not debate or negotiate. Treat the person with dignity.
3. **Same-day actions:** Systems access deactivated. Equipment collected. Personal items
   gathered (or shipped). Remaining team informed with appropriate messaging (what to say,
   what not to say).
4. **Severance and legal:** Offer severance in exchange for release of claims (if
   applicable). Provide COBRA information. Comply with WARN Act for large layoffs (60-day
   notice for 100+ employees).
5. **Team communication:** Manager communicates departure to the team. Acknowledge the
   person's contributions. Explain transition plan. Do not share reasons for termination
   beyond what is necessary and legally appropriate.

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge): HR-Specific Guidance

**Questions to ask:**
- What people problem are you trying to solve? (Force specificity. "We need to improve
  culture" is not a problem statement. "We are losing 30% of engineers in their first
  year and exit interviews cite unclear career paths" is a problem statement.)
- What is the business impact of this people issue? (Quantify: cost of attrition, lost
  productivity, compliance risk, competitive disadvantage)
- What have you already tried? What worked and what did not?
- What constraints exist? (Budget, timeline, political, legal, contractual)
- Who are the stakeholders? (Leadership, managers, employees, legal, finance, union)
- What does success look like in 90 days? 6 months? 12 months?

**Patterns to look for:**
- Is this a symptom or a root cause? (High attrition might be a compensation problem,
  a management problem, a role clarity problem, or an onboarding problem.)
- Is this an HR process problem or a leadership/management problem? (Many "HR problems"
  are actually management problems that HR cannot solve with a new process.)
- Is the company at a stage transition? (10 to 50 employees, 50 to 200, 200 to 500. Each
  transition breaks existing people processes.)
- Are there legal or compliance dimensions that need to be addressed first?

### Stage 2 (Design Approach): HR-Specific Guidance

**Framework selection guide:**
- "We can't hire fast enough" -> Hiring Funnel Metrics + sourcing strategy redesign +
  employer branding assessment
- "We're losing too many people" -> Retention Risk Assessment + stay interviews +
  compensation benchmarking + engagement survey
- "Our performance management is broken" -> Performance Review Calibration + goal-setting
  framework redesign + manager training
- "We need to build an HR function from scratch" -> Job Architecture + Compensation
  Philosophy + core policies + HRIS selection
- "We're doing a reorg" -> Organizational Health Assessment + Change Management + role
  architecture + workforce planning
- "We need a DEI strategy" -> DEI Strategy Framework + current state assessment +
  process audit + goal setting
- "We need to go remote/hybrid" -> Remote work policy framework + compensation
  philosophy review + compliance assessment + manager enablement

**Non-obvious moves:**
- Before designing an engagement program, check if the basics are broken first
  (fair pay, clear roles, competent managers). No engagement program overcomes
  fundamentally broken foundations.
- Before building a new process, map the employee journey to understand the current
  experience. Many HR initiatives fail because they add complexity to an already
  overwhelming experience.
- Before hiring more people, validate that the work cannot be done with better processes,
  automation, or role redesign. Headcount is the most expensive solution to most problems.

### Stage 3 (Structure Engagement): HR-Specific Guidance

**Typical engagement structure:**
- **Assessment phase** (Stages 1-2): 25% of effort. Understand the current state through
  data, interviews, and observation. Diagnose root causes.
- **Design phase** (Stage 4): 40% of effort. Build the solution (policy, program, process,
  system).
- **Validation phase** (Stages 5-6): 20% of effort. Legal review, manager usability test,
  equity impact analysis.
- **Implementation phase** (Stages 7-8): 15% of effort. Communication plan, training,
  rollout, measurement.

**Common deliverable types:**
- HR policy document or employee handbook section
- Compensation analysis and salary band recommendation
- Performance management system design
- Recruiting process redesign with metrics framework
- Organizational design with role architecture and career ladders
- DEI strategy with goals, initiatives, and accountability
- Workforce plan with headcount model and hiring roadmap
- HR technology evaluation and recommendation
- Manager training curriculum and materials
- Change management plan for organizational transition
- People analytics dashboard design

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables): HR-Specific Guidance

**HR document design principles:**
- Lead with the business case (why this matters to the company, not just to HR)
- Include the employee experience perspective (how this affects real people)
- Provide manager enablement materials alongside every employee-facing program (toolkit,
  talking points, FAQ, scripts for difficult conversations)
- Quantify impact: cost, time savings, risk reduction, performance improvement
- Include implementation timeline with milestones and owners
- Build in measurement from the start (what metrics will prove this is working?)

**Policy writing standards:**
- Plain language. If an employee needs a lawyer to understand a policy, rewrite it.
- Clear scope (who does this apply to?)
- Clear expectations (what should employees do?)
- Clear consequences (what happens if the policy is violated?)
- Clear resources (where to go for questions or help)
- Review by employment counsel before publication

### Stage 5 (Quality Assurance): HR-Specific Review Criteria

In addition to the universal review checklist:
- [ ] Business impact is quantified with stated assumptions
- [ ] Legal compliance has been evaluated for all applicable jurisdictions
- [ ] Employee experience has been mapped from the employee's perspective
- [ ] Manager enablement materials are complete (toolkit, talking points, FAQ)
- [ ] DEI impact has been assessed
- [ ] Communication plan is ready
- [ ] Metrics and measurement plan are defined
- [ ] Stakeholder alignment is documented
- [ ] The deliverable follows writing style rules (no semicolons, no em dashes, no
  "not X, but Y" constructions)
- [ ] Disclaimer language is included where required

### Stage 6 (Validate): HR-Specific Validation

Apply these validation methods:
1. **Legal Compliance Review** -- for all policies, processes, and employee-affecting
   decisions
2. **Equity Impact Analysis** -- for compensation changes, promotion processes, policy
   changes
3. **Manager Usability Test** -- for any process that managers need to execute
4. **Employee Experience Walkthrough** -- for any employee-facing process or program
5. **Business Impact Projection** -- for initiatives requiring significant investment

Minimum for Tier 2: Methods 1 + 3
Full suite for Tier 3: All five methods

### Stage 7 (Plan Delivery): HR-Specific Delivery

**Delivery format guidance:**
- C-Suite / Board: Executive summary (1-2 pages) with business impact, cost, and risk.
  Detailed analysis in appendix.
- People Managers: Manager toolkit with step-by-step guides, scripts, FAQs, templates.
  Training session if the change is significant.
- All Employees: Clear, empathetic communication (email + town hall + FAQ + intranet
  page). Multiple channels. Multiple repetitions. Feedback mechanism.
- Legal / Compliance: Policy document with full legal review trail. Compliance checklist
  for each jurisdiction.

**Always include:**
- Executive summary that stands alone
- Manager enablement package
- Employee FAQ
- Measurement plan with leading and lagging indicators
- Feedback mechanism (how employees and managers can flag issues during rollout)
- Escalation path (who decides when adjustments are needed)

### Stage 8 (Deliver): HR-Specific Follow-up

**After delivery:**
- Monitor adoption metrics weekly for the first 60 days
- Conduct pulse check with managers at 2 weeks and 6 weeks
- Address issues and iterate based on feedback
- Report progress to leadership at 30, 60, and 90 days
- Schedule formal program review at 6 months
- Document lessons learned for future HR initiatives
- Update the domain file (this file) with any new frameworks or insights gained

**Iteration cadence:**
- Compensation: Annual review cycle. Mid-year market check for hot roles.
- Performance management: Quarterly pulse. Annual full cycle. Continuous improvement.
- Recruiting: Monthly metrics review. Quarterly process optimization.
- Engagement: Annual full survey. Quarterly pulse. Monthly action tracking.
- Policies: Annual review. Ad-hoc updates for legal changes.
- DEI: Quarterly metrics. Semi-annual strategy review. Annual full assessment.

## Core Frameworks

> These frameworks represent the foundational tools of evidence-based education
> practice. Each one has decades of research behind it. The skill is knowing
> which framework to apply to which problem and how to combine them effectively.

### Framework 1: Bloom's Revised Taxonomy

**What:** A hierarchical classification of cognitive processes that learners use
when engaging with content. Six levels from lowest to highest: Remember, Understand,
Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create. Originally published by Benjamin Bloom in 1956
and revised by Anderson and Krathwohl in 2001 to swap the top two levels and use
verb forms.

**When to use:** Writing learning objectives. Designing assessments. Sequencing
instruction. Evaluating whether a course reaches appropriate cognitive depth.
Communicating with stakeholders about what students will be able to do.

**How to apply:**
1. Identify the target cognitive level for each learning objective. Most courses
   should have objectives across multiple levels, weighted toward the levels that
   matter for real-world application.
2. Write objectives using verbs that match the intended level:
   - **Remember:** list, define, identify, recall, name, state, match
   - **Understand:** explain, summarize, paraphrase, classify, compare, interpret
   - **Apply:** use, solve, demonstrate, implement, calculate, execute
   - **Analyze:** differentiate, organize, attribute, deconstruct, examine, contrast
   - **Evaluate:** judge, critique, justify, defend, assess, argue, prioritize
   - **Create:** design, construct, produce, develop, formulate, compose, generate
3. Align assessments to the same cognitive level as the objectives. An objective
   at the "Analyze" level requires an assessment that demands analysis. Multiple
   choice questions rarely reach above "Understand."
4. Design instruction that scaffolds from lower to higher levels within each unit.
   Students need a foundation of remembering and understanding before they can
   apply. They need application experience before they can analyze effectively.
5. Use the Knowledge Dimension (factual, conceptual, procedural, metacognitive)
   as a second axis. A complete taxonomy table crosses cognitive process levels
   with knowledge types.

**Common misapplication:** Treating it as a strict linear progression where you
must complete all of "Remember" before any "Understand." In practice, levels
overlap and interact. A student can create something novel with partial understanding
if the creative task itself builds understanding. The taxonomy is a planning tool
for ensuring appropriate cognitive depth. It is a compass for curriculum design.
It is not a rigid sequential mandate.

**Research base:** Anderson & Krathwohl (2001). Hattie (2009) found that teaching
strategies targeting higher-order thinking produce effect sizes of 0.62 when
properly scaffolded.

### Framework 2: Backward Design (Understanding by Design)

**What:** A curriculum design framework developed by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe.
You start with the end (what students should understand and be able to do), then
design assessments that would prove they got there, then plan the learning
experiences that prepare them for those assessments. Three stages: (1) Identify
desired results, (2) Determine acceptable evidence, (3) Plan learning experiences.

**When to use:** Designing any course, unit, or module. Especially valuable when
you need tight alignment between what you teach, what you assess, and what students
actually need to know. The default design framework for any curriculum project.

**How to apply:**
1. **Stage 1: Identify Desired Results**
   - Establish long-term transfer goals: What should students be able to do with
     this knowledge in the real world, months or years later?
   - Identify essential understandings: What are the big ideas and enduring
     understandings? Frame these as statements students should be able to explain.
   - Write essential questions: Open-ended questions that guide inquiry and have
     no single right answer. These drive the intellectual work of the unit.
   - Specify knowledge and skills: What facts, concepts, and procedures do
     students need? These are the building blocks for understanding.
   - Align to standards where applicable.
2. **Stage 2: Determine Acceptable Evidence**
   - Design performance tasks that require students to demonstrate transfer of
     understanding to new situations. These are your summative assessments.
   - Define criteria for success using rubrics with clear performance levels.
   - Plan additional evidence: quizzes, tests, observations, homework, self-
     assessments that check for prerequisite knowledge and skills.
   - Use the GRASPS framework for performance tasks: Goal, Role, Audience,
     Situation, Product, Standards.
3. **Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences**
   - Now (and only now) plan the actual instruction.
   - Use the WHERETO framework: Where are we going and why? Hook the student.
     Explore, experience, enable, equip. Rethink, revise, reflect. Evaluate
     progress. Tailor to individual needs. Organize for maximum engagement.
   - Sequence instruction to build toward the performance tasks.
   - Include formative assessments throughout to check progress.

**Common misapplication:** Doing Stage 1 and then jumping straight to planning
activities without designing assessments first (Stage 2). This leads to the
classic problem: engaging activities that don't actually build toward measurable
outcomes. The assessment design step is what makes backward design powerful.
Skipping it reverts to traditional "coverage" planning.

**Research base:** Wiggins & McTighe (2005). Studies on aligned instruction show
significant improvements in student achievement when assessments match objectives
and instruction matches assessments. Hattie's meta-analysis ranks "teacher clarity"
(which backward design produces) at d=0.75.

### Framework 3: Cognitive Load Management

**What:** Based on John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988), this framework
manages the demands placed on working memory during learning. Three types of
cognitive load: intrinsic (inherent difficulty of the material), extraneous
(caused by poor instructional design), and germane (productive mental effort
that builds schemas). The goal is to minimize extraneous load, manage intrinsic
load through sequencing and scaffolding, and maximize germane load.

**When to use:** Designing any instructional material. Evaluating why students
are struggling. Redesigning content that produces confusion. Building multimedia
presentations. Structuring practice activities.

**How to apply:**
1. **Analyze intrinsic load.** How many interacting elements does the learner
   need to hold in working memory simultaneously? High element interactivity
   means high intrinsic load. You cannot reduce intrinsic load without
   simplifying the content, but you can manage it through sequencing.
   - Teach component skills in isolation first, then combine them.
   - Use part-whole sequencing: master the parts, then practice the whole.
   - Provide worked examples early, then fade scaffolding as expertise grows
     (the expertise reversal effect means scaffolding that helps beginners
     can actually hurt experts).
2. **Eliminate extraneous load.** Remove anything that demands mental effort
   without contributing to learning.
   - Apply the spatial contiguity principle: place text near the relevant
     graphic, not in a separate caption or page.
   - Apply the temporal contiguity principle: present narration and animation
     simultaneously, not sequentially.
   - Apply the coherence principle: cut interesting-but-irrelevant content.
     Seductive details hurt learning.
   - Apply the signaling principle: use headings, highlighting, and spatial
     organization to direct attention.
   - Apply the redundancy principle: do not present the same information in
     multiple redundant formats simultaneously (e.g., reading text aloud
     verbatim from slides).
3. **Optimize germane load.** Channel freed cognitive capacity toward schema
   construction.
   - Use variability in practice examples to promote transfer.
   - Encourage self-explanation during worked examples.
   - Prompt elaboration and connection to prior knowledge.
   - Space practice over time rather than massing it.

**Common misapplication:** Assuming all difficulty is bad. Desirable difficulties
(Bjork & Bjork, 2011) are forms of germane load that slow initial learning but
improve long-term retention and transfer. Spacing, interleaving, and retrieval
practice all feel harder but produce better learning. The framework is about
reducing unproductive load, not all load.

**Research base:** Sweller (1988, 2011). Mayer (2009) Multimedia Learning
principles. Clark, Nguyen, & Sweller (2006) Efficiency in Learning. Effect
sizes for reducing extraneous load range from d=0.4 to d=1.2 depending on
the specific principle and population.

### Framework 4: Formative Assessment Cycle

**What:** A continuous feedback loop where teachers gather evidence of student
understanding during instruction and use that evidence to adjust teaching in
real time. Five key strategies identified by Dylan Wiliam (2011): clarify
learning intentions, elicit evidence of learning, provide actionable feedback,
activate students as instructional resources for each other, and activate
students as owners of their own learning.

**When to use:** Every lesson. Every training session. Every learning interaction.
Formative assessment is the single highest-leverage teaching practice identified
by research. Hattie ranks "formative evaluation" at d=0.90.

**How to apply:**
1. **Clarify intentions and criteria.** Students cannot assess their own
   progress without knowing where they are going. Share learning objectives
   in student-friendly language. Show examples of strong and weak work.
   Co-construct success criteria when appropriate.
2. **Elicit evidence.** Use techniques that show you what ALL students
   understand, not just the volunteers who raise their hands.
   - Exit tickets: short written responses at the end of class
   - Think-pair-share: individual thinking, partner discussion, whole class share
   - Whiteboards or response cards: all students show answers simultaneously
   - Hinge questions: multiple-choice questions where each wrong answer reveals
     a specific misconception
   - Diagnostic items: questions designed to surface common errors
3. **Provide actionable feedback.** Feedback that improves learning is specific,
   timely, and tells the student what to do next. "Good job" is not feedback.
   "Your thesis statement makes a clear claim but needs supporting evidence.
   Add two specific examples from the text" is feedback.
   - Focus on the task, not the person (Kluger & DeNisi, 1996)
   - Give feedback at the level of the learning goal, not just surface features
   - Limit feedback to 2-3 actionable points. More than that overwhelms.
   - Use "comment-only" marking (Butler, 1988). Grades alongside comments
     neutralize the learning benefit of the comments.
4. **Activate peer assessment.** Train students to give each other quality
   feedback using rubrics and structured protocols. Peer assessment develops
   evaluative thinking and metacognition. It also multiplies the feedback
   capacity of the classroom.
5. **Activate self-assessment.** Teach students to monitor their own
   understanding. Provide self-assessment checklists. Train metacognitive
   skills: "What do I understand? Where am I confused? What should I do
   about it?"

**Common misapplication:** Using formative assessment as graded mini-tests.
The purpose is information for instructional decisions, not evaluation for
grades. Once you grade formative work, students shift from learning mode to
performance mode. They start guessing what you want to hear instead of
revealing what they actually think. Keep formative assessment low-stakes.

**Research base:** Black & Wiliam (1998) "Inside the Black Box" found effect
sizes of d=0.4 to d=0.7. Wiliam (2011) "Embedded Formative Assessment."
Hattie (2009) ranks formative evaluation at d=0.90. Shute (2008) meta-analysis
on formative feedback.

### Framework 5: Spaced Retrieval Practice

**What:** Two evidence-based learning strategies combined. Retrieval practice
means actively pulling information from memory rather than passively re-reading
or re-watching. Spacing means distributing practice over time rather than massing
it in a single session. Together, they produce the most robust long-term retention
of any known study strategy.

**When to use:** Any time you need students to remember information long-term.
Vocabulary, facts, procedures, concepts, problem-solving approaches. Works
across ages, content areas, and learning contexts.

**How to apply:**
1. **Build retrieval into every lesson.** Start class with a brief quiz on
   previous material. No-stakes or low-stakes. The act of retrieving is the
   learning mechanism. Whether students get the answer right or wrong matters
   less than the act of trying to retrieve.
2. **Design spacing schedules.** After initial learning, practice retrieval at
   expanding intervals: 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days. Each
   successful retrieval extends the interval. Each failure resets to a
   shorter interval. This is the algorithm behind Anki and similar spaced
   repetition systems.
3. **Use varied retrieval formats.** Flashcards work. Free recall works
   better (writing everything you remember about a topic from memory).
   Practice tests work. Concept maps from memory work. The format matters
   less than the retrieval demand.
4. **Interleave topics during retrieval.** Mix questions from different topics
   in the same practice session. This feels harder (and students often dislike
   it) but produces substantially better discrimination and transfer. Blocked
   practice (all Topic A, then all Topic B) creates an illusion of mastery that
   collapses on delayed tests.
5. **Combine with elaboration.** After retrieving, explain why the answer is
   correct. Connect it to other concepts. Generate examples. This deepens the
   memory trace and builds more connections for future retrieval.

**Common misapplication:** Confusing retrieval practice with testing. Retrieval
practice is a learning strategy, not an assessment strategy. The quiz is not
for grades. It is for memory strengthening. Also: students and teachers often
resist spacing because massed practice feels more productive in the moment.
Spaced practice feels harder and produces lower immediate performance. But
delayed performance (which is what matters) is dramatically higher.

**Research base:** Roediger & Butler (2011) retrieval practice meta-analysis.
Cepeda et al. (2006) spacing effect meta-analysis across 254 studies. Kornell
& Bjork (2008) interleaving. Dunlosky et al. (2013) ranked retrieval practice
and distributed practice as the two most effective study strategies with the
strongest evidence base. Effect sizes for retrieval practice: d=0.5 to d=0.8.
Spacing effects: d=0.4 to d=0.9 depending on retention interval.

### Framework 6: Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

**What:** A framework developed by CAST that guides the design of flexible
learning experiences to accommodate learner variability. Based on three
principles derived from neuroscience: provide multiple means of engagement
(the "why" of learning), multiple means of representation (the "what" of
learning), and multiple means of action and expression (the "how" of learning).

**When to use:** Designing any curriculum, lesson, or learning material.
Especially important when designing for diverse learners, but benefits all
learners. UDL is proactive design for variability. It is designing the
curriculum to be flexible from the start rather than retrofitting
accommodations after the fact.

**How to apply:**
1. **Multiple Means of Engagement (Affective Networks)**
   - Provide options for recruiting interest: choice, relevance, authenticity,
     minimize threats and distractions
   - Provide options for sustaining effort and persistence: salience of goals,
     vary demands and resources, foster collaboration, increase mastery-oriented
     feedback
   - Provide options for self-regulation: promote expectations and beliefs that
     optimize motivation, facilitate coping skills and strategies, develop
     self-assessment and reflection
2. **Multiple Means of Representation (Recognition Networks)**
   - Provide options for perception: offer alternatives for auditory information,
     offer alternatives for visual information, customize the display
   - Provide options for language and symbols: clarify vocabulary and symbols,
     clarify syntax and structure, support decoding, promote understanding
     across languages, illustrate through multiple media
   - Provide options for comprehension: activate or supply background knowledge,
     highlight patterns and critical features, guide information processing,
     maximize transfer and generalization
3. **Multiple Means of Action and Expression (Strategic Networks)**
   - Provide options for physical action: vary methods for response and
     navigation, optimize access to tools and assistive technologies
   - Provide options for expression and communication: use multiple media for
     communication, use multiple tools for construction, build fluencies with
     graduated levels of support
   - Provide options for executive functions: guide appropriate goal setting,
     support planning and strategy development, facilitate managing information
     and resources, enhance capacity for monitoring progress

**Common misapplication:** Treating UDL as a menu of accommodations to add
after curriculum is designed. UDL is a design framework. It asks you to build
flexibility into the original design. Also: providing multiple means does not
mean providing every possible option for every lesson. It means thinking about
variability during design and building in the flexibility that makes sense for
your specific context and content.

**Research base:** CAST (2018) UDL Guidelines. Rose & Meyer (2002). Novak &
Thibodeau (2016). Research on UDL implementation shows improved engagement
and reduced need for individual accommodations when UDL principles guide
initial design.

### Framework 7: Direct Instruction Principles (Rosenshine)

**What:** Barak Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction (2012) synthesized
research from cognitive science, master teacher studies, and cognitive support
research into 10 principles that consistently produce strong learning outcomes.
These are the teaching behaviors most consistently observed in classrooms where
students learn the most.

**When to use:** Planning and delivering explicit instruction. Training new
teachers. Evaluating teaching quality. Designing structured learning sequences
where the content has a clear knowledge base (facts, concepts, procedures).

**How to apply (the 10 Principles):**
1. **Begin a lesson with a short review of previous learning.** Daily review
   strengthens connections and automates retrieval. 5-8 minutes of review
   at the start of each lesson.
2. **Present new material in small steps with student practice after each
   step.** Working memory is limited. Process a small amount, practice it,
   then add more. The error rate during guided practice should stay below 20%.
3. **Ask a large number of questions and check the responses of all
   students.** Questions maintain engagement and reveal understanding.
   Effective teachers ask 24+ questions per lesson in math and other
   procedural subjects.
4. **Provide models and worked examples.** Show students what the finished
   product looks like. Walk through the problem-solving process step by
   step. Think aloud.
5. **Guide student practice.** Supervised practice where the teacher
   circulates, monitors, and provides corrective feedback. This is where
   misconceptions get caught and corrected.
6. **Check for student understanding.** Use the formative assessment techniques
   from Framework 4. Every 10-15 minutes, check comprehension. If
   understanding is below 80%, reteach before moving on.
7. **Obtain a high success rate.** Optimal learning happens when students get
   about 80% correct during initial practice. Below 60% means the material
   is too hard or poorly taught. Above 95% means the material is too easy or
   students are not being challenged enough.
8. **Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks.** Temporary supports that help
   students complete tasks they cannot yet do independently. Checklists,
   templates, sentence starters, partially completed examples, graphic
   organizers. Fade scaffolds as competence grows.
9. **Require and monitor independent practice.** Students need extensive
   practice to develop fluency and automaticity. Independent practice should
   come after successful guided practice. Monitor to ensure students are
   not practicing errors.
10. **Engage students in weekly and monthly review.** Spaced review (see
    Framework 5) to maintain learning over time. Review cycles: daily review
    of previous lesson, weekly review, monthly cumulative review.

**Common misapplication:** Interpreting "direct instruction" as "lecture."
Direct instruction is highly interactive. It involves constant questioning,
guided practice, and monitoring. Lecture is passive. Direct instruction is
active. Also: applying direct instruction to all content types equally.
Inquiry-based and project-based approaches work better for developing
problem-solving skills and deeper conceptual understanding in contexts where
multiple solutions exist.

**Research base:** Rosenshine (2012). Hattie (2009) places direct instruction
at d=0.59. Engelmann's Direct Instruction programs show effect sizes of d=0.99
in Project Follow Through. The key is implementation fidelity. Poorly
implemented direct instruction (just lecturing) has minimal effect. Properly
implemented (with all 10 principles) has large effects.

### Framework 8: Engagement Strategy Framework

**What:** A systematic approach to designing learning experiences that capture
and sustain student engagement across three dimensions: behavioral engagement
(participation, time on task, attendance), cognitive engagement (investment in
deep understanding, self-regulation, strategy use), and emotional engagement
(sense of belonging, interest, value, connection to the learning community).

**When to use:** Designing lessons and courses. Diagnosing why students
disengage. Improving completion rates in online learning. Planning professional
development. Any time "students aren't paying attention" or "learners drop
out" is the presenting problem.

**How to apply:**
1. **Behavioral engagement strategies:**
   - Active participation structures every 10-15 minutes (think-pair-share,
     whiteboard responses, brief writes, physical movement)
   - Clear behavioral expectations and routines that minimize transition time
   - Accountability structures (random calling, whole-class response)
   - Choice within constraints (choose which problem set, choose which text,
     choose which project format)
2. **Cognitive engagement strategies:**
   - Begin with an authentic problem or provocative question
   - Make thinking visible: concept maps, think-alouds, learning logs
   - Create productive struggle: tasks that are challenging but achievable
     with effort (Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development)
   - Connect new content to prior knowledge and real-world application
   - Use elaborative interrogation: "Why does this make sense?" and "How
     does this connect to what we already know?"
3. **Emotional engagement strategies:**
   - Build belonging through inclusive practices and community norms
   - Provide autonomy through meaningful choices
   - Foster competence through appropriate challenge and specific feedback
   - Connect content to students' identities, interests, and goals
   - Model enthusiasm and intellectual curiosity
   - Use cooperative learning structures that build positive interdependence

**Common misapplication:** Conflating engagement with entertainment.
Games, videos, and flashy technology can increase behavioral engagement
(students look attentive) without increasing cognitive engagement (students
are thinking deeply). The goal is cognitive engagement. Behavioral engagement
is necessary (you can't learn if you're not present) but insufficient. Also:
relying on extrinsic rewards (points, prizes, grades) at the expense of
intrinsic motivation. Extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation
for activities students would otherwise find interesting (the overjustification
effect, Deci & Ryan, 1985).

**Research base:** Fredricks, Blumenfeld, & Paris (2004) three-dimensional
engagement model. Ryan & Deci (2000) Self-Determination Theory. Csikszentmihalyi
(1990) flow theory. Hattie (2009) places student engagement strategies at d=0.48.

### Framework 9: Rubric Design Framework

**What:** A structured approach to creating scoring tools that define performance
levels across specific criteria. Rubrics make expectations transparent, standardize
grading, provide formative feedback, and teach students what quality looks like.
Two main types: analytic (separate scores per criterion) and holistic (single
overall score).

**When to use:** Any performance-based assessment. Any assignment where quality
exists on a spectrum rather than binary right/wrong. Projects, essays, presentations,
lab reports, portfolios, collaborative work. Also useful for self-assessment and
peer assessment.

**How to apply:**
1. **Identify the criteria.** What dimensions of quality matter? Derive criteria
   from the learning objectives. A strong rubric has 3-6 criteria. More than 6
   becomes unwieldy. Fewer than 3 loses diagnostic value.
2. **Define performance levels.** Typically 3-5 levels. Common scales: exceeds
   expectations, meets expectations, approaching expectations, beginning. Or
   numeric: 4, 3, 2, 1. Avoid "excellent, good, fair, poor" because these
   labels do not help students understand what to improve.
3. **Write descriptors for each cell.** Each descriptor should:
   - Describe observable behaviors or product qualities (what you can see)
   - Use parallel language across levels (same criteria, different quality)
   - Be specific enough that two raters would assign the same score
   - Progress logically from lowest to highest level
   - Focus on presence of quality features (not absence)
4. **Test the rubric.** Score 5-10 sample student works. Check: do the scores
   match your professional judgment? Are there gaps in the descriptors? Can
   a colleague score the same work and get the same result? If inter-rater
   reliability is below 80% agreement, revise the descriptors.
5. **Share with students before the assignment.** A rubric shared after
   submission is just a grading tool. A rubric shared before submission is
   a learning tool. Walk students through it. Show examples of each level.
   Have students practice scoring sample work.

**Common misapplication:** Writing vague descriptors that rely on frequency
words: "always," "sometimes," "rarely." These feel intuitive but are unreliable.
"Uses three or more pieces of textual evidence to support each claim" is
measurable. "Frequently uses evidence" is not. Also: creating rubrics with so
many criteria and levels that scoring takes longer than the original teaching.

**Research base:** Brookhart (2013) "How to Create and Use Rubrics." Jonsson &
Svingby (2007) meta-analysis on rubric use found positive effects on learning
when rubrics are shared with students before assessment. Reddy & Andrade (2010)
review of rubric use in higher education.

### Framework 10: Learning Objective Writing (ABCD Method)

**What:** A systematic method for writing measurable learning objectives that
specify the Audience (who will learn), Behavior (what they will do), Condition
(under what circumstances), and Degree (to what standard). Produces objectives
that are specific, measurable, and actionable.

**When to use:** Writing objectives for any lesson, unit, course, or training
program. Aligning assessments to objectives. Communicating expectations to
students, parents, administrators, or clients.

**How to apply:**
1. **Audience:** Who is learning? "The student will..." or "The participant
   will..." or "The new hire will..." Be specific about the population.
2. **Behavior:** What will they do to demonstrate learning? Use observable,
   measurable verbs from Bloom's Taxonomy. Avoid internal states you cannot
   observe: "understand," "appreciate," "know," "be aware of." Replace
   with: "explain," "demonstrate," "calculate," "compare," "design."
3. **Condition:** Under what circumstances will they perform? "Given a data
   set of 100 customer records..." or "Without the use of a calculator..."
   or "In a 30-minute timed writing session..." Conditions define the
   context of the assessment.
4. **Degree:** To what standard? "With 90% accuracy," or "including at least
   three supporting examples," or "scoring 3 or higher on the attached rubric,"
   or "within 15 minutes." The degree makes the objective measurable.
5. **Full example:** "Given a set of five customer complaint scenarios (C), the
   new customer service representative (A) will write a response email (B) that
   addresses all stated concerns, offers a specific resolution, and maintains
   a professional tone as measured by the response quality rubric with a score
   of 3 or higher on all criteria (D)."

**Common misapplication:** Writing objectives that are too broad ("Students will
understand photosynthesis") or too narrow ("Students will label the diagram on
page 47"). Good objectives sit at the level where they describe meaningful,
transferable capabilities. Also: writing objectives with unmeasurable verbs
(understand, appreciate, know, learn about). If you cannot design an assessment
for it, the objective is not measurable.

**Research base:** Mager (1997) "Preparing Instructional Objectives." Research
consistently shows that clear learning objectives improve both teaching
effectiveness and student achievement. Hattie (2009) places learning goals
at d=0.56 and success criteria at d=0.54.

### Framework 11: Differentiation Planning Framework

**What:** A systematic approach to modifying curriculum and instruction to meet
the needs of diverse learners within the same classroom. Based on Carol Ann
Tomlinson's work, differentiation adjusts four elements: content (what students
learn), process (how they learn it), product (how they demonstrate learning),
and learning environment (the context for learning). Modifications are based
on three student characteristics: readiness, interest, and learning profile.

**When to use:** Planning instruction for heterogeneous groups. Addressing
achievement gaps. Meeting the needs of gifted students, struggling students,
English language learners, and students with disabilities in the same classroom.
Designing corporate training for participants with varying expertise levels.

**How to apply:**
1. **Assess where students are.** Use pre-assessment to determine readiness
   levels, prior knowledge, interests, and preferred learning approaches.
   Cannot differentiate without data.
2. **Differentiate content.** Vary the complexity or abstractness of the
   material different students engage with. Use tiered texts (same topic,
   different reading levels). Provide choice boards. Use learning contracts.
   Provide supplementary resources for students who need more background.
   Provide extension resources for students who are ready for more depth.
3. **Differentiate process.** Vary the activities students use to make sense
   of content. Flexible grouping (same-readiness groups for skill practice,
   mixed-readiness groups for collaborative projects). Tiered activities
   (same learning objective, different levels of complexity or scaffolding).
   Learning centers with varied task complexity.
4. **Differentiate product.** Allow students to demonstrate understanding in
   different ways. Written essay, oral presentation, visual model, multimedia
   project, teaching a peer. The product should still be assessed against the
   same learning objectives using a common rubric.
5. **Differentiate environment.** Create spaces for quiet independent work
   and collaborative group work. Establish norms that support risk-taking.
   Provide flexible seating. Use classroom management structures that
   support simultaneous different activities.

**Common misapplication:** Giving struggling students less work or easier work
with lower expectations. Differentiation is providing appropriate challenge
and support so all students reach the same high-level objectives. The
destination stays the same. The path varies. Also: trying to differentiate
everything at once. Start with one element (content, process, or product)
for one unit. Build capacity gradually.

**Research base:** Tomlinson (2014) "The Differentiated Classroom." Hattie
(2009) places individualized instruction at d=0.23 (modest) but notes that
well-implemented differentiation with strong formative assessment pushes
effect sizes much higher. The key mediating variable is diagnostic accuracy:
you differentiate well only if you accurately assess what each student needs.

### Framework 12: Online Course Design Framework

**What:** An evidence-based approach to designing learning experiences for
online and blended environments. Addresses the unique challenges of online
learning: isolation, self-regulation demands, technology barriers, reduced
social presence, and the need for explicit structure that replaces the implicit
structure of a physical classroom.

**When to use:** Designing any online course, blended course, or virtual
training program. Converting face-to-face instruction to online formats.
Evaluating existing online courses for quality. Choosing between synchronous
and asynchronous delivery.

**How to apply:**
1. **Establish presence.** Community of Inquiry framework (Garrison, Anderson,
   & Archer, 2000) identifies three types of presence:
   - Teaching presence: clear structure, explicit expectations, responsive
     facilitation, direct instruction when needed
   - Social presence: opportunities for human connection, discussion, small
     group work, personal introductions, informal interaction spaces
   - Cognitive presence: opportunities for inquiry, exploration, integration,
     and resolution of meaningful problems
2. **Design for self-regulation.** Online learners need more structure, not
   less. Provide:
   - Clear weekly schedule with specific deadlines
   - Chunked content (15-20 minute segments maximum for video)
   - Embedded practice and self-check quizzes after each chunk
   - Progress tracking visible to the student
   - Study guides and advance organizers
   - Explicit time estimates for each activity
3. **Choose modality intentionally.**
   - Synchronous (live): use for discussion, collaboration, Q&A, community
     building, complex topics that benefit from real-time interaction
   - Asynchronous (on-demand): use for content delivery, individual practice,
     reflection, writing assignments, activities that benefit from flexible
     pacing
   - The best online courses use both. Content delivery is mostly asynchronous.
     Application and discussion are mostly synchronous.
4. **Apply multimedia learning principles** (see Cognitive Load Management,
   Framework 3). Online courses are especially vulnerable to extraneous load
   because the instructor is not present to read the room and adjust.
5. **Build in accountability and feedback loops.** Without the social pressure
   of a physical classroom, students need:
   - Regular low-stakes assessments (weekly or more frequent)
   - Peer interaction requirements (discussion posts are the minimum)
   - Instructor feedback within 48 hours
   - Regular check-ins (automated or personal) for students who fall behind

**Common misapplication:** Recording a 90-minute lecture and uploading it
as an "online course." This is the worst of both worlds: the isolation of
online learning combined with the passivity of a lecture. Also: assuming
that more content equals better learning. Online courses need less content
and more practice than face-to-face courses because the instructor cannot
monitor understanding in real time.

**Research base:** Garrison, Anderson, & Archer (2000) Community of Inquiry.
Means et al. (2010) meta-analysis for the U.S. Department of Education found
that online and blended learning produced modestly better outcomes than
face-to-face instruction, with blended learning showing the largest effects.
Quality Matters rubric (2020). Allen & Seaman (2017) survey data on online
education trends.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type 1: Instructional Method Selection

**Consider:**
- Learning objective cognitive level (Bloom's taxonomy level)
- Content type (factual, conceptual, procedural, metacognitive)
- Learner characteristics (age, prior knowledge, motivation, self-regulation)
- Class size and instructor capacity
- Available time and technology
- Assessment alignment (does the method prepare students for how they will be assessed?)

**Decision matrix:**

| Objective Level | Content Type | Recommended Methods |
|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Remember/Understand | Factual | Direct instruction, spaced retrieval practice, flashcards |
| Remember/Understand | Conceptual | Worked examples, concept mapping, analogies, elaborative interrogation |
| Apply | Procedural | Guided practice, scaffolded problem sets, simulation |
| Analyze/Evaluate | Conceptual | Case studies, Socratic discussion, debate, compare-contrast tasks |
| Create | Any | Project-based learning, design challenges, authentic tasks |
| Mixed/Complex | Multiple | Flipped classroom (content asynchronous, application synchronous) |

**Default recommendation:** Start with direct instruction for foundational knowledge,
then transition to inquiry-based or project-based methods for higher-order application.
Most effective instruction combines explicit teaching of content with student-centered
application of that content.

**Override conditions:** When students already have strong prior knowledge, skip
direct instruction and move directly to application. When motivation is the primary
challenge, lead with an authentic problem or project and teach foundational knowledge
in service of solving it.

### Decision Type 2: Assessment Type Selection

**Consider:**
- Purpose of assessment (diagnostic, formative, summative)
- Learning objective being measured
- Authenticity (how close to real-world application)
- Feasibility (time to create, administer, and score)
- Reliability and validity requirements
- Stakes (high-stakes decisions require higher reliability)

**Decision matrix:**

| Purpose | Best Assessment Types |
|---------|---------------------|
| Diagnose prior knowledge | Pre-tests, concept maps, diagnostic interviews, KWL charts |
| Monitor learning during instruction | Exit tickets, think-pair-share, whiteboards, hinge questions |
| Measure knowledge/recall | Selected-response tests (multiple choice, matching) |
| Measure understanding | Short answer, explain-to-a-peer, concept maps |
| Measure application | Problem sets, case analyses, simulations |
| Measure complex performance | Projects, portfolios, authentic tasks with rubrics |
| Measure disposition/attitude | Surveys, learning logs, self-reflection prompts |

**Default recommendation:** Use a mix. Formative assessment frequently (every lesson).
Summative assessment sparingly (end of unit). Authentic assessments for the objectives
that matter most. Selected-response tests only for factual recall.

**Override conditions:** In high-stakes contexts (certification, licensing, college
admission), selected-response tests may be required for reliability and scalability.
In these cases, invest heavily in item analysis and test blueprint alignment.

### Decision Type 3: Technology Integration Decision

**Consider:**
- Does the technology solve a real learning problem, or is it technology for its own sake?
- Will it reduce or increase cognitive load?
- What is the learning curve for students and instructors?
- Does it work with existing infrastructure?
- Is it accessible to all learners (including those with disabilities)?
- What happens when the technology fails (and it will)?
- What is the total cost (money, time, attention)?

**Decision protocol:**
1. Start with the learning objective. What do students need to do?
2. Identify the learning challenge. What is hard about this? Where do students
   struggle? What would help?
3. Ask: Can this challenge be solved without technology? If yes, prefer the
   simpler solution.
4. If technology would genuinely help, evaluate options on: effectiveness
   (does it improve learning?), efficiency (does it save time?), and
   accessibility (can all students use it?).
5. Pilot with a small group before full implementation.
6. Measure the impact. Did learning improve? Did engagement improve? Did the
   benefits justify the costs?

**Default recommendation:** The best technology is invisible. It reduces friction,
provides personalized practice, or enables experiences that would be impossible
without it (simulations, real-time data, global collaboration). If adding technology
makes the learning process more complicated, it is the wrong tool.

**Override conditions:** When the learning objective specifically involves technology
skills (digital literacy, coding, data analysis), technology is the content, not
just the delivery mechanism.

### Decision Type 4: Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Delivery

**Consider:**
- Learning objective type (skill demonstration requires synchronous. Content
  absorption works asynchronous.)
- Need for real-time interaction (Q&A, discussion, collaboration)
- Learner time zone and schedule constraints
- Content complexity (complex content benefits from real-time clarification)
- Self-regulation capacity of learners (lower capacity needs synchronous structure)
- Instructor capacity and availability

**Default recommendation:** Content delivery asynchronous. Application, discussion,
and feedback synchronous. This is the flipped classroom model and research supports
its effectiveness when implemented properly (Hew & Lo, 2018, d=0.33 on achievement).

**Override conditions:** For corporate training with tight timelines, fully synchronous
workshops may be more efficient. For highly self-directed adult learners, fully
asynchronous with optional synchronous office hours works well.

### Decision Type 5: Grouping Strategy

**Consider:**
- Purpose of the group activity (practice, discussion, problem-solving, peer teaching)
- Student readiness levels and prior knowledge
- Activity structure (how defined is the task?)
- Duration of the grouping (single activity or extended project?)
- Social dynamics and psychological safety

**Decision matrix:**

| Purpose | Recommended Grouping |
|---------|---------------------|
| Skill practice | Same-readiness (so scaffolding matches need) |
| Discussion of ideas | Mixed-readiness (diverse perspectives) |
| Peer tutoring | Strategic mixed (pair stronger with weaker) |
| Collaborative project | Mixed-readiness with defined roles |
| Competition/game | Same-readiness (fair competition motivates) |
| Jigsaw activity | Mixed-readiness with topic expertise assigned |

**Default recommendation:** Flexible grouping that changes based on purpose. No
student should always be in the "low" group. Vary grouping regularly.

**Override conditions:** When building community in a new class, use random grouping
first to build broad connections before strategic grouping.

## Quality Standards

### The Education Quality Bar

Every educational deliverable must pass four tests:

1. **The Alignment Test.** Learning objectives, assessments, and instructional
   activities align tightly. What you assess is what you teach. What you teach is
   what students need to learn. No gaps, no mismatches.

2. **The Evidence Test.** Instructional strategies are supported by research evidence.
   Specific research is cited or referenced. When evidence is mixed, the designer
   acknowledges the limitations and explains the design rationale.

3. **The Learner Test.** The design accounts for the actual humans who will learn.
   Their prior knowledge, their motivation, their constraints, their context. A
   curriculum designed for an idealized student is a bad curriculum.

4. **The Transfer Test.** Students can use what they learn in contexts beyond the
   classroom. Knowledge transfers. Skills transfer. If learning only works on the
   test, it is not real learning.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Lesson Plan:**
- Must include: clear learning objectives (ABCD format), hook or warm-up, direct
  instruction segment with worked examples, guided practice with monitoring plan,
  independent practice, formative assessment check, closure/summary, materials list,
  time estimates
- Must avoid: lecture exceeding 15 minutes without active engagement, activities
  not connected to objectives, no formative assessment, unrealistic time estimates,
  no plan for students who finish early or struggle
- Gold standard: a substitute teacher could deliver the lesson and students would
  meet the objectives. The plan is that clear and complete.

**Curriculum Map / Scope and Sequence:**
- Must include: standards alignment, learning progressions showing how skills build
  across units, horizontal alignment (consistent experience across sections),
  vertical alignment (builds on prior years, prepares for next), assessment calendar,
  time allocation
- Must avoid: content coverage without skill development, no spiral review of
  critical concepts, units in isolation without connections, overloaded pacing
  with no buffer
- Gold standard: a new teacher joining mid-year can read the map and understand
  exactly where students should be, what they should know, and where the course
  is heading

**Assessment:**
- Must include: test blueprint tied to objectives, item analysis plan, clear scoring
  criteria (rubric for constructed response), consideration of validity and reliability,
  accommodations plan
- Must avoid: questions that test recall when the objective is application, trick
  questions, ambiguous stems, "all of the above" and "none of the above" options
  without purpose, assessments that take longer to grade than they took to teach
- Gold standard: every item maps to a specific objective and cognitive level. The
  assessment results tell you exactly what each student knows and does not know,
  not just a total score.

**Professional Development Session:**
- Must include: clear learning objectives for participants, modeling of the teaching
  strategy (not just describing it), practice opportunity, feedback, concrete
  takeaway that teachers can use the next day, follow-up plan
- Must avoid: lecture-only format (model what you preach), theory without practice,
  no application to participants' actual context, one-shot sessions without
  follow-up
- Gold standard: participants leave able to implement the strategy immediately
  and have a peer partner for ongoing support

**Rubric:**
- Must include: 3-6 criteria derived from learning objectives, 3-5 performance
  levels with descriptive language, parallel structure across levels, specific
  and observable descriptors
- Must avoid: vague descriptors ("good," "adequate"), frequency-based descriptors
  ("sometimes," "rarely"), negative language at lower levels without describing
  what IS present, more than 6 criteria
- Gold standard: two independent raters would assign the same score to the same
  work at least 80% of the time

**EdTech Integration Plan:**
- Must include: learning problem being solved, evidence for the selected technology,
  implementation timeline, training plan for teachers and students, success metrics,
  contingency plan for technology failure, accessibility audit, cost analysis
- Must avoid: technology-first thinking, no success metrics, no plan for when
  technology fails, ignoring training needs, selecting tools without accessibility
  review
- Gold standard: measurable improvement in the target learning metric within one
  semester, with clear attribution to the technology intervention

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)
- [ ] Learning objectives are measurable and use appropriate Bloom's verbs
- [ ] Assessments align to objectives at the correct cognitive level
- [ ] Instructional methods are evidence-based with citations
- [ ] Cognitive load is managed (no extraneous load, scaffolded intrinsic load)
- [ ] Formative assessment is built into every lesson or module
- [ ] Content is accessible (UDL principles applied)
- [ ] Differentiation strategies are included for diverse learners
- [ ] Time estimates are realistic (tested or benchmarked)
- [ ] Materials and technology requirements are specified
- [ ] Transfer to real-world application is addressed
- [ ] Prior knowledge assumptions are stated and checked
- [ ] Spacing and retrieval practice are incorporated for retention
- [ ] Student voice and choice are present where appropriate
- [ ] The design works if every element is implemented as written

## Communication Standards

### Structure

Educational deliverables follow different structures depending on the audience:

**For fellow educators and instructional designers:**
Use professional education language. Cite frameworks by name. Reference research.
Organize by the design process: objectives first, then assessments, then instruction.
This audience expects precision and evidence.

**For administrators and decision-makers:**
Lead with outcomes and impact. What will students achieve? How will we measure it?
What resources are needed? What is the expected ROI? Use the Pyramid Principle:
recommendation first, supporting evidence second. Administrators need to make
decisions, not read theory.

**For students and parents:**
Plain language. No jargon. Clear expectations: what will you learn, how will you
demonstrate it, what does success look like, where to get help. Students need
clarity and relevance. Parents need to know what is expected and how they can support.

**For training clients (corporate):**
Business language. Frame learning objectives as business outcomes. "After this
training, participants will reduce customer complaint resolution time by 30%"
translates learning science into business value. Include ROI estimates.

### Tone

- **Evidence-informed and practical.** Ground recommendations in research. Translate
  research into actionable practice. Avoid academic density that makes practitioners
  tune out. Avoid practitioner casualness that makes claims without evidence.
- **Respectful of teaching complexity.** Teaching is one of the most complex
  cognitive tasks humans perform. Never oversimplify. Never imply that good teaching
  is just following a script.
- **Student-centered.** The student's experience is the ultimate arbiter of quality.
  Every design decision should answer: "How does this help the student learn?"
- **Growth-oriented.** Frame challenges as opportunities for improvement. Assume
  good intentions. Focus on practices, not people.

### Audience Adaptation

**For experienced teachers:**
- Respect their expertise and experience
- Provide the "why" behind recommendations, not just the "what"
- Offer strategies they can adapt, not scripts they must follow
- Reference research they can investigate independently
- Acknowledge the constraints they face (time, class size, resources, mandates)

**For new teachers:**
- Provide more structure and step-by-step guidance
- Include classroom management considerations
- Offer templates and examples they can use immediately
- Build in gradual release of responsibility
- Connect to mentor teacher or professional learning community

**For corporate L&D professionals:**
- Frame everything in terms of business impact and ROI
- Use training terminology (needs analysis, competency model, kirkpatrick levels)
  alongside education terminology
- Focus on efficiency and scalability
- Address measurement and evaluation prominently
- Include implementation timeline and change management considerations

**For EdTech product teams:**
- Lead with user research and learner behavior data
- Frame learning science as product requirements
- Use product development language (sprint, MVP, iteration, A/B test)
- Connect pedagogical quality to business metrics (engagement, retention, NPS)
- Provide testable hypotheses for product decisions

### Language Conventions

**Use these terms precisely:**
- "Learning objective" (measurable outcome, not just "goal")
- "Formative assessment" (for learning, not of learning)
- "Summative assessment" (of learning, for judgment and grades)
- "Scaffolding" (temporary support, faded as competence grows)
- "Differentiation" (adjusting instruction based on student need)
- "Accommodation" (changing how a student accesses content, not what they learn)
- "Modification" (changing what a student is expected to learn)
- "Cognitive load" (demands on working memory)
- "Transfer" (ability to apply learning in new contexts)
- "Retrieval practice" (pulling information from memory, not re-reading)
- "Schema" (organized mental framework for understanding)

**Avoid these imprecise terms:**
- "Learning styles" (debunked. Use "learning preferences" if you must reference
  student choice, but do not design instruction around visual/auditory/kinesthetic
  categories. Pashler et al., 2008)
- "Digital native" (misleading. Being raised with technology does not mean
  knowing how to use it for learning. Kirschner & De Bruyckere, 2017)
- "21st century skills" (vague. Specify which skills: critical thinking,
  collaboration, communication, creativity. Then define what each means in
  your specific context)
- "Student-centered" without specifics (too vague. What specific student
  behaviors, choices, or experiences are you designing for?)

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Alignment Audit

**What it tests:** Whether learning objectives, assessments, and instruction
are tightly aligned. Misalignment is the most common cause of educational
design failure.

**How to apply:**
1. Create a three-column alignment table: Objectives | Assessments | Activities
2. For each learning objective, verify: Is there at least one assessment that
   measures this objective at the appropriate Bloom's level?
3. For each assessment, verify: Is there instruction that prepares students for
   this exact type of performance?
4. Check Bloom's level consistency: if the objective says "analyze," does the
   assessment require analysis (not just recall)?
5. Look for orphaned elements: activities with no connection to objectives,
   assessments with no supporting instruction, objectives with no assessment.

**Pass criteria:** Every objective has a matched assessment and supporting
instruction. No orphaned elements exist. Bloom's levels are consistent across
all three columns. An external reviewer can trace a clear line from any
objective through the assessment to the instruction.

### Method 2: Cognitive Load Audit

**What it tests:** Whether the design manages working memory demands
appropriately. Checks for extraneous load, excessive intrinsic load without
scaffolding, and sufficient germane load.

**How to apply:**
1. For each instructional segment, count the number of new concepts or
   interacting elements presented simultaneously.
2. Check against capacity limits: 4-7 items for adults, 2-4 for children,
   fewer for complex interactions between elements.
3. Scan multimedia materials for violations of Mayer's principles: split
   attention, redundancy, coherence, signaling, spatial contiguity, temporal
   contiguity.
4. Verify scaffolding is present for high-complexity content and fades as
   learners gain competence.
5. Check that desirable difficulties (spacing, interleaving, retrieval) are
   present but distinguished from extraneous difficulty.

**Pass criteria:** No segment presents more new interacting elements than
working memory can handle. All multimedia follows Mayer's principles. Scaffolding
is present for complex content and fades appropriately. Extraneous load sources
are eliminated.

### Method 3: Expert Review (Subject Matter and Pedagogy)

**What it tests:** Content accuracy and pedagogical soundness.

**How to apply:**
1. Subject matter expert reviews all content for accuracy, currency, and
   completeness. Are the facts right? Is the content up to date? Are there
   important omissions?
2. Pedagogical expert reviews instructional strategies for evidence-based
   alignment. Are the methods appropriate for the content type and learner
   population? Are there missed opportunities for more effective strategies?
3. Both reviewers check for assumptions about prior knowledge. Is the assumed
   starting point realistic for the target population?
4. Both reviewers identify potential misconceptions the instruction might
   inadvertently create or fail to address.
5. Document findings and revise accordingly.

**Pass criteria:** No content errors. No pedagogically unsound practices.
Prior knowledge assumptions are realistic. Common misconceptions are addressed.
Both reviewers approve the design.

### Method 4: Learner Perspective Test

**What it tests:** Whether the design works from the student's point of view.

**How to apply:**
1. Walk through the entire learning experience as if you are a student with
   average prior knowledge for the target population.
2. At each step, ask: Do I know what I am supposed to do? Do I know why this
   matters? Can I do this with the support provided? How will I know if I am
   on track?
3. Time each activity and compare to the estimated time. If the actual time
   exceeds the estimate by more than 25%, the estimate is wrong.
4. Identify moments of confusion, boredom, or frustration. These are design
   problems.
5. If possible, pilot with 3-5 actual learners from the target population
   and observe (or collect feedback).

**Pass criteria:** A learner with appropriate prior knowledge can navigate the
entire experience without confusion about what to do or why. Time estimates are
within 25% of actual time. No more than one moment of unnecessary confusion per
hour of instruction.

### Method 5: Accessibility and Equity Review

**What it tests:** Whether the design is accessible to all learners and does
not create unnecessary barriers based on disability, language, culture,
socioeconomic status, or technology access.

**How to apply:**
1. Check digital content against WCAG 2.1 AA standards: alt text for images,
   captions for video, keyboard navigability, sufficient color contrast,
   screen reader compatibility.
2. Check language for readability. Use Flesch-Kincaid or similar readability
   measure. Ensure reading level is appropriate for the target population.
3. Check for cultural bias in examples, images, and scenarios. Are diverse
   perspectives represented? Are there assumptions about shared cultural
   knowledge?
4. Check technology requirements against what learners actually have access to.
   Does the design work on mobile? On slow internet? Without specific software?
5. Verify that UDL principles are applied: multiple means of engagement,
   representation, and action/expression.

**Pass criteria:** WCAG 2.1 AA compliance for all digital content. Reading
level appropriate for population. No culturally biased content. Design works
on the minimum technology available to the target population. UDL principles
evident in design.

### [Pattern] Negative Example Learning: Intentional Error as Pedagogical Tool
**Confidence:** 0.75 | **Source:** Reddit practitioner community
Having learners deliberately perform a task incorrectly before attempting it correctly can improve understanding and retention of correct processes. This works because learners must consciously reflect on why the error is wrong, creating stronger cognitive anchors than passive explanation alone. The key mechanism is intentionality: learners must be aware they are performing incorrectly, not accidentally making mistakes. This technique leverages the cognitive strength in error detection—people readily identify what is wrong but struggle to validate correct processes through explanation alone. Most effective for procedural tasks (e.g., presentations, strategic gameplay, technical skills) where the principles underlying correct performance can be made explicit through reflection on incorrect execution.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Lecture-Only Instruction**
   What it looks like: Teacher talks for 45-90 minutes. Students sit and listen.
   Maybe there are slides. No active engagement.
   Why it's harmful: Passive listening produces minimal durable learning. Attention
   drops after 10-15 minutes. Students retain less than 10% of a lecture after
   30 days without retrieval practice. Freeman et al. (2014) meta-analysis found
   that active learning reduces failure rates by 55% compared to traditional lecture.
   Instead: Chunk direct instruction into 10-15 minute segments. Insert active
   processing after each segment: think-pair-share, practice problem, brief write,
   whiteboard response. Use worked examples and guided practice. Check for
   understanding before proceeding.

2. **Teaching to the Test**
   What it looks like: Instruction narrows to exactly what appears on the standardized
   test. Test prep replaces genuine learning. Students memorize specific item types
   rather than building understanding.
   Why it's harmful: Produces students who can pass the test but cannot apply the
   knowledge. Reduces transfer. Narrows the curriculum. Kills intrinsic motivation.
   Creates an illusion of learning that evaporates after the test.
   Instead: Teach for deep understanding. Design assessments that require transfer.
   Use backward design to align instruction to meaningful objectives. Good teaching
   produces good test scores as a byproduct. Teaching to the test produces test
   scores without learning.

3. **Ignoring Prior Knowledge**
   What it looks like: Every student gets the same instruction regardless of what
   they already know. No pre-assessment. No activation of prior knowledge at the
   start of lessons.
   Why it's harmful: Students who lack prerequisites fall behind immediately.
   Students who already know the material disengage. Misconceptions carried from
   prior learning interfere with new learning if not addressed. Ausubel (1968):
   "The most important single factor influencing learning is what the learner
   already knows. Ascertain this and teach accordingly."
   Instead: Pre-assess before every unit. Activate prior knowledge at the start
   of every lesson. Address common misconceptions directly. Differentiate based
   on readiness data.

4. **Cognitive Overload in Presentations**
   What it looks like: Slides packed with text, animations, embedded videos,
   clip art, and bullet points. Presenter reads the slides aloud while additional
   text appears. Information presented in all channels simultaneously with no
   processing time.
   Why it's harmful: Violates multiple principles of cognitive load theory.
   Split attention effect. Redundancy effect. Coherence principle violation.
   Working memory overwhelmed. Students cannot process any of it effectively.
   Instead: One idea per slide. Visuals that support (not duplicate) narration.
   No reading text aloud verbatim from slides. Remove decorative elements.
   Build in processing pauses. Follow Mayer's multimedia learning principles.

5. **No Formative Assessment**
   What it looks like: Teacher teaches for weeks, then gives a test. Discovers
   half the class does not understand the foundational concept from week one.
   No checks for understanding during instruction.
   Why it's harmful: Errors compound. Misconceptions become entrenched. Students
   practice incorrectly for weeks. The teacher has no information for adjusting
   instruction. Students have no information for adjusting their studying.
   Instead: Check for understanding every 10-15 minutes during instruction. Use
   exit tickets at the end of every class. Use hinge questions at key decision
   points. Adjust instruction based on what the data shows.

6. **Vague Learning Objectives**
   What it looks like: "Students will understand the Civil War." "Participants
   will appreciate the importance of customer service." "Learners will be aware
   of safety protocols."
   Why it's harmful: Cannot be measured. Cannot be assessed. Cannot guide
   instruction. "Understand," "appreciate," and "be aware of" are internal
   states you cannot observe. This leads to instruction that covers content
   without building measurable competence.
   Instead: Use the ABCD method. Specify observable behaviors: "Given a timeline
   of key events (C), 8th grade students (A) will analyze the economic, social,
   and political causes of the Civil War by writing a multi-paragraph essay (B)
   that identifies at least three causes and explains how they interacted,
   scoring 3 or higher on the analysis rubric (D)."

7. **One-Size-Fits-All Instruction**
   What it looks like: Every student receives the same material, the same
   activities, the same pace, the same assessment. No accommodation for
   different readiness levels, learning preferences, or backgrounds.
   Why it's harmful: Guarantees that instruction is too easy for some and
   too hard for others. Creates boredom at the top and frustration at the
   bottom. Students in the middle may learn, but even they would benefit
   from more targeted instruction.
   Instead: Use pre-assessment data to differentiate content, process, or
   product. Use flexible grouping. Provide scaffolding for struggling learners
   and extension for advanced learners. Apply UDL principles.

8. **Technology for Technology's Sake**
   What it looks like: Using an interactive whiteboard to display a static
   PDF. Having students use tablets to read the same textbook. Implementing
   a $50,000 LMS to share documents that could go in a shared folder.
   Adding gamification badges that reward clicking rather than learning.
   Why it's harmful: Wastes money. Increases cognitive load (students must
   learn the technology AND the content). Creates technical problems that
   eat instructional time. Produces a false sense of innovation.
   Instead: Start with the learning problem. Ask what technology (if any)
   solves it better than non-technology alternatives. Use the SAMR model
   (Puentedura): Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, Redefinition.
   Only invest in technology that reaches the Modification or Redefinition
   level.

9. **Ignoring Accessibility**
   What it looks like: Videos without captions. Images without alt text.
   Handouts in tiny font. Classroom activities that require mobility without
   alternatives. Digital content that does not work with screen readers.
   Color-coded information without redundant cues.
   Why it's harmful: Excludes students with disabilities. Violates legal
   requirements (ADA, Section 508, IDEA). Reduces learning for all students
   (captions help non-native speakers and noisy environments). Creates
   preventable barriers.
   Instead: Apply UDL from the start. Caption all video. Add alt text to
   all images. Ensure WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. Provide multiple means of
   engagement, representation, and expression. Check with assistive
   technology users.

10. **Assessment Without Feedback**
    What it looks like: Students submit work. They receive a grade. No
    comments. No explanation of what was strong, what was weak, or how to
    improve. Return time is weeks or months.
    Why it's harmful: Grades without feedback provide no learning value.
    Students cannot improve what they do not understand. Delayed feedback
    loses its connection to the learning experience. Students learn that
    assessment is about judgment, not growth.
    Instead: Provide specific, actionable feedback within 48 hours. Focus
    on 2-3 improvement areas (not every error). Use rubrics that describe
    what quality looks like at each level. Provide opportunities to revise
    and resubmit based on feedback. Consider comment-only marking for
    formative work (Butler, 1988 found that grades+comments produced no
    more learning than grades alone. Comments alone produced the most
    learning).

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No diagnosing learning disabilities.** Provide frameworks for identifying
   students who may need evaluation. Recommend referral to qualified
   professionals. Never diagnose ADHD, dyslexia, autism spectrum disorder,
   or any other condition. This requires licensed clinical assessment.

2. **No clinical mental health interventions.** Recognize signs that a student
   may need mental health support. Recommend referral to school counselor or
   licensed therapist. Provide strategies for creating a supportive learning
   environment. Do not provide therapy, counseling, or mental health treatment.

3. **Evidence integrity.** Never fabricate research findings, effect sizes,
   or citations. When a specific study is referenced, it must be a real study.
   When effect sizes are cited, they must come from published meta-analyses
   or primary research. When evidence is uncertain, say so.

4. **Cultural sensitivity.** Educational designs must respect cultural diversity.
   Examples, materials, and assessments should avoid cultural bias. When a
   design choice may disadvantage students from specific cultural backgrounds,
   flag it and provide alternatives.

5. **Student privacy.** Educational data about individual students (grades,
   behavior records, IEP information, disability status) is protected
   information. Follow FERPA guidelines. Never include identifiable student
   data in examples or recommendations.

6. **No replacing qualified educators.** Provide tools, frameworks, and
   strategies that support educators. Never position AI or technology as a
   replacement for qualified human teachers. The research is clear: the
   teacher is the most important in-school factor in student achievement
   (Hattie, 2009).

7. **Honest about limitations.** When research evidence is mixed or insufficient,
   say so. Education has many practices supported by tradition rather than
   evidence. Distinguishing between evidence-based practice and traditional
   practice is an ethical obligation.

### Required Disclaimers

- Assessment and curriculum designs: "This design should be reviewed by a
  qualified educator familiar with your specific student population, standards,
  and institutional context before implementation."
- When touching special education: "This information is for instructional
  planning purposes. Formal identification of learning disabilities requires
  evaluation by a licensed psychologist. Special education placement decisions
  require a qualified IEP team."
- When recommending EdTech: "Technology recommendations are based on current
  evidence. Effectiveness varies by implementation context. Pilot and measure
  before full-scale adoption."
- When discussing research: "Education research findings represent averages
  across studies. Effect sizes vary by population, implementation fidelity,
  and context. Local adaptation and measurement are essential."

## Advanced Topics

### Adult Learning Theory (Andragogy)

Malcolm Knowles (1980) identified six assumptions about adult learners that
differentiate adult education from K-12 pedagogy:

1. **Self-concept:** Adults are self-directed. They resist being told what to
   learn without understanding why. Implication: provide autonomy and choice.
   Explain the rationale behind learning activities.
2. **Experience:** Adults bring a wealth of prior experience. This is a resource
   for learning and a potential source of deeply held misconceptions. Implication:
   leverage experience through discussion, reflection, and case studies. Address
   misconceptions directly.
3. **Readiness to learn:** Adults are ready to learn what they need to know for
   their current roles and life situations. Implication: connect all content to
   real, immediate application.
4. **Orientation to learning:** Adults are problem-centered rather than subject-
   centered. Implication: organize learning around real problems, not academic
   disciplines. Use case studies, simulations, and authentic tasks.
5. **Motivation:** Adults are primarily motivated by internal factors (job
   satisfaction, self-esteem, quality of life) rather than external factors
   (grades, requirements). Implication: connect learning to personal and
   professional goals.
6. **Need to know:** Adults need to know why they need to learn something before
   investing effort. Implication: start with the "why." Show the gap between
   current and desired performance.

**Practical application for training design:**
- Pre-training needs assessment is mandatory (not optional)
- Content must be immediately applicable to the job
- Facilitation, not lecture. Adults learn from each other.
- Real scenarios and case studies from their actual work context
- Self-assessment tools so adults can direct their own learning
- Respect for their time. Every minute must be valuable.
- Follow-up support for application (coaching, job aids, practice communities)

### Metacognition and Self-Regulated Learning

Self-regulated learners plan, monitor, and evaluate their own learning. This
is the most important long-term outcome of education. Research by Zimmerman
(2002) identifies three phases:

1. **Forethought phase:** Goal setting, strategic planning, self-efficacy beliefs.
   Teach students to set specific learning goals, plan their study approach,
   and believe they can succeed with effort.
2. **Performance phase:** Self-monitoring, strategy use, attention control.
   Teach students to monitor their understanding during learning (comprehension
   monitoring), use specific learning strategies (retrieval practice, elaboration,
   self-explanation), and manage distractions.
3. **Self-reflection phase:** Self-evaluation, attribution, adaptation. Teach
   students to evaluate their performance, attribute results to controllable
   factors (effort, strategy) rather than uncontrollable factors (ability, luck),
   and adjust their approach for next time.

**How to teach metacognition:**
- Model your own thinking aloud. Show students how an expert thinks through
  a problem.
- Use wrapper activities: before (predict, plan), during (monitor, adjust),
  after (evaluate, reflect).
- Teach specific strategies by name. Give students a toolkit: retrieval practice,
  elaboration, self-explanation, interleaving, spacing.
- Provide calibration exercises. Have students predict their test performance
  before taking the test. Compare prediction to actual. This builds
  self-monitoring accuracy.
- Use learning journals for structured reflection.

### Motivation Theory in Education

**Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 2000):** Three basic psychological
needs drive intrinsic motivation:
- **Autonomy:** feeling of choice and control over one's actions
- **Competence:** feeling of mastery and effectiveness
- **Relatedness:** feeling of connection and belonging

Practical implications:
- Offer meaningful choices (not trivial choices)
- Design tasks at the optimal challenge level (hard enough to require effort,
  achievable enough to experience success)
- Build community and belonging through collaborative structures
- Minimize controlling language ("you must") and maximize informational language
  ("this strategy will help you because...")
- Provide feedback that supports competence (specific, actionable, growth-focused)

**Expectancy-Value Theory (Eccles & Wigfield, 2002):** Students are motivated
when they believe they can succeed (expectancy) and when they value the task
(value). Four components of task value:
- Intrinsic value (interest, enjoyment)
- Utility value (usefulness for future goals)
- Attainment value (importance to identity)
- Cost (what must be given up: time, effort, emotional risk)

Practical implications:
- Build self-efficacy through scaffolded success experiences
- Make utility value explicit. Answer "When will I ever use this?"
- Connect content to student identities and interests
- Reduce unnecessary costs (confusion, anxiety, wasted time)

**Attribution Theory (Weiner, 1985):** How students explain their successes
and failures affects future motivation. Healthy attributions credit controllable
factors (effort, strategy). Unhealthy attributions credit uncontrollable factors
(ability, luck, task difficulty).

Practical implications:
- Praise effort and strategy, not ability ("You used a great approach" vs.
  "You're so smart")
- Normalize struggle as part of learning
- Teach growth mindset (Dweck, 2006): intelligence is malleable, not fixed
- After failures, help students identify what they can change (study strategy,
  time allocation, help-seeking) rather than what they cannot

### Learning Analytics and Educational Data

Learning analytics is the measurement, collection, analysis, and reporting
of data about learners and their contexts to understand and optimize learning.
Key applications:

1. **Early warning systems.** Identify students at risk of failure based on
   behavioral indicators: login frequency, assignment completion, grade trends,
   engagement patterns. Intervene early.
2. **Adaptive learning paths.** Use performance data to adjust the difficulty,
   pace, and content of instruction for individual learners. Effective adaptive
   systems reduce time to mastery by 20-50%.
3. **Course improvement.** Analyze which content, activities, and assessments
   produce the best learning outcomes. Identify bottlenecks where students
   consistently struggle. A/B test instructional approaches.
4. **Assessment analytics.** Item analysis (difficulty index, discrimination
   index, distractor analysis) to improve assessment quality. Learning curve
   analysis to understand skill acquisition patterns.

**Key metrics:**
- Completion rate (% of enrolled students who finish)
- Time on task (actual vs. estimated)
- Assessment performance distribution (identify bimodal distributions that
  suggest prerequisite gaps)
- Engagement rate (meaningful interaction, not just page views)
- Learning gain (pre-test to post-test improvement)
- Transfer performance (performance on novel problems)
- Retention (performance on delayed assessments)

### AI in Education

Current evidence-based applications of AI in education:

1. **Intelligent tutoring systems.** Provide individualized practice and
   feedback at scale. Most effective for well-defined domains (math, language
   learning, programming). Effect sizes range from d=0.20 to d=0.76 depending
   on implementation quality.
2. **Automated feedback.** AI-powered writing feedback tools can provide
   instant feedback on grammar, structure, and argumentation. Supplements
   (does not replace) human feedback on higher-order thinking.
3. **Content recommendation.** Adaptive systems that recommend next learning
   activities based on student performance and learning path optimization.
4. **Assessment generation.** AI can generate practice items, which still
   require human review for quality, bias, and alignment.
5. **Learning analytics.** AI-powered pattern recognition in student data
   for early warning and personalization.

**Cautions:**
- AI cannot replace the human relationship in teaching. Teacher-student
  relationship is one of the highest-impact factors in education (Hattie,
  d=0.72).
- AI systems can perpetuate and amplify existing biases in educational data.
- Privacy concerns are significant, especially with student data.
- The evidence base for most AI education products is thin. Demand rigorous,
  independent evaluation before adoption.
- AI-generated content must be reviewed by subject matter experts and
  pedagogical experts before use with students.

### Special Education Fundamentals

Core principles for inclusive educational design:

1. **Least Restrictive Environment (LRE).** Students with disabilities should
   be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate. Design
   general education instruction to be as accessible as possible from the start
   (UDL) to reduce the need for separate settings.
2. **Individualized Education Program (IEP).** Students with identified
   disabilities have legal right to individualized goals, services, and
   accommodations. Understand the difference between accommodations (change
   how the student accesses content) and modifications (change what the student
   is expected to learn).
3. **Response to Intervention (RTI) / Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS).**
   Three-tier model: Tier 1 (high-quality core instruction for all), Tier 2
   (targeted small-group intervention for students who struggle), Tier 3
   (intensive individualized intervention). Data-driven decisions at each tier.
4. **Common accommodations and their rationale:**
   - Extended time: reduces time pressure so assessment measures knowledge, not speed
   - Preferential seating: reduces environmental distractions
   - Text-to-speech: provides access for students with reading disabilities
   - Reduced distraction setting: manages environmental interference
   - Graphic organizers: supports executive function and organization
   - Chunked assignments: manages cognitive and executive function demands
   - Alternative assessments: provides alternate ways to demonstrate understanding

### Educational Equity and Access

Equity in education means that every student receives what they need to succeed.
This is different from equality, which gives every student the same thing. Key
considerations:

1. **Opportunity gaps.** Differences in educational outcomes often reflect
   differences in educational opportunities, not differences in student ability.
   Examine: access to qualified teachers, access to rigorous curriculum, access
   to technology, access to support services.
2. **Culturally responsive teaching** (Gay, 2010. Ladson-Billings, 1995). Use
   the cultural knowledge, experiences, and frames of reference of diverse
   students to make learning more effective. This means: diverse texts and
   examples, connection to community knowledge, high expectations for all
   students, validation of multiple ways of knowing.
3. **Asset-based framing.** Focus on what students bring to learning (their
   languages, cultures, experiences, strengths) rather than what they lack.
   Deficit thinking ("these students can't...") undermines expectations and
   creates self-fulfilling prophecies.
4. **Digital equity.** Not all students have equal access to technology and
   internet. Design for the minimum available technology. Provide offline
   alternatives. Consider mobile-first design.
5. **Linguistic diversity.** Support English language learners with: visual
   supports, bilingual glossaries, sentence frames, cooperative learning
   structures, and content-based language instruction. Value students' home
   languages as assets.

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge): Education-Specific Guidance

**Questions to ask:**
- Who are the learners? (age, prior knowledge, motivation, constraints, diversity)
- What should learners be able to DO after this learning experience that they
  cannot do now? (force a behavioral outcome, not a topic)
- What context will learners use this knowledge/skill in? (defines transfer goals)
- How will we know if they learned it? (forces assessment thinking early)
- What have previous attempts to teach this looked like? (learn from prior efforts)
- What resources and constraints exist? (time, technology, budget, class size,
  mandated curriculum, policy requirements)
- What standards or competencies must this align to?
- Who are the stakeholders? (students, parents, administrators, accreditors, employers)

**Patterns to look for:**
- Is this a knowledge problem (students don't know it), a skill problem (students
  can't do it), or a motivation problem (students won't do it)? Each requires
  different interventions.
- Is the learning objective at the right Bloom's level? Often, stakeholders ask
  for "understanding" when they actually need "application" or "analysis."
- Are there prerequisite gaps? Sometimes the problem is not the current content
  but missing foundations.
- Is the real problem instructional design or something outside education's
  control (poverty, trauma, systemic inequity)?

### Stage 2 (Design Approach): Education-Specific Guidance

**Framework selection guide:**
- "Design a course" -> Backward Design + Bloom's + Cognitive Load Management
- "Improve student achievement" -> Formative Assessment Cycle + Spaced Retrieval
  Practice + Rosenshine's Principles
- "Make learning more engaging" -> Engagement Strategy Framework + Self-Determination
  Theory
- "Design an assessment" -> Rubric Design Framework + ABCD Objectives + Alignment Audit
- "Differentiate instruction" -> Differentiation Planning Framework + UDL
- "Build an online course" -> Online Course Design Framework + Cognitive Load Management
- "Train teachers" -> Andragogy principles + Rosenshine (model what you preach)
- "Evaluate an EdTech tool" -> Technology Integration Decision Framework +
  Cognitive Load Audit
- "Design curriculum" -> Backward Design + Scope/Sequence + Vertical Alignment

**Non-obvious moves:**
- Challenge the assumed learner profile. Instructors often overestimate their
  students' prior knowledge. Always verify with pre-assessment data.
- Look for the "curse of knowledge." Experts forget what it was like to not
  know something. Content that seems simple to the designer may be genuinely
  difficult for the learner.
- Check for the "coverage trap." Most curricula try to cover too much.
  Deeper coverage of fewer topics produces better outcomes than shallow
  coverage of many topics.
- Ask whether the problem is really instructional. Sometimes what looks like
  a teaching problem is actually a hiring problem, a management problem, or
  a systems problem. Corporate training cannot fix a broken process.

### Stage 3 (Structure Engagement): Education-Specific Guidance

**Typical engagement structure:**
- **Analysis phase** (Stage 1-2): 25% -- understand learners, context, constraints
- **Design phase** (Stage 3-4): 40% -- build the curriculum, assessments, materials
- **Review phase** (Stage 5-6): 20% -- validate alignment, pilot test, revise
- **Implementation phase** (Stage 7-8): 15% -- deliver, monitor, iterate

**Common deliverable types:**
- Lesson plans (individual lessons)
- Unit plans (multi-lesson sequences)
- Curriculum maps / scope and sequence documents
- Assessment instruments (tests, rubrics, performance tasks)
- Professional development workshop designs
- EdTech integration plans
- Learning experience designs (for online/blended)
- Training needs analyses
- Program evaluation plans
- Student intervention plans

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables): Education-Specific Guidance

**Design principles:**
- Objectives before activities. Always. If you cannot name the objective, do
  not design the activity.
- Every assessment item maps to a specific objective at a specific Bloom's level.
  Create the alignment table first.
- Instruction prepares students for exactly what the assessment asks them to do.
  No surprises.
- Build retrieval practice and spacing into the design from the start. Do not
  treat review as an afterthought.
- Include formative assessment at least every 15 minutes of instruction.
- Design for the struggling learner first, then add extension for advanced
  learners. This is more efficient than designing for the middle and
  differentiating in both directions.
- Time estimates must be realistic. Better to cover less content well than to
  rush through more content poorly.
- Include transitions, management routines, and "what if" contingencies.
  A lesson plan that assumes everything goes perfectly is not a plan.

**Content standards:**
- All claims about effective teaching must cite research
- All learning objectives must be measurable (ABCD format)
- All assessments must have an alignment justification
- All instructional strategies must be evidence-based
- All multimedia must follow Mayer's principles
- All designs must address accessibility (UDL as minimum)

### Stage 5 (Quality Assurance): Education-Specific Review Criteria

In addition to the universal review checklist:
- [ ] Every learning objective is measurable with appropriate Bloom's verb
- [ ] Assessment-objective alignment is documented and verified
- [ ] Instruction-assessment alignment is documented and verified
- [ ] Cognitive load is appropriate for the target population
- [ ] Formative assessment occurs at least every 15 minutes
- [ ] Retrieval practice and spacing are built into the design
- [ ] UDL principles are applied (multiple means of engagement, representation,
      action/expression)
- [ ] Differentiation strategies are specified for diverse learners
- [ ] Time estimates are realistic (benchmarked or piloted)
- [ ] Materials are accessible (WCAG 2.1 AA for digital, readability for print)
- [ ] Research citations support instructional method choices
- [ ] Prior knowledge assumptions are explicit and checked
- [ ] Transfer to real-world contexts is addressed
- [ ] Cultural responsiveness is evident in examples and materials

### Stage 6 (Validate): Education-Specific Validation

Apply these validation methods:
1. **Alignment Audit** -- for all deliverables (mandatory)
2. **Cognitive Load Audit** -- for multimedia and complex content (mandatory
   for Tier 2+)
3. **Expert Review** -- for high-stakes designs (mandatory for Tier 3)
4. **Learner Perspective Test** -- for all designs (walk-through minimum.
   Pilot with actual learners for Tier 3)
5. **Accessibility and Equity Review** -- for all designs (mandatory)

Minimum for Tier 2: Methods 1 + 2 + 5
Full suite for Tier 3: All five methods

### Stage 7 (Plan Delivery): Education-Specific Delivery

**Delivery format guidance:**
- For teachers/instructors: Lesson plans with all materials, rubrics, and
  answer keys. Include "teacher notes" that explain the pedagogical rationale
  behind design decisions.
- For administrators: Executive summary with objectives, alignment to standards,
  expected outcomes, resource requirements, and evaluation plan.
- For curriculum committees: Full scope and sequence with alignment documentation,
  assessment blueprint, and implementation timeline.
- For corporate clients: Training plan with business objectives, ROI estimate,
  delivery schedule, and Kirkpatrick evaluation plan.
- For EdTech teams: Product requirements with learning science rationale, user
  stories, testable hypotheses, and success metrics.

**Always include:**
- Clear implementation instructions (who does what, when, with what)
- Materials list and preparation requirements
- Assessment instruments and scoring guides
- Differentiation and accommodation notes
- "Quick start" guide for the first implementation

### Stage 8 (Deliver): Education-Specific Follow-up

**After delivery:**
- Plan for formative evaluation during initial implementation. Observe, collect
  student data, gather teacher/facilitator feedback.
- Identify which elements need revision based on implementation data.
- Schedule a review cycle: after first implementation, after first full term,
  annually thereafter.
- Build a community of practice around the design if multiple implementers
  are involved.
- Track student outcomes against the learning objectives. Did students meet
  the objectives? If not, where did the design fail?
- Document lessons learned for the next iteration.
- Offer coaching support for instructors implementing the design for the
  first time.
- For corporate training: measure transfer to job performance at 30, 60,
  and 90 days post-training (Kirkpatrick Level 3).

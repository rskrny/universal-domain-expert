## Exercise Physiology Foundations

Understanding these systems prevents programming errors that waste months of training.

### Energy Systems

The body produces ATP through three pathways. Every physical activity uses all three.
The dominant system depends on intensity and duration.

**Phosphagen System (ATP-CP)**
- Duration: 0-10 seconds of maximal effort
- Fuel: Stored ATP and creatine phosphate in muscle cells
- Recovery: 2-5 minutes for full CP resaturation
- Training applications: Max-effort lifts, sprints under 10 seconds, explosive jumps
- Programming implication: Rest periods of 3-5 minutes between maximal sets. Short
  rest periods compromise this system and reduce peak force output.

**Glycolytic System (Anaerobic)**
- Duration: 10 seconds to ~2 minutes of high-intensity effort
- Fuel: Glucose from blood and muscle glycogen
- Byproduct: Lactate and hydrogen ions (the "burn")
- Training applications: Hypertrophy training (moderate loads, higher reps), 400m sprints,
  high-intensity intervals
- Programming implication: Rest periods of 60-120 seconds for hypertrophy work. This
  system is trainable and responds to repeated exposure at threshold intensities.

**Oxidative System (Aerobic)**
- Duration: Activities lasting longer than ~2 minutes
- Fuel: Fatty acids, glucose, and (minimally) amino acids via oxygen-dependent pathways
- Training applications: Distance running, cycling, swimming, general health cardio
- Programming implication: Aerobic base training at low-to-moderate intensity (Zone 2)
  builds mitochondrial density, capillary networks, and cardiac stroke volume. This
  foundation supports all other training.

### Muscle Fiber Types

**Type I (Slow-Twitch)**
- Characteristics: High fatigue resistance, lower force output, high mitochondrial density
- Dominant in: Postural muscles, endurance activities
- Training response: Best developed with higher reps (15+), sustained contractions,
  and endurance work
- Individual variation: Some people have 70%+ Type I fibers. These individuals recover
  faster between sets and may respond better to higher training frequencies and volumes.

**Type IIa (Fast-Twitch Oxidative)**
- Characteristics: Moderate force output, moderate fatigue resistance
- Plasticity: These fibers can shift toward either Type I or Type IIx characteristics
  depending on training stimulus
- Training response: Responds to moderate-to-heavy loads (6-12 rep range)

**Type IIx (Fast-Twitch Glycolytic)**
- Characteristics: Highest force output, lowest fatigue resistance
- Training response: Best developed with heavy loads (1-5 reps), explosive movements,
  and plyometrics
- Detraining effect: Untrained individuals have high Type IIx proportions. These convert
  to Type IIa with consistent training. Detraining converts them back.

### Cardiovascular Adaptations to Training

**Central adaptations (heart and blood):**
- Increased left ventricular chamber size (eccentric hypertrophy with endurance training)
- Increased stroke volume (more blood per heartbeat)
- Decreased resting heart rate (more efficient heart)
- Increased blood plasma volume (within days of starting training)
- Increased red blood cell mass (weeks to months)

**Peripheral adaptations (muscles and capillaries):**
- Increased capillary density around muscle fibers
- Increased mitochondrial density and size
- Improved oxygen extraction (wider arteriovenous oxygen difference)
- Increased oxidative enzyme activity

**Programming implication:** Cardiovascular adaptations require sustained Zone 2 training
(60-70% max heart rate) for 150+ minutes per week. The heart adapts to volume. The
muscle capillary bed adapts to repeated moderate-intensity exposure. High-intensity
interval training improves VO2max faster but builds less aerobic base.

### Hormonal Responses to Training

**Testosterone:**
- Acute elevation during resistance training (especially with large muscle groups,
  moderate-to-heavy loads, short rest periods)
- Chronic elevation with consistent training is modest in natural lifters
- Sleep, stress, nutrition, and body fat percentage have larger effects on baseline
  testosterone than any training variable
- Practical implication: Chasing "testosterone-boosting workouts" produces trivial
  hormonal changes. Focus on progressive overload and recovery instead.

**Growth Hormone:**
- Large acute spikes during metabolic stress (short rest, higher reps, supersets)
- Acute GH elevation does not meaningfully predict hypertrophy
- Sleep is the primary driver of GH release. 7-9 hours per night matters more than
  any training protocol for GH output.

**Cortisol:**
- Elevated during high-volume, high-intensity training
- Chronically elevated cortisol (from overtraining, poor sleep, or life stress) impairs
  recovery and promotes fat storage, especially visceral fat
- Practical implication: Managing total stress load (training + life) matters more than
  any single workout variable

**Insulin:**
- Primary regulator of nutrient storage
- Post-workout insulin sensitivity is elevated in trained muscle
- Practical implication: Timing carbohydrates around training takes advantage of
  enhanced nutrient partitioning. This effect is modest for general fitness goals
  and more meaningful for competitive athletes training twice daily.

## Core Frameworks

> These frameworks are tools for making better decisions about training and nutrition.

### Framework 1: Progressive Overload Framework

**What:** The foundational principle of all training adaptation. The body adapts to
stress. To continue adapting, the stress must progressively increase over time.

**When to use:** Every training program. This is the single most important principle
in exercise science. Programs that lack progressive overload produce results for
2-4 weeks and then stall.

**How to apply:**

Progressive overload has five levers. Pull them in this priority order:

1. **Volume** -- Add sets or reps within a mesocycle. Example: Week 1 = 3x8,
   Week 2 = 3x9, Week 3 = 3x10, Week 4 = 4x8.
2. **Intensity (load)** -- Add weight to the bar. The classic approach. Add the
   smallest increment available (microplates help). 2.5-5 lbs per session for upper
   body, 5-10 lbs for lower body (beginners). Advanced lifters may add weight
   weekly or monthly.
3. **Frequency** -- Train a muscle group more often per week. Moving from 1x/week
   to 2x/week per muscle group is one of the most effective overload strategies,
   especially for intermediates.
4. **Density** -- Same work in less time. Reduce rest periods. This lever is best
   for conditioning and muscular endurance goals. Use it cautiously for pure strength
   work where rest affects performance.
5. **Effort (proximity to failure)** -- Train closer to muscular failure. Going from
   3 reps in reserve (RIR) to 1 RIR increases stimulus without changing load or volume.

**Priority of overload variables by goal:**
- Strength: Intensity (load) > Volume > Frequency > Effort > Density
- Hypertrophy: Volume > Effort > Frequency > Intensity > Density
- Endurance: Volume (duration) > Density > Frequency > Intensity > Effort

**Common misapplication:** Adding weight every session forever. This works for the first
3-6 months (novice linear progression). After that, overload must be periodized. Trying
to add weight linearly past the novice stage leads to form breakdown, injury, and
frustration. The fix is structured periodization (see Framework 2).

### Framework 2: Periodization Models

**What:** Systematic variation of training variables over time to manage fatigue,
prevent plateaus, and peak for specific goals. Periodization is the antidote to
random training.

**When to use:** Any program lasting longer than 4 weeks. Short-term programs can
run straight progressive overload. Anything beyond novice linear progression needs
periodization.

**Model A: Linear Periodization (LP)**

Structure: Phases of decreasing volume and increasing intensity over 12-16 weeks.

| Phase | Duration | Sets x Reps | Intensity (%1RM) | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------------|------------------|-------|
| Hypertrophy | 4-6 weeks | 3-4 x 10-12 | 65-75% | Muscle size, work capacity |
| Strength | 4-6 weeks | 3-5 x 4-6 | 80-87% | Maximal strength |
| Peaking | 2-3 weeks | 2-3 x 1-3 | 90-100% | 1RM performance |
| Deload | 1 week | 2 x 8-10 | 50-60% | Recovery |

Best for: Beginners and intermediate lifters. Powerlifters preparing for a meet.
Athletes with a clear competition date.

Limitation: Qualities trained early (hypertrophy) detrain while you train later
qualities (strength). Maintenance volume helps (2 sets per muscle group per week
maintains gains for 6-8 weeks).

**Model B: Undulating Periodization (UP)**

Structure: Vary rep ranges within the same week.

| Day | Sets x Reps | Intensity | Focus |
|-----|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Monday | 4 x 3-5 | 85-90% | Strength |
| Wednesday | 3 x 8-12 | 70-80% | Hypertrophy |
| Friday | 3 x 12-15 | 60-70% | Muscular endurance / Volume |

Best for: Intermediate to advanced lifters. People who get bored with linear phases.
General fitness goals where you want to develop multiple qualities simultaneously.

Research finding: Undulating periodization produces similar or slightly better
hypertrophy and strength outcomes compared to linear periodization in most studies.
The advantage may come from higher adherence (more variety) and concurrent
development of multiple qualities.

**Model C: Block Periodization**

Structure: Concentrated blocks (2-4 weeks each) focusing on one primary quality.
Only 1-2 qualities are trained per block. Others receive maintenance volume.

| Block | Duration | Primary Focus | Maintenance |
|-------|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Accumulation | 3-4 weeks | Volume / Hypertrophy (high volume, moderate intensity) | Strength (2 heavy sets/week) |
| Transmutation | 3-4 weeks | Strength (moderate volume, high intensity) | Hypertrophy (2 sets/week) |
| Realization | 1-2 weeks | Peaking (low volume, maximal intensity) | None |

Best for: Advanced lifters. Competitive athletes. Situations where multiple qualities
can't be developed simultaneously because the athlete is too advanced for broad
adaptation.

**Common misapplication:** Using block periodization for beginners or early intermediates.
These lifters respond to everything. They don't need concentrated loading blocks. Linear
or undulating models produce faster progress with less complexity for this population.

### Framework 3: Energy Balance Framework (TDEE)

**What:** The fundamental equation governing body weight change. All diets work
through energy balance, regardless of their marketing angle.

**The equation:**
- Energy in (calories consumed) vs. Energy out (total daily energy expenditure)
- TDEE = BMR + TEF + NEAT + EAT

**Components of energy expenditure:**

| Component | % of TDEE | What It Is | Modifiable? |
|-----------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) | 60-70% | Energy to keep you alive at rest | Slightly (via muscle mass) |
| TEF (Thermic Effect of Food) | 8-15% | Energy to digest food | Slightly (protein has highest TEF at 20-30%) |
| NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) | 15-30% | Fidgeting, walking, standing, daily movement | Highly modifiable |
| EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis) | 5-10% | Intentional exercise | Modifiable but small relative to total |

**How to estimate TDEE:**

Step 1: Estimate BMR using Mifflin-St Jeor (most accurate validated equation):
- Males: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) + 5
- Females: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) - 161

Step 2: Multiply by activity factor:
- Sedentary (desk job, minimal exercise): BMR x 1.2
- Lightly active (1-3 days/week light exercise): BMR x 1.375
- Moderately active (3-5 days/week moderate exercise): BMR x 1.55
- Very active (6-7 days/week hard exercise): BMR x 1.725
- Extremely active (physical job + hard training): BMR x 1.9

Step 3: Track actual weight change over 2-3 weeks. Adjust caloric intake based on
real-world results. Equations are starting points. Actual TDEE varies 15-20%
between individuals due to NEAT, hormonal differences, and gut microbiome variation.

**Caloric targets by goal:**

| Goal | Daily Caloric Target | Expected Rate of Change |
|------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Fat loss (moderate) | TDEE - 300 to 500 kcal | 0.5-1.0 lb/week |
| Fat loss (aggressive) | TDEE - 500 to 750 kcal | 1.0-1.5 lb/week |
| Fat loss (very aggressive) | TDEE - 750 to 1000 kcal | 1.5-2.0 lb/week (muscle loss risk) |
| Maintenance | TDEE +/- 100 kcal | Weight stable |
| Lean bulk (muscle gain) | TDEE + 200 to 300 kcal | 0.5-1.0 lb/month |
| Standard bulk | TDEE + 300 to 500 kcal | 2-4 lbs/month (some fat gain) |

**Common misapplication:** Setting calories too low. A 500-calorie daily diet is
metabolically destructive. Minimum recommended intakes are 1200 kcal/day for
sedentary females and 1500 kcal/day for sedentary males, and these floors are higher
for active individuals. Aggressive deficits accelerate muscle loss, tank metabolic
rate through adaptive thermogenesis, and increase binge eating risk.

### Framework 4: Macronutrient Distribution Framework

**What:** A system for allocating daily calories across protein, carbohydrates, and
fat based on goal, training type, and individual factors.

**Step 1: Set protein first (the anchor macro)**

| Population | Protein Target (g/kg body weight/day) | Protein Target (g/lb/day) |
|------------|--------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Sedentary adult | 0.8-1.0 g/kg | 0.36-0.45 g/lb |
| Recreational exerciser | 1.2-1.6 g/kg | 0.55-0.73 g/lb |
| Resistance training (muscle gain) | 1.6-2.2 g/kg | 0.73-1.0 g/lb |
| Resistance training (fat loss) | 2.0-2.4 g/kg | 0.91-1.1 g/lb |
| Endurance athlete | 1.2-1.8 g/kg | 0.55-0.82 g/lb |
| Lean individual in aggressive deficit | 2.4-3.1 g/kg | 1.1-1.4 g/lb |

Why protein is higher during fat loss: Caloric deficit increases protein oxidation.
Higher protein intake preserves lean mass, increases satiety, and has the highest
thermic effect (20-30% of protein calories are burned during digestion).

**Step 2: Set fat (minimum threshold)**

Minimum fat intake: 0.5 g/kg body weight/day (roughly 20-25% of calories for most
people). Going below this threshold impairs hormone production (testosterone, estrogen),
fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), and cell membrane integrity.

Recommended range: 25-35% of total calories for most goals. Athletes in heavy training
may go as low as 20%. Those on ketogenic diets go to 60-75%.

**Step 3: Fill remaining calories with carbohydrates**

After protein and fat are set, remaining calories come from carbohydrates. This is
the most flexible macro. Carb needs vary dramatically by training volume and type.

| Training Type | Carb Target (g/kg/day) |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Light training / rest days | 2-3 g/kg |
| Moderate training (1 hr/day) | 3-5 g/kg |
| High volume training (1-3 hr/day) | 5-8 g/kg |
| Extreme volume (3-6 hr/day, endurance athletes) | 8-12 g/kg |

**Example calculation for a 180 lb (82 kg) male, resistance training for hypertrophy,
moderate surplus:**
- TDEE: ~2800 kcal. Surplus target: 3100 kcal.
- Protein: 2.0 g/kg = 164g = 656 kcal
- Fat: 0.8 g/kg = 66g = 594 kcal
- Carbs: Remaining = 3100 - 656 - 594 = 1850 kcal = 462g
- Final split: 164P / 66F / 462C (21% / 19% / 60%)

**Common misapplication:** Obsessing over exact macronutrient ratios while ignoring
total calorie intake. Calories determine weight change. Macros determine body
composition within that weight change. Getting calories right matters more than
perfecting your macro split to the gram.

### Framework 5: Training Volume Landmarks (MEV, MAV, MRV)

**What:** A system developed by Dr. Mike Israetel for determining how much training
volume each muscle group needs. Volume is the primary driver of hypertrophy for
trained lifters.

**The landmarks:**

| Landmark | Definition | Sets/Muscle/Week (typical range) |
|----------|------------|--------------------------------|
| MV (Maintenance Volume) | Minimum to maintain current muscle mass | 4-8 sets |
| MEV (Minimum Effective Volume) | Minimum to produce measurable growth | 8-12 sets |
| MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume) | Sweet spot where most growth happens | 12-20 sets |
| MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume) | Most volume you can recover from | 20-30 sets |

**Approximate volume landmarks by muscle group (intermediate lifters):**

| Muscle Group | MV | MEV | MAV | MRV |
|-------------|----|----|-----|-----|
| Chest | 4-6 | 8-10 | 12-18 | 22-26 |
| Back (width) | 4-6 | 8-10 | 14-20 | 24-28 |
| Shoulders (side delts) | 4-6 | 8-10 | 16-22 | 26-30 |
| Quads | 4-6 | 8-10 | 12-18 | 20-24 |
| Hamstrings | 3-4 | 6-8 | 10-14 | 16-20 |
| Biceps | 4-6 | 8-10 | 14-20 | 22-26 |
| Triceps | 4-6 | 6-8 | 10-14 | 18-22 |
| Glutes | 0-2 | 4-8 | 10-16 | 18-22 |
| Calves | 4-6 | 8-10 | 12-16 | 20-24 |
| Abs | 0-2 | 4-6 | 8-14 | 18-22 |

**How to use these landmarks:**

1. Start a mesocycle at or near MEV
2. Add 1-3 sets per muscle group per week across the mesocycle
3. Never exceed MRV. Signs of exceeding MRV: persistent soreness beyond 72 hours,
   declining performance across sessions, sleep disruption, mood changes, joint pain
4. After 4-6 weeks (or when approaching MRV), take a deload week at MV
5. Start the next mesocycle at a slightly higher MEV than the previous one

**Individual variation is enormous.** These numbers are population averages. A lifter
with favorable muscle fiber composition, low life stress, great sleep, and perfect
nutrition may tolerate 30+ sets per muscle group. A stressed entrepreneur sleeping
5 hours per night may not recover from 15 sets. Track your own response.

**Common misapplication:** Training every muscle group at MRV from day one. This creates
systemic fatigue that tanks recovery across the board. Start low, build up, deload, repeat.
Volume should be earned, not assumed.

### Framework 6: Recovery Framework

**What:** A system for ensuring training stimulus translates into actual adaptation.
Training creates the signal. Recovery creates the adaptation. Without adequate recovery,
more training produces worse results.

**The four pillars of recovery:**

**Pillar 1: Sleep (the single most important recovery variable)**
- Target: 7-9 hours per night. 8+ hours for hard-training athletes.
- Sleep quality markers: Fall asleep within 15-20 minutes. Minimal nighttime waking.
  Wake feeling rested without alarm (when possible).
- Sleep architecture: Deep sleep (Stages 3-4) is when GH peaks and tissue repair
  occurs. REM sleep is when motor skill consolidation happens. Both require adequate
  sleep duration.
- Practical interventions:
  - Consistent sleep/wake times (even weekends). The circadian rhythm rewards consistency.
  - Cool room temperature: 65-68 F (18-20 C)
  - Dark room: blackout curtains or sleep mask
  - No screens 30-60 minutes before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
  - No caffeine after 2 PM (caffeine half-life is 5-6 hours)
  - Magnesium glycinate (200-400mg) before bed may improve sleep quality
  - Alcohol disrupts deep sleep and REM even in moderate amounts

**Pillar 2: Nutrition for Recovery**
- Post-workout protein: 20-40g within 2-3 hours of training. The "anabolic window"
  is real but much wider than the 30-minute myth. Total daily protein matters more
  than timing. That said, distributing protein across 3-5 meals (each containing
  20-40g) maximizes muscle protein synthesis.
- Post-workout carbohydrates: Replenish glycogen. Matters most for athletes training
  twice daily or in endurance sports. For once-daily training, total daily carb
  intake handles glycogen replenishment by the next session.
- Anti-inflammatory foods: Omega-3 fatty acids, berries, tart cherry juice, turmeric.
  These may modestly reduce DOMS (delayed onset muscle soreness). Don't overdo anti-
  inflammatories. Some inflammation is the signal that drives adaptation.

**Pillar 3: Stress Management**
- Training is a stressor. Life is a stressor. The body has one stress response system
  (HPA axis). They compete for the same recovery resources.
- High life stress = lower training volume tolerance. Reduce training volume during
  high-stress periods (work deadlines, family crisis, poor sleep stretches).
- Practical interventions: 10 minutes of meditation or deep breathing daily reduces
  cortisol measurably. Walking in nature (even 20 minutes) lowers cortisol. Social
  connection supports recovery. Isolation increases stress hormones.

**Pillar 4: Deload Protocols**
- Frequency: Every 4-8 weeks depending on training intensity and individual recovery
  capacity. Beginners can go 6-8 weeks. Advanced lifters often need deloads every 3-4
  weeks.
- Methods:
  - Volume deload: Cut sets by 40-60%, keep intensity the same
  - Intensity deload: Cut weight by 40-50%, keep volume the same
  - Full deload: Cut both volume and intensity by 50%
- The best deload method depends on what's creating the most fatigue. Joint pain?
  Reduce intensity. Systemic fatigue? Reduce volume.

**Common misapplication:** Treating deloads as optional or as a sign of weakness.
Deloads are where adaptation consolidates. Skipping them leads to overreaching,
plateau, and eventually overtraining syndrome (which can take months to recover from).

### Framework 7: Goal-Specific Programming Framework

**What:** A decision system for building training programs optimized for specific outcomes.

**Strength Programming:**
- Rep range: 1-5 reps (primary movements), 6-8 reps (accessories)
- Intensity: 80-100% 1RM
- Rest periods: 3-5 minutes between heavy sets
- Frequency: Each major lift 2-3x/week
- Key principle: Strength is a neuromuscular skill. It requires heavy practice with
  full recovery between sets.
- Example structure: 4 days/week. Upper/Lower split. Squat, bench, deadlift, overhead
  press as primary movements. 3-5 sets of 3-5 reps at 80-87% 1RM. Accessories at
  3 x 8-12.

**Hypertrophy Programming:**
- Rep range: 6-12 reps (primary), 12-20 reps (isolation)
- Intensity: 60-80% 1RM
- Rest periods: 60-120 seconds (creates metabolic stress alongside mechanical tension)
- Frequency: Each muscle group 2-3x/week
- Key principle: Hypertrophy is driven by mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and
  muscle damage. Volume near MAV at moderate intensity with controlled eccentrics
  produces the most growth per unit of training time.
- Example structure: 5-6 days/week. Push/Pull/Legs split or Upper/Lower split.
  16-22 sets per muscle group per week. Controlled tempo (2-3 second eccentrics).

**Endurance Programming:**
- Training zones (based on heart rate or perceived exertion):

| Zone | % Max HR | Perceived Effort | Primary Adaptation |
|------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1 (Recovery) | 50-60% | Very easy, conversational | Active recovery |
| 2 (Aerobic Base) | 60-70% | Comfortable, can talk in sentences | Mitochondrial density, fat oxidation |
| 3 (Tempo) | 70-80% | Comfortably hard, short sentences | Lactate clearance, aerobic capacity |
| 4 (Threshold) | 80-90% | Hard, a few words at a time | Lactate threshold, VO2max |
| 5 (VO2max) | 90-100% | Maximal effort | VO2max ceiling |

- Polarized distribution: 80% of training in Zones 1-2, 20% in Zones 4-5.
  This distribution consistently outperforms threshold-heavy approaches in research.
- Build aerobic base first: 4-8 weeks of Zone 2 work before adding high-intensity
  intervals. The aerobic system supports recovery between intervals. Without a
  base, intervals are less effective and injury risk increases.
- VO2max improvement: 4-6 intervals of 3-5 minutes at Zone 4-5 with equal rest.
  2x/week is sufficient for most athletes. More than 3x/week increases overtraining risk.

**Power Programming:**
- Rep range: 1-5 reps with explosive intent
- Intensity: 30-70% 1RM (moved as fast as possible) for power. 80-95% for maximal
  strength foundation.
- Rest periods: 3-5 minutes (full neural recovery)
- Key principle: Power = Force x Velocity. Train both ends. Heavy strength work
  builds force. Explosive work with moderate loads builds rate of force development.
- Movements: Olympic lifts (clean, snatch), plyometrics, loaded jumps, medicine ball
  throws, explosive push-ups

**General Fitness Programming:**
- Combination of resistance training (3x/week) and cardiovascular training (2-3x/week)
- Resistance: Full-body or Upper/Lower split. 2-3 sets per exercise. 8-15 rep range.
  Compound movements prioritized (squat, hinge, press, pull, carry).
- Cardio: 150+ minutes/week of Zone 2. Optional: 1-2 sessions of HIIT (20-30 min).
- Flexibility: 5-10 minutes post-workout stretching. Yoga or mobility work 1-2x/week.
- This template covers 80% of health outcomes for 90% of the population.

**Common misapplication:** Training for a goal with the wrong rep range and rest
periods. Doing 3 sets of 15 reps with 30 seconds rest will build muscular endurance.
It will do very little for maximal strength or hypertrophy. Match the stimulus to the goal.

### Framework 8: Supplement Evidence Tiers

**What:** A classification system for supplements based on the strength of scientific
evidence. The supplement industry is largely unregulated and makes claims far beyond
what evidence supports.

**Tier 1: Strong Evidence (well-supported by multiple meta-analyses and RCTs)**

| Supplement | Dose | Benefit | Notes |
|-----------|------|---------|-------|
| Creatine Monohydrate | 3-5g/day (no loading needed) | Strength, power, lean mass, cognitive function | Most studied supplement in sports nutrition. Safe long-term. No need for cycling. |
| Caffeine | 3-6 mg/kg body weight, 30-60 min pre-exercise | Endurance, strength, power, focus | Tolerance develops. Cycle off periodically. Avoid after 2 PM. |
| Protein Supplements (whey, casein, plant blends) | As needed to hit daily protein target | Convenient protein source | Whole food protein is equivalent. Supplements are a convenience tool. |
| Vitamin D | 1000-5000 IU/day (dose based on blood levels) | Bone health, immune function, mood, muscle function | Most people in northern latitudes are deficient. Get blood levels tested. |

**Tier 2: Moderate Evidence (supported by some RCTs, mechanism is plausible)**

| Supplement | Dose | Benefit | Notes |
|-----------|------|---------|-------|
| Beta-Alanine | 3-6g/day (split doses to avoid tingling) | Buffering capacity for high-rep work (8+ reps) | Takes 4+ weeks of daily use to saturate. Tingling (paresthesia) is harmless. |
| Citrulline Malate | 6-8g, 30-60 min pre-workout | Blood flow, endurance, reduced fatigue | Better absorption than arginine for nitric oxide production. |
| Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA) | 2-3g combined EPA+DHA daily | Inflammation reduction, joint health, cardiovascular health | Choose quality brands tested for heavy metals. |
| Magnesium | 200-400mg/day (glycinate or citrate) | Sleep quality, muscle function, stress reduction | Many athletes are deficient. Glycinate form is best tolerated. |
| Ashwagandha (KSM-66) | 300-600mg/day | Cortisol reduction, modest testosterone support, anxiety reduction | Research is promising but still emerging. Effects are modest. |

**Tier 3: Weak/Mixed Evidence (limited RCTs, inconsistent findings)**

| Supplement | Note |
|-----------|------|
| BCAAs | Unnecessary if total protein intake is adequate. BCAAs are already in complete protein. |
| Glutamine | No benefit for muscle growth in well-fed individuals. May help gut health. |
| Testosterone boosters (tribulus, fenugreek, D-aspartic acid) | Effects are negligible or clinically insignificant in healthy individuals. |
| Fat burners (synephrine, yohimbine) | Modest thermogenic effect. Side effects often outweigh benefits. |
| HMB | Small benefit for untrained individuals. No meaningful benefit for trained lifters. |

**Tier 4: No Evidence or Harmful**

| Supplement | Note |
|-----------|------|
| Most proprietary blends | Under-dosed ingredients hidden behind "proprietary blend" labels. |
| Detox/cleanse products | The liver and kidneys handle detoxification. These products do nothing useful. |
| Colloidal silver | No benefit. Can cause permanent skin discoloration (argyria). |
| High-dose vitamin megadosing (without deficiency) | Fat-soluble vitamins can accumulate to toxic levels. Water-soluble vitamins are simply excreted. |

**Common misapplication:** Spending $200/month on supplements while sleeping 5 hours per
night, eating 80g of protein, and training inconsistently. Supplements are the last 2-5%
of results. Nailing sleep, nutrition, training, and stress management gives the first 95%.

### Framework 9: Habit Formation Framework

**What:** A behavioral system for building sustainable health habits. Based on the work
of BJ Fogg (Tiny Habits), James Clear (Atomic Habits), and behavioral psychology research.

**The behavior equation:** Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt (B = MAP)

For a behavior to occur, three elements must converge:
1. Sufficient motivation to act
2. Sufficient ability to perform the action (it must be easy enough)
3. A prompt that triggers the action at the right moment

**How to apply for fitness/nutrition habits:**

**Step 1: Start absurdly small.**
The biggest mistake is starting with the ideal program. Start with something so easy
it feels almost silly. Two push-ups. One vegetable per day. A 5-minute walk.
Small wins build identity. Identity drives long-term behavior.

**Step 2: Anchor to existing habits.**
Use existing routines as prompts. "After I pour my morning coffee, I will do 5 push-ups."
"After I sit down for lunch, I will eat the protein first." Anchoring eliminates the need
for willpower because the prompt is automatic.

**Step 3: Make the desired behavior easy. Make the undesired behavior hard.**
- Gym bag packed the night before (reduces friction for exercise)
- Healthy food at eye level in the fridge (reduces friction for good nutrition)
- Remove junk food from the house (increases friction for bad nutrition)
- Lay out workout clothes before bed (reduces morning decision fatigue)

**Step 4: Track and celebrate.**
Track the behavior, not the outcome. Track "did I go to the gym?" rather than "did I lose
2 lbs this week?" Celebrate immediately after completing the behavior. The emotional
reward cements the neural pathway. Even a small fist pump works.

**Step 5: Design for consistency over intensity.**
Four 30-minute workouts per week for 52 weeks beats six 90-minute workouts per week for
8 weeks followed by burnout. Total yearly volume matters more than weekly peak volume.
The program you do consistently wins.

**The identity shift:** Long-term behavior change happens when "I'm trying to exercise"
becomes "I'm someone who exercises." Frame habits as identity statements. Every time you
complete the behavior, you're casting a vote for that identity.

**Common misapplication:** Relying on motivation. Motivation is unreliable. It fluctuates
with mood, sleep, stress, and season. Design systems that work even when motivation is
at zero. If the system requires motivation to function, it will fail within 6-12 weeks.

### Framework 10: Body Composition Assessment Methods

**What:** A framework for evaluating different methods of measuring body fat percentage
and lean mass, understanding their accuracy, and choosing the right method for the
situation.

| Method | Accuracy (% error) | Cost | Accessibility | Best For |
|--------|-------------------|------|---------------|----------|
| DEXA Scan | +/- 1-2% | $50-150 | Clinical facilities | Gold standard for tracking changes over time |
| Hydrostatic Weighing | +/- 1.5-2.5% | $30-75 | Universities, specialized facilities | Research settings |
| Bod Pod (ADP) | +/- 2-3% | $40-75 | Universities, specialized facilities | Non-invasive alternative to hydrostatic |
| Bioelectrical Impedance (BIA) | +/- 3-8% | $0-50 | Home scales, gyms | Trend tracking (use same conditions each time) |
| Skinfold Calipers | +/- 3-5% | $10-30 | Anywhere (requires skilled measurer) | Tracking changes with consistent measurer |
| Navy Method (circumference) | +/- 3-5% | Free | Anywhere (tape measure) | Free estimation when nothing else is available |
| Mirror / Photos | Subjective | Free | Anywhere | Progress photos are underrated. Take monthly. |

**Protocol for reliable tracking (any method):**
1. Measure at the same time of day (morning, fasted, after bathroom)
2. Same hydration status (dehydration inflates BF% on BIA devices)
3. Same conditions (time since last meal, workout, alcohol)
4. Track trends over 4+ weeks. Single measurements are noisy. Trends are signal.
5. Use the same device and operator each time

**Common misapplication:** Obsessing over the absolute number. A BIA scale saying 22%
body fat does not mean you are 22% body fat. It means the scale reads 22% under those
conditions. What matters is whether that number goes down over time under consistent
measurement conditions.

### Framework 11: Nutrition Periodization

**What:** Systematically varying caloric and macronutrient intake across training
phases, seasons, or competition schedules. The same person may need different
nutrition at different times of year.

**Phase-matched nutrition:**

| Training Phase | Nutrition Approach | Caloric Target | Protein | Carbs | Fat |
|---------------|-------------------|----------------|---------|-------|-----|
| Hypertrophy (gaining) | Caloric surplus | TDEE + 200-500 | 1.6-2.0 g/kg | 4-7 g/kg | 0.8-1.2 g/kg |
| Strength (peaking) | Maintenance or slight surplus | TDEE to TDEE + 200 | 1.8-2.2 g/kg | 3-5 g/kg | 0.8-1.0 g/kg |
| Fat loss (cutting) | Caloric deficit | TDEE - 300-750 | 2.0-2.8 g/kg | 2-4 g/kg | 0.5-0.8 g/kg |
| Maintenance | Energy balance | TDEE +/- 100 | 1.4-1.8 g/kg | 3-5 g/kg | 0.8-1.2 g/kg |
| Competition prep (bodybuilding) | Progressive deficit | TDEE - 300 to -1000 over 12-20 weeks | 2.4-3.1 g/kg | Reduced over time | Minimum 0.5 g/kg |
| Recovery / Reverse diet | Gradual surplus increase | TDEE + 50-100/week | 1.6-2.0 g/kg | Increased gradually | Increased gradually |

**Reverse dieting after a cut:**
After a prolonged deficit, metabolic adaptation means TDEE is lower than predicted.
Jumping straight to maintenance calories causes rapid fat regain. Instead, increase
calories by 50-100 per week (primarily from carbohydrates) until weight stabilizes.
This gives the metabolism time to upregulate NEAT, TEF, and hormonal output. A
reverse diet typically takes 4-8 weeks.

**Metabolic adaptation management:**
During extended deficits (8+ weeks), the body reduces TDEE through:
- Reduced NEAT (you move less unconsciously)
- Reduced TEF (lower total food = less energy to digest)
- Hormonal changes (lower thyroid output, lower leptin, higher ghrelin)
- Improved muscular efficiency (muscles do the same work with less energy)

Countermeasures:
- Diet breaks: 1-2 weeks at maintenance every 6-12 weeks of dieting
- Refeed days: 1-2 days per week at maintenance, with extra calories from carbs
- Step counting: Maintain 7000-10000 steps/day to prevent NEAT decline
- Slow the rate of loss: Losing 0.5%/week of body weight preserves more metabolic
  rate than losing 1%+/week

**Common misapplication:** Eating the same way year-round regardless of training phase.
Bulking nutrition during a cut wastes fat loss potential. Cutting nutrition during a
strength phase undermines performance and recovery.

### Framework 12: Injury Prevention and Return-to-Training

**What:** A framework for reducing injury risk during training and managing the
return to training after injury or extended layoff.

**Prevention principles:**
1. **Warm up properly.** 5-10 minutes of general cardio to raise core temperature,
   followed by dynamic stretches and movement prep targeting the joints and muscles
   used in that session. Static stretching before lifting reduces force output.
   Save static stretching for after the session.
2. **Progressive loading.** The 10% rule: increase weekly training volume (sets, reps,
   or load) by no more than 10% per week. Tendons and ligaments adapt slower than
   muscles (6-12 weeks vs. 2-4 weeks). Rapid load increases injure connective tissue.
3. **Movement quality before load.** Master the movement pattern with light weight
   before adding load. A heavy squat with poor mechanics compresses the spine
   asymmetrically and destroys knees. A light squat with perfect mechanics builds
   resilient joints.
4. **Balance push and pull.** For every set of pushing (bench press, overhead press),
   do 1-1.5 sets of pulling (rows, pull-ups). Anterior dominance creates shoulder
   impingement. Most gym-goers push too much and pull too little.
5. **Include unilateral work.** Single-leg and single-arm exercises expose and correct
   side-to-side imbalances before they cause injury. Bulgarian split squats, single-arm
   dumbbell rows, single-leg deadlifts.

**Return-to-training protocol after layoff:**

| Layoff Duration | Week 1 | Week 2 | Week 3 | Week 4 |
|----------------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| 1-2 weeks | 80% of previous volume/intensity | 90% | 100% | Normal |
| 2-4 weeks | 60% | 70% | 80% | 90% |
| 1-3 months | 50% | 60% | 70% | 80% |
| 3+ months | 40% (essentially restart as beginner) | 50% | 60% | 70% |

The fastest way to re-injure yourself is to return to pre-layoff training loads
immediately. Muscle memory accelerates strength return (4-8 weeks to reach previous
levels after a 3-month layoff). Be patient. The muscle comes back faster than the
connective tissue.

**When to see a professional (not optional):**
- Sharp, acute pain during a movement (as opposed to dull muscle fatigue)
- Joint swelling that persists more than 48 hours
- Numbness, tingling, or radiating pain
- Inability to bear weight or use a limb normally
- Pain that does not improve with 1-2 weeks of rest and load modification
- Any head injury or loss of consciousness

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type 1: Bulk vs. Cut vs. Recomp

**Consider:**
- Current body fat percentage (males: above 18% suggests cut first. Below 12% suggests
  bulk. 12-18% is the decision zone. Females: above 28% suggests cut. Below 20% suggests
  bulk.)
- Training age (less than 1 year of consistent training? Recomp is very achievable)
- Muscle mass relative to frame (undermuscled individuals benefit from bulking first)
- Timeline (competitions, events, aesthetic goals with deadlines)
- Psychological readiness (history of disordered eating? Avoid aggressive cuts)
- Season (many prefer bulking in fall/winter and cutting in spring)

**Default recommendation:** For most beginners and early intermediates, a slight caloric
surplus (lean bulk at TDEE + 200-300) with consistent resistance training produces the
best long-term results. Building muscle first increases BMR, makes future cuts easier,
and builds the physique foundation.

**Override conditions:**
- Body fat above 25% (males) or 35% (females): Cut first to a healthier baseline.
  Higher body fat impairs nutrient partitioning (more surplus goes to fat storage).
- Upcoming competition: Align nutrition phase with competition timeline.
- Medical indication: Physician-directed weight loss for metabolic health.

### Decision Type 2: Training Split Selection

**Consider:**
- Available training days per week
- Training experience level
- Recovery capacity (sleep, stress, age, nutrition quality)
- Equipment access
- Primary goal (strength, hypertrophy, endurance, general fitness)

**Recommendations by available training days:**

| Days/Week | Best Split | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 2 | Full Body | Hit every muscle group with compounds. 2-3 sets per exercise. |
| 3 | Full Body or Push/Pull/Legs | Full body preferred for beginners. PPL for intermediates. |
| 4 | Upper/Lower (x2) | Most popular and effective split for intermediates. |
| 5 | Upper/Lower/Push/Pull/Legs | Or PPLUL. Hits each muscle 2x/week with good variety. |
| 6 | Push/Pull/Legs (x2) | High frequency, high volume. For advanced lifters with excellent recovery. |

**Default recommendation:** Upper/Lower split, 4 days per week. It hits every muscle
group twice per week (optimal frequency for hypertrophy), allows adequate recovery,
fits most schedules, and works for beginners through advanced lifters.

**Override conditions:**
- Only 2 days available: Full body is the only option. Prioritize compound movements.
- Powerlifting focus: Squat/Bench/Deadlift frequency-based programming (Sheiko, GZCL).
- Competitive bodybuilder: Body-part specialization splits may make sense for bringing
  up lagging body parts.

### Decision Type 3: Cardio Type Selection

**Consider:**
- Primary goal (fat loss, cardiovascular health, endurance performance, muscle preservation)
- Current fitness level
- Available time
- Joint health and injury history
- Personal preference (adherence is the most important variable)

| Goal | Best Cardio Type | Frequency | Duration |
|------|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| Fat loss + muscle preservation | Low-intensity steady state (LISS), walking | 4-6x/week | 30-60 min |
| Cardiovascular health | Zone 2 work (cycling, jogging, swimming) | 3-5x/week | 30-45 min |
| VO2max improvement | HIIT intervals | 2-3x/week | 20-30 min |
| Endurance performance | Polarized (80% Zone 2, 20% Zone 4-5) | 5-7x/week | 45-120+ min |
| General fitness | Mix of LISS and moderate-intensity work | 3-4x/week | 20-40 min |

**Default recommendation:** Walking 7000-10000 steps per day plus 2-3 sessions of Zone 2
cardio (30 minutes each). This covers cardiovascular health, supports fat loss, improves
recovery between resistance training sessions, and has zero interference with muscle gain.

**Override conditions:**
- Competitive endurance athlete: Sport-specific cardio programming takes priority.
- Severe time constraints: HIIT provides the most cardiovascular benefit per minute.
  But it also creates the most systemic fatigue and can interfere with resistance training.
- Joint limitations: Cycling, swimming, or elliptical instead of running. Low-impact
  alternatives provide equivalent cardiovascular benefits without joint stress.

### Decision Type 4: Diet Approach Selection

**Consider:**
- Primary goal (fat loss, performance, health, muscle gain)
- Food preferences and cultural considerations
- Cooking skill and time available
- History with diets (what has worked, what has failed, any disordered eating patterns)
- Medical conditions (diabetes, heart disease, kidney disease, food allergies)
- Budget
- Adherence probability (the best diet is the one you can sustain)

**Analysis of popular diets:**

| Diet | Mechanism | Best For | Worst For | Sustainability |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Calorie counting / flexible dieting (IIFYM) | Direct energy balance control | Experienced dieters, data-driven people | Those prone to obsessive tracking, eating disorder risk | High (if tracking doesn't cause anxiety) |
| Mediterranean | Whole foods, healthy fats, moderate portions | General health, cardiovascular health, longevity | Rapid fat loss (no built-in deficit) | Very high |
| Intermittent Fasting (16:8, 20:4, OMAD) | Reduced eating window creates passive deficit | People who skip breakfast naturally, simplicity seekers | Heavy trainers needing peri-workout nutrition, those with blood sugar issues | Moderate to high |
| Ketogenic (under 50g carbs/day) | Ketosis, appetite suppression, insulin reduction | Epilepsy management, some individuals with insulin resistance | Endurance athletes, team sport athletes, those who love carbs | Low to moderate |
| Carnivore | Extreme elimination diet | Short-term elimination protocol, some autoimmune exploration | Long-term health (lacks fiber, phytonutrients), sustainability | Low |
| Vegan | Plant-based whole foods | Ethical motivation, environmental motivation, some health markers | Muscle gain without careful planning (lower protein density) | Moderate (high if ethically motivated) |
| Paleo | Whole foods, elimination of processed food | General health improvement, autoimmune protocols | Strict adherence in social settings, unnecessary food restriction | Moderate |
| Zone Diet (40/30/30) | Balanced macros, portion control | Balanced approach seekers, CrossFit community | Those who dislike measuring portions | Moderate |

**Default recommendation:** Flexible dieting with a whole-foods emphasis. Count calories
and protein. Let carbs and fat fall where they may within reasonable ranges. Eat 80%
whole foods, 20% whatever you enjoy. This approach maximizes adherence, provides nutritional
adequacy, and achieves any body composition goal.

**Override conditions:**
- Medical condition requiring specific diet: Follow physician/dietitian guidance.
- Ethical motivation: Respect the individual's values. Design a nutritionally complete
  vegan or vegetarian plan with attention to protein combining, B12, iron, zinc, and omega-3.
- Severe insulin resistance or type 2 diabetes: Lower-carb approaches (100-150g/day)
  improve glycemic control for many individuals. Work with their medical team.

## Hydration Science

Water is involved in every metabolic process. Dehydration of just 2% body weight
reduces exercise performance by 10-20%. Cognitive function declines before physical
performance does.

**Daily water intake targets:**
- Baseline: 30-40 mL per kg body weight per day (roughly 0.5-0.67 oz per lb)
- Add 500-750 mL per hour of exercise
- Add more in hot/humid environments, at altitude, or with high caffeine intake

**For an 80 kg (176 lb) person:**
- Baseline: 2.4-3.2 liters per day
- With 1 hour of exercise: 2.9-3.9 liters

**Electrolyte considerations:**
- Sodium: 500-1000 mg per liter of sweat lost. Heavy sweaters (white salt stains on
  clothing) need more. Add salt to food or use electrolyte supplements during long
  sessions (60+ minutes).
- Potassium: 3500-4700 mg/day from food (bananas, potatoes, avocados, leafy greens)
- Magnesium: 310-420 mg/day. Many athletes are deficient. Supplement if needed.

**Hydration timing:**
- Pre-workout: 500 mL in the 2 hours before training
- During workout: 200-300 mL every 15-20 minutes
- Post-workout: 1.5x fluid lost during training (weigh before and after to estimate)

**Signs of dehydration:** Dark urine (aim for pale yellow), headache, fatigue, decreased
performance, dizziness, dry mouth. Thirst is a late indicator. If you are thirsty, you
are already mildly dehydrated.

## Meal Timing and Nutrient Timing

**What the evidence actually says:**

**Total daily intake matters most.** For 90% of people and goals, hitting daily calorie
and protein targets is what drives results. Meal timing is a secondary optimization.

**Where timing matters (the 10%):**

1. **Pre-workout nutrition (1-3 hours before):**
   - 20-40g protein + 30-60g carbs. This ensures amino acids and glucose are available
     during training.
   - Training completely fasted is fine for moderate-intensity work. For heavy resistance
     training or high-intensity intervals, some pre-workout food improves performance
     by 5-15% in most studies.

2. **Post-workout nutrition (within 2-3 hours):**
   - 20-40g protein triggers muscle protein synthesis. The "anabolic window" exists
     but is 4-6 hours wide, not 30 minutes.
   - Carbs replenish glycogen. Critical for athletes training twice daily. Less urgent
     for once-daily training (you have 24 hours to refill glycogen).

3. **Before bed:**
   - 30-40g casein protein (slow-digesting) before bed supports overnight muscle protein
     synthesis. Research shows modest benefit for muscle gain.
   - Avoid large high-fat meals within 2 hours of bed. They disrupt sleep quality.

4. **Protein distribution:**
   - Distribute protein across 3-5 meals, each containing 20-40g. This maximizes muscle
     protein synthesis (MPS) response.
   - MPS has a "refractory period" of about 3-5 hours. Eating 100g of protein in one
     meal does not stimulate more MPS than 40g. The excess is oxidized for energy.
   - Leucine threshold: Each meal needs 2-3g of leucine to maximally stimulate MPS.
     This is roughly 20-30g of animal protein or 30-40g of plant protein.

**Common misapplication:** Stressing about meal timing while under-eating protein by 50g
per day. Fix the big rocks first (total calories, total protein, food quality). Timing
is a fine-tuning lever for people who already have the basics locked in.

## Special Populations

### Beginners (0-1 Year of Training)

The best time to be a lifter. Beginners experience "newbie gains" where simultaneous
muscle gain and fat loss happen easily, even in a slight caloric deficit.

**Programming recommendations:**
- Full-body training 3x/week
- Focus on compound movements: squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, barbell row
- Linear progression: Add weight every session (5 lbs upper body, 10 lbs lower body)
- 3 sets of 5-8 reps per exercise
- Programs that work: Starting Strength, StrongLifts 5x5, GZCLP, Phrak's Greyskull LP
- Duration of novice phase: 3-9 months depending on the individual

**Nutrition recommendations:**
- Slight surplus if lean (TDEE + 200-300). Maintenance or slight deficit if overweight.
- 1.6-2.0 g/kg protein. Keep it simple.
- Don't overcomplicate nutrition at this stage. Eat enough protein, eat mostly whole
  foods, get enough sleep. That covers 90% of novice results.

**Common mistakes:**
- Doing too many exercises (program hopping to "advanced" bodybuilding splits)
- Not learning proper form before adding weight
- Ignoring the value of rest days
- Comparing their progress to enhanced (steroid-using) lifters on social media

### Intermediate Lifters (1-3 Years of Training)

Linear progression stalls. Weekly or biweekly progression becomes the norm. Training
must become more structured.

**Programming recommendations:**
- Upper/Lower split or Push/Pull/Legs, 4-5 days/week
- Weekly periodization (undulating within the week)
- 10-20 sets per muscle group per week
- Focus on progressive overload through volume and intensity cycling
- Programs that work: PHUL, PHAT, nSuns 5/3/1, Jeff Nippard's programs, GZCL

**Nutrition recommendations:**
- Calorie and macro tracking becomes more important
- Dedicated bulk and cut phases (3-6 months each)
- Supplement with creatine and vitamin D if not already

### Advanced Lifters (3+ Years of Consistent Training)

Gains are slower. Fractional improvements require precision. Training and nutrition
must be highly individualized.

**Programming recommendations:**
- Block periodization or conjugate methods
- 15-25+ sets per muscle group per week for stubborn body parts
- Planned deloads every 3-5 weeks
- Specialization blocks for lagging muscle groups
- Autoregulation (RPE-based training) becomes essential
- Programs that work: Juggernaut Method, Renaissance Periodization templates, custom
  programming based on individual response data

**Nutrition recommendations:**
- Precise macro tracking with adjustments every 1-2 weeks based on data
- Nutrition periodization aligned with training phases
- Supplement stack optimization (Tier 1 and 2 supplements)
- Blood work monitoring for health markers

### Older Adults (50+)

Resistance training is the most important intervention for healthy aging. Sarcopenia
(age-related muscle loss) begins around age 30 and accelerates after 50. Resistance
training slows and reverses this process.

**Programming recommendations:**
- Resistance training 2-4x/week. Full body or upper/lower split.
- Prioritize: Squat pattern, hinge pattern, push, pull, carry, single-leg work
- Higher reps (8-15) with moderate loads reduce joint stress while still building muscle
- Include balance training (single-leg exercises, stability work) to prevent falls
- Include mobility work (daily stretching, yoga, foam rolling) to maintain range of motion
- Warm up thoroughly (10-15 minutes instead of 5-10)
- Recovery between sessions may need to be longer (48-72 hours per muscle group)

**Nutrition recommendations:**
- Protein needs are HIGHER with age, not lower. Target 1.6-2.2 g/kg/day.
  Anabolic resistance means older adults need more protein per meal (30-40g) to
  stimulate the same MPS response as younger adults.
- Calcium and vitamin D supplementation is critical for bone density
- Omega-3 fatty acids for joint health and inflammation management
- Creatine (3-5g/day) has evidence for cognitive benefits in addition to strength

**Common mistakes with older adults:**
- Avoiding resistance training due to fear of injury. The opposite is true.
  Sedentary aging is far more dangerous than appropriate resistance training.
- Using weights that are too light. Muscles need to be challenged to grow. "Pink
  dumbbell syndrome" produces minimal adaptation.
- Skipping protein because appetite decreases with age

### Athletes (Sport-Specific Considerations)

**Concurrent training (strength + endurance):**
- The interference effect: Endurance training can blunt strength and hypertrophy
  adaptations when performed in high volumes
- Mitigation strategies:
  - Separate strength and endurance sessions by 6+ hours (or different days)
  - Prioritize the primary quality in each session
  - Do strength training first when both are on the same day
  - Cycling produces less interference than running (less eccentric muscle damage)
  - Keep weekly running volume under 20-25 miles if hypertrophy is a priority

**Weight class athletes (powerlifting, wrestling, MMA, boxing):**
- Water manipulation for weigh-in is common but risky. Losing more than 5% body weight
  via dehydration impairs performance and is medically dangerous.
- Long-term strategy: Compete at a weight class you can reach with modest water
  manipulation (2-3% body weight). Walk around within 5-8% of competition weight.
- Sodium and water loading protocols require experience and carry risk. Work with a
  knowledgeable coach.

## Quality Standards

### The Nutrition & Fitness Quality Bar

Every fitness or nutrition recommendation must meet these criteria:
1. **Evidence-based.** Cite the level of evidence (meta-analysis, RCT, observational,
   mechanistic, expert opinion). If the evidence is weak or conflicting, say so.
2. **Individually appropriate.** Account for the individual's training age, goals,
   medical history, preferences, and constraints. Generic advice is bad advice.
3. **Actionable.** Every recommendation should tell the person what to do, how much,
   and when. "Eat more protein" is useless. "Eat 40g of protein at each of your 4
   meals, targeting 160g/day" is actionable.
4. **Sustainable.** Programs that work for 3 weeks and lead to burnout are failures.
   Sustainability beats optimization every time.
5. **Safe.** Never recommend something that increases injury risk without explicit
   risk/benefit analysis and appropriate progression.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Training Program:**
- Must include: Exercise selection, sets, reps, rest periods, tempo (when relevant),
  weekly split, progression scheme, deload protocol, and program duration
- Must avoid: Random exercise lists without logical progression. Programs that exceed
  MRV from week one. Programs that lack compound movements.
- Gold standard: A periodized 12-16 week program with built-in progression, deload
  weeks, autoregulation guidance (RPE/RIR), exercise substitution options, and
  clear success metrics

**Nutrition Plan:**
- Must include: Daily calorie target with rationale, macronutrient breakdown (grams
  and percentages), meal frequency recommendation, hydration target, and food
  suggestions (not rigid meal plans unless specifically requested)
- Must avoid: Arbitrary calorie targets without calculating TDEE. Protein below
  evidence-based minimums. Fat below hormonal health thresholds. Extreme restriction
  of any macronutrient without medical justification.
- Gold standard: A flexible nutrition framework with calorie and macro targets,
  sample meal templates, grocery list, supplement recommendations (with evidence tier),
  adjustment protocol based on progress, and adherence strategies

**Supplement Recommendation:**
- Must include: Specific product/ingredient, dose, timing, evidence tier, expected
  benefit magnitude, potential side effects, and interactions
- Must avoid: Recommending supplements without first evaluating diet quality. Claiming
  supplements can replace whole food nutrition. Recommending anything in Tier 3 or 4
  without explicit disclaimers about weak evidence.
- Gold standard: A prioritized supplement stack with cost-benefit analysis, evidence
  citations, and clear statement that supplements are the last 2-5% of results

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)

- [ ] All caloric and macronutrient targets are calculated from the individual's data (not generic)
- [ ] Protein target meets evidence-based minimums for the stated goal
- [ ] Fat intake is above hormonal health threshold (minimum 0.5 g/kg)
- [ ] Training volume is within MEV-MRV range for each muscle group
- [ ] Progressive overload is built into the program
- [ ] Deload protocol is specified
- [ ] Rest periods match the training goal (strength, hypertrophy, endurance)
- [ ] Exercise selection includes compound movements as the foundation
- [ ] Medical disclaimer is included
- [ ] Supplement recommendations include evidence tier
- [ ] Sustainability of the plan has been evaluated (can the person actually do this?)
- [ ] Writing follows all rules in writing-style.md (no banned phrases, no filler)

## Communication Standards

### Structure

Fitness and nutrition deliverables follow this structure:
1. **Goal statement** -- what we are trying to achieve and why
2. **Current assessment** -- where the person is now (training age, body comp, diet quality)
3. **The plan** -- specific, actionable recommendations with numbers
4. **The rationale** -- why this approach (briefly, with evidence level noted)
5. **Expected outcomes** -- realistic timeline and milestones
6. **Adjustment protocol** -- when and how to modify the plan based on results
7. **Disclaimer** -- consult a healthcare provider before making changes

### Tone

Authoritative and practical. Direct without being harsh. Evidence-based without being
academic. Use simple language. Explain jargon when using it for the first time. Avoid
fitness industry buzzwords ("gains," "shredded," "beast mode") unless matching the
user's own communication style.

### Audience Adaptation

**For beginners:** Minimize jargon. Focus on the 3-5 most important variables. Provide
exact instructions. Explain the "why" in simple terms. Prioritize adherence and habit
formation over optimization.

**For intermediate/advanced:** Use technical terminology freely. Discuss trade-offs and
nuances. Provide programming variables with ranges for autoregulation. Reference research
when relevant. Treat them as informed decision-makers.

**For coaches and trainers:** Deep physiological mechanisms. Programming logic and
periodization theory. Client management and communication strategies. Evidence hierarchy
and research interpretation.

**For medical professionals:** Precise physiological terminology. Risk/benefit analysis.
Contraindications and precautions. Defer appropriately to their medical expertise.

### Language Conventions

- "Resistance training" (preferred over "weight lifting" which implies barbells only)
- "Body composition" (preferred over "weight" when discussing fat loss and muscle gain)
- "Caloric deficit/surplus" (preferred over "eating less/more")
- "Evidence suggests" or "Research shows" (with implied evidence level)
- "1RM" = one-repetition maximum
- "RPE" = rate of perceived exertion (1-10 scale)
- "RIR" = reps in reserve (how many more reps could you have done?)
- "TDEE" = total daily energy expenditure
- "MPS" = muscle protein synthesis
- "NEAT" = non-exercise activity thermogenesis
- "DOMS" = delayed onset muscle soreness

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Physiological Plausibility Check

**What it tests:** Whether recommendations align with known physiology.
**How to apply:**
1. Trace each recommendation back to a physiological mechanism
2. Verify that the dose-response relationship is within established ranges
3. Check that training variables match the stated goal (e.g., 3x15 at 60% 1RM
   is not a strength protocol)
4. Verify caloric targets produce the claimed rate of change (3500 kcal deficit
   per pound of fat loss is approximate but directionally correct)
**Pass criteria:** Every recommendation has a clear physiological basis. No claims
contradict established exercise physiology.

### Method 2: Adherence Feasibility Check

**What it tests:** Whether a real person can actually follow this plan.
**How to apply:**
1. Count total time commitment per week (training + meal prep + cardio)
2. Assess meal complexity (does this require chef-level cooking skills?)
3. Evaluate social compatibility (can this person eat at restaurants?)
4. Check equipment requirements (does the person have access to what's needed?)
5. Assess psychological sustainability (is this plan monotonous? Extreme?)
**Pass criteria:** The plan fits the person's schedule, skills, equipment access,
and psychological profile. If it wouldn't survive contact with a real Tuesday, it fails.

### Method 3: Safety Review

**What it tests:** Whether the plan could cause harm.
**How to apply:**
1. Check caloric floor (above minimum recommended intake)
2. Check training volume ceiling (not exceeding estimated MRV from week one)
3. Verify progressive loading protocol (no sudden jumps in volume or intensity)
4. Check for exercise contraindications based on stated injuries or conditions
5. Verify supplement doses are within safe ranges
6. Confirm medical disclaimer is present
**Pass criteria:** No recommendation increases injury risk, nutritional deficiency
risk, or metabolic harm risk beyond acceptable levels.

### Method 4: Internal Consistency Check

**What it tests:** Whether all parts of the plan work together.
**How to apply:**
1. Verify caloric target aligns with stated goal (surplus for muscle gain, deficit
   for fat loss)
2. Check that training volume and nutrition support each other (high-volume training
   needs adequate carbs and calories)
3. Verify recovery recommendations match training intensity
4. Check that supplement timing aligns with training schedule
**Pass criteria:** No contradictions between nutrition plan, training plan, and
recovery recommendations. The pieces reinforce each other.

### Method 5: Evidence Level Audit

**What it tests:** Whether claims are appropriately supported.
**How to apply:**
1. Flag any claim presented as certain that is based on weak evidence
2. Verify that Tier 1 supplement recommendations match current meta-analyses
3. Check that specific numbers (rep ranges, macro targets) have research support
4. Identify any claims that rely on single studies or animal models
**Pass criteria:** Every claim is presented with appropriate confidence relative to
its evidence base. Strong evidence gets strong language. Weak evidence gets hedging.

## Anti-Patterns

> These are the mistakes that waste time, cause injury, or produce no results. They are
> common in both amateur and professional fitness culture.

1. **Extreme Caloric Restriction**
   What it looks like: Recommending 800-1000 calorie diets for rapid weight loss.
   Crash diets. Very low calorie diets (VLCDs) without medical supervision.
   Why it's harmful: Accelerates muscle loss (up to 25% of weight lost is lean mass at
   extreme deficits). Tanks metabolic rate through adaptive thermogenesis. Increases
   binge eating risk. Disrupts hormones (thyroid, testosterone, estrogen, leptin).
   Can trigger or worsen eating disorders.
   Instead: Moderate deficit of 300-750 kcal below TDEE. Slower fat loss preserves
   muscle, metabolic rate, and psychological health. Include diet breaks and refeeds
   for deficits lasting more than 8 weeks.

2. **No Periodization (Same Workout Forever)**
   What it looks like: Doing the same exercises, sets, reps, and weights for months
   or years. The "Monday is chest day and it always looks exactly the same" pattern.
   Why it's harmful: The body adapts to a stimulus within 4-6 weeks. Without systematic
   variation, progress stalls completely. The lifter trains hard but goes nowhere.
   Instead: Structured periodization. Change rep ranges, exercises, volume, or intensity
   every 4-6 weeks in a planned sequence. Use the periodization models in Framework 2.

3. **Ego Lifting**
   What it looks like: Loading more weight than can be handled with proper form.
   Quarter squats. Bouncing bench presses. Swinging curls with full-body momentum.
   Why it's harmful: High injury risk (spinal disc herniation, rotator cuff tears,
   muscle strains). Reduces the stimulus to the target muscle because momentum and
   leverage do the work. Builds a false sense of progress.
   Instead: Full range of motion with controlled tempo. The muscle does not know how
   much weight is on the bar. It knows tension, stretch, and time under load. A
   controlled 185 lb bench press builds more chest muscle than a bounced 225 lb press.

4. **Supplement Dependency**
   What it looks like: Spending $200+/month on supplements while eating fast food,
   sleeping 5 hours, and skipping workouts. Believing fat burners cause fat loss
   independent of caloric deficit. Replacing meals with supplements.
   Why it's harmful: Creates a financial drain for minimal results. Distracts from the
   fundamentals that produce 95% of results. Some supplements (proprietary blends,
   high-stimulant fat burners) carry genuine health risks.
   Instead: Master the fundamentals first (sleep, training, whole food nutrition, stress
   management). Add Tier 1 supplements (creatine, vitamin D, protein powder) if gaps exist.
   Everything else is optional optimization for people who have the basics locked in.

5. **Program Hopping**
   What it looks like: Starting a new program every 2-3 weeks. Constantly chasing the
   "best" routine. Never finishing a complete training cycle.
   Why it's harmful: Progressive overload requires consistency on the same movements for
   weeks to produce adaptation. Switching programs every 2 weeks means you never
   accumulate enough stimulus on any movement to progress. You stay a perpetual beginner.
   Instead: Pick a program appropriate for your level. Run it for the full prescribed
   duration (usually 8-16 weeks). Evaluate results after completion. Then adjust.

6. **Ignoring Recovery**
   What it looks like: Training 7 days per week at maximum intensity. Sleeping 5 hours.
   Viewing rest days as weakness. "More is always better" mentality.
   Why it's harmful: Overreaching leads to performance decline, increased injury risk,
   hormonal disruption, immune suppression, and eventually overtraining syndrome. OTS can
   take 3-6 months to resolve. The training doesn't build the muscle. Recovery does.
   Instead: Schedule rest days. Take deloads. Monitor sleep quality, morning heart rate,
   and mood. If performance declines for more than 2 consecutive sessions, reduce volume
   or take a recovery day.

7. **Cardio-Only Approach to Fat Loss**
   What it looks like: Doing only treadmill/elliptical/running for fat loss with no
   resistance training. The "cardio bunny" pattern.
   Why it's harmful: Cardio without resistance training leads to muscle loss during
   caloric deficit. Less muscle means lower BMR. Lower BMR means lower TDEE. The person
   needs to eat less and less to continue losing weight. This creates a metabolic trap.
   Instead: Resistance training is the priority during a fat loss phase. It preserves
   muscle mass, maintains metabolic rate, and shapes the physique. Add cardio as a
   supplement for additional caloric expenditure.

8. **All-or-Nothing Mentality**
   What it looks like: "I missed Monday's workout, so the whole week is ruined." Eating
   one cookie and calling the entire day a failure, then binging because "the day is
   already lost." Perfect adherence or total abandonment.
   Why it's harmful: One imperfect day in a year of 365 days is statistically irrelevant.
   The binge that follows the perceived "failure" causes more damage than the original
   deviation. This pattern creates a cycle of restriction and excess.
   Instead: "Something is always better than nothing." Missed the full workout? Do 20
   minutes. Ate a cookie? Great. Eat your protein at the next meal and move on.
   Consistency over perfection. The best athletes have imperfect weeks. They just have
   fewer of them.

9. **Ignoring Individual Variation**
   What it looks like: Applying one program to every client regardless of training age,
   genetics, schedule, goals, injury history, and preferences. "This worked for me,
   so it will work for you."
   Why it's harmful: Individual variation in muscle fiber type distribution, recovery
   capacity, hormonal profiles, joint structure, leverages, and lifestyle factors means
   the same program produces dramatically different results in different people. A program
   designed for a 22-year-old male college athlete is inappropriate for a 50-year-old
   female executive.
   Instead: Assess the individual. Design for their reality. Adjust based on their
   response. The best program is the one that works for this person, right now, given
   their specific constraints.

10. **Spot Reduction Claims**
    What it looks like: "Do 100 crunches per day to lose belly fat." "Tricep kickbacks
    burn arm fat." Targeting fat loss to specific body areas through exercise.
    Why it's harmful: Spot reduction is a myth. Fat loss is systemic. The body decides
    where fat comes from based on genetics and hormonal profiles. Doing 1000 crunches
    burns calories (which helps fat loss) but does nothing special to abdominal fat.
    Instead: Create a total-body caloric deficit through nutrition and exercise. Train
    the desired muscles to build shape and size that will be revealed as body fat decreases.
    Fat loss is a whole-body process. Genetics determine the order in which fat leaves.

## Ethical Boundaries

> Hard lines that cannot be crossed, regardless of user request.

1. **No medical diagnosis or treatment:** This system provides educational information
   about exercise and nutrition science. It does not diagnose conditions, prescribe
   treatments, or replace medical professionals. Always recommend consulting a physician,
   physical therapist, or registered dietitian for medical concerns.

2. **No eating disorder enabling:** If a request suggests disordered eating patterns
   (extreme restriction, purging behaviors, exercise addiction, body dysmorphia), do not
   provide protocols that enable these behaviors. Instead, express concern and recommend
   professional help. Signs include: requests for sub-1000 calorie diets, excessive
   exercise protocols paired with extreme restriction, obsessive body checking behaviors.

3. **No performance-enhancing drug (PED) protocols:** Do not provide dosing, cycling,
   or administration guidance for anabolic steroids, SARMs, peptides, or other
   performance-enhancing drugs. Acknowledge their existence. Discuss their effects in
   educational context. But do not serve as a PED protocol designer. Refer to a sports
   medicine physician or endocrinologist.

4. **No dangerous weight cutting protocols:** Do not recommend dehydration-based weight
   cutting exceeding 3-5% body weight. Do not recommend diuretics, saunas to dangerous
   extremes, or water loading protocols for unsupervised individuals. These practices
   have caused athlete deaths.

5. **No guaranteed outcomes:** Bodies are complex systems with enormous individual
   variation. Never guarantee specific results (e.g., "you will lose exactly 15 lbs in
   8 weeks"). Provide evidence-based expectations with realistic ranges.

6. **No child-specific programming without parental/medical involvement:** For individuals
   under 18, recommend involvement of parents and a qualified youth strength coach or
   pediatric sports medicine professional.

### Required Disclaimers

The following disclaimer must accompany every training program, nutrition plan, or
health recommendation:

> *This information is for educational purposes only. Consult a qualified healthcare
> provider before beginning any exercise program or making significant dietary changes.
> This is especially important if you have pre-existing health conditions, injuries,
> are pregnant, or take medications. Individual results vary based on genetics, adherence,
> starting point, and other factors.*

For supplement recommendations, add:

> *Supplements are not regulated by the FDA for safety or efficacy before reaching the
> market. Quality varies between manufacturers. Choose products tested by third-party
> organizations (NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Choice, USP Verified). Discuss
> supplement use with your healthcare provider, especially if you take medications.*

## Stress Management for Recovery

Stress is a training variable. Ignoring it is like ignoring sleep. The body does not
distinguish between training stress and life stress at the hormonal level.

**The stress-recovery model:**

Training creates a stress signal. The body supercompensates during recovery. If the
next training session occurs before recovery is complete, performance degrades.
If life stress is high, recovery takes longer. This means training volume must decrease
during high-stress periods to maintain the same adaptation rate.

**Practical stress management strategies:**

1. **Breathing protocols:** 5 minutes of box breathing (inhale 4 sec, hold 4 sec,
   exhale 4 sec, hold 4 sec) reduces cortisol within a single session. Physiological
   sighs (double inhale through nose, long exhale through mouth) activate the
   parasympathetic nervous system in seconds.

2. **Walking:** 20-30 minutes of walking in nature reduces cortisol by 12-16% in
   research. Walking is also low-level cardio that supports recovery without adding
   training stress.

3. **Social connection:** Isolation elevates stress hormones. Meaningful social
   interaction reduces them. Training with a partner improves both adherence and recovery.

4. **Screen breaks:** Chronic screen time elevates sympathetic nervous system activity.
   20-minute breaks from screens during the day, and screen-free time before bed,
   support better recovery.

5. **Journaling:** 10 minutes of expressive writing reduces perceived stress and
   improves sleep quality in research. Write about what's stressing you, then shift
   to what you're grateful for.

**Auto-regulation based on stress:**

On days when life stress is high (poor sleep, work crisis, emotional distress):
- Reduce training volume by 20-40%
- Reduce intensity by 10-20%
- Focus on movement quality over load
- Prioritize Zone 2 cardio over high-intensity work
- Consider converting the session to mobility and light movement

This is not weakness. This is smart training. The body has finite recovery resources.
Allocate them wisely.

## Sleep Optimization Protocol

Sleep is the single highest-return investment for any fitness goal. Every study on
sleep restriction shows worse outcomes for muscle gain, fat loss, strength, endurance,
cognitive function, and injury risk.

**What happens when sleep is inadequate:**
- Testosterone decreases 10-15% after one week of 5-hour sleep (in young healthy males)
- Growth hormone secretion drops dramatically (80% of GH is released during deep sleep)
- Insulin sensitivity decreases (the body partitions more nutrients toward fat storage)
- Cortisol stays elevated (promotes muscle breakdown, visceral fat storage)
- Appetite hormones shift (ghrelin increases, leptin decreases -- you feel hungrier)
- Reaction time and coordination decline (injury risk increases)
- Perceived effort increases (the same workout feels harder)

**The sleep optimization stack:**

| Priority | Intervention | Impact |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | Consistent sleep/wake times (within 30 min, even weekends) | High |
| 2 | Dark sleeping environment (blackout curtains or sleep mask) | High |
| 3 | Cool room (65-68 F / 18-20 C) | High |
| 4 | No caffeine after 2 PM (or 8+ hours before bed) | High |
| 5 | No screens 30-60 min before bed (or use blue light blockers) | Medium |
| 6 | No alcohol within 3 hours of bed (alcohol fragments sleep architecture) | Medium |
| 7 | Morning sunlight exposure within 30 min of waking (sets circadian rhythm) | Medium |
| 8 | Magnesium glycinate 200-400mg before bed | Low-Medium |
| 9 | Keep bedroom for sleep and intimacy only (no work, no TV) | Low-Medium |
| 10 | White noise machine or ear plugs for noise disruption | Low |

**Tracking sleep quality:**

Simple metrics anyone can track:
- Time to fall asleep (target: under 20 minutes)
- Nighttime awakenings (target: 0-1 brief awakenings)
- Morning alertness without alarm (target: able to wake naturally)
- Daytime energy (target: no afternoon crash requiring caffeine)

Wearable devices (Whoop, Oura Ring, Apple Watch) provide sleep stage data that helps
identify patterns. The data is directionally useful even if not perfectly accurate.

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge): Domain-Specific Guidance

**Questions to ask every fitness/nutrition request:**
- What is the specific goal? (Fat loss, muscle gain, strength, endurance, general health,
  sport performance, event preparation)
- What is the timeline? (Competition date, vacation, wedding, or ongoing lifestyle change)
- What is the current training status? (Beginner, intermediate, advanced. Years of
  consistent training.)
- What is the current body composition? (Height, weight, estimated body fat, waist
  circumference)
- What is the current nutrition? (Rough caloric intake, protein intake, diet style)
- What equipment is available? (Full gym, home gym, bodyweight only, limited equipment)
- How many days per week can be dedicated to training?
- Any injuries, medical conditions, or medications?
- Any dietary restrictions (allergies, intolerances, ethical preferences)?
- Sleep quality and quantity?
- Life stress level? (Work demands, family obligations, mental health)
- What has been tried before? What worked? What failed?

**Patterns to look for:**
- Goals that conflict (wanting to lose 20 lbs of fat and gain 20 lbs of muscle in 4 weeks)
- Unrealistic timelines (dropping from 30% to 10% body fat in 6 weeks)
- Signs of disordered eating or exercise addiction
- Medical red flags that require professional referral
- Mismatch between training volume and recovery capacity

### Stage 2 (Design Approach): Domain-Specific Guidance

**Framework selection logic:**
- Body composition goal identified? Load Energy Balance Framework + Macronutrient Framework
- Training program needed? Load Goal-Specific Programming + Volume Landmarks + Periodization
- Plateau reported? Load Progressive Overload Framework + Recovery Framework
- Supplement question? Load Supplement Evidence Tiers
- Behavior change challenge? Load Habit Formation Framework
- Multiple goals? Prioritize by impact and sequence phases

**Approach design priorities:**
1. Safety first (does this plan avoid harm?)
2. Effectiveness second (does this plan produce results?)
3. Sustainability third (can the person maintain this for 12+ months?)
4. Optimization last (fine-tuning for the final 5% of results)

### Stage 3 (Structure Engagement): Domain-Specific Guidance

**Common deliverable types:**
- Training program (4-16 weeks)
- Nutrition plan (calorie and macro targets with food suggestions)
- Supplement stack recommendation
- Body composition strategy (bulk/cut/recomp decision with timeline)
- Competition preparation plan (12-20 weeks)
- Recovery and deload protocol
- Habit change plan
- Exercise technique guide
- Diet comparison analysis
- Progress troubleshooting report

**Typical engagement structures:**
- Tier 1 (Quick answer): Direct answer with evidence level. Example: "Is creatine safe?"
- Tier 2 (Standard): Assessment + plan + rationale. Example: "Design a 12-week hypertrophy program."
- Tier 3 (Full engagement): Comprehensive assessment, multi-phase plan, nutrition
  integration, supplement protocol, progress tracking framework, adjustment triggers.
  Example: "Prepare me for my first powerlifting meet in 16 weeks."

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables): Domain-Specific Guidance

**Creation standards:**
- Every training program includes specific sets, reps, rest periods, and progression scheme
- Every nutrition plan includes calculated calorie and macro targets based on individual data
- Every recommendation includes evidence level (meta-analysis, RCT, observational, mechanistic)
- Use tables for training programs and nutrition breakdowns (easier to scan and follow)
- Include exercise alternatives for each movement (for equipment limitations or injuries)
- Include adjustment protocols (what to change when, based on what data)
- Follow all writing rules from writing-style.md

### Stage 5 (Quality Assurance): Domain-Specific Review Criteria

Apply the Quality Checklist from the Quality Standards section. Pay special attention to:
- Caloric and macro math (recalculate independently to verify)
- Training volume vs. recovery capacity match
- Progression logic (is it realistic for the person's level?)
- Safety of exercise selection for the person's injury history
- Supplement evidence tier accuracy
- Medical disclaimer presence
- Writing quality (no AI slop, no banned phrases, concrete language)

### Stage 6 (Validate): Domain-Specific Validation

Apply all five validation methods:
1. Physiological Plausibility Check
2. Adherence Feasibility Check
3. Safety Review
4. Internal Consistency Check
5. Evidence Level Audit

For Tier 3 engagements, run every validation method explicitly. For Tier 2, run
methods 1, 2, and 3 at minimum. For Tier 1, methods 1 and 3 are sufficient.

### Stage 7 (Plan Delivery): Domain-Specific Delivery

**Delivery formats by deliverable type:**
- Training program: Table format with weekly layout. Include warm-up protocol, exercise
  links or descriptions for unfamiliar movements, and a program notes section.
- Nutrition plan: Macro targets with flexible food lists (not rigid meal plans unless
  requested). Include a grocery list template and meal prep suggestions.
- Supplement protocol: Prioritized list with dose, timing, evidence tier, and
  expected benefit. Include cost estimate if possible.
- Progress report: Data-driven analysis of what's working and what needs adjustment.

**Delivery considerations:**
- Beginners need more explanation and fewer options
- Advanced trainees need less explanation and more programming detail
- All deliverables need a "what to do if" troubleshooting section
- Include clear next steps (when to reassess, when to change phases)

### Stage 8 (Deliver): Domain-Specific Follow-up

**Follow-up rhythm:**
- Weekly check-in recommended for active programs (weight trend, training performance,
  adherence rating, energy level)
- Bi-weekly adjustment cadence for nutrition (adjust calories by 100-200 kcal based on
  weight trend)
- Monthly program review for training (are progression targets being met? Deload needed?)
- Quarterly strategy review (is the macro approach still correct? Time to switch phases?)

**Iteration triggers:**
- Weight plateaus for 2+ weeks (adjust calories or add cardio)
- Strength plateaus for 3+ sessions on the same movement (deload, change rep scheme,
  or modify exercise variation)
- Adherence drops below 80% (simplify the plan, address behavioral barriers)
- New injury or medical condition (pause, assess, modify program)
- Goal changes (reassess from Stage 1)
- Life circumstances change (new job, baby, travel -- adjust volume and complexity)

**What success looks like at each tier:**
- Tier 1: The person has a clear, accurate, evidence-based answer they can act on today
- Tier 2: The person has a structured plan with specific numbers, clear progression,
  and adjustment protocols
- Tier 3: The person has a comprehensive system covering training, nutrition, recovery,
  and behavioral strategy with built-in feedback loops and phase transitions

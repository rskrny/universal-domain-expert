## Core Frameworks

> These frameworks are the operating system for e-commerce decision-making. Each one
> solves a specific class of problem. Use the right tool for the situation.

### Framework 1: E-Commerce Unit Economics Framework

**What:** The financial model that determines whether a store makes or loses money on every order. This is the foundation. Everything else is noise without healthy unit economics.

**When to use:** Before launching a store. Before scaling ad spend. Before any major investment. Quarterly for ongoing operations.

**How to apply:**

1. Calculate **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** per unit. Include product cost, packaging, inserts, labels. Be precise.

2. Calculate **Landed Cost** per unit. COGS plus inbound shipping, duties, warehousing cost per unit.

3. Calculate **Average Order Value (AOV)**. Revenue per order after discounts and before shipping charges.

4. Calculate **Gross Margin per order.** AOV minus COGS minus payment processing fees (typically 2.9% + $0.30 for Stripe) minus shipping cost to customer.

5. Calculate **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**. Total marketing spend divided by new customers acquired. Break this out by channel: paid social CAC, Google CAC, organic CAC, blended CAC.

6. Calculate **Contribution Margin.** Gross margin minus CAC. This is profit per new customer before fixed costs.

7. Calculate **Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)**. Average revenue per customer over their full relationship. For a quick estimate: AOV x purchase frequency x customer lifespan. For precision: use cohort-based LTV from historical data.

8. Calculate **LTV:CAC Ratio.** Target 3:1 or higher. Below 1:1, you are losing money on every customer. Between 1:1 and 3:1, you are likely unprofitable after fixed costs.

9. Calculate **Payback Period.** How many months until a customer's cumulative contribution margin repays their CAC. Target under 6 months. Under 3 months is excellent.

10. Calculate **Break-even ROAS.** The return on ad spend needed to cover COGS, shipping, and processing. Formula: 1 / gross margin percentage. At 40% gross margin, break-even ROAS is 2.5x.

**Benchmark ranges:**
- Healthy gross margins: 60-80% for DTC brands, 30-50% for resellers
- Healthy blended CAC: $15-45 for consumables, $30-80 for apparel, $50-150 for premium goods
- Healthy LTV:CAC: 3:1 to 5:1
- Healthy payback period: 1-6 months
- E-commerce average AOV: $50-120 depending on category

**Common misapplication:** Using blended CAC when you should analyze by channel. Paid social CAC of $80 might look terrible blended, but if organic and email bring the blended CAC to $30, paid is doing its job as a top-of-funnel driver.

### Framework 2: Conversion Funnel Analysis

**What:** A stage-by-stage breakdown of where customers drop off between first visit and completed purchase. Each stage has a measurable conversion rate. Improving any stage compounds across all traffic.

**When to use:** When diagnosing low revenue relative to traffic. When planning CRO priorities. Monthly as an operational check.

**How to apply:**

1. **Map the funnel stages with benchmarks:**
   - Site Visit to Product Page View: 40-60%
   - Product Page View to Add to Cart: 8-12%
   - Add to Cart to Checkout Initiated: 45-65%
   - Checkout Initiated to Purchase Complete: 45-65%
   - Overall site visitor to purchase: 1.5-3.5% (varies by category and traffic source)

2. **Identify the biggest drop-off point.** This is your highest-leverage CRO opportunity. A 10% relative improvement at the worst stage creates the biggest absolute gain.

3. **Diagnose the cause at each leaky stage:**
   - Low visit-to-PDP: Poor navigation, weak merchandising, irrelevant traffic
   - Low PDP-to-ATC: Weak product pages, pricing issues, missing trust signals, poor photography
   - Low ATC-to-checkout: Surprise shipping costs, no guest checkout, cart UX issues
   - Low checkout-to-purchase: Complex checkout, limited payment options, trust concerns

4. **Prioritize fixes by impact x effort.** Quick wins first. Fix the stage with the biggest leak and the easiest fix.

5. **Measure before and after.** Track each stage weekly. Compare against your own baselines.

**Industry benchmarks (2024-2025 averages):**
- Overall e-commerce conversion rate: 2.5-3.5% desktop, 1.5-2.5% mobile
- Cart abandonment rate: 69-72% across all industries
- Checkout abandonment rate: 15-25%
- Mobile share of traffic: 65-75%
- Mobile share of revenue: 45-55% (the gap is the opportunity)

**Common misapplication:** Optimizing top-of-funnel (more traffic) when the bottom of funnel leaks badly. Doubling traffic to a 1% converting site doubles your ad bill. Doubling the conversion rate doubles revenue at the same cost.

### Framework 3: Platform Selection Decision Matrix

**What:** A structured evaluation for choosing the right e-commerce platform based on business requirements, technical constraints, and growth trajectory.

**When to use:** Starting a new store. Outgrowing the current platform. Evaluating a migration.

**How to apply:**

1. **Score each platform across ten dimensions (1-5 scale):**

| Dimension | Shopify | Shopify Plus | WooCommerce | BigCommerce | Magento | Headless |
|-----------|---------|-------------|-------------|-------------|---------|----------|
| Time to launch | 5 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 2 | 1 |
| Monthly cost (low rev) | 4 | 1 | 5 | 3 | 2 | 2 |
| Monthly cost (high rev) | 3 | 3 | 4 | 3 | 3 | 4 |
| Customization depth | 3 | 4 | 5 | 3 | 5 | 5 |
| App ecosystem | 5 | 5 | 5 | 3 | 4 | 3 |
| SEO capability | 3 | 3 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 5 |
| Multi-channel | 5 | 5 | 3 | 4 | 3 | 4 |
| Scalability | 4 | 5 | 3 | 4 | 4 | 5 |
| Maintenance burden | 5 | 4 | 2 | 4 | 1 | 2 |
| Developer availability | 4 | 4 | 5 | 3 | 3 | 3 |

2. **Weight dimensions by business priority.** A solo founder weights time-to-launch and maintenance burden heavily. A VC-backed brand weights scalability and customization.

3. **Calculate weighted scores.** The highest score wins, with override conditions below.

**Decision shortcuts:**
- Solo founder, first store, under $1M revenue target: **Shopify Basic or Standard**
- Established brand, $1-10M revenue, need customization: **Shopify Plus or BigCommerce**
- Content-heavy brand, SEO is primary channel: **WooCommerce**
- Enterprise, $50M+, complex catalog: **Magento/Adobe Commerce or Headless**
- Developer-first team, API-driven: **Headless (Medusa, Saleor, Commerce.js)**
- Want maximum control, have developer resources: **WooCommerce**

**Common misapplication:** Over-engineering the platform choice for a pre-revenue store. Shopify gets you live in a week. You can migrate later when you have revenue to justify the investment. Speed to market beats theoretical platform superiority.

### Framework 4: Pricing Strategy Framework

**What:** A systematic approach to setting and optimizing prices across the product catalog. Price is the single most powerful profit lever in e-commerce.

**When to use:** Launching new products. Reviewing pricing quarterly. When margins are under pressure. When testing pricing tiers.

**How to apply:**

1. **Establish the pricing floor.** Landed cost plus all variable costs per order (processing, shipping, packaging). You cannot sell below this without losing money on every sale.

2. **Research the price ceiling.** What do competitors charge for equivalent products? What is the maximum a customer would pay based on perceived value? Use competitor scraping tools (Prisync, Competera) and customer surveys.

3. **Choose a pricing method:**
   - **Cost-plus:** Floor price plus target margin. Simple. Works for commodity goods.
   - **Value-based:** Price based on perceived customer value. Works for differentiated products. Requires understanding willingness to pay.
   - **Competitive:** Match or undercut competitor prices. Works in commodity categories. Race to the bottom if you lack cost advantage.
   - **Dynamic:** Algorithmic pricing that adjusts based on demand, inventory, competition. Works for large catalogs (1000+ SKUs). Requires tooling.
   - **Psychological:** Price points ending in .99 or .97. Charm pricing. Anchor pricing with a higher-priced option. Decoy pricing with three tiers.

4. **Test pricing.** Run price tests on 10-20% of traffic. Measure revenue per visitor, conversion rate, and margin per order. A 10% price increase with a 5% conversion drop is usually profitable.

5. **Build a pricing ladder.** Good-Better-Best across the catalog. The middle option should be the target. The premium option makes the middle look reasonable. The entry option captures price-sensitive customers.

**Key insight:** A 1% price increase drops directly to profit. For a business with 10% net margins, a 1% price increase yields a 10% profit increase. Price is the most powerful lever in the business.

**Common misapplication:** Racing to the lowest price without a cost advantage. If you compete on price without Walmart-scale cost structure, you die on price. Compete on value, brand, experience, or convenience instead.

### Framework 5: Email Lifecycle Framework

**What:** The complete set of automated email flows that generate revenue on autopilot. Email is the highest-ROI channel in e-commerce, averaging $36-42 return per dollar spent.

**When to use:** Setting up a new store's email program. Auditing an underperforming email channel. Optimizing existing flows for more revenue.

**How to apply:**

1. **Build the six core automated flows (in priority order):**

   **Flow 1: Abandoned Cart (highest revenue impact)**
   - Trigger: Item added to cart, no purchase within 1 hour
   - Email 1 (1 hour): Reminder with cart contents and product images
   - Email 2 (24 hours): Social proof, reviews, urgency
   - Email 3 (48 hours): Incentive if margins allow (5-10% off or free shipping)
   - Expected recovery rate: 5-15% of abandoned carts
   - Benchmark revenue: 3-8% of total store revenue

   **Flow 2: Welcome Series (highest long-term impact)**
   - Trigger: Email signup (popup, footer, checkout)
   - Email 1 (immediate): Welcome, deliver promised incentive, brand story
   - Email 2 (Day 2): Best sellers, social proof, what makes you different
   - Email 3 (Day 4): Customer testimonials, UGC
   - Email 4 (Day 7): Final incentive reminder with urgency
   - Target: Convert 10-20% of new subscribers within 7 days

   **Flow 3: Post-Purchase**
   - Trigger: Order completed
   - Email 1 (immediately): Order confirmation with upsell opportunity
   - Email 2 (shipping): Tracking info with content/tips
   - Email 3 (delivered + 3 days): How-to content, ask for review
   - Email 4 (delivered + 14 days): Cross-sell related products
   - Email 5 (delivered + 30 days): Replenishment reminder (if consumable)

   **Flow 4: Browse Abandonment**
   - Trigger: Viewed product 2+ times, no add to cart
   - Email 1 (2 hours): "Still looking at [product]?"
   - Email 2 (24 hours): Similar products, social proof
   - Lower urgency than cart abandonment. No discounts needed.

   **Flow 5: Win-Back**
   - Trigger: No purchase in 60-90 days (adjust based on typical purchase cycle)
   - Email 1 (60 days): "We miss you" with personalized product recommendations
   - Email 2 (75 days): Incentive offer
   - Email 3 (90 days): Final offer, stronger incentive
   - Email 4 (120 days): Last chance before suppression

   **Flow 6: Sunset / List Hygiene**
   - Trigger: No email engagement (opens/clicks) in 90-120 days
   - Email 1: "Do you still want to hear from us?"
   - If no engagement: Suppress from list
   - Protects deliverability. A smaller, engaged list outperforms a large, dead one.

2. **Layer on campaigns.** Flows run on autopilot. Campaigns are one-time sends: product launches, sales events, seasonal promotions, content newsletters. Send 2-4 campaigns per week to engaged segments.

3. **Segment the list.** At minimum: engaged (opened/clicked in 30 days), lapsed (30-90 days no engagement), and dead (90+ days). Send your best offers to engaged. Send win-backs to lapsed. Suppress dead.

4. **Measure flow performance.** Track revenue per recipient, conversion rate, and flow-attributed revenue as a percentage of total revenue. Target: automated flows should generate 25-40% of total email revenue.

**Benchmarks (Klaviyo industry data):**
- Email revenue as % of total revenue: 25-35% for mature programs
- Abandoned cart flow conversion: 3-8%
- Welcome series conversion: 10-20%
- Open rate (flows): 40-60%
- Click rate (flows): 5-10%
- Unsubscribe rate: under 0.3% per send

**Common misapplication:** Building flows and forgetting them. Flows need quarterly review. Test subject lines, timing, offers, and content. A stale welcome series degrades over time as the market evolves.

### Framework 6: Fulfillment Strategy Decision Tree

**What:** A decision framework for choosing the right fulfillment model based on order volume, product characteristics, and business stage.

**When to use:** Launching a new store. Outgrowing current fulfillment. Evaluating 3PL options. Considering FBA.

**How to apply:**

1. **Start with order volume:**
   - Under 50 orders/month: Self-fulfill from home/garage. Keep costs zero.
   - 50-500 orders/month: Evaluate 3PL vs continued self-fulfillment. 3PL makes sense when packing takes more than 15 hours/week.
   - 500-5000 orders/month: 3PL is almost always the right call. Negotiate volume rates.
   - 5000+ orders/month: 3PL with dedicated account management. Consider multiple warehouses for faster shipping.

2. **Evaluate product characteristics:**
   - Small, light, standardized: Strong FBA candidate. Also cheap to ship via 3PL.
   - Large, heavy, fragile: 3PL with specialization. FBA fees will eat margins.
   - Custom/personalized: Self-fulfillment or specialized 3PL. FBA cannot handle customization.
   - Temperature-sensitive: Specialized cold chain 3PL required.
   - Subscription boxes: 3PL with kitting capabilities.

3. **Compare total cost per order:**
   - Self-fulfillment: Labor time x hourly rate + shipping cost + packaging cost + storage cost
   - 3PL: Pick/pack fee ($2-5/order) + storage fee ($15-40/pallet/month) + shipping cost + receiving fee
   - FBA: FBA fees ($3-15/unit depending on size/weight) + storage ($0.87-2.40/cubic foot/month) + inbound shipping

4. **Factor in speed and experience:**
   - FBA Prime badge increases conversion 10-30% on Amazon
   - 2-day shipping expectation is standard. 3-5 days is acceptable with free shipping.
   - Same-day/next-day is a competitive advantage worth paying for in metro areas

**Decision shortcuts:**
- Selling on Amazon as a channel: Use FBA for Amazon orders, 3PL for DTC orders
- Pure DTC brand: 3PL with 2 warehouse locations (East and West coast for US)
- Dropshipping: Supplier ships directly. Your job is marketing and customer service.
- Hybrid: Self-fulfill custom/kitted orders, 3PL for standard orders

**3PL evaluation checklist:**
- Minimum order volume requirements
- Pick/pack fees and how they scale
- Storage fees (per pallet, per bin, per cubic foot)
- Receiving fees (per unit or per shipment)
- Shipping carrier rates (do they get better rates than you?)
- Technology integration (Shopify, WooCommerce, marketplace connectors)
- Returns processing capability and cost
- Accuracy rate (target: 99.5%+ order accuracy)
- Geographic location relative to your customer base

**Common misapplication:** Choosing FBA for everything. FBA fees are steep for low-margin or large products. Run the math on FBA vs 3PL for every product category. Some products are profitable via 3PL and unprofitable via FBA.

### Framework 7: Customer Segmentation (RFM Analysis)

**What:** Segments customers by Recency (how recently they purchased), Frequency (how often they purchase), and Monetary value (how much they spend). Creates actionable customer tiers for targeted marketing.

**When to use:** Building retention strategy. Planning email campaigns. Allocating marketing budget across customer segments. Quarterly customer health checks.

**How to apply:**

1. **Score each customer on three dimensions (1-5 scale):**
   - **Recency:** 5 = purchased in last 30 days, 4 = 31-90 days, 3 = 91-180 days, 2 = 181-365 days, 1 = 365+ days
   - **Frequency:** 5 = 10+ orders, 4 = 6-9 orders, 3 = 3-5 orders, 2 = 2 orders, 1 = 1 order
   - **Monetary:** 5 = top 10% spend, 4 = top 25%, 3 = top 50%, 2 = top 75%, 1 = bottom 25%

2. **Create customer segments from RFM combinations:**
   - **Champions (555, 554, 545):** Best customers. Buy often, recently, and spend big. Reward them. Ask for referrals. Give early access to new products.
   - **Loyal (534, 435, 535):** Regular buyers. Keep them happy with loyalty perks. Cross-sell.
   - **Potential Loyalists (513, 412, 512):** Recent customers with moderate frequency. Nurture with post-purchase flows and loyalty program enrollment.
   - **New Customers (511, 411):** Just made their first purchase. Critical window. Nail the post-purchase experience.
   - **At Risk (254, 244, 343):** Used to buy frequently but haven't recently. Win-back campaigns. Ask what changed.
   - **Lost (111, 112, 121):** Haven't bought in a long time, low frequency, low value. One more attempt to re-engage, then suppress.

3. **Tailor marketing by segment:**
   - Champions get early access, referral asks, and VIP treatment
   - At Risk get surveys, incentives, and personalized win-back offers
   - New Customers get education, brand story, and second-purchase incentives
   - Lost customers get a final offer before suppression

**Common misapplication:** Treating all customers the same. Sending the same email to a Champion and a Lost customer wastes money on the lost customer and under-invests in the champion. Segment first, market second.

### Framework 8: Marketplace vs DTC Decision Framework

**What:** A structured analysis for deciding where to sell: your own store (DTC), marketplaces (Amazon, eBay, Walmart), or both.

**When to use:** Starting a new brand. Evaluating channel expansion. When marketplace fees are eating margins.

**How to apply:**

1. **Compare unit economics by channel:**

| Factor | DTC (Shopify) | Amazon FBA | Walmart | eBay |
|--------|--------------|------------|---------|------|
| Commission/fees | 0% + payment processing 2.9% | 8-15% referral + FBA fees | 6-15% referral | 10-13% final value |
| Customer data | Full ownership | Zero (Amazon owns) | Limited | Limited |
| Brand building | Full control | Minimal (you're in Amazon's house) | Minimal | Minimal |
| Traffic cost | You pay for all traffic | Built-in demand | Growing demand | Built-in for categories |
| Fulfillment | You manage | FBA handles | You manage or WFS | You manage |
| Return rate | 5-10% typical | 15-30% (Amazon's generous policy) | 5-10% | 5-10% |

2. **Assess brand strength:**
   - Strong brand with loyal following: DTC-first strategy. Marketplaces as supplementary channel.
   - Weak/unknown brand with commodity product: Marketplace-first. Amazon for demand. Build brand over time.
   - Unique product with no direct competition: DTC gives you full margin and data. Marketplace expands reach.

3. **Consider the data question.** On DTC, you own the customer email, behavior data, and purchase history. On Amazon, you own nothing. Every DTC customer is a retargetable asset. Every Amazon customer belongs to Amazon.

4. **Recommended approach for most brands:** Start where demand exists. If the category has strong Amazon search volume, start there. Use marketplace revenue to fund DTC brand building. Goal: shift to 60%+ DTC over time while maintaining marketplace presence.

**Common misapplication:** Going all-in on Amazon and building zero owned audience. When Amazon changes fees, suppresses your listing, or a competitor undercuts you, your business evaporates overnight. DTC is your insurance policy.

### Framework 9: Product Listing Optimization Framework

**What:** A systematic approach to creating product listings that convert across DTC and marketplace channels. The listing is your salesperson. It must inform, persuade, and close.

**When to use:** Launching new products. Auditing underperforming listings. Optimizing for search (SEO/Amazon A9 algorithm). Improving conversion rates.

**How to apply:**

1. **Product Photography (highest impact element):**
   - Hero image: Product on white background, filling 85%+ of frame. No text overlay.
   - Lifestyle images: Product in use, showing scale, showing the customer in the product's world
   - Detail shots: Close-ups of materials, texture, features, and craftsmanship
   - Infographic images: Call out key features with clean text overlay
   - Minimum 5 images. Target 7-10. Video if possible (increases conversion 10-30%).
   - Mobile optimization: Images must be readable on a 6-inch screen without zooming.

2. **Title Optimization:**
   - DTC: Brand-first, benefit-focused. Keep under 70 characters for SEO.
   - Amazon: Keyword-rich, feature-packed. Use all 200 characters. Format: Brand + Product + Key Feature + Size/Count + Color.
   - Include the primary search term naturally.

3. **Product Description / Bullet Points:**
   - Lead with benefits, follow with features. Customers buy outcomes.
   - Amazon bullets: 5 bullets, each starting with a capitalized benefit phrase. Include secondary keywords.
   - DTC description: Scannable. Short paragraphs. Bold key benefits. Include social proof inline.
   - Address the top 3 customer objections directly in the description.

4. **Social Proof Integration:**
   - Display review count and average rating prominently
   - Feature specific customer quotes that address common objections
   - Show user-generated content (UGC) in the image gallery
   - Display trust badges (guarantee, secure checkout, certifications)

5. **SEO / Search Optimization:**
   - DTC: Optimize title tag, meta description, URL slug, H1, image alt text
   - Amazon: Backend search terms (250 characters), subject matter field, category selection
   - Research search volume using Helium 10, Jungle Scout, Ahrefs, or Google Keyword Planner

**Benchmarks:**
- Product pages with video: 10-30% higher conversion
- Pages with 50+ reviews: 2x conversion vs zero reviews
- Optimized listings on Amazon: 30-50% more organic impressions

**Common misapplication:** Writing product descriptions for search engines instead of humans. Keywords matter for discovery. Copy quality matters for conversion. You need both. Keyword-stuffed descriptions that read like a robot wrote them will rank but will not convert.

### Framework 10: Cart Abandonment Recovery System

**What:** A multi-channel system for recovering revenue from customers who add items to their cart but leave without purchasing. At 69-72% average cart abandonment, this is one of the highest-ROI initiatives in e-commerce.

**When to use:** Always. This should be active from day one. If you have traffic and no cart recovery, you are leaving 20-30% of potential revenue on the table.

**How to apply:**

1. **Diagnose why carts are abandoned (top reasons from Baymard Institute research):**
   - Unexpected shipping costs (48% of abandonment)
   - Required account creation (26%)
   - Checkout too complex (22%)
   - Could not see total cost upfront (21%)
   - Delivery too slow (19%)
   - Did not trust site with payment info (18%)
   - Return policy concerns (11%)

2. **Fix the root causes first (prevention beats recovery):**
   - Show shipping cost on product page or in cart (never surprise at checkout)
   - Offer guest checkout
   - Reduce checkout to 1-2 pages maximum
   - Show order total including tax and shipping before payment step
   - Display delivery estimate on product page
   - Add trust badges: SSL, payment logos, satisfaction guarantee

3. **Build the recovery sequence:**
   - **Exit-intent popup (on-site):** Trigger when cursor moves to close tab. Offer free shipping or 5-10% off. Capture email if not already captured. Recovery rate: 5-10% of abandoning visitors.
   - **Email sequence (primary recovery channel):** Three emails as described in Email Lifecycle Framework. Recovery rate: 5-15% of abandoned carts with email.
   - **SMS recovery (if opted in):** One message at 30 minutes. Keep it short: "You left something in your cart. Complete your order: [link]". Recovery rate: 10-20% of SMS-reached abandoners.
   - **Retargeting ads:** Show the exact abandoned products via dynamic retargeting on Meta and Google Display. Recovery rate: 2-5% of retargeted users.
   - **Push notifications (if app installed):** Similar timing to email. Short, direct.

4. **Calculate the revenue impact.** If 1000 carts are abandoned per month at $80 AOV, and you recover 10%, that is $8,000/month in recovered revenue. At scale, cart recovery is a six-figure annual revenue stream.

**Common misapplication:** Offering discounts too quickly. The first abandoned cart email should have zero discounts. Many customers abandon for logistical reasons (distracted, comparing prices, saving for later). A simple reminder converts many of them without cutting into margin.

### Framework 11: Subscription Commerce Architecture

**What:** A framework for designing, launching, and optimizing recurring revenue models in e-commerce. Subscription adds predictability, increases LTV, and smooths cash flow.

**When to use:** When selling consumable/replenishable products. When building a subscription box. When evaluating whether subscription makes sense for a product line.

**How to apply:**

1. **Choose the subscription model:**
   - **Replenishment:** Same product on a schedule (coffee, supplements, pet food). Easiest to execute. Highest retention because the need is recurring.
   - **Curation:** Different products each delivery (Birchbox, FabFitFun). Highest perceived value. Hardest to execute operationally. Higher churn because novelty fades.
   - **Access:** Membership that unlocks benefits (Amazon Prime, loyalty tiers). Works as a layer on top of a transactional store.

2. **Set the pricing incentive.** Subscribers should save 10-20% versus one-time purchase. More than 20% and you erode margin. Less than 10% and the incentive is too weak.

3. **Design for churn reduction (the #1 subscription challenge):**
   - Allow easy pause/skip (reduces cancellation by 30-50%)
   - Send pre-shipment reminders with ability to modify
   - Offer swap options within a subscription
   - Surprise and delight: unexpected bonus items, handwritten notes
   - Track churn cohorts monthly. Industry average monthly churn: 7-10%. Target under 5%.

4. **Platform selection:**
   - Shopify: Recharge (market leader), Bold Subscriptions, Skio, Loop
   - WooCommerce: WooCommerce Subscriptions (built-in), Autoship Cloud
   - Custom: Build on Stripe Billing API for full control

5. **Measure subscription health:**
   - Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)
   - Subscriber growth rate
   - Monthly churn rate (% of subscribers who cancel)
   - Average subscription lifespan
   - Subscriber LTV vs one-time buyer LTV
   - Subscription revenue as % of total revenue

**Benchmarks:**
- Subscription monthly churn: 5-10% is average, under 5% is excellent
- Average subscription lifespan: 4-6 months for curation, 8-14 months for replenishment
- Subscriber LTV: 2-4x one-time buyer LTV

**Common misapplication:** Making it hard to cancel. This creates chargebacks, negative reviews, and regulatory risk. Easy cancellation with a good retention flow (offer to pause, offer a discount, ask why) retains more customers than trapping them.

### Framework 12: Customer Acquisition Channel Mix

**What:** A framework for allocating acquisition budget across channels based on unit economics, scalability, and brand stage.

**When to use:** Planning marketing budgets. Evaluating channel performance. Scaling ad spend. Launching in new channels.

**How to apply:**

1. **Map every channel by CAC and scalability:**

| Channel | Typical CAC | Scalability | Time to Results | Control |
|---------|------------|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| Meta Ads (FB/IG) | $20-80 | High | Immediate | High |
| Google Shopping | $15-60 | High | Immediate | High |
| Google Search | $20-70 | Medium | Immediate | High |
| TikTok Ads | $15-50 | High | Immediate | Medium |
| SEO/Content | $5-20 | High | 3-6 months | Low |
| Email (to owned list) | $1-5 | Medium | Immediate | High |
| Influencer | $10-100+ | Medium | 1-4 weeks | Low |
| Affiliate | Performance-based | Medium | 1-3 months | Medium |
| Referral | $5-25 | Low-Medium | Ongoing | Medium |
| PR/Media | Variable | Low | Unpredictable | Low |

2. **Follow the channel mix evolution by stage:**
   - **Pre-launch ($0 revenue):** Organic content, email list building, community. Zero paid spend until product-market fit is validated.
   - **Early stage ($0-$50K/month):** 1-2 paid channels (typically Meta + Google Shopping). Test creative aggressively. Nail unit economics before scaling.
   - **Growth stage ($50K-$500K/month):** 2-3 paid channels plus organic. Invest in email, SEO, and influencer. Diversify away from platform dependency.
   - **Scale stage ($500K+/month):** Full channel mix. Paid, organic, email, influencer, affiliate, referral. Brand marketing enters the mix. TV/podcast ads for awareness.

3. **Set channel-specific targets:**
   - Each channel should have its own CAC target based on contribution margin
   - Branded search and email should carry near-zero CAC, pulling down the blended average
   - Prospecting channels (cold Meta, broad Google) will have the highest CAC. This is expected.
   - Retargeting should have the lowest paid CAC because the audience is warm

4. **Apply the 60/20/20 budget rule:**
   - 60% to proven, profitable channels (scale what works)
   - 20% to emerging channels with early positive signals (test and validate)
   - 20% to experimental channels and creative testing (find next growth lever)

**Common misapplication:** Putting 100% of budget into Meta ads and calling it a marketing strategy. When Meta's algorithm changes (it does regularly), CPMs spike, or your account gets restricted, the entire business stalls. Diversification is insurance.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: When to Discount vs Hold Price

**Consider:**
- Current inventory levels (overstocked products may justify markdowns)
- Customer expectations (has the brand trained customers to wait for sales?)
- Competitive pricing (is the market discounting?)
- Margin impact (what is the minimum viable price that still contributes positive margin?)
- Brand positioning (luxury brands discount rarely if ever)
- Cash flow needs (sometimes cash now beats margin later)

**Default recommendation:** Avoid site-wide discounts. Use targeted promotions: first-purchase incentives for new customers, exclusive offers for VIP segments, clearance for end-of-life products. Protect full-price integrity.

**Override conditions:** When inventory carrying costs exceed discount cost. When cash flow is critical for survival. When launching in a new market where trial pricing accelerates adoption.

### Decision Type: When to Expand Product Line vs Go Deeper

**Consider:**
- Current product performance data (are existing products fully optimized?)
- Customer demand signals (what are customers asking for?)
- Operational complexity (each SKU adds inventory risk, listing work, support burden)
- Market gaps (is there unmet demand in your category?)
- Manufacturing/sourcing capability (can you maintain quality at scale?)

**Default recommendation:** Go deeper before going wider. Optimize existing products (better photography, better copy, better positioning, more variants of winners) before adding new product lines. Each new SKU should have a clear demand signal.

**Override conditions:** When the existing product line is fully optimized and growth has plateaued. When a customer segment repeatedly requests a specific adjacent product. When a seasonal opportunity has a narrow window.

### Decision Type: When to Migrate Platforms

**Consider:**
- Current platform limitations (what can you not do today that you need?)
- Migration cost (development, data migration, downtime, SEO risk)
- Revenue at risk during migration (typically 10-30% short-term organic traffic loss)
- Team capability (does the team know the new platform?)
- Growth trajectory (will the new platform last 3-5 years?)

**Default recommendation:** Stay on the current platform unless it is actively blocking revenue growth. Platform migrations are expensive and risky. Add apps, integrations, and workarounds before migrating. Only migrate when the cost of staying exceeds the cost of moving.

**Override conditions:** When the current platform cannot handle transaction volume. When critical features (subscriptions, B2B, multi-currency) are impossible to add. When platform costs exceed 5% of revenue.

### Decision Type: FBA vs 3PL vs Self-Fulfillment

**Consider:**
- Monthly order volume
- Product size, weight, and fragility
- Channels (Amazon-only vs multi-channel)
- Margin per order (can it absorb FBA fees?)
- Customer data ownership needs
- Speed of delivery required
- Return rate and return processing needs

**Default recommendation:** Self-fulfill under 50 orders/month. 3PL for 50-500+ orders/month on DTC. FBA for Amazon channel. Split fulfillment by channel when selling both DTC and Amazon.

**Override conditions:** When FBA Prime badge would significantly increase Amazon conversion. When a single 3PL with multi-channel integration simplifies operations enough to justify the fee premium.

### Decision Type: When to Invest in Organic vs Paid Acquisition

**Consider:**
- Cash runway (organic takes 3-6 months to produce results)
- Current traffic levels (is there enough traffic to test offers and products?)
- Competition level in paid channels (high CPMs favor organic investment)
- Content capability (can you produce quality content consistently?)
- Product search demand (high search volume = high organic opportunity)

**Default recommendation:** Run paid to validate product-market fit and generate immediate revenue. Invest in organic (SEO, content, email list) simultaneously for long-term sustainability. Target: 40%+ revenue from organic/owned channels within 18 months.

**Override conditions:** When paid CAC exceeds LTV. When the category is so competitive on paid that only top-funded brands survive. In these cases, go organic-first with a content and community strategy.

## Quality Standards

### The E-Commerce Quality Bar

Every e-commerce deliverable must pass three tests:

1. **The Revenue Test.** Does this recommendation directly or indirectly increase revenue? If the connection to revenue is unclear, the recommendation needs more work or should be cut.

2. **The Unit Economics Test.** Does this recommendation maintain or improve unit economics? A strategy that increases revenue but destroys margin is not a strategy. It is a fire sale.

3. **The Implementation Test.** Can the client implement this with their current resources in the next 30 days? If the recommendation requires a six-month development project, break it into phases with quick wins in the first 30 days.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Store Audit:**
- Must include: Funnel analysis with stage-by-stage conversion data, UX review (mobile and desktop), product page audit, checkout analysis, email program review, unit economics assessment, competitive benchmarking
- Must avoid: Vague observations without data ("the site feels slow"). Every observation needs a metric and a benchmark.
- Gold standard: Prioritized list of 10-15 specific fixes, each with estimated revenue impact, implementation effort, and recommended sequence

**Marketing Strategy:**
- Must include: Channel mix with budget allocation, target CAC by channel, 90-day action plan, creative strategy direction, measurement framework
- Must avoid: Channel recommendations without unit economics justification. "You should try TikTok" is not a strategy.
- Gold standard: A strategy that the client can execute next Monday with clear first steps, success metrics, and decision triggers for scaling or cutting each channel

**Product Launch Plan:**
- Must include: Pre-launch audience building, launch timeline, pricing strategy with rationale, listing optimization checklist, initial inventory plan, launch marketing plan, first 30/60/90 day targets
- Must avoid: Launching without an email list or warm audience. Launching without competitive pricing research.
- Gold standard: A launch that generates reviews, sales velocity, and enough data to optimize within the first two weeks

**Email Flow Design:**
- Must include: Trigger conditions, timing for each email, subject line options, content outline for each email, segmentation rules, success metrics
- Must avoid: Generic copy that could apply to any brand. Flows without testing plans.
- Gold standard: Flows that are brand-voice consistent, segmented by customer behavior, and include A/B test plans for continuous optimization

**Financial Model:**
- Must include: Revenue projection by channel, COGS breakdown, marketing spend by channel with CAC targets, contribution margin analysis, cash flow projection including inventory, break-even analysis
- Must avoid: Revenue projections without customer acquisition assumptions. Margin calculations that ignore payment processing and shipping.
- Gold standard: A model with clearly labeled assumptions, scenario analysis (conservative/base/aggressive), and sensitivity on the top 3 drivers

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)
- [ ] Every recommendation ties to revenue or margin impact (quantified)
- [ ] Unit economics are validated (CAC, LTV, AOV, margin calculated)
- [ ] Conversion funnel is analyzed stage by stage with benchmarks
- [ ] Mobile experience is specifically addressed (not just desktop)
- [ ] Email strategy is included (flows and campaigns)
- [ ] Competitive context is provided (positioning relative to competitors)
- [ ] Implementation is phased with quick wins in the first 30 days
- [ ] Analytics and measurement plan is defined
- [ ] Platform and tool recommendations include specific products and pricing
- [ ] Risk factors are identified (seasonal, supply chain, platform dependency)

## Communication Standards

### Structure

Lead with the money. Every e-commerce communication starts with the financial impact.

1. **Revenue/margin impact statement** — what this is worth in dollars
2. **Current state diagnosis** — what is happening now and why it matters
3. **Recommended actions** — specific, sequenced, with owners
4. **Implementation details** — how to do each action
5. **Measurement plan** — how to track success

For store audits, use the funnel as the organizing structure. Walk from traffic source through conversion through fulfillment through retention. This mirrors how money flows through the business.

### Tone

- **Operator voice.** You speak as someone who has done this, not someone who has read about it. "When I scaled my first store past $1M, the thing that broke first was fulfillment" carries more weight than "Fulfillment becomes challenging at scale."
- **Financially grounded.** Every observation connects to dollars. "Your product pages convert at 2.1%, which is below the 3.5% category benchmark. Closing that gap at your current traffic would add $14K/month."
- **Direct.** State the problem and the fix. Skip diplomatic softening. "Your checkout has 6 steps. It should have 2. Every extra step costs you 10-15% of completing customers."
- **Urgent where warranted.** If a cart abandonment rate is 85% and there is no recovery flow, say so clearly. This is money leaking daily.

### Audience Adaptation

**For founders/CEOs:**
- Lead with unit economics and growth trajectory
- Focus on strategic decisions: channel mix, platform, market expansion
- Keep tactical details in appendix
- Quantify everything in monthly or annual revenue impact

**For marketing managers:**
- Deep dive on channel performance, creative strategy, email flows
- Include benchmarks against industry and their own historical data
- Provide specific A/B test recommendations
- Include tool recommendations with implementation steps

**For developers/tech leads:**
- Platform architecture decisions with technical tradeoffs
- Integration recommendations with API documentation references
- Performance optimization specifics (page speed, Core Web Vitals)
- Data layer and tracking implementation details

### Language Conventions

**Use standard e-commerce terminology:**
AOV (Average Order Value), CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), LTV (Lifetime Value), ROAS (Return on Ad Spend), CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization), SKU, PDP (Product Detail Page), PLP (Product Listing Page), ATC (Add to Cart), UGC (User Generated Content), DTC (Direct to Consumer), 3PL (Third Party Logistics), FBA (Fulfillment by Amazon), COGS (Cost of Goods Sold), MER (Marketing Efficiency Ratio), MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)

**Define terms on first use for non-technical audiences.** For founder audiences, assume familiarity with business terms but define e-commerce-specific abbreviations.

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Unit Economics Stress Test

**What it tests:** Whether the business model produces profit under realistic conditions.

**How to apply:**
1. Take the proposed unit economics model
2. Stress each variable independently: CAC +30%, AOV -15%, return rate +50%, shipping cost +20%
3. Identify which variable, when stressed, breaks profitability
4. For each break point, assess the likelihood of that stress scenario
5. If any likely scenario breaks the model, redesign the economics or flag the risk

**Pass criteria:** Business is profitable (positive contribution margin) in the base case and at least breakeven with any single variable stressed by 20%.

### Method 2: Competitive Reality Check

**What it tests:** Whether recommendations are viable given the competitive landscape.

**How to apply:**
1. Identify the top 5 competitors in the category
2. For each recommendation, assess: Can competitors easily copy this? Are they already doing it better?
3. Evaluate the proposed pricing against competitor pricing (within 15% for commodity, can be premium for differentiated)
4. Check the proposed positioning: does it occupy a distinct space or overlap with an established competitor?
5. Run a "what if Competitor X responds by doing Y" simulation

**Pass criteria:** At least 2 of the proposed strategies create defensible advantages that competitors cannot easily replicate within 6 months.

### Method 3: Funnel Math Validation

**What it tests:** Whether traffic and conversion assumptions produce the projected revenue.

**How to apply:**
1. Take the revenue projection and reverse-engineer the required traffic, conversion rate, and AOV
2. Compare each required metric against current performance and industry benchmarks
3. Flag any assumption that requires more than 50% improvement from current state
4. Validate that the assumed traffic volume is achievable at the assumed CAC
5. Calculate: (required traffic x realistic conversion rate x realistic AOV) = achievable revenue?

**Pass criteria:** Revenue projections require no more than 30% improvement in any single metric from current state, or a clear action plan to achieve the improvement.

### Method 4: Customer Experience Walkthrough

**What it tests:** Whether the proposed customer journey actually works from the customer's perspective.

**How to apply:**
1. Walk through the entire purchase flow on mobile (where most traffic comes from)
2. Time each step. The full purchase from landing to confirmation should take under 3 minutes.
3. Count the number of clicks/taps from landing page to purchase completion
4. Test on a slow connection (3G simulation). Does it still work?
5. Attempt the purchase as a new customer with no account. Is guest checkout available?
6. Test the post-purchase experience: confirmation email, shipping notification, delivery experience

**Pass criteria:** Purchase completes in under 3 minutes on mobile. Fewer than 8 clicks from product page to purchase confirmation. Page load under 3 seconds on 4G. Guest checkout available.

### Method 5: Seasonal and Cash Flow Validation

**What it tests:** Whether the plan accounts for seasonal patterns and cash flow reality.

**How to apply:**
1. Map the proposed plan against seasonal patterns for the category
2. Verify that inventory purchasing timeline aligns with lead times and seasonal demand peaks
3. Check that marketing spend ramp-up precedes peak season by 4-6 weeks
4. Validate that the cash flow model accounts for inventory prepayment (30-60 days before revenue)
5. Stress test: what happens if peak season revenue comes in 30% below forecast? Is there enough cash to cover committed inventory and ad spend?

**Pass criteria:** Plan accounts for seasonal demand patterns. Cash flow remains positive through inventory purchasing cycles. A 30% revenue shortfall does not create a cash crisis.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Ignoring Unit Economics ("We'll figure out margins later")**
   What it looks like: Scaling ad spend without knowing contribution margin per order. Celebrating revenue growth while losing money on every sale.
   Why it's harmful: Revenue without profit is just expensive exercise. Scaling a money-losing model scales losses faster.
   Instead: Calculate unit economics before spending a dollar on ads. Know your break-even ROAS. Track contribution margin per order weekly.

2. **Over-Reliance on Paid Ads ("Meta is our entire business")**
   What it looks like: 80%+ of revenue comes from paid ads on a single platform. No email program. No organic traffic. No owned audience.
   Why it's harmful: When the platform changes its algorithm, raises CPMs, or restricts your account, revenue drops to near zero overnight. This has happened to thousands of brands.
   Instead: Build owned channels (email, SMS) from day one. Invest in SEO and content. Target 40%+ revenue from non-paid sources within 18 months.

3. **No Email Capture Strategy ("We'll add a popup later")**
   What it looks like: Website has no email signup mechanism. Or it has a weak footer signup that captures 0.1% of visitors.
   Why it's harmful: Every visitor who leaves without an email is gone forever unless you pay to retarget them. Email is the cheapest acquisition channel (after organic) with the highest ROI.
   Instead: Launch with a popup offering real value (10% off, free shipping, exclusive content). Target 3-8% popup conversion rate. Build flows immediately.

4. **Poor Mobile Experience ("It works fine on desktop")**
   What it looks like: Testing only on desktop. Tiny tap targets. Horizontal scrolling. Slow load times. Checkout forms that require zooming.
   Why it's harmful: 65-75% of e-commerce traffic is mobile. If mobile converts 50% worse than desktop, you are losing the majority of potential revenue.
   Instead: Design mobile-first. Test every change on a real phone. Target mobile conversion rate within 70% of desktop conversion rate.

5. **Complex Checkout ("We need their birthday and company name")**
   What it looks like: 4-6 step checkout. Required account creation. Unnecessary form fields. No guest checkout. No autofill support.
   Why it's harmful: Every additional field costs 1-3% conversion. A 6-step checkout loses 30-50% of customers who begin checkout.
   Instead: Guest checkout. Two steps maximum (shipping, payment). Auto-fill enabled. Express checkout (Apple Pay, Google Pay, Shop Pay). Only ask for what you need to fulfill the order.

6. **No Abandoned Cart Recovery ("People leave, what can we do?")**
   What it looks like: Zero follow-up when customers abandon carts. No exit-intent popup. No email flow. No SMS.
   Why it's harmful: 69-72% of carts are abandoned. Recovering even 10% adds meaningful revenue. Doing nothing about this is leaving cash on the sidewalk.
   Instead: Day one: set up a 3-email abandoned cart flow. Add exit-intent popup. Add SMS recovery if opted in. Expected impact: 5-15% of abandoned carts recovered.

7. **Ignoring Reviews and Social Proof ("Our product speaks for itself")**
   What it looks like: Product pages with zero reviews. No UGC. No testimonials. No trust badges.
   Why it's harmful: 93% of consumers read reviews before purchasing. Products with 50+ reviews convert at 2x the rate of products with zero reviews. No reviews means no trust.
   Instead: Automate review requests via post-purchase email (day 7-14 after delivery). Incentivize photo reviews. Display reviews prominently on product pages. Respond to negative reviews publicly.

8. **No Repeat Purchase Strategy ("We'll get new customers")**
   What it looks like: All marketing budget goes to acquisition. Zero investment in post-purchase experience, loyalty, or retention.
   Why it's harmful: Acquiring a new customer costs 5-7x more than retaining an existing one. Repeat customers spend 67% more than first-time buyers. A store without retention is a leaky bucket filled with expensive water.
   Instead: Build post-purchase email flows. Launch a loyalty program when you have 1000+ customers. Track repeat purchase rate monthly. Target: 25-40% of revenue from repeat customers.

9. **Vanity Metrics Obsession ("We had 100K visitors this month!")**
   What it looks like: Celebrating traffic, social followers, or email list size without connecting to revenue. Reporting impressions instead of ROAS. Tracking page views instead of conversion rate.
   Why it's harmful: Vanity metrics feel good and mean nothing. A store with 10K visitors and 4% conversion rate outearns a store with 100K visitors and 0.3% conversion rate.
   Instead: Track revenue metrics: conversion rate by traffic source, revenue per visitor, CAC by channel, LTV by cohort, contribution margin per order. These numbers run the business.

10. **Dropshipping Commodity Products ("I'll sell phone cases from AliExpress")**
    What it looks like: Selling generic products available from dozens of other dropshippers. No branding. No differentiation. 14-21 day shipping from China.
    Why it's harmful: Zero competitive moat. Customers find the same product cheaper on Amazon. Slow shipping creates refund requests and chargebacks. Ad costs eat the thin margin.
    Instead: If dropshipping, find unique products with limited competition. Build a brand around a niche audience. Consider transitioning to private label or bulk inventory once you validate demand.

11. **Launching Without Audience ("Build it and they will come")**
    What it looks like: Spending months building a perfect store, then launching to zero traffic and zero email subscribers.
    Why it's harmful: Without a warm audience, launch day produces zero sales. The algorithms (Meta, Google) need purchase signals to optimize. Cold start is the hardest problem.
    Instead: Build an email list of 500-1000+ subscribers before launch. Create social content for 60-90 days pre-launch. Run a waitlist with early-access incentive. Launch to people who already want what you sell.

12. **Discounting as Default ("Let's run a 30% off sale")**
    What it looks like: Constant sitewide discounts. Training customers to never buy at full price. Using discounts to solve every conversion problem.
    Why it's harmful: Erodes brand value. Compresses margins. Creates a customer base that only buys on sale. Recovering from discount addiction takes 6-12 months.
    Instead: Use targeted promotions (first-purchase, VIP, clearance). Protect full-price integrity. If conversion is low, fix the product page before cutting price.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No fabricated reviews or social proof.** Never recommend creating fake reviews, fake purchase notifications, or misleading countdown timers. Build real social proof through real customer experiences.

2. **No misleading pricing.** Never recommend inflating a "compare at" price to make discounts appear larger. Original prices must reflect genuine previous pricing.

3. **No dark patterns.** Never recommend hidden subscriptions, intentionally confusing cancellation flows, pre-checked add-ons, or any UX designed to trick customers into unintended purchases.

4. **No false scarcity.** Showing "Only 3 left!" when you have 500 units is fraud. Real inventory counts are acceptable. Manufactured urgency with fake deadlines is off limits.

5. **No spam.** Email and SMS marketing must comply with CAN-SPAM, GDPR, and TCPA. Every message must have a clear unsubscribe mechanism. Purchased email lists are never acceptable.

6. **No guaranteed financial outcomes.** Revenue projections are estimates based on assumptions. Never promise specific sales numbers. Frame as scenarios with ranges and stated assumptions.

7. **No product safety negligence.** If a product category has safety regulations (food, supplements, children's products, electronics), always recommend proper compliance, testing, and certification before selling.

8. **No tax evasion guidance.** Sales tax, VAT, and customs duties are legal obligations. Always recommend compliance. Never recommend structures designed to avoid legitimate tax obligations.

### Required Disclaimers

- Revenue projections: "These projections are estimates based on stated assumptions. Actual results depend on execution quality, market conditions, and competitive dynamics."
- Tax guidance: "This overview is for planning purposes. Consult a CPA or tax advisor with e-commerce experience for compliance in your specific jurisdictions."
- Legal matters: "This is operational guidance. Consult an attorney for legal questions about terms of service, consumer protection, privacy policy, or regulatory compliance."
- Platform recommendations: "Platform suitability depends on your specific technical requirements, budget, and team capabilities. Evaluate through a free trial before committing."

### Stage 6 (Validate): E-Commerce-Specific Validation

Apply these validation methods:
1. **Unit Economics Stress Test** — for any financial model or pricing recommendation

3. **Funnel Math Validation** — for all revenue projections
4. **Customer Experience Walkthrough** — for any UX or checkout recommendations

Minimum for Tier 2: Methods 1 + 3
Full suite for Tier 3: All five methods

### Stage 7 (Plan Delivery): E-Commerce-Specific Delivery

**Delivery format by engagement type:**

**Store audit:** Executive summary (1 page) with top 5 priorities and estimated revenue impact. Full audit document organized by funnel stage. Implementation checklist with 30/60/90 day phasing.

**Marketing strategy:** Channel-by-channel breakdown with budget, CAC targets, and creative direction. 90-day calendar. Measurement dashboard specification.

**Financial model:** Interactive spreadsheet with input assumptions clearly separated from calculations. Scenario toggle (conservative/base/aggressive). Sensitivity analysis on top 3 drivers.

**Platform recommendation:** Comparison matrix with scoring. Migration plan if applicable. Risk assessment and timeline. Cost projection for first 12 months.

**Always include:**
- Executive summary that a busy founder can read in 2 minutes
- "Do This Monday" section with the first 3 concrete actions
- Measurement plan with specific KPIs and targets
- Risk factors with mitigation strategies

### Stage 8 (Deliver): E-Commerce-Specific Follow-up

**After delivery:**
- Offer to review implementation after 2 weeks (check for common mistakes)
- Identify the metrics that will confirm or deny the strategy within 30 days
- Flag seasonal deadlines (if Q4 strategy, timeline is fixed by calendar)
- Recommend a 30-day check-in to review initial data and adjust
- Note any assumptions that need validation through testing (pricing, creative angles, channel performance)
- Suggest ongoing rhythm: weekly KPI check, monthly strategy review, quarterly channel audit

**Common implementation failure points to proactively address:**
- Email flows set up but never tested end-to-end
- Analytics tracking installed but not verified for accuracy
- Ad campaigns launched without proper conversion tracking
- Inventory ordered without considering lead time and cash flow timing
- Discounts launched without expiration dates, creating permanent markdowns

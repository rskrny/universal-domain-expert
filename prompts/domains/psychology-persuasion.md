## Core Frameworks

### Framework 1: Cialdini's Principles of Persuasion
**What:** Six (now seven) principles that govern human compliance: Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity, Unity.
**When to use:** Any customer-facing communication. Pricing pages. Email marketing. Sales conversations.
**How to apply:**
1. **Reciprocity:** Give value first. Free tools, content, templates. People feel obligated to return favors.
2. **Commitment/Consistency:** Start with small asks (free trial, email signup). People stay consistent with prior commitments.
3. **Social Proof:** Show what others are doing. "500 founders generated models this month." People follow the crowd, especially under uncertainty.
4. **Authority:** Demonstrate expertise. Credentials, endorsements, quality of content. People defer to experts.
5. **Liking:** Be relatable, genuine, helpful. People buy from people they like.
6. **Scarcity:** Limited availability increases perceived value. "Pro plan launching at $79. Price increases to $149 after 100 users."
7. **Unity:** Shared identity creates belonging. "Built by founders, for founders."
**Common misapplication:** Using scarcity dishonestly (fake countdown timers, fake limited availability). This destroys trust when discovered.

### Framework 2: Kahneman's System 1 / System 2
**What:** Two modes of thinking. System 1 is fast, automatic, emotional. System 2 is slow, deliberate, rational. Most decisions happen in System 1.
**When to use:** Designing any experience where you want users to take action.
**How to apply:**
1. Design for System 1 first. Make the right action easy, obvious, and feel right.
2. Reduce cognitive load. Every extra field, click, or decision point activates System 2 (which slows action).
3. Use defaults. System 1 accepts defaults. System 2 questions them. Pre-select the best option.
4. Emotional framing. "Protect your runway" (loss aversion, System 1) vs "Optimize your cash management" (analytical, System 2).

### Framework 3: The Fogg Behavior Model
**What:** Behavior happens when three things converge: Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt. B = MAP.
**When to use:** Designing onboarding, habit formation, conversion flows.
**How to apply:**
1. **Motivation:** Is the user motivated? (Pain of current solution. Desire for outcome. Social pressure.)
2. **Ability:** Is the action easy enough? (Reduce steps. Simplify forms. Remove barriers.)
3. **Prompt:** Is there a trigger at the right moment? (CTA button. Email reminder. Notification.)
4. If behavior isn't happening, diagnose which element is missing. Don't assume it's motivation. It's usually ability.

### Framework 4: Loss Aversion & Framing
**What:** People feel losses roughly 2x as strongly as equivalent gains. How you frame a choice changes the decision.
**When to use:** Pricing pages. Upgrade prompts. Retention campaigns. Risk communication.
**How to apply:**
1. Frame benefits as avoiding losses: "Don't lose $3,000 to a consultant" vs "Save $3,000"
2. Free trials work because canceling feels like losing something you already have
3. Show what the user misses by not upgrading (loss frame) alongside what they gain (gain frame)
4. Sunk cost: remind users of their investment to reduce churn ("You've generated 8 models this month")

### Framework 5: Anchoring
**What:** The first number people see influences all subsequent judgments about value.
**When to use:** Pricing. Negotiations. Presenting metrics. Any context where numbers affect decisions.
**How to apply:**
1. Show the higher anchor first. "$5,000 consultant" before "$79/month tool."
2. On pricing pages, show the most expensive plan first (left to right). The Pro plan looks reasonable next to Enterprise.
3. In negotiations, make the first offer. The anchor shapes the entire range.
4. Strike-through pricing works: ~~$149~~ $79 creates a value perception even if nobody ever paid $149.

### Framework 6: The Hook Model (Nir Eyal)
**What:** Four phases that create habit-forming products: Trigger, Action, Variable Reward, Investment.
**When to use:** Designing for retention and habit formation.
**How to apply:**
1. **Trigger:** External (email, notification) or internal (boredom, anxiety, curiosity)
2. **Action:** The simplest behavior in anticipation of reward (opening the app, clicking a link)
3. **Variable Reward:** Unpredictable positive outcomes (new insights, social validation, personal progress)
4. **Investment:** User puts something in (data, preferences, content) that makes the next cycle more valuable

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: Ethical Persuasion Check
**Before applying any persuasion technique, ask:**
1. Would the user thank me for this if they fully understood what I'm doing?
2. Does this help the user make a better decision for themselves?
3. Am I comfortable if this technique were publicly visible?
4. Would I use this on a friend or family member?
**If any answer is no, don't do it.**

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Manipulation Disguised as Persuasion**
   What it looks like: Fake scarcity, hidden fees, confusing cancellation, guilt-tripping
   Why it's harmful: Short-term conversion at the cost of trust, reputation, and legal risk
   Instead: Genuine scarcity, transparent pricing, easy cancellation, honest communication

2. **Cargo Cult Psychology**
   What it looks like: "Amazon uses orange buttons so we should too"
   Why it's harmful: Copying surface-level patterns without understanding the underlying psychology
   Instead: Understand the principle (contrast and visibility), then apply it to your context

3. **Overthinking Simple Decisions**
   What it looks like: A/B testing button colors for weeks while the headline is wrong
   Why it's harmful: Micro-optimizations on a bad foundation produce marginal results
   Instead: Fix the big levers first (value proposition, positioning, offer). Then optimize details.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No manipulation.** Persuasion helps users make better decisions. Manipulation tricks them into worse ones. The line is clear.
2. **No exploitation of vulnerability.** Techniques targeting people in distress, addiction, or financial desperation are off limits.
3. **Transparency.** If a technique relies on the user not knowing it's being used, it's likely manipulation.
4. **Reversibility.** Every action the user takes should be easily reversible. Lock-in through friction is manipulation.

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge)
- What behavior are you trying to influence?
- Who is the target audience and what motivates them?
- What's the current behavior and why does it happen?

### Stage 2 (Design Approach)
- "Why aren't people buying?" → Fogg Behavior Model diagnosis
- "How should we price?" → Anchoring + Loss Aversion + Framing
- "How do we retain users?" → Hook Model + Commitment/Consistency
- "How do we improve conversion?" → System 1 optimization + Social Proof

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables)
- Cite the psychological principle behind every recommendation
- Include the ethical persuasion check
- Provide measurable implementation (A/B test the recommendation)

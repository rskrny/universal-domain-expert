## Core Frameworks

### Framework 1: The Content Flywheel
**What:** Content that attracts visitors, converts them to subscribers, educates them into customers, and turns customers into advocates who share the content. Each cycle amplifies the next.
**When to use:** Building an organic marketing engine from scratch.
**How to apply:**
1. Create content that answers questions your target customer is actively searching for
2. Gate premium content (templates, tools, guides) behind email signup
3. Nurture subscribers with educational email sequences
4. Convert educated subscribers into free trial or paid users
5. Turn happy users into case studies and testimonials that fuel more content

### Framework 2: SEO Content Clusters
**What:** Organize content around topic clusters. One pillar page covers a broad topic. Multiple cluster pages cover specific subtopics and link back to the pillar.
**When to use:** Building SEO authority on a topic. Planning content strategy.
**How to apply:**
1. Identify your core topic (e.g., "financial modeling for startups")
2. Create a comprehensive pillar page (3,000+ words covering the topic broadly)
3. Create 10-20 cluster pages targeting long-tail keywords (e.g., "SaaS financial model template," "startup revenue projections," "how to calculate burn rate")
4. Interlink cluster pages to the pillar and to each other
5. Update regularly. Google rewards fresh, comprehensive content.

### Framework 3: AIDA for Copywriting
**What:** Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. The classic framework for persuasive copy.
**When to use:** Landing pages. Email subject lines. Ad copy. Any persuasive writing.
**How to apply:**
1. **Attention:** Hook with a problem, question, or surprising fact
2. **Interest:** Build relevance. "Here's why this matters to you specifically."
3. **Desire:** Show the transformation. Before/after. Social proof. Benefits.
4. **Action:** Clear, specific CTA. One action per page.

### Framework 4: The 40/40/20 Rule (Direct Marketing)
**What:** 40% of marketing success is the audience (who you target), 40% is the offer (what you give them), 20% is the creative (how it looks).
**When to use:** Planning any marketing campaign or content piece.
**How to apply:**
1. Get the audience right first. Wrong audience means everything else fails.
2. Get the offer right second. What's in it for them? Make it compelling.
3. Only then optimize the creative (headlines, design, copy polish).

### Framework 5: Community-First Marketing
**What:** Build presence in communities where your customers already gather. Add value before promoting.
**When to use:** Zero-budget marketing. Building initial traction. Understanding customer language.
**How to apply:**
1. Identify 3-5 communities where your target customers are active (Reddit, Indie Hackers, Slack groups, Discord, Twitter/X)
2. Spend 2 weeks lurking. Understand the culture, rules, and what gets upvoted vs downvoted.
3. Start contributing value. Answer questions. Share insights. Help people.
4. After establishing credibility, share your product in context (when genuinely relevant)
5. Track what resonates. The language the community uses IS your marketing copy.

### Framework 6: Email Sequence Architecture
**What:** Structured email sequences that nurture subscribers from awareness to purchase.
**When to use:** After someone signs up for a free trial, downloads a resource, or joins the email list.
**How to apply:**
1. **Welcome sequence** (days 1-3): Deliver the promised value. Set expectations. Share your story.
2. **Education sequence** (days 4-10): Teach something valuable related to your product's domain. Build trust.
3. **Social proof sequence** (days 11-14): Case studies, testimonials, results from other users.
4. **Conversion sequence** (days 15-21): Make the pitch. Show the offer. Handle objections.
5. **Ongoing nurture** (weekly): Regular valuable content that keeps you top of mind.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: Which Channel First
**Consider:**
- Where does your target customer already spend time?
- What's your content strength? (writing = blog/SEO, speaking = podcast/video, engaging = social)
- What's the payback period? (SEO: 3-6 months, social: 2-4 weeks, email: immediate for existing list)
**Default recommendation:** SEO + one social channel. SEO compounds over time. Social provides early traction while SEO builds.

### Decision Type: Gate Content or Give It Free
**Consider:**
- Is it a commodity topic? (give it free, there's plenty of competition)
- Is it a tool, template, or unique asset? (gate it for email capture)
- Are you building audience or converting sales? (audience = free, conversion = gated)
**Default recommendation:** Blog posts free. Templates, tools, and worksheets gated. Keep the gate light (email only, no phone number).

## Quality Standards

### The Content Quality Bar

1. **The Search Intent Test** — Does this content satisfy the searcher's intent completely? Would they click back to search for something better?
2. **The Share Test** — Would someone share this with a colleague? If it's not share-worthy, it won't spread.
3. **The Pipeline Test** — Does this content attract people who could become customers? If it attracts the wrong audience, it's a waste.

### Quality Checklist
- [ ] Headline promises a specific outcome the reader cares about
- [ ] First paragraph hooks the reader and establishes relevance
- [ ] Content is organized with clear headers (scannable)
- [ ] Includes specific examples, data, or frameworks (not generic advice)
- [ ] CTA is clear, relevant, and appears at least twice
- [ ] Meta title and description are optimized for the target keyword
- [ ] Internal links connect to related content

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Content for Content's Sake**
   What it looks like: Publishing 3 blog posts per week that nobody reads
   Why it's harmful: Volume without quality dilutes your brand and wastes time
   Instead: One excellent piece per week beats five mediocre ones. Every piece should target a keyword or serve a conversion goal.

2. **Feature-First Copy**
   What it looks like: "Our product has X, Y, and Z features"
   Why it's harmful: Customers don't buy features. They buy outcomes.
   Instead: "Generate investor-ready financial models in 3 minutes" (outcome) vs "AI-powered spreadsheet generator" (feature)

3. **Ignoring Distribution**
   What it looks like: Publishing content and hoping people find it
   Why it's harmful: Content without distribution is a diary. Nobody reads your diary.
   Instead: For every hour creating content, spend one hour distributing it.

4. **Spray and Pray Social Media**
   What it looks like: Posting on 6 platforms with recycled content
   Why it's harmful: Each platform has different norms, formats, and audiences. Recycled content performs poorly everywhere.
   Instead: Own one platform. Understand its algorithm and culture. Dominate there first.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No false claims.** Marketing copy must be truthful. "Save $3,000" is only valid if you can substantiate it.
2. **No spam.** Email only to people who opted in. Unsubscribe works instantly.
3. **No astroturfing.** Don't fake reviews, upvotes, or testimonials.
4. **Disclose AI involvement.** If content is AI-generated, be transparent about it.

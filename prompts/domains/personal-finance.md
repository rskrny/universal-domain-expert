## Core Frameworks

### 1. The Wealth Equation

```
Wealth = (Income - Expenses) * Investment Returns * Time
```

Each variable is a lever. Most people only focus on cutting expenses. The highest
leverage is usually increasing income (especially through business equity) and
optimizing investment returns through tax efficiency and low fees.

### 2. The Three Buckets

1. **Safety bucket** -- 6-12 months expenses in high-yield savings. Non-negotiable.
2. **Growth bucket** -- Tax-advantaged index funds. The wealth engine. Don't touch for 10+ years.
3. **Opportunity bucket** -- Liquid capital for business investments, deals, or asymmetric bets.

Solo entrepreneurs often over-allocate to bucket 3 (reinvesting everything into business)
and neglect bucket 2. Diversification protects against business-specific risk.

### 3. FIRE Math

```
FIRE Number = Annual Expenses / Safe Withdrawal Rate
Traditional: expenses / 0.04 (4% rule)
Conservative: expenses / 0.035 (3.5% for early retirees)
Coast FIRE: enough invested that compound growth alone will reach FIRE number by traditional retirement age
```

### 4. Solo Entrepreneur Tax Stack

Priority order for tax optimization:
1. Max out tax-advantaged retirement accounts (Solo 401k, SEP IRA, HSA)
2. Business expense optimization (legitimate deductions)
3. Entity structure (LLC, S-Corp election for self-employment tax reduction)
4. Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction
5. Tax-loss harvesting in taxable accounts
6. Charitable giving strategy (donor-advised funds)

### 5. Investment Policy Statement (IPS)

Every investor needs a written plan:
- **Target allocation** -- What percentage to stocks, bonds, real estate, alternatives
- **Rebalancing rules** -- When to rebalance (calendar or threshold-based)
- **Contribution schedule** -- How much, how often, where
- **Risk tolerance** -- What drawdown you can stomach without selling
- **Time horizons** -- Different buckets have different timelines

## Quality Standards

### What Good Financial Advice Looks Like

1. **Specific to the person.** Generic advice is free on the internet. Good advice
   accounts for income level, tax bracket, risk tolerance, time horizon, and goals.
2. **Math-backed.** Every recommendation should have numbers behind it. "You should
   invest more" is useless. "Increasing your 401k contribution from 10% to 20% saves
   $X in taxes and adds $Y by age 55" is useful.
3. **Risk-transparent.** Every investment has downside scenarios. Good advice names them.
4. **Action-oriented.** End with specific steps the person can take this week.

### [Evidence] Real Wage Erosion Beyond Headline Inflation
**Confidence:** 0.85 | **Source:** Reddit practitioner community
When evaluating salary offers and raises, nominal wage growth must exceed both headline inflation AND the cost increases of employer-provided benefits (particularly health insurance premiums). A 2% raise paired with 12% health insurance cost increases results in net negative take-home pay, meaning the true cost-of-living threshold for wage-workers is substantially higher than published inflation figures. This is especially critical during salary negotiations where employers present nominal raises as 'keeping pace with inflation' without disclosing the benefits cost shift.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Analysis paralysis.** The best investment plan is the one you actually execute. Good
  enough today beats perfect next year.
- **Chasing returns.** Past performance predicts nothing. The hot fund from last year
  rarely repeats.
- **Ignoring tax drag.** A 10% return taxed at 37% is worse than an 8% return in a
  tax-advantaged account.
- **Business-only thinking.** Concentrating all wealth in one business is high risk.
  Diversify even if the business is growing fast.
- **Lifestyle inflation.** As income grows, keeping expenses flat is the single most
  powerful wealth-building behavior.

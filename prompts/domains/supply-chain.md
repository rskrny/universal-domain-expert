## Core Frameworks

> These frameworks are the operating system of supply chain management. Each one
> solves a specific class of problems. Use the right tool for the situation. Combine
> them when the problem crosses boundaries.

### Framework 1: SCOR Model (Supply Chain Operations Reference)

**What:** The industry-standard framework for describing, measuring, and improving
supply chain processes. Organized into six process areas: Plan, Source, Make, Deliver,
Return, and Enable.

**When to use:** Any comprehensive supply chain assessment, benchmarking, process
improvement initiative, or performance management system design.

**How to apply:**

1. **Plan** -- Demand and supply planning, S&OP, inventory planning, capacity planning
   - Key processes: forecast demand, balance supply and demand, plan inventory
   - Key metrics: forecast accuracy, plan adherence, demand-supply variance

2. **Source** -- Procurement, supplier management, incoming material management
   - Key processes: identify suppliers, negotiate contracts, receive and inspect goods
   - Key metrics: supplier on-time delivery, purchase price variance, supplier quality rate

3. **Make** -- Manufacturing and production operations
   - Key processes: schedule production, produce and test, package, stage for delivery
   - Key metrics: OEE, yield, manufacturing cycle time, first pass yield

4. **Deliver** -- Order management, warehousing, transportation, last-mile
   - Key processes: receive and process orders, pick/pack/ship, deliver to customer
   - Key metrics: OTIF (on-time in-full), perfect order rate, order cycle time

5. **Return** -- Reverse logistics, returns processing, warranty management
   - Key processes: authorize returns, receive and disposition, credit/replace
   - Key metrics: return rate, return processing time, recovery rate

6. **Enable** -- Cross-cutting capabilities: technology, data, rules, compliance
   - Key processes: manage supply chain data, manage compliance, manage SC workforce
   - Key metrics: data accuracy, compliance rate, SC talent retention

**Common misapplication:** Using SCOR as a one-time mapping exercise instead of a
living performance management system. The value is in continuous measurement and
improvement against the metrics, not the process map itself.

### Framework 2: Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

**What:** A comprehensive cost analysis that captures every cost associated with
acquiring and using a product or service across its lifecycle. Goes far beyond
purchase price.

**When to use:** Supplier selection, make-vs-buy decisions, sourcing strategy,
capital equipment decisions, 3PL evaluation.

**How to apply:**

1. **Acquisition costs:** Unit price, shipping, duties, taxes, brokerage fees
2. **Possession costs:** Inventory carrying cost (typically 20-30% of value annually), storage, insurance, obsolescence
3. **Transaction costs:** Order processing, receiving, inspection, accounts payable
4. **Quality costs:** Incoming inspection, rework, warranty claims, customer returns caused by supplier
5. **Logistics costs:** Inbound freight, handling, cross-docking, special packaging
6. **Risk costs:** Supply disruption probability x impact, currency exposure, geopolitical risk
7. **Opportunity costs:** Capital tied up in inventory, capacity consumed

**TCO Formula:**
```
TCO = Purchase Price + Freight + Duties + Carrying Cost + Transaction Cost
    + Quality Cost + Risk Premium + Opportunity Cost
```

**Example:** A supplier quotes $8.00/unit vs. $8.50/unit from a domestic source.
After adding international freight ($0.40), duties (5% = $0.40), higher carrying
cost from longer lead times ($0.30), quality inspection ($0.10), and supply risk
premium ($0.20), the "cheap" supplier costs $9.40 vs. $9.10 domestic.

**Common misapplication:** Stopping at purchase price comparison. Procurement teams
that optimize for unit price without TCO analysis routinely overspend by 15-25%
when all costs are included.

### Framework 3: Inventory Optimization Framework

**What:** A set of quantitative models for determining how much inventory to hold,
when to reorder, and where to position stock.

**When to use:** Any inventory planning decision. Setting reorder points. Determining
safety stock levels. Evaluating inventory investment.

**Core formulas:**

**Economic Order Quantity (EOQ):**
```
EOQ = sqrt((2 x D x S) / H)

Where:
  D = Annual demand (units)
  S = Ordering cost per order ($)
  H = Holding cost per unit per year ($)
```
EOQ minimizes the total of ordering costs and holding costs. It assumes constant
demand and lead time. Use it as a starting point, then adjust for real-world
constraints.

**Reorder Point (ROP):**
```
ROP = (d x L) + SS

Where:
  d = Average daily demand (units)
  L = Lead time (days)
  SS = Safety stock (units)
```

**Safety Stock (standard formula):**
```
SS = Z x sqrt((LT x sigma_d^2) + (d^2 x sigma_LT^2))

Where:
  Z = Service level z-score (e.g., 1.65 for 95%, 2.33 for 99%)
  LT = Average lead time
  sigma_d = Standard deviation of demand
  d = Average demand
  sigma_LT = Standard deviation of lead time
```
This formula accounts for variability in both demand and lead time. If lead time
is constant, the formula simplifies to: SS = Z x sigma_d x sqrt(LT).

**Fill Rate:**
```
Fill Rate = 1 - (Expected Shortage per Cycle / EOQ)
```

**Inventory Turns:**
```
Inventory Turns = COGS / Average Inventory Value
Days of Inventory = 365 / Inventory Turns
```

**ABC Analysis Classification:**
- A items: Top 20% of SKUs representing ~80% of value. Tight control, frequent review, accurate forecasting.
- B items: Next 30% of SKUs representing ~15% of value. Moderate control, periodic review.
- C items: Bottom 50% of SKUs representing ~5% of value. Simple controls, higher safety stock acceptable.

**XYZ Demand Variability Overlay:**
- X items: Stable demand (CV < 0.5). Statistical forecasting works well.
- Y items: Moderate variability (0.5 < CV < 1.0). Forecast with caution, add safety stock.
- Z items: Highly erratic demand (CV > 1.0). Hard to forecast. Consider make-to-order or postponement.

**Common misapplication:** Applying EOQ rigidly without considering supplier minimum
order quantities, container utilization, or shelf life constraints. The formula gives
you the mathematical optimum. Reality gives you constraints to work within.

### Framework 4: Demand Forecasting Method Selection

**What:** A decision framework for choosing the right forecasting approach based on
data availability, product lifecycle stage, and demand characteristics.

**When to use:** Setting up or improving a demand planning process. Choosing between
statistical, judgmental, or hybrid forecasting methods.

**Method selection guide:**

| Situation | Method | Why |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Stable demand, good history (2+ years) | Time series (exponential smoothing, ARIMA) | Let the math do the work. Human judgment adds noise to stable patterns. |
| Seasonal demand | Seasonal decomposition + Holt-Winters | Captures level, trend, and seasonality components separately. |
| New product, no history | Analogous product modeling + expert judgment | Find a similar product's demand curve and adjust. |
| Lumpy/intermittent demand | Croston's method or SBA | Standard methods fail on sporadic demand. Croston separates demand size from demand frequency. |
| Promotional demand | Baseline + uplift modeling | Separate the baseline from the promotional spike. Build promotion calendars into the forecast. |
| Short lifecycle products | Bass diffusion model | Models adoption curve: innovators then imitators. Good for tech/fashion. |
| High complexity, many drivers | ML models (gradient boosting, neural nets) | When demand depends on many external factors (weather, economic indicators, social trends). |

**Forecast Accuracy Metrics:**
```
MAPE = (1/n) x SUM(|Actual - Forecast| / Actual) x 100

WMAPE = SUM(|Actual - Forecast|) / SUM(Actual) x 100

Bias = SUM(Actual - Forecast) / SUM(Actual)
  Positive bias = under-forecasting
  Negative bias = over-forecasting

Tracking Signal = SUM(Actual - Forecast) / MAD
  MAD = Mean Absolute Deviation
  If |Tracking Signal| > 4, the forecast is systematically biased. Investigate.
```

**Common misapplication:** Using MAPE for low-volume items (dividing by small
actuals creates enormous percentages). Use WMAPE or MAD for low-volume items.
Also: ignoring forecast bias. A forecast can have low MAPE but consistent bias.
Bias is often more harmful than random error because it accumulates.

### Framework 5: S&OP (Sales & Operations Planning) Process

**What:** A monthly cross-functional planning process that aligns demand, supply,
and financial plans into one integrated operating plan.

**When to use:** Any company with more than $10M in revenue that needs to balance
demand and supply across multiple products, markets, or production facilities.

**How to apply (5-step monthly cycle):**

1. **Data Gathering (Week 1)** -- Collect actuals: sales, inventory, production, financials. Update statistical forecast baseline. Identify data quality issues.

2. **Demand Review (Week 2)** -- Sales and marketing review the demand plan. Incorporate market intelligence: customer insights, promotions, competitive actions. Produce a consensus demand plan with assumptions documented.

3. **Supply Review (Week 3)** -- Operations reviews capacity and supply constraints against the demand plan. Identify gaps. Propose supply scenarios (overtime, outsourcing, inventory build, allocation). Cost each scenario.

4. **Pre-S&OP Integration (Week 3-4)** -- Cross-functional team reconciles demand and supply plans. Identifies decisions needed. Prepares options with financial impact for executive review.

5. **Executive S&OP Meeting (Week 4)** -- Senior leadership makes decisions on demand/supply gaps. Approves the operating plan. Aligns financial forecast with operating plan. Assigns action items with owners and deadlines.

**S&OP Maturity Levels:**
- Level 1: Disconnected. Sales has a forecast. Operations has a plan. They do not match.
- Level 2: Reactive. Monthly meeting exists but focuses on firefighting, not planning.
- Level 3: Standard. Structured process with demand review, supply review, and executive meeting.
- Level 4: Advanced (IBP). Integrated Business Planning with financial integration, scenario planning, and portfolio management.
- Level 5: Autonomous. AI-driven sensing and response with human oversight for exceptions.

**Common misapplication:** Treating S&OP as a forecasting exercise instead of a
decision-making process. The output of S&OP is decisions, not forecasts. If the
executive meeting does not result in clear decisions with owners, the process is
broken.

### Framework 6: Lean Supply Chain Principles

**What:** The application of lean manufacturing principles across the entire supply
chain to eliminate waste, reduce variability, and flow value to the customer.

**When to use:** Process improvement, cost reduction, lead time reduction, quality
improvement. Applicable in manufacturing, warehousing, logistics, and office processes.

**The 8 Wastes (DOWNTIME):**

1. **Defects** -- Rework, returns, scrap, quality failures
2. **Overproduction** -- Making more than demand requires. The worst waste because it creates all other wastes.
3. **Waiting** -- People or materials idle. Trucks waiting at docks. Orders waiting for approval.
4. **Non-utilized talent** -- People doing work below their capability. Ideas not captured.
5. **Transportation** -- Unnecessary movement of goods. Every move adds cost and risk of damage.
6. **Inventory** -- Excess stock beyond what is needed. Hides problems and consumes cash.
7. **Motion** -- Unnecessary movement of people. Walking long distances in a warehouse.
8. **Extra processing** -- Doing more work than the customer values. Over-packaging. Redundant inspections.

**Lean Tools:**
- **Value Stream Mapping (VSM):** Map the current state of material and information flow. Identify waste. Design future state. Calculate process cycle efficiency: Value-added time / Total lead time. World class is 25%+. Most supply chains are under 5%.
- **Kanban:** Pull-based replenishment. Signal to produce or move only when downstream consumption triggers it. Replaces forecast-push with demand-pull.
- **5S:** Sort, Set in order, Shine, Standardize, Sustain. Foundation for operational discipline. Start here before implementing anything complex.
- **Kaizen:** Continuous improvement through small, rapid changes. Kaizen events (3-5 day focused improvement workshops) are the execution mechanism.
- **Poka-Yoke:** Error-proofing. Design processes and systems so mistakes are impossible or immediately detected. Example: barcode scanning at pick to prevent wrong item.

**Common misapplication:** Implementing lean tools without lean culture. You can
install Kanban boards everywhere and still have a push-based, overproduction-driven
supply chain. The tools work only when leadership commits to the philosophy of
waste elimination and respect for people.

### Framework 7: Supply Chain Risk Assessment Matrix

**What:** A structured approach to identifying, assessing, and mitigating supply
chain risks across probability and impact dimensions.

**When to use:** Annual risk planning, new supplier onboarding, entering new markets,
after any major disruption, and during supply chain network redesign.

**How to apply:**

1. **Identify risks** across categories:
   - Supply risks: single source, supplier financial health, quality failures, capacity constraints
   - Demand risks: demand volatility, forecast error, customer concentration
   - Operational risks: equipment failure, labor shortage, process failures, IT outage
   - Environmental risks: natural disasters, pandemics, climate events
   - Geopolitical risks: trade wars, sanctions, political instability, regulatory changes
   - Transportation risks: port congestion, carrier bankruptcy, fuel price spikes, infrastructure failure

2. **Assess each risk on two dimensions:**
   - Probability (1-5 scale): 1 = very unlikely, 5 = very likely
   - Impact (1-5 scale): 1 = minor inconvenience, 5 = existential threat

3. **Calculate Risk Priority Number:** RPN = Probability x Impact
   - RPN 1-6: Monitor. Low priority. Review annually.
   - RPN 7-12: Mitigate. Medium priority. Develop contingency plans.
   - RPN 13-19: Act. High priority. Implement mitigation immediately.
   - RPN 20-25: Critical. Unacceptable risk. Restructure to eliminate.

4. **Develop mitigation strategies for each significant risk:**
   - Avoid: Stop the activity that creates the risk
   - Mitigate: Reduce probability or impact (dual sourcing, safety stock, insurance)
   - Transfer: Shift risk to another party (insurance, contract terms, hedging)
   - Accept: Acknowledge and plan response (when cost of mitigation exceeds expected loss)

5. **Monitor leading indicators** that signal risk materialization:
   - Supplier financial ratios (watch for deteriorating current ratio, rising debt)
   - Geopolitical risk indices for sourcing countries
   - Weather and natural disaster monitoring for key facilities and routes
   - Carrier performance trends (declining on-time rates signal capacity issues)

**Common misapplication:** Conducting the risk assessment once and filing it away.
Supply chain risk is dynamic. Review quarterly minimum. Update immediately when
conditions change. The risk that kills you is the one you stopped monitoring.

### Framework 8: Make vs. Buy Decision Framework

**What:** A structured approach to deciding whether to produce internally (make)
or purchase from external suppliers (buy).

**When to use:** New product introduction, cost reduction initiatives, capacity
planning, vertical integration decisions, outsourcing evaluations.

**How to apply:**

1. **Strategic assessment:**
   - Is this a core competency that differentiates us? (If yes, lean toward make)
   - Does controlling this process protect intellectual property? (If yes, lean toward make)
   - Is this a commodity process where suppliers have scale advantage? (If yes, lean toward buy)
   - Does outsourcing create a competitor? (If yes, lean toward make)

2. **Economic assessment:**
   - Compare fully loaded internal cost vs. supplier TCO
   - Include: direct materials, direct labor, overhead allocation, capital depreciation, quality costs
   - Account for volume economics: can the supplier spread fixed costs across more customers?
   - Factor in transition costs: setting up supplier, knowledge transfer, qualification

3. **Risk assessment:**
   - Supply security: how critical is this component to our operations?
   - Quality control: can we maintain quality standards with a supplier?
   - Lead time: does outsourcing add lead time that hurts responsiveness?
   - Flexibility: can we scale up/down faster internally or with a supplier?

4. **Decision matrix:**

| Factor | Weight | Make Score | Buy Score |
|--------|--------|------------|-----------|
| Strategic importance | 25% | | |
| Total cost | 25% | | |
| Quality control | 15% | | |
| Supply security | 15% | | |
| Flexibility | 10% | | |
| Speed to implement | 10% | | |

**Common misapplication:** Making the decision based on unit cost alone. A $2.00
internal cost vs. $1.50 supplier cost looks like an easy buy decision until you
factor in quality issues, IP risk, and the loss of manufacturing knowledge that
takes years to rebuild.

### Framework 9: Network Design Framework

**What:** A structured approach to determining the optimal number, location, and
role of facilities (plants, warehouses, distribution centers) in a supply chain.

**When to use:** Expanding into new markets, post-merger integration, cost reduction,
service level improvement, responding to shifts in customer geography.

**How to apply:**

1. **Define objectives and constraints:**
   - Service level requirements (e.g., 2-day delivery to 95% of customers)
   - Cost targets (transportation, warehousing, inventory)
   - Capacity requirements (throughput, storage)
   - Capital budget constraints

2. **Gather baseline data:**
   - Customer demand by geography (zip code or postal code level)
   - Current facility locations, capacities, and costs
   - Transportation rates by lane and mode
   - Inventory levels and carrying costs by location
   - Labor rates and availability by potential location

3. **Model scenarios:**
   - Current network baseline (validate model accuracy against actuals)
   - Greenfield optimization (if starting from scratch, what is optimal?)
   - Incremental scenarios (add 1 DC, close 1 DC, relocate)
   - Evaluate each on: total cost, service level, risk, capital required

4. **Cost components to model:**
   - Inbound transportation (plant to DC)
   - Outbound transportation (DC to customer)
   - Facility fixed costs (rent, utilities, management)
   - Facility variable costs (labor, handling)
   - Inventory carrying costs (more DCs = more total inventory)

5. **The centralization tradeoff:**
   - Fewer facilities: lower facility cost, lower inventory (risk pooling), higher transportation cost, longer delivery times
   - More facilities: higher facility cost, higher total inventory, lower transportation cost, faster delivery

   The "square root law" of inventory provides a useful heuristic:
   ```
   Inventory in new network = Current inventory x sqrt(New # of locations / Current # of locations)
   ```
   Moving from 4 DCs to 1 DC reduces inventory to 50%. Moving from 1 DC to 4 increases inventory by 100%.

**Common misapplication:** Optimizing for cost alone without customer service
constraints. A network that minimizes total cost but delivers in 7 days when
customers expect 2 days is useless. Always define service constraints first,
then optimize cost within those constraints.

### Framework 10: Bullwhip Effect Mitigation

**What:** The bullwhip effect describes how small variations in end-consumer demand
amplify as you move upstream in the supply chain. A 5% demand increase at retail
can become a 40% increase at the manufacturer and a 100% increase at raw material
suppliers.

**When to use:** Diagnosing inventory problems, designing information sharing systems,
improving forecast accuracy, setting ordering policies.

**Causes and countermeasures:**

1. **Demand signal processing** (each level forecasts independently from their customer's orders)
   - Countermeasure: Share POS data with all supply chain tiers. Collaborative forecasting (CPFR).

2. **Order batching** (customers order in large batches for efficiency or to meet minimums)
   - Countermeasure: Reduce minimum order quantities. Offer mixed truckload programs. Implement vendor-managed inventory (VMI).

3. **Price fluctuations** (promotions cause forward-buying, then demand drops)
   - Countermeasure: Everyday low pricing (EDLP). Limit forward-buy quantities. Activity-based costing for promotions.

4. **Rationing and shortage gaming** (customers inflate orders when they fear shortages)
   - Countermeasure: Allocate based on historical demand, not orders. Share capacity information transparently.

**Measurement:**
```
Bullwhip Ratio = Variance of Orders / Variance of Demand
  Ratio = 1.0: No amplification (perfect)
  Ratio > 1.0: Bullwhip present (orders more variable than demand)
  Ratio < 1.0: Dampening (orders smoother than demand, usually due to inventory buffering)
```

**Common misapplication:** Blaming the bullwhip effect on downstream customers.
The amplification is a system property created by the ordering policies and
information architecture of the entire chain. Fixing it requires system-level
changes, not finger-pointing.

### Framework 11: Supplier Evaluation Matrix (Weighted Scorecard)

**What:** A quantitative framework for evaluating and comparing suppliers across
multiple performance dimensions.

**When to use:** New supplier selection, annual supplier reviews, supplier
rationalization, sourcing decisions.

**Standard evaluation dimensions:**

| Dimension | Weight (adjust per category) | Metrics |
|-----------|----------------------------|---------|
| Quality | 25% | Defect rate (PPM), first pass yield, quality certifications (ISO 9001), corrective action responsiveness |
| Cost | 20% | Unit price, TCO, cost reduction initiatives, price stability |
| Delivery | 20% | On-time delivery rate, lead time, lead time variability, delivery flexibility |
| Capacity & Capability | 15% | Technical capability, capacity utilization, R&D investment, scalability |
| Financial Health | 10% | Revenue trend, profitability, debt ratio, credit rating |
| Risk | 10% | Geographic concentration, single points of failure, business continuity plans, compliance record |

**Scoring:** Rate each metric 1-5. Multiply by weight. Sum for total score.

**Classification:**
- 90-100: Strategic partner. Invest in the relationship. Collaborative development.
- 75-89: Preferred supplier. Grow the business with them. Support improvement.
- 60-74: Approved supplier. Monitor closely. Improvement plan required.
- Below 60: At risk. Develop alternative source. Phase out if no improvement.

**Common misapplication:** Using the scorecard as a checkbox exercise without
consequence. If a supplier scores below 60 and nothing happens, the entire
evaluation process loses credibility. Scores must drive action.

### Framework 12: Theory of Constraints (TOC) for Supply Chains

**What:** Eli Goldratt's theory that every system has one constraint (bottleneck)
that limits throughput. Improving anything other than the constraint does not improve
the system.

**When to use:** Identifying the limiting factor in a supply chain. Prioritizing
improvement efforts. Capacity planning. Production scheduling.

**The 5 Focusing Steps:**

1. **Identify** the constraint. Where does work pile up? What resource is at 100%
   utilization while others have idle time? In a supply chain, the constraint could
   be a machine, a supplier, a warehouse dock, a customs clearance process, or
   transportation capacity.

2. **Exploit** the constraint. Get maximum throughput from it without spending money.
   Reduce setup times. Eliminate downtime. Ensure the constraint never starves for input.
   Ensure the constraint never produces defects (rework wastes constraint capacity).

3. **Subordinate** everything else to the constraint. Non-constraints should run at
   the pace of the constraint. Running non-constraints faster just builds WIP inventory
   before the bottleneck.

4. **Elevate** the constraint. Now spend money. Add capacity at the bottleneck. Add a
   shift. Buy another machine. Qualify another supplier. Expand the dock.

5. **Repeat.** Once you elevate, the constraint moves. Find the new constraint and
   start again.

**Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR) scheduling:**
- Drum: The constraint sets the pace (the drumbeat of the system)
- Buffer: Time buffer before the constraint to protect it from upstream variability
- Rope: Signal from the constraint to the starting point to control WIP release

**Common misapplication:** Trying to improve everything simultaneously. If you have
a bottleneck at the packing station, investing in faster picking equipment does not
help. It just builds more WIP between picking and packing. Find the bottleneck first.
Everything else follows.

## Decision Frameworks

### Decision Type: Sourcing Strategy (Sole Source vs. Dual Source vs. Multi-Source)

**Consider:**
- Supply criticality: How severely does a disruption impact operations?
- Supplier market concentration: How many qualified suppliers exist?
- Volume leverage: Does consolidating volume give meaningful price advantage?
- Risk tolerance: What is the cost of a supply failure?
- Total cost of managing multiple suppliers vs. risk reduction benefit

**Default recommendation:** Dual source for all critical materials. Target 70/30 or 60/40 split
to maintain competitive tension while giving the primary supplier enough volume to invest in
the relationship. Sole source only for patented or highly specialized components where no
alternative exists.

**Override conditions:** When a single supplier offers a cost or capability advantage so
large that the economics overwhelm the risk. In this case, maintain a qualified backup
supplier (tested and approved, even if receiving zero volume) and hold additional safety stock.

### Decision Type: Push vs. Pull Inventory Strategy

**Consider:**
- Lead time vs. customer tolerance time: If lead time > what customer will wait, you must hold inventory (push). If lead time < customer tolerance, you can make-to-order (pull).
- Demand variability: High variability favors pull (avoid overproduction). Low variability tolerates push.
- Product value: High-value items favor pull (reduces carrying cost). Low-value items tolerate push.
- Product customization: High customization favors pull with postponement. Standard products tolerate push.

**Default recommendation:** Push the decoupling point as far downstream as possible. Use
push for standard components and pull for finished goods customization. The ideal is a
hybrid where you push to a strategic inventory position and pull from there based on
actual demand.

**Override conditions:** When customer service requirements demand immediate availability
(e.g., medical devices, emergency parts), push finished goods inventory closer to the
customer even if it is more expensive.

### Decision Type: In-House Warehousing vs. 3PL

**Consider:**
- Volume stability: Is throughput consistent enough to justify fixed facility costs?
- Geographic coverage: Do you need presence in regions where you lack it?
- Capital availability: Do you have capital for facilities, equipment, and WMS?
- Core competency: Is warehousing central to your competitive advantage?
- Flexibility need: Do you need to scale up/down quickly (seasonal, growth uncertainty)?
- Control requirements: Do you need tight control over operations (pharma, high-value)?

**Default recommendation:** Use 3PL when entering new markets, during rapid growth phases,
or when volume is unpredictable. Bring in-house when volume justifies it and operations
are a competitive differentiator.

**Override conditions:** When the 3PL is handling your largest customer relationships
and you are losing visibility and control over service quality. Customer experience
is too important to outsource without robust SLAs and real-time monitoring.

### Decision Type: Transportation Mode Selection

**Consider:**
- Speed requirement: How fast must it arrive?
- Cost sensitivity: What can the product margin support?
- Volume: Full truckload, less-than-truckload, parcel?
- Distance: Domestic, cross-border, international?
- Product characteristics: Weight, dimensions, fragility, temperature sensitivity, hazmat?

**Mode comparison:**

| Mode | Speed | Cost per kg (rough) | Best for |
|------|-------|-------------------|----------|
| Air freight | 1-3 days | $4-8/kg | Urgent, high-value, low-weight |
| Express parcel | 1-5 days | $5-15/kg | Small shipments, e-commerce |
| Full truckload (FTL) | 1-5 days domestic | $0.04-0.08/kg | Full loads, domestic |
| Less-than-truckload (LTL) | 3-7 days | $0.08-0.15/kg | Partial loads, pallets |
| Intermodal (rail+truck) | 5-10 days | $0.03-0.06/kg | Heavy, non-urgent, long distance |
| Ocean container (FCL) | 20-45 days | $0.02-0.05/kg | International, bulk, non-urgent |
| Ocean LCL | 25-50 days | $0.04-0.08/kg | International, small volumes |

**Default recommendation:** Default to the slowest (cheapest) mode that meets the
service requirement. Ship by ocean when you can. Ship by air only when you must.
The difference between ocean and air freight on a container of goods can be $20,000+.

**Override conditions:** When product margin is high enough that the cost of faster
shipping is negligible compared to revenue impact of late delivery. Or when
inventory carrying costs of holding more pipeline stock exceed the shipping premium.

### Decision Type: Inventory Segmentation Strategy

**Consider the ABC-XYZ matrix:**

| | X (Stable demand) | Y (Variable demand) | Z (Erratic demand) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A (High value)** | AX: Tight control, JIT, VMI, frequent replenishment | AY: Safety stock + demand sensing, moderate buffer | AZ: Make-to-order if possible, strategic safety stock |
| **B (Medium value)** | BX: Standard replenishment, moderate review cycle | BY: Statistical safety stock, regular forecast review | BZ: Higher safety stock or postponement |
| **C (Low value)** | CX: Automate replenishment, min/max | CY: Higher safety stock, less frequent review | CZ: Consider stocking vs. drop-ship, accept higher stock |

**Default recommendation:** Invest planning effort proportional to the ABC-XYZ
classification. AX and AY items get daily attention. CX and CZ items get automated
rules. The danger is spending equal effort on all SKUs.

## Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

### Customer-Facing KPIs

**On-Time In-Full (OTIF):**
```
OTIF = (Orders Delivered On-Time AND In-Full) / Total Orders x 100

Benchmark: 95%+ for most industries
World class: 98%+
```
OTIF is the single most important supply chain KPI. It measures what the customer
actually experiences. A company with 98% on-time and 97% in-full has an OTIF of
~95% (0.98 x 0.97). Both dimensions matter.

**Perfect Order Rate:**
```
Perfect Order = On-time + In-full + Damage-free + Correct documentation
Perfect Order Rate = Perfect Orders / Total Orders x 100

Benchmark: 90%+
World class: 95%+
```

**Order Cycle Time:**
```
Order Cycle Time = Order Receipt to Customer Delivery (calendar days)
```
Measures end-to-end speed. Break into components: order processing time, production
time (if make-to-order), pick/pack time, transit time.

**Customer Fill Rate:**
```
Line Fill Rate = Lines Shipped Complete / Total Lines Ordered x 100
Unit Fill Rate = Units Shipped / Units Ordered x 100
```

### Operational KPIs

**Inventory Turns:**
```
Inventory Turns = Annual COGS / Average Inventory Value

Benchmark varies hugely by industry:
  Grocery: 15-25 turns
  Retail apparel: 4-6 turns
  Industrial manufacturing: 6-10 turns
  Heavy equipment: 2-4 turns
```

**Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO):**
```
DIO = (Average Inventory / COGS) x 365
```
Lower is better, but too low creates stockout risk. Target depends on lead times
and service level requirements.

**Warehouse Productivity:**
```
Lines Picked per Hour per Picker
Cost per Order Shipped
Orders Shipped per Labor Hour
Warehouse Utilization = Used Cubic Space / Total Cubic Space
  Target: 80-85% (100% means no room to operate)
```

**Transportation KPIs:**
```
Cost per Unit Shipped
Cost as % of Revenue (benchmark: 3-8% depending on industry)
Truck Utilization = Actual Load / Truck Capacity x 100 (target: 85%+)
On-Time Pickup Rate
Claims Ratio = Freight Claims $ / Total Freight $ (target: < 0.5%)
```

**Manufacturing KPIs:**

**Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE):**
```
OEE = Availability x Performance x Quality

Availability = Run Time / Planned Production Time
Performance = (Ideal Cycle Time x Total Count) / Run Time
Quality = Good Count / Total Count

World class OEE: 85%+
Average OEE: 60%
```
OEE below 60% signals significant improvement opportunity. Focus on the lowest
scoring factor first.

### Financial KPIs

**Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time:**
```
C2C = DIO + DSO - DPO

DIO = Days Inventory Outstanding
DSO = Days Sales Outstanding (how fast customers pay you)
DPO = Days Payable Outstanding (how slow you pay suppliers)
```
A negative C2C means you collect from customers before paying suppliers. Amazon
operates with negative C2C. This is the holy grail of supply chain finance.

**Supply Chain Cost as % of Revenue:**
```
Total SC Cost = Procurement + Manufacturing + Warehousing + Transportation + Returns
Benchmark: 5-15% of revenue depending on industry
```

**Cost-to-Serve by Customer/Channel:**
```
Allocate all supply chain costs to specific customers or channels.
Reveals which customers are profitable after logistics costs.
Often shows that 20% of customers consume 80% of logistics cost.
```

## Quality Standards

### The Supply Chain Quality Bar

Every supply chain deliverable must pass three tests:

1. **The Numbers Test.** Every recommendation includes quantified impact. Cost savings
   in dollars, not percentages alone. Service level targets with current baseline.
   Inventory reduction in units, dollars, and days. If you cannot quantify it, you
   cannot manage it.

2. **The Feasibility Test.** Recommendations work within the physical, financial, and
   organizational constraints of the real operation. A warehouse redesign that requires
   demolishing a wall is feasible. One that requires demolishing the building is not.
   Consider labor availability, technology readiness, capital budget, and change
   management capacity.

3. **The Risk Test.** Every recommendation identifies what could go wrong and how to
   handle it. A sourcing change that saves $2M but has a 30% chance of a 6-week
   supply disruption during transition needs a mitigation plan. The risk of doing
   nothing must also be quantified.

### Deliverable-Specific Standards

**Supply Chain Assessment / Diagnostic:**
- Must include: Current state performance (KPIs with benchmarks), gap analysis, prioritized improvement opportunities, estimated financial impact, implementation roadmap
- Must avoid: Generic best practices without current state context, recommendations that ignore organizational capability, laundry lists without prioritization
- Gold standard: A diagnostic that identifies $X million in addressable opportunity ranked by effort-to-impact ratio with a phased implementation plan

**Inventory Optimization Plan:**
- Must include: SKU-level analysis (or at minimum ABC-XYZ segmentation), current vs. recommended inventory levels with formulas, expected service level impact, cash release calculation, implementation timeline
- Must avoid: Blanket inventory reduction targets without SKU-level analysis, ignoring lead time variability, recommending cuts without service level trade-off analysis
- Gold standard: An inventory plan that simultaneously improves service levels AND reduces inventory investment by reallocating stock from overstocked C items to understocked A items

**Network Design Recommendation:**
- Must include: Scenario comparison (cost, service level, risk, capital), sensitivity analysis on key assumptions (fuel cost, demand growth, labor rates), implementation timeline with milestones, transition risk mitigation plan
- Must avoid: Single-scenario analysis, ignoring tax and incentive implications of facility location, failing to account for the inventory impact of adding/removing nodes
- Gold standard: A network design that quantifies total landed cost by customer segment under multiple demand scenarios

**Supplier Sourcing Strategy:**
- Must include: Category spend analysis, supplier market assessment, TCO comparison, risk assessment, negotiation strategy, recommended supplier portfolio with rationale
- Must avoid: Selecting suppliers on price alone, ignoring switching costs, failing to assess supplier financial health, recommending sole source for critical categories without risk mitigation
- Gold standard: A sourcing strategy that reduces TCO, improves supply security, and includes a negotiation playbook with BATNA analysis

### Quality Checklist (used in Pipeline Stage 5)
- [ ] All financial claims quantified with stated assumptions
- [ ] KPIs include current baseline and target with benchmark references
- [ ] Recommendations are specific and actionable (who, what, when, how much)
- [ ] Risks identified with probability, impact, and mitigation strategies
- [ ] Implementation timeline is realistic given organizational constraints
- [ ] Formulas and calculations shown (not just results)
- [ ] Data sources identified and methodology transparent
- [ ] Trade-offs explicitly stated (cost vs. service, risk vs. efficiency)
- [ ] Change management requirements acknowledged
- [ ] Technology requirements specified with realistic implementation timelines

## Communication Standards

### Structure

Supply chain deliverables follow the Pyramid Principle with a quantitative emphasis:

1. **Executive summary** -- Lead with the answer. "We recommend consolidating from 6
   DCs to 3, which will save $4.2M annually while maintaining 97% OTIF." One page maximum.
2. **Current state assessment** -- Data-driven baseline with KPIs and benchmarks.
3. **Analysis and findings** -- The work that supports the recommendation.
4. **Recommendations** -- Specific actions, owners, timelines, expected outcomes.
5. **Implementation roadmap** -- Phased plan with milestones, resources, risks.
6. **Financial impact** -- Cost-benefit analysis, ROI, payback period.

### Tone

- **Data-driven and precise.** Supply chain professionals respect numbers. Lead with data.
- **Operationally grounded.** Use language that resonates on the warehouse floor and in the boardroom. Avoid purely academic frameworks without operational translation.
- **Direct about trade-offs.** Supply chain is full of trade-offs. Name them explicitly. "Reducing inventory by $5M will decrease fill rate from 97% to 94% on B-items."
- **Pragmatic about implementation.** Acknowledge the difficulty of change. A perfect plan poorly executed loses to a good plan well executed.

### Audience Adaptation

**For C-Suite / Board:**
- Focus on financial impact: cost savings, cash release from inventory, revenue at risk
- Frame in strategic terms: competitive advantage, customer service, resilience
- 3-5 slides maximum. One page executive summary.
- Express inventory in dollars and days, not units

**For Supply Chain / Operations Leaders:**
- Include methodology and formulas
- Show sensitivity analysis and scenarios
- Detail implementation requirements: systems, people, process changes
- Discuss trade-offs and constraints explicitly

**For Warehouse / Plant Floor Managers:**
- Focus on what changes in daily operations
- Provide SOPs and work instructions
- Include training requirements and timeline
- Use visual process flows, not text-heavy documents

**For Finance:**
- Show working capital impact (inventory reduction = cash release)
- Include P&L impact by line item
- Map to budget cycles and approval processes
- Show payback period and IRR for capital investments

### Language Conventions

**Use:** OTIF, fill rate, lead time, safety stock, EOQ, carrying cost, TCO, MOQ,
SKU, BOM, WIP, COGS, landed cost, dwell time, cross-dock, milk run, VMI, CPFR,
Incoterms, FTL, LTL, FCL, LCL, OEE, DIO, C2C, 3PL, RFP, RFQ

**Define on first use for non-supply-chain audiences:** These terms are standard
among practitioners. When communicating with executives, finance, or other functions,
define acronyms on first use.

**Avoid:** Vague language without quantification ("significant improvement," "major
savings," "best in class"). Every claim needs a number. If you do not have a precise
number, provide a range with stated assumptions.

## Specialized Knowledge Areas

### Warehouse Operations Deep Dive

**Layout Design Principles:**

1. **Flow pattern selection:**
   - U-flow: Receiving and shipping on same side. Best for cross-docking. Efficient dock utilization.
   - I-flow (through-flow): Receiving on one end, shipping on other. Best when high volume separates inbound and outbound.
   - L-flow: Receiving and shipping on adjacent sides. Compromise when space constraints exist.

2. **Slotting optimization:**
   - Place fast-moving items (A items) closest to shipping docks at waist height (ergonomic pick zone)
   - Group items frequently ordered together in adjacent locations
   - Reserve floor-level and top-level slots for slow-moving or bulky items
   - Re-slot quarterly based on demand changes

3. **Pick methodology selection:**

| Method | Best for | Productivity |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Discrete (single order) | Low volume, high accuracy needed | 50-80 lines/hour |
| Batch picking | Many orders with common items | 100-150 lines/hour |
| Zone picking | Large warehouse, many SKUs | 120-180 lines/hour |
| Wave picking | Combining batch + zone + schedule | 150-200 lines/hour |
| Goods-to-person (AS/RS) | High volume, high SKU count | 200-400 lines/hour |

4. **Warehouse automation decision framework:**
   - Manual operations: < 1,000 orders/day, low capital budget, high product variability
   - Semi-automated (conveyors, pick-to-light): 1,000-5,000 orders/day, moderate investment
   - Highly automated (AS/RS, goods-to-person, AMRs): > 5,000 orders/day, large capital budget, stable product mix
   - Robotic fulfillment (full automation): > 20,000 orders/day, very high capital, standardized products

### International Trade and Global Logistics

**Incoterms 2020 -- Key Terms:**

| Term | Risk Transfer Point | Seller's Obligation | Buyer's Obligation |
|------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| EXW (Ex Works) | Seller's premises | Make goods available | Everything from pickup |
| FOB (Free On Board) | On board vessel at port | Deliver to vessel, export clearance | Ocean freight, insurance, import |
| CIF (Cost Insurance Freight) | On board vessel at origin (risk), destination (cost) | Ocean freight + insurance to destination | Import clearance, inland delivery |
| DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) | Buyer's premises | Everything including import duties | Accept delivery |
| DAP (Delivered At Place) | Named destination | Deliver to named place, unloaded | Import clearance, unloading |

**Incoterm selection heuristic:**
- You control logistics, you want risk managed: Use DDP (buyer perspective) or FOB (seller perspective)
- You have no logistics capability in origin country: Use FOB or CIF from the supplier
- You want maximum control: Use EXW (but you handle everything)
- You want simplicity: Use DDP (supplier handles everything)

**Landed Cost Calculation:**
```
Landed Cost = Product Cost + International Freight + Insurance + Customs Duties
            + Brokerage Fees + Port/Terminal Handling + Inland Transportation
            + Inspection/Testing + Currency Conversion Costs

Duties = Product Value x Duty Rate (check HTS code for applicable rate)
```

**Free Trade Zone (FTZ) Benefits:**
- Defer duties on imported goods until they enter domestic commerce
- Eliminate duties on re-exported goods (never enter domestic commerce)
- Reduce duty rates through manufacturing in FTZ (pay on finished good rate, which may be lower)
- Cash flow benefit: duty deferral reduces working capital need

### Last-Mile Delivery Economics

**The last mile is the most expensive mile.** It accounts for 40-55% of total
delivery cost. Understanding last-mile economics is essential for any B2C or
D2C supply chain.

**Key cost drivers:**
- Delivery density: Orders per square mile per delivery window
- Failed delivery rate: Each failed attempt costs $5-15
- Returns pickup: Adding returns to delivery routes increases cost 10-15%
- Time window requirements: Narrow windows (2-hour) cost 2-3x vs. all-day windows
- Urban vs. suburban vs. rural: Urban density is 3-5x more efficient per stop

**Cost per delivery benchmarks:**
- Dense urban, standard delivery: $3-5
- Suburban, standard delivery: $5-8
- Rural, standard delivery: $8-15
- Same-day urban: $8-15
- Same-day suburban: $12-25

**Delivery density formula:**
```
Stops per Route Hour = f(Density, Dwell Time, Drive Time Between Stops)
Cost per Delivery = (Driver Cost/Hour + Vehicle Cost/Hour) / Stops per Route Hour
```

**Strategies to improve last-mile economics:**
1. Increase delivery density: batch orders to same area into same delivery window
2. Offer pickup points (lockers, partner locations) to reduce individual deliveries
3. Dynamic routing: real-time route optimization based on actual order pool
4. Right-size vehicles: use vans for dense urban, trucks for suburban, gig drivers for rural
5. Incentivize flexible delivery: offer discounts for wider delivery windows

### Reverse Logistics and Returns Management

**Returns are a profit leak that most supply chains ignore.** In e-commerce,
return rates range from 15-30%. Each return costs $10-25 to process. The math
adds up fast.

**Returns processing workflow:**
1. **Authorization:** Customer initiates return (RMA). Capture reason code. Determine if return is necessary (some items cost more to return than to replace).
2. **Transportation:** Prepaid label, drop-off location, or pickup. Consolidate returns shipments where possible.
3. **Receiving and inspection:** Grade the returned item. Categories: A (resellable as-is), B (refurbish/repackage), C (liquidate/secondary market), D (recycle/dispose).
4. **Disposition:** Execute the disposition decision. Speed matters. Every day a return sits unprocessed, it loses value.
5. **Credit/replacement:** Process the customer credit or ship replacement.
6. **Root cause analysis:** Why did the customer return it? Feed data back to product, marketing, and sourcing teams.

**Return rate reduction strategies:**
- Improve product descriptions and images (reduce "not as expected" returns)
- Size guides and virtual try-on for apparel (reduce fit-related returns)
- Better packaging to reduce damage in transit
- Quality control at source to reduce defective arrivals
- Bracketing prevention (customers ordering multiple sizes/colors to try)

**Circular supply chain considerations:**
- Design for disassembly: products that can be efficiently broken down for recycling
- Refurbishment programs: extend product life through repair and resale
- Take-back programs: manufacturer accepts returns for recycling/reuse
- Secondary markets: liquidation channels for returned/excess inventory

### Supply Chain Finance

**Working Capital Optimization:**

Working capital in supply chain = Inventory + Accounts Receivable - Accounts Payable

Every day of improvement in any component releases cash:
```
Cash Released per Day of DIO Improvement = Annual COGS / 365
Cash Released per Day of DSO Improvement = Annual Revenue / 365
Cash Consumed per Day of DPO Reduction = Annual Purchases / 365
```

**Example:** A company with $100M COGS. Reducing DIO by 10 days releases $100M/365 x 10 = $2.74M in cash.

**Supply Chain Finance Instruments:**

1. **Reverse factoring (supply chain financing):** Buyer's bank pays supplier early at a discount, buyer pays bank on normal terms. Supplier gets faster payment. Buyer extends DPO without harming supplier.
   - Typical discount: 1-3% for 30-60 days early payment
   - Benefit to supplier: cheaper than their own financing (uses buyer's credit rating)
   - Benefit to buyer: extends payment terms, strengthens supplier relationships

2. **Dynamic discounting:** Buyer offers to pay early in exchange for a discount. The earlier the payment, the larger the discount. Buyer earns a return on cash. Supplier gets liquidity.
   - Typical structure: 2/10 net 30 (2% discount for paying in 10 days vs. 30 days)
   - Annualized return for buyer: 2% for 20 days early = 36.5% annualized return on cash

3. **Inventory financing:** Lender provides financing against inventory as collateral. Useful for seasonal businesses that need to build inventory before peak season.

4. **Purchase order financing:** Lender advances funds against confirmed purchase orders. Useful when a company receives a large order but lacks capital to fulfill it.

**Payment Terms Negotiation:**
- Know your leverage: large buyers can push for 60-90 day terms. Small buyers may accept 30 days.
- Consider supplier health: pushing a critical supplier to financial distress is self-destructive.
- Link terms to volume commitments: "We will guarantee $5M annual spend if you accept 60-day terms."
- Use supply chain finance to make extended terms palatable to suppliers.

### Supply Chain Technology Landscape

**Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP):**
- SAP S/4HANA, Oracle Cloud ERP, Microsoft Dynamics 365
- The backbone. Manages master data, transactions, financials.
- ERP is necessary. ERP alone is insufficient for supply chain excellence.

**Supply Chain Management (SCM) Suites:**
- Kinaxis RapidResponse, Blue Yonder (JDA), o9 Solutions, SAP IBP
- Planning layer: demand planning, supply planning, S&OP, inventory optimization
- These sit on top of ERP and provide the analytical and planning capabilities ERP lacks.

**Warehouse Management System (WMS):**
- Manhattan Associates, Blue Yonder, SAP EWM, Oracle WMS
- Manages inventory within the warehouse: receiving, putaway, picking, packing, shipping
- Critical when warehouse complexity exceeds what ERP can handle (multiple pick methods, wave planning, labor management)

**Transportation Management System (TMS):**
- Oracle OTM, Blue Yonder TMS, MercuryGate, project44
- Route optimization, carrier selection, freight audit, shipment visibility
- ROI typically 5-15% reduction in transportation spend

**IoT and Sensor Technology:**
- Temperature monitoring for cold chain (pharmaceutical, food)
- GPS tracking for fleet and shipment visibility
- RFID for inventory accuracy (replaces manual cycle counts)
- Predictive maintenance sensors for manufacturing equipment

**Control Tower / Visibility Platforms:**
- FourKites, project44, Overhaul
- End-to-end visibility across the supply chain
- Exception-based management: alerts when shipments deviate from plan
- Increasingly using AI for predictive ETAs and proactive issue resolution

**Supply Chain Digital Twin:**
- Create a virtual replica of the physical supply chain
- Run simulations and scenarios without disrupting operations
- Test network changes, demand scenarios, risk events
- Still emerging technology. Most valuable for complex, multi-echelon networks.

## Validation Methods (used in Pipeline Stage 6)

### Method 1: Scenario Stress Test

**What it tests:** Robustness of supply chain recommendations under different conditions.

**How to apply:**
1. Define 3-5 plausible scenarios that would stress the recommendation:
   - Demand surge (+30% above forecast)
   - Supply disruption (key supplier offline for 6 weeks)
   - Cost shock (freight rates increase 50%)
   - Demand collapse (-30% below forecast)
   - Lead time extension (ocean transit +2 weeks)
2. Model the impact of each scenario on the recommended plan
3. Identify which scenarios break the plan
4. Build contingency triggers: "If X happens, execute contingency plan Y"

**Pass criteria:** Recommendation remains viable (positive ROI, acceptable service level)
under at least 3 of 5 scenarios. For scenarios that break the plan, contingency actions
are defined with trigger points.

### Method 2: Total Cost Validation

**What it tests:** Whether the financial analysis captures all relevant costs.

**How to apply:**
1. List every cost category affected by the recommendation
2. For each category, verify: Is this cost included in the analysis? Is the estimate
   based on data or assumption? What is the confidence level?
3. Add 10-20% contingency for unforeseen costs (supply chain projects consistently
   underestimate implementation costs)
4. Validate unit costs against industry benchmarks or actual quotes
5. Check: are there costs shifted to other functions that are not captured? (e.g.,
   inventory reduction that increases expedited freight)

**Pass criteria:** All material cost categories included. No single unverified assumption
represents more than 15% of total benefit. Total benefit exceeds total cost by at least
1.5x even with contingency.

### Method 3: Operational Feasibility Check

**What it tests:** Whether the recommendation can be physically executed.

**How to apply:**
1. Walk through the recommendation step by step as if implementing it tomorrow
2. For each step, identify: Who does this? Do they have the skills? Do they have the time?
3. Check system requirements: Does the current ERP/WMS/TMS support this? If not,
   what changes are needed?
4. Check physical constraints: warehouse capacity, dock doors, equipment, labor
   availability
5. Check regulatory constraints: customs requirements, safety regulations, permits

**Pass criteria:** Every implementation step has an identified owner with confirmed
capability. System and physical constraints are addressed in the plan. No regulatory
blockers without a defined resolution path.

### Method 4: Historical Pattern Validation

**What it tests:** Whether recommendations align with or reasonably depart from
historical performance patterns.

**How to apply:**
1. Pull 2-3 years of historical data for the relevant metrics
2. Check: Do the improvement targets align with what similar initiatives have achieved?
3. Look for seasonal patterns that might affect implementation timing
4. Identify past initiatives in this area: what worked, what failed, and why?
5. Validate demand assumptions against historical trends and known future events

**Pass criteria:** Improvement targets are within 2x of historical best performance
or comparable industry benchmarks. If targets exceed historical precedent, the plan
includes specific capabilities or changes that justify the stretch.

### Method 5: Stakeholder Impact Assessment

**What it tests:** Whether the recommendation accounts for all affected parties.

**How to apply:**
1. Map every stakeholder affected: suppliers, customers, warehouse teams, transport
   teams, finance, IT, senior leadership
2. For each stakeholder: What changes for them? What do they need to do differently?
   What is their likely reaction?
3. Identify potential resistance points and change management requirements
4. Verify that customer-facing changes have been evaluated for service impact
5. Confirm supplier changes have been discussed or socialized

**Pass criteria:** No stakeholder group experiences a negative surprise. Change
management plan exists for groups with significant process changes. Customer
service impact is neutral or positive.

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Single Source Dependency**
   What it looks like: 100% of a critical component from one supplier, often justified by "they give us the best price." No qualified backup.
   Why it is harmful: One supplier disruption (fire, bankruptcy, natural disaster, quality failure) shuts down your production line. The cost of a 4-week shutdown dwarfs any price savings.
   Instead: Dual source all critical materials. Maintain at least one qualified backup supplier. Hold strategic safety stock calibrated to supplier lead time plus qualification time for backup.

2. **Ignoring Total Cost of Ownership**
   What it looks like: Procurement optimizing for purchase price. Selecting the cheapest supplier without considering freight, quality costs, inventory carrying costs, or risk.
   Why it is harmful: The "cheapest" supplier often costs 15-25% more when all costs are included. Quality issues cascade through the supply chain. Longer lead times require more safety stock.
   Instead: Mandate TCO analysis for all sourcing decisions above a threshold (e.g., $100K annual spend). Include all 7 cost categories from the TCO framework.

3. **Reactive Inventory Management**
   What it looks like: Ordering when you run out or when the buyer "feels" like it is time. No safety stock calculations. No systematic reorder points. Frequent stockouts followed by panic buying.
   Why it is harmful: Oscillates between stockouts (lost sales, expediting costs) and overstocks (carrying costs, obsolescence). Generates the bullwhip effect upstream.
   Instead: Implement calculated reorder points and safety stock levels using the formulas in Framework 3. Automate replenishment for stable-demand items. Reserve human judgment for exceptions.

4. **No Demand Signal Integration**
   What it looks like: The supply chain operates on a forecast that was created 30-60 days ago by the sales team. No visibility to actual POS data, customer inventory levels, or leading demand indicators.
   Why it is harmful: The supply chain responds to signals that are weeks old. By the time the plan adjusts, demand has already shifted. Every level of the supply chain adds its own lag.
   Instead: Connect to downstream demand signals. POS data, customer forecasts, channel inventory. Implement demand sensing. Shorten the planning cycle from monthly to weekly where possible.

5. **Over-Reliance on Spreadsheets**
   What it looks like: The demand plan is in Excel. The inventory model is in Excel. The transportation plan is in Excel. Each maintained by one person who is the only one who understands the formulas.
   Why it is harmful: Spreadsheets break at scale. Version control is nonexistent. Key-person risk is extreme. Errors compound undetected. Scenario analysis is manual and slow.
   Instead: Spreadsheets are fine for prototyping and one-time analysis. For recurring planning processes, implement purpose-built tools (SCM suite, WMS, TMS). The investment pays for itself in accuracy, speed, and reduced key-person risk.

6. **Siloed Planning**
   What it looks like: Sales has a demand forecast. Operations has a production plan. Finance has a budget. None of them match. Nobody meets to reconcile.
   Why it is harmful: The company operates on three different plans. Sales promises delivery dates that operations cannot meet. Finance budgets for inventory levels that do not exist. Everyone is surprised when results do not match expectations.
   Instead: Implement S&OP (Framework 5). One number. One plan. Monthly reconciliation with executive decision-making authority. The S&OP process is the alignment mechanism.

7. **Ignoring Reverse Logistics**
   What it looks like: All attention on the forward supply chain. Returns handled as an afterthought. No returns processing infrastructure. Returned inventory sits in a corner of the warehouse for months.
   Why it is harmful: Returns are 15-30% of e-commerce volume. Unprocessed returns lose value daily (especially electronics, fashion, perishables). Return costs erode margins that the forward supply chain worked hard to generate.
   Instead: Design the returns process with the same rigor as the forward flow. Invest in returns processing capability. Track returns by reason code and feed insights back to product and sourcing. Establish disposition channels (refurbish, liquidate, recycle) and execute quickly.

8. **Manual Order Processing at Scale**
   What it looks like: Orders are received via email or phone, manually entered into the ERP, manually checked for inventory, manually allocated. At 50 orders/day this is manageable. At 500/day it breaks.
   Why it is harmful: Human error rate in manual data entry is 1-3%. At scale, this means dozens of incorrect orders per day. Processing speed is limited by staffing. Scaling requires linear headcount increase.
   Instead: Implement EDI, API-based order integration, or an order management system. Automate the routine (standard orders from known customers). Reserve human attention for exceptions (new customers, unusual quantities, special requirements).

9. **Gold-Plating Service Levels**
   What it looks like: Offering same-day or next-day delivery to all customers regardless of profitability. Holding 99.9% fill rate targets on all items. Maintaining every possible SKU "just in case."
   Why it is harmful: The last percentage point of service level costs exponentially more. Going from 95% to 99% fill rate might double safety stock. Same-day delivery to rural customers can cost $25/order on a $30 order.
   Instead: Segment service levels by customer value and product criticality. Premium service for premium customers. Standard service as the default. Match the supply chain cost to the customer value it serves.

10. **Technology-First, Process-Second**
    What it looks like: Buying a WMS, TMS, or demand planning tool before understanding the current process. Expecting software to fix a broken process.
    Why it is harmful: Technology amplifies whatever process it is applied to. If the process is broken, technology makes it fail faster. WMS implementations have a 30-40% failure rate, mostly due to poor process design before implementation.
    Instead: Map the current process. Design the future process. Then select technology that enables the future process. Process first, technology second. Always.

## Ethical Boundaries

1. **No guaranteed savings or service levels.** Supply chain performance depends on execution, market conditions, supplier behavior, and demand patterns. Frame all projections as estimates with ranges and assumptions. Never guarantee specific outcomes.

2. **No supplier manipulation.** Negotiation leverage is legitimate. Providing false information to suppliers (fake competitive quotes, fabricated demand volumes, false urgency) is unethical and destroys trust that takes years to rebuild.

3. **No exploitation of information asymmetry to harm trading partners.** Using privileged information about a supplier's financial distress to extract punitive terms is short-sighted and unethical. Healthy suppliers serve you better than desperate ones.

4. **No recommendations that externalize costs to communities or environment without acknowledgment.** Recommending a warehouse in a low-cost area must acknowledge impact on local infrastructure, labor market, and environment. Routing decisions that push emissions to disadvantaged communities must be flagged.

5. **Transparency about uncertainty.** When data is insufficient for a confident recommendation, say so. Provide the best analysis possible and identify what additional data would strengthen the recommendation. Do not present speculation as analysis.

6. **Worker safety is non-negotiable.** No cost reduction recommendation should compromise worker safety. Ergonomics, training, equipment maintenance, and safety standards are costs that pay for themselves and should never be cut.

7. **No substitution for licensed professionals.** Supply chain recommendations that touch customs law, labor law, environmental regulation, or food/drug safety must include a disclaimer and referral to qualified professionals.

### Required Disclaimers

- Cost projections: "These savings estimates are based on stated assumptions and current market conditions. Actual results may vary based on execution, supplier negotiations, and market fluctuations."
- Network design: "Facility location recommendations should be validated with legal, tax, and real estate professionals before committing to leases or purchases."
- International trade: "This analysis is for planning purposes. Consult a licensed customs broker and trade compliance attorney for specific duty classifications and regulatory requirements."
- Safety stock recommendations: "Service level projections assume demand and lead time distributions consistent with historical data. Significant changes in market conditions may require recalibration."

## Domain-Specific Pipeline Integration

### Stage 1 (Define Challenge): Supply Chain-Specific Guidance

**Questions to ask:**
- What is the specific supply chain problem? (Cost too high? Service levels too low? Too much inventory? Too little visibility? Supply risk? Capacity constraints?)
- What are the current KPIs and how do they compare to benchmarks?
- What is the scope? (End-to-end chain, or a specific function like warehousing or transportation?)
- What has already been tried? What worked and what did not?
- What systems are in place? (ERP, WMS, TMS, planning tools?)
- What data is available? (Historical demand, inventory levels, transportation costs, supplier performance?)
- What constraints exist? (Capital budget, timeline, labor agreements, technology limitations?)
- Who are the stakeholders and what are their priorities? (Finance wants cost reduction. Sales wants service. Operations wants stability.)

**Patterns to look for:**
- Is this a planning problem (forecast, inventory) or an execution problem (warehouse, transport)?
- Is this a capability gap (no tools, no process) or a performance gap (tools exist but underperform)?
- Is this a single-function issue or a cross-functional alignment issue (siloed planning)?
- Are there quick wins (reoptimize existing processes) or does this require structural change (new facilities, new suppliers, new systems)?

### Stage 2 (Design Approach): Supply Chain-Specific Guidance

**Framework selection guide:**
- "Our inventory is too high" -> Inventory Optimization Framework + ABC-XYZ analysis + Safety stock recalculation
- "Our delivery performance is poor" -> SCOR Model assessment + Theory of Constraints + Root cause analysis on OTIF failures
- "We need to reduce supply chain costs" -> TCO analysis + Network Design + Value Stream Mapping + 80/20 on cost categories
- "We are too dependent on one supplier" -> Risk Assessment Matrix + Supplier Evaluation + Dual sourcing strategy
- "We need to redesign our distribution network" -> Network Design Framework + Scenario modeling + Landed cost analysis
- "Our forecast accuracy is terrible" -> Demand Forecasting Method Selection + S&OP process assessment + Demand segmentation
- "We are expanding internationally" -> Incoterms analysis + Landed cost + FTZ evaluation + Global logistics design
- "Our warehouse cannot keep up" -> Warehouse layout redesign + Pick methodology evaluation + Automation assessment
- "We need better visibility" -> Supply chain technology assessment + Control tower evaluation + IoT opportunities

### Stage 3 (Structure Engagement): Supply Chain-Specific Guidance

**Typical engagement structure:**
- **Assessment phase** (Stages 1-2): 25% of effort. Gather data, baseline KPIs, diagnose root causes.
- **Analysis and design phase** (Stage 4): 45% of effort. Model scenarios, build recommendations, quantify impact.
- **Validation phase** (Stages 5-6): 20% of effort. Stress test, validate feasibility, refine.
- **Delivery phase** (Stages 7-8): 10% of effort. Present, plan implementation, hand off.

**Common deliverable types:**
- Supply chain assessment with KPI scorecard and improvement roadmap
- Inventory optimization plan with SKU-level recommendations
- Network design analysis with scenario comparison
- Sourcing strategy with supplier evaluation and negotiation playbook
- S&OP process design with governance model
- Warehouse optimization plan with layout, process, and technology recommendations
- Transportation optimization with mode selection and carrier strategy
- Supply chain risk assessment with mitigation plans
- Business case for supply chain technology investment

### Stage 4 (Create Deliverables): Supply Chain-Specific Guidance

**Analysis standards:**
- Always include current state baseline (you cannot measure improvement without a starting point)
- Financial projections: show 3 scenarios (base, optimistic, conservative) with sensitivity on key assumptions
- Use industry benchmarks to contextualize performance (APICS, Gartner, SCOR benchmarks)
- Show formulas and calculation methodology (builds credibility and enables future updates)
- Include implementation timeline that accounts for system changes, training, and change management
- Visualize data: charts over tables, maps for network design, process flows for warehouse operations

**Data standards:**
- Minimum 12 months of historical data for any trending analysis
- Minimum 24 months for seasonal decomposition
- State the data source and any cleaning/transformation applied
- Identify data quality issues and their potential impact on conclusions
- When data is unavailable, state assumptions and sensitivity-test them

### Stage 5 (Quality Assurance): Supply Chain-Specific Review Criteria

In addition to the universal review checklist:
- [ ] KPIs include current baseline, target, and industry benchmark
- [ ] Formulas are shown and can be independently verified
- [ ] Inventory calculations use appropriate demand and lead time variability measures
- [ ] Cost analysis includes all 7 TCO categories where applicable
- [ ] Network design scenarios include sensitivity analysis on fuel, labor, and demand
- [ ] Supplier recommendations include risk assessment and backup plans
- [ ] Implementation timeline includes system changes, training, and change management
- [ ] Trade-offs are quantified (e.g., "reducing inventory $3M increases stockout risk by X%")
- [ ] Seasonal and cyclical patterns are accounted for in recommendations
- [ ] Data sources and methodology are transparent and verifiable

### Stage 6 (Validate): Supply Chain-Specific Validation

Apply these validation methods:
1. **Scenario Stress Test** -- for all recommendations involving structural changes
2. **Total Cost Validation** -- for all financial analyses and cost reduction claims
3. **Operational Feasibility Check** -- for all implementation plans
4. **Historical Pattern Validation** -- for demand forecasts and performance targets
5. **Stakeholder Impact Assessment** -- for changes affecting multiple functions or external parties

Minimum for Tier 2: Methods 2 + 3
Full suite for Tier 3: All five methods

### Stage 7 (Plan Delivery): Supply Chain-Specific Delivery

**Delivery format guidance:**
- C-Suite audience: Executive summary (1 page) + strategic recommendation deck (5-8 slides) + financial impact summary
- Supply Chain / Operations leadership: Full analysis (15-25 slides) + implementation roadmap + KPI tracking plan
- Warehouse / Plant floor: Visual SOPs, process flow diagrams, training materials
- Finance: Working capital impact model, P&L line item mapping, capital expenditure justification

**Always include:**
- Executive summary that stands alone (the recommendation, the financial impact, the ask)
- Phased implementation roadmap with milestones and owners
- KPI tracking plan with baseline, targets, and measurement frequency
- Risk register with mitigation actions and owners
- Quick wins that can be executed within 30 days to build momentum

### Stage 8 (Deliver): Supply Chain-Specific Follow-up

**After delivery:**
- Offer to support implementation planning in more detail
- Identify 2-3 quick wins that can demonstrate value within 30 days
- Recommend a 30/60/90 day review cadence for tracking implementation progress
- Flag assumptions that should be monitored and when they should be re-evaluated
- Suggest pilot scope for testing recommendations before full rollout
- Note data collection requirements for ongoing performance tracking
- Identify training needs for teams adopting new processes or systems
